## The Moment Europe Pressed the Brakes

While OpenAI launched increasingly powerful models and Big Tech rushed to integrate AI into all their products, the Council of Europe did something different: it slowed the pace and wrote the rules. On June 26, 2024, the European Commission proposed the signing of the **Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence, Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law** — the first internationally binding legal treaty that obliges States to protect their institutions, citizens, and democratic processes against the risks of AI.

It is not a recommendation. It is not a guide. It is a legal commitment.

## Why Europe Could No Longer Wait

The deployment speed of AI systems has outpaced any regulatory framework. But the risks are clear: systems that generate discriminatory profiles, automated surveillance that erodes privacy, manipulation of electoral processes, failures in the administration of justice. The Council of Europe document identifies something that no authoritative body had said so explicitly: **"certain activities within the lifecycle of artificial intelligence systems can undermine human dignity and individual autonomy, human rights, democracy and the Rule of Law."**

That is not speculation. It is the diagnosis of an institution representing 46 European States.

## The Architecture of the Convention: Obligations Without Excuses

The treaty structures obligations in chapters that close usual escape routes:

**Protection of Human Rights (Article 4):** Each State must guarantee that activities within the lifecycle of AI systems are consistent with human rights protection obligations. There is no ambiguity: it is not optional, not "to the extent possible." It is an obligation.

**Defense of Democratic Processes (Article 5):** It explicitly prohibits using AI systems to undermine democratic institutions, elections, separation of powers, and judicial independence. And it goes further: it protects the right of persons to participate in public debate without algorithmic manipulation, to form opinions freely.

**Mandatory Transparency (Article 8):** It requires mechanisms to exist to identify when content is AI-generated, when a decision comes from an algorithm. Opacity ceases to be a technical option to become legal non-compliance.

**Effective Accountability (Article 9):** It is not enough to have good principles. Accountability must be rendered for actual adverse effects. Someone must be responsible.

## What Separates This Treaty from Vague Promises

This is where the document dares to do what others have not: **Article 16 obliges continuous risk assessment and mitigation.** Not before launch. Continuous. With documentation, testing, monitoring. And when the risk is too high — when a use is clearly incompatible with human rights or democracy — **each State will assess the need for a moratorium or prohibition.**

That is new. That is binding.

Additionally, the treaty requires effective independent supervision mechanisms in each State (Article 26). Regulation is not enough. There must be capable, independent, resourced authorities that verify compliance.

And when someone is harmed by an AI system — a discriminatory decision, unwarranted surveillance, manipulation — Article 14 guarantees **accessible and effective remedies.** Affected persons have the right to information about how the decision was made, to challenge it, and to file complaints with competent authorities.

## The Risks the Convention Acknowledges

The document is brutally honest about what could go wrong:

- Discrimination in digital contexts that exacerbates existing inequalities
- Repressive surveillance and censorship that erode privacy and autonomy
- Manipulation of public debates and electoral processes
- Impacts on children and persons in vulnerable situations
- Systemic bias in justice and administration systems
- Environmental risks not considered in most regulations

It does not claim that AI is dangerous in itself. But yes: there are uses that are incompatible with human dignity and democracy. And States have the responsibility to identify them and stop them.

## What Comes in the Complete Convention

The document establishes a monitoring system through a Conference of the Parties that meets periodically. States will report on how they fulfill their obligations. They will exchange information about risks they have detected. They will cooperate in mitigation. If a State seriously breaches, there are mechanisms to make it public.

It also provides for how to apply these obligations to private actors. Not all AI companies are the same: obligations are graduated according to severity and probability of harm to human rights. And in research and development, it allows controlled environments for experimentation, always under the supervision of competent authorities (safe innovation, Article 13).

What you will not find here are broad exceptions that allow States to avoid protections. Article 21 makes clear: nothing in this Convention limits already recognized human rights. And Article 22: any State may adopt stronger protections than those in the Convention.

## The Question Nobody Can Avoid

What happens when the speed of innovation collides head-on with the pace of democracy? Europe responds with this Convention: **we slow down together, define clear limits, and then innovate within those limits.** It is not prohibition. It is responsible regulation.

But there is an unresolved tension: how much does this slow down legitimate research? How do States evaluate risks that lack technical capabilities or specialized knowledge? What is the real scope of a moratorium if each State decides differently?

The Convention proposes answers, but the most uncomfortable question remains: **is Europe building guardrails for AI, or building walls that others will jump over?**

## What Matters Now

- **Entry into Force:** The Convention will enter into force three months after five signatories (including three Council of Europe member States) ratify it. The race for ratification has already begun.

- **Binding Application:** It is not soft law. States that ratify are legally obligated. Non-compliance may lead to proceedings before supervisory bodies.

- **Coexistence with Other Regulations:** It operates in parallel with the EU AI Act, but on a global scale. States outside the EU can also be party.

- **Continuous Supervision:** The Conference of the Parties will periodically evaluate how it is applied. It is not fire and forget. It is living regulation.

- **Implementation Gap:** The Convention establishes obligations, but each State decides how to fulfill them. Here is the risk: some will comply rigorously, others will find escape routes.
