# The Federal Assault on Algorithms: Trump's Grand Preemption and the Future of AI in the United States

A struggle for regulatory supremacy between the centralized innovation mandate and the laboratory of state safeguards.

## Introduction: The End of the Mosaic

On March 20, 2026, the Trump administration made public its official proposal for a single national framework on artificial intelligence (AI). This document is not merely a technical guideline; it represents the culmination of a strategy that began on the first day of its mandate to eliminate "policy barriers that act as obstacles to American innovation." Essentially, the AI National Legislative Framework Proposal marks the end of the era of caution and the beginning of a deregulatory centralization era.

While the previous administration, under Joe Biden, prioritized equity, transparency, and risk mitigation through voluntary standards and structured oversight, the new White House approach is based on a bold premise: AI is too vital to national security and economic competitiveness to allow it to be stifled by bureaucracy, whether at the state or federal level.

This transition has not been subtle. In January 2025, President Trump revoked Biden's Executive Order 14110 through the Executive Order on "Establishing and Implementing the President's National Policy for AI," which established safety protocols and red-teesting for high-risk AI models, replacing it with a mandate focused on removing regulatory obstacles. The central thesis defended by the current administration is that American success in the AI space requires a uniform national strategy, preventing a "discordant mosaic" of fifty state legislatures from undermining the country's ability to compete against powers like China.

However, beneath this rhetoric of efficiency lies a profound regulatory conflict. By invoking the preemption doctrine (federal supremacy), the government seeks to overturn pioneering state laws that addressed everything from algorithmic bias to data privacy. What we are witnessing is a bet on "total dominance." The Framework argues that the development and deployment of AI models is an inherently interstate phenomenon with key implications for foreign policy.

But this centralization of regulatory power raises critical questions: Is the federal government filling the gap with adequate protections, or simply clearing the path for its allies in Big Tech? While Framework defenders see it as the necessary oxygen for a "Golden Age of Innovation," critics warn that this "move fast" strategy could leave citizens vulnerable to fraud, deepfakes, and civil rights violations, potentially alienating international markets that operate under much stricter safety standards, such as the European Union's AI Act. At the heart of this debate is not just technology, but the very future of American federalism in the digital age.

## I. The 2026 National Framework: Innovation Freedom or Regulatory Vacuum?

March 20, 2026, will be marked on the legislative calendar as the moment the Trump administration attempted to redefine the legal architecture of technological modernity in the United States. The release of the National Artificial Intelligence Legislative Framework Proposal is not a regulation in itself with independent legal force, but a "legislative recommendation" directed at Congress with a surgical objective: to establish a single federal standard that displaces the growing "mosaic" of state laws.

This proposal is the culmination of a doctrinal pivot that began in January 2025 with the revocation of Biden-era policies, replacing a risk-mitigation approach with one of "light-touch" intervention aimed at guaranteeing American dominance in the global AI race.

The architecture of the Proposal rests on a conceptual metaphor that the administration and its allies have termed the "five-layer cake" of AI governance: energy, chips, infrastructure, models, and applications. Under this vision, the federal government claims exclusive authority over the deepest layers—frontier model training and deployment—arguing that these activities have intrinsic implications for national security and foreign policy that transcend state borders.

The document is explicit in instructing Congress to apply the preemption doctrine over any state law that imposes "undue burdens," seeking what it terms a "minimally burdensome national standard." From a legal analysis perspective, this approach raises a fundamental tension between innovation freedom and legal certainty.

On one hand, the Proposal is presented as the necessary oxygen for American companies to compete without the shackles of fragmented bureaucracy, promoting regulatory sandboxes for a "test first" mentality. On the other hand, analysts and critics argue that the document is an "empty shell" that eliminates existing state protections—such as Colorado's algorithmic discrimination laws or California's frontier model transparency laws—without offering enforceable federal safeguards or clear technical standards in return.

The administration defends that existing general laws against fraud and discrimination are sufficient, but the Proposal is careful not to specify that states should not be able to penalize AI developers for the conduct of third parties who use their models, which would operate, in practice, as a liability shield for Big Tech.

This intentional regulatory vacuum is the core of Trump's bet. While the European Union has opted for a governance model based on risk levels and mandatory external audits, the 2026 U.S. Proposal leans toward industry self-regulation and private-sector-led standards. The government thesis is that AI is too young a technology to be "smothered" by rigid frameworks.

However, by stripping states of their ability to act as "laboratories of democracy" in protecting their citizens, the administration is assuming the risk that any systemic AI failure will lack a clear legal correction mechanism, leaving accountability at the mercy of complex civil litigation and future court interpretation of what constitutes an "undue burden."

## II. The Federalism Tension: The Battlefield of the States

If the 2026 National Framework Proposal is the sledgehammer with which the Trump administration intends to unify technology policy, American federalism is the wall that threatens to resist the blow. The federal preemption doctrine has not been deployed in a vacuum; rather, it has collided head-on with unprecedented state activism.

At the end of 2025, a bipartisan coalition of 36 Attorneys General signed a letter to Congress expressing their unequivocal opposition to any federal moratorium on state AI laws. Their argument is fundamentally constitutional: states must act as "laboratories of democracy" (classic doctrine from *New State Ice Co. v. Liebmann*, 285 U.S. 262 (1932)) to test clear safeguards before such a disruptive technology becomes nationally immutable.

The conflict is not merely theoretical. Pioneering laws like Colorado's AI Act, designed to prevent algorithmic discrimination in high-impact decisions (health, hiring, housing), and California's Frontier AI Transparency Act (SB 53), which requires annual risk assessments, are now in the federal government's crosshairs.

The Proposal explicitly instructs a newly created AI Litigation Task Force to challenge these laws, arguing that AI model development is an "inherently interstate phenomenon" with national security implications that states are not equipped to manage. The legislative response on Capitol Hill was not long in coming.

On the same day the Proposal was released, a group of Democratic legislators introduced the GUARDRAILS Act (Guaranteeing and Upholding Americans' Right to Decide Responsible AI Laws and Standards). This legislative piece seeks to specifically repeal Trump's executive order that imposes the regulatory moratorium, denouncing what they consider an act of "illegal coercion." According to Representative Doris Matsui, the administration has gone so far as to threaten to withhold federal funds for broadband expansion (BEAD program) as a pressure tool for states to abandon their own regulatory agendas.

The tension centers on whether the federal standard should be a "floor" or a "ceiling." While the National Conference of State Legislatures (NCSL) argues that any federal law should be merely a minimum level of protection allowing states to go further in defense of their citizens, Trump's Proposal seeks to impose a maximum national standard.

For civil rights defenders, this federal "ceiling" is actually an "empty shell" that leaves citizens vulnerable to fraud and biases that the central government lacks the technical staff or political will to monitor. Thus, the AI battlefield has reopened one of the oldest gaps in American jurisprudence: the struggle to decide where state sovereignty ends and the need for a uniform nation begins.

## III. The Mirage of Efficacy: Between Rhetoric and Implementation Reality

The ambition of the 2026 National Legislative Framework Proposal to project a "Golden Age of Innovation" collides head-on with a much grimmer technical and bureaucratic reality: the history of systemic non-compliance within the federal apparatus itself. While the Trump administration promises centralized leadership and agile execution, data on the implementation of fundamental pillars of the U.S. AI strategy reveals what legal experts have termed an institutional "capacity void."

A comprehensive analysis conducted by Stanford Law School's RegLab on prior executive orders and AI laws shows that less than 40% of legal requirements imposed on federal agencies have been verifiably met.

This "efficacy mirage" manifests with particular harshness in human talent infrastructure. The 2020 AI in Government Act required the Office of Personnel Management (OPM) to create an "AI occupational series" to identify and hire technical experts before July 2022; however, this mandate has remained dead letter, leaving agencies without the professional profiles needed to evaluate the frontier models that the new Proposal intends to supervise.

The contradiction is glaring: the administration requires that national agencies possess "sufficient technical capacity" to understand the capabilities of frontier AI models, but the federal employment system itself has failed to close the gap in qualified STEM workers.

Opacity also undermines the promise of responsible governance. Despite Executive Order 13960 requiring agencies to prepare and publish annual inventories of their AI use cases, 76% of the 220 identified federal agencies have failed to comply with this transparency obligation. Even in cases of apparent compliance, information is often incomplete or misleading. For example, Customs and Border Protection (CBP) omitted in its official reports the use of its Traveler Verification Service (TVS) facial recognition system, a high-impact technology that has already been subject to scrutiny for privacy risks. This lack of "institutional telemetry" suggests that the federal government is trying to regulate the future of AI blindly, without a real accounting of how these algorithms are deployed in its own departments.

Ultimately, the implementation failure responds to a phenomenon of "unfunded mandates" and a lack of top-level leadership. Agencies treat governance requirements as mere peremptory checklists rather than opportunities for strategic planning. For the 2026 Proposal to not become another empty document, analysts warn that the gap between "policies that promise governance" and "infrastructures that deliver it" must be closed with real resources and verifiable technical oversight.

Without a solid foundation of internal compliance, the U.S. pretense of establishing a single national standard for the private sector risks being seen as a purely rhetorical construction, lacking the technical authority necessary to lead the global market.

## IV. The New Corporate Paradigm: From Direct Regulation to Evidence-Based Accountability

For the corporate sector, the arrival of the 2026 National Framework Proposal has been met with a mix of relief and dangerous complacency. Organizations that interpret the Trump administration's proposal simply as a reduction in regulatory burden are making a misreading of the new risk map.

What the document proposes is not an elimination of accountability, but a surgical redirection: by attempting to eliminate the "mosaic" of state laws through federal preemption, the Proposal shifts the legal battlefield from compliance with specific rules to the technical ability to demonstrate system safety in real time. Under this paradigm, AI governance ceases to be an exercise in office policy drafting and becomes a discipline of pure infrastructure.

The emerging thesis is clear: in an unregulated environment, infrastructure is the law. While frameworks like the European Union's AI Act operate under a preventive compliance model, the 2026 U.S. approach is based on post-hoc evidence. This means that, before a Federal Trade Commission (FTC) investigation or civil litigation, a company can no longer hide behind an administrative compliance checklist, but must produce audit logs, pipeline telemetry, and data provenance records to avoid claims for deceptive or unfair practices under Section 5 of the FTC Act.

This transition manifests with particular rigor in liability management. Although the Proposal seeks to act as a "liability shield" by preventing states from penalizing developers for the illegal conduct of third parties who use their models, this shield is not absolute. For an AI platform to successfully invoke this defense, it must demonstrate through an "evidence stack"—including model inventories, runtime behavior logs, and supply chain verifications—that the harm was caused exclusively by the user and not by an intrinsic flaw in the design or functioning of the algorithm. Without this legal telemetry, the shield becomes porous, leaving companies vulnerable to a judicial system that, in the absence of specific laws, will create precedents based on "bad facts."

Consequently, the Proposal imposes on corporations a "dual-track" strategy. On one hand, they must follow the legislative trail of the federal proposal to identify which state protections survive under the "general application laws" exceptions against fraud or child protection. On the other hand, they must align with the expectations of sector regulators—such as the SEC in finance or the FDA in health—who are moving more rapidly and with more technical specificity than Congress itself.

In this new scenario, the primary legal risk is no longer the existence of strict regulation, but the unbridgeable gap between a company's policy documents and the verifiable reality of its technical infrastructure. Organizations that cannot respond in seconds to questions about the traceability of a specific algorithmic output will discover that federal deregulation was not a refuge, but an open field of litigation exposure.

## Conclusion: The Global Dominance Dilemma

The 2026 National Legislative Framework Proposal represents the Trump administration's definitive bet on speed over caution. By attempting to dismantle the "mosaic" of state regulations through federal preemption, the government has cleared the path for American developers to operate with a freedom not seen since the early Internet days. However, this radical shift from the Biden-era risk-mitigation model to a "pure dominance" doctrine places the United States in a position of regulatory isolation that could prove counterproductive.

The major legal and political question is not just whether this approach will accelerate innovation, but whether the proposed legal architecture is capable of sustaining public trust and long-term international competitiveness. The most immediate risk is global divergence. While the United States leans toward a "minimally burdensome" national standard, the European Union and other strategic allies like Japan, Canada, and the United Kingdom are consolidating frameworks based on the precautionary principle and strict security audits.

The absence of explicit ethical safeguards in the U.S. Proposal could weaken the ability of domestic companies to compete in foreign markets where compliance with the EU AI Act is a legal requirement for market access. Analysts warn that if the United States relinquishes leadership in creating global safety standards, it risks becoming a regulatory "follower," allowing rival powers like China to fill the void in international AI governance.

Internally, the Proposal leaves a protection void that states will hardly accept without a fight. The resistance of the 36 Attorneys General and the legislative counterattack of the GUARDRAILS Act suggest that the "regulatory peace" the White House seeks is, for now, a mirage. American federalism, traditionally a laboratory for democracy, is being forced into a moratorium that could leave citizens vulnerable to algorithmic failures that federal agencies, burdened by a history of non-compliance and lack of technical capacity, are not yet prepared to manage.

Ultimately, the success of this policy will be measured not by the absence of laws, but by the robustness of technical evidence. In this new unregulated environment, infrastructure is the law. Accountability has not disappeared; it has merely mutated from checklist-based administrative compliance to accountability based on data telemetry and traceability.

Organizations that will thrive under the 2026 Proposal will be those that understand that the freedom to innovate carries the technical burden of demonstrating, at every step, that their systems are safe. The future of American dominance in AI will depend on whether this framework can balance the agility needed to defeat geopolitical competitors with the integrity needed to protect civil rights and maintain citizen faith in the technology that will define the 21st century.
