## Introduction

The ruling issued on February 11, 2025, by Judge Stephanos Bibas in *Thomson Reuters Enterprise Centre GmbH et al. v. Ross Intelligence Inc.* (No. 1:20-cv-613-SB, D. Del.) is, without exaggeration, one of the most relevant decisions on copyright applied to artificial intelligence produced by North American jurisdiction in recent years. And it is so, in part, precisely because its author recognizes it: this is a judge who was wrong in 2023, publicly admits it, and revises his own criterion before trial. That intellectual honesty—so infrequent in judicial practice—converts the revised opinion not only into positive law but into an exercise in legal methodology worthy of doctrinal attention.

The case pits Thomson Reuters, owner of Westlaw, against Ross Intelligence, a startup that developed an AI-based legal search engine. Ross needed training data; Thomson Reuters refused to license it; Ross obtained it indirectly through a third party. Result: 2,243 headnotes infringed, fair use defense rejected, and a precedent that directly challenges the entire legaltech industry.

## The Central Legal Question: Are Headnotes Original Protectable Works?

The first front of litigation is the validity of copyright over Westlaw headnotes. A headnote is an editorial synthesis of the central legal point of a judicial decision. The text of judicial decisions is not protectable by copyright in the U.S.—they are government works—but the edition performed on them by a private jurist can be. To what extent?

The applicable rule is that of *Feist Publications, Inc. v. Rural Tel. Serv. Co.* (499 U.S. 340, 1991): the threshold of originality for copyright is "extremely low," requiring only "some degree of minimum creativity." In 2023, Judge Bibas himself had considered that the originality of headnotes depended on the degree of overlap with the non-protectable text of the underlying opinion. In 2025, he revokes that reading.

The doctrinal correction is articulated through an especially happy analogy: that of the sculptor. A marble block—like a judicial opinion—is not protectable by copyright. But the sculptor who decides what to cut and what to preserve creates a protectable work. Similarly, the jurist-editor who identifies which words of a hundreds-of-pages opinion constitute the relevant legal point, and "chisels" it into a headnote, exercises sufficient editorial creativity. The fact that the headnote takes verbatim the text of the opinion does not destroy that originality: the choice of which fragment to reproduce is already a creative expression.

This correction has systemic consequences: it consolidates the protectability of secondary legal editorial materials against those who would freely use them as raw material for AI systems.

## The Fair Use Framework for AI Training Copying

The most influential part of the opinion is the fair use analysis (17 U.S.C. §107). Ross argued that its copy was "intermediate"—the headnotes do not appear in the final product the user sees—and that cases like *Sony Computer Entertainment v. Connectix Corp.* (9th Cir. 2000), *Sega Enterprises v. Accolade* (9th Cir. 1992), and *Google LLC v. Oracle America* (593 U.S. 1, 2021) protect that type of copying.

Judge Bibas dismantles the argument with surgical precision. The precedents of tolerated intermediate copying have two conditions that do not concur here: (1) they deal with computer code, whose functional nature justifies different treatment, and (2) copying was necessary to access the non-protectable elements of the program. Headnotes are written text, not code; and nothing prevented Ross from creating its own training materials without copying Thomson Reuters'.

The analysis of the four factors yields the following result:

**Factor 1 (purpose and character of use):** Unfavorable to Ross. The use is commercial and not transformative. Applying the test of *Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith* (598 U.S. 508, 2023), when the original and secondary use share the same substantial purpose—facilitating legal research—and the use is commercial, the absence of transformation is determinative. The fact that headnotes do not appear in the final output does not make the use transformative.

**Factor 2 (nature of the original):** Favorable to Ross. Headnotes have sufficient originality for copyright but are less creative than a novel or artistic work. This factor, however, rarely proves decisive.

**Factor 3 (amount used):** Favorable to Ross. What is relevant is not how much was copied internally, but what is made accessible to the public. Ross does not expose headnotes to the end user.

**Factor 4 (effect on the market):** Unfavorable to Ross, and decisive. The judge identifies two affected markets: that of legal research platforms (where Ross is a competitive substitute for Westlaw) and the potential market for data to train legal AI. This second market is what pivots the entire analysis: although Thomson Reuters has not yet licensed its data for AI training, that market exists or can exist, and Ross has eroded it by extracting it without license.

## Applied Analysis: The Architecture of Infringement

The facts of the copying are of special analytical richness. Ross did not copy directly: it hired LegalEase, which in turn instructed lawyers to create legal questions using Westlaw headnotes as a guide—without literally copying and pasting. The judge analyzes one by one the 3,384 headnotes of the main batch, comparing the Bulk Memo question, the headnote, and the judicial opinion. His conclusion: in 2,243 cases, the question resembles the headnote more than the underlying opinion, which constitutes irrefutable circumstantial evidence of effective copying.

This tripartite comparison methodology—question vs. headnote vs. opinion—is itself a relevant procedural contribution: it establishes a reproducible procedure for detecting copying of editorial materials in AI training contexts, capable of being adopted in future litigation.

## Doctrinal Implications

The ruling has projection beyond the U.S. and beyond legaltech. Its doctrinal consequences can be synthesized into four planes.

First, **copying for AI training is not neutral**. The argument that AI "transforms" any data it ingests—that the output does not reproduce the input—is not sufficient to justify fair use when the competitive purpose is identical to that of the original.

Second, **the training data market is legally relevant**. Copyright holders have a legitimate interest in potential licensing markets for their works for model training, although those markets are still forming.

Third, **secondary editorial creativity deserves robust protection**. The sculptor analogy reinforces the protectability of all types of compilation, selection, and synthesis materials, with implications for databases, taxonomies, and proprietary classification systems.

Fourth, **the code/text distinction is operationally legal in AI**. Judge Bibas explicitly draws a line between source code copying jurisprudence and written text copying, with implications for litigation over LLMs trained with textual corpora.

## Conclusion

*Thomson Reuters v. Ross Intelligence* is not just a case about headnotes. It is the first robust pronouncement by a federal court on the perimeter of fair use in AI training with protected legal content, and anticipates the terrain where the great copyright litigation of the next decade will be fought. Judge Bibas has the additional merit of having corrected his own error with exemplary intellectual honesty. For European legal operators, the ruling is also a reminder: when the AI Act and DSM Directive face similar cases, Anglo-American doctrine will already be consolidated, and transatlantic dialogue will be inevitable.
