# Artificial Intelligence as Technical Evidence in Judicial Proceedings: The Founded Rejection of ChatGPT by the Cali Superior Court

**Reference:** [Tribunal Superior, Distrito Judicial de Cali, Sala Civil de Decisión. Sentencia de 17 de marzo de 2026. Radicado 76-001-31-03-008-2023-00322-01](https://www.ramajudicial.gov.co/) | **Presiding Justice:** Homero Mora Insuasty

On March 17, 2026, the Cali Superior Court issued a judgment of great legal significance that examines, with technical and constitutional rigor, the admissibility of generative artificial intelligence tools as means of evidence in civil proceedings. The decision, which confirms the rejection of a request for ChatGPT Pro to appear at a hearing as "artificial intelligence-based technical evidence," transcends the particular case of medical malpractice liability and establishes a robust analytical framework on the limits of due process of evidence in the face of new technologies.

This analysis examines the constitutional, procedural, and technical foundations of the judgment, its implications for evidence law in the era of generative AI, and the boundaries it establishes for future requests to incorporate artificial intelligence systems in the administration of justice.

## The Case: Procedural Context and the Contested Request

The underlying litigation is a medical malpractice civil action in which the plaintiffs sought to establish medical negligence and incompetence in the care provided to a minor who died on July 23, 2017. In their complaint, the parties requested an unusual evidentiary means: the "appearance at the hearing of the ChatGPT Pro Artificial Intelligence-based Large Language Model," for the purpose of allowing both the judge and the parties "to ask the model questions about the expert reports, medical history, and other relevant facts of the case."

The proposal included that a forensic analysis company, Tecnovask S.A.S., would provide its OpenAI account and the technical support necessary for conducting the proceeding. The open and conversational nature of the request—without a prior questionnaire, without delimitation of the evidentiary object, without prior technical analysis—would constitute, in the court's assessment, the first indication of its procedural unsustainability.

When the Eighth Civil Circuit Court denied the request on December 10, 2025, the plaintiff filed a motion for reconsideration and subsidiary appeal, arguing that there was no reason to "exclude" a validly requested evidence when the judge, in the exercise of sound judgment, could determine whether it served to illuminate his criterion. He added that artificial intelligence constituted a technology in full development that was revolutionizing multiple fields and that, according to his thesis, it had even allowed inconsistencies or falsehoods to be demonstrated.

The Cali Superior Court therefore had to determine whether this evidentiary request found support in the constitutional and procedural principles governing evidentiary activity under Colombian law.

## The Constitutional Foundation: Due Process of Evidence as a Fundamental Guarantee

The judgment does not begin its analysis in procedural technicality, but in the constitutional heart of evidence law. It invokes Article 29 of the Political Constitution of Colombia, which establishes the fundamental right to evidence as one of the most significant elements of due process of law, developed by the jurisprudence of the Constitutional Court through the concept of "due process of evidence."

The court precisely recites the formulation of Constitutional Court Judgment C-163 of 2019, which defines due process of evidence as "the minimum evidentiary guarantees that must be observed in every judicial or administrative proceeding, without which the right to defense and rebuttal, due process, and access to justice are inevitably attacked." These guarantees are not ornamental nor available at the parties' discretion: they constitute a set of essential legal positions that protect the integrity of the proceeding.

The fundamental character of these guarantees sets the tone for all subsequent argumentation. It is not simply that AI is not a "useful" evidence means in the sense that the judge may discard it; it is that its admission without safeguards could violate non-derogable constitutional guarantees. The court thus establishes an unassailable floor: **no technology, however sophisticated, may evade the minimum standards of transparency, rebuttal, and control that comprise due process of evidence**.

## Evidentiary Freedom and Public Order Ritualism: A Constitutional Balance

Article 165 of the Colombian General Procedure Code establishes the principle of evidentiary freedom: in addition to traditionally recognized means (party declaration, testimony, expert witness, inspection, documents, indications, reports), "any other means that are useful for the formation of the judge's conviction" may be employed. It follows that procedural law does not establish a closed catalog of evidentiary means, opening the door, prima facie, to innovative forms of obtaining or processing information derived from contemporary scientific and technological developments.

However—and here lies the Gordian knot of the analysis—the court establishes with clarity that evidentiary freedom is not absolute or unrestricted. "The manner of requesting, ordering, and practicing evidence inherently requires satisfaction of certain requirements established in procedural law, which constitute a legal ordering, a public order ritualism; that is, they are rules of strict law and mandatory compliance, not only by the parties but also by the judge."

This architecture is crucial: **evidentiary freedom operates within the limits of public order procedural ritualism**. The judge is not a passive arbiter who must accept any proposed evidentiary means under the premise that "he will already decide if it is useful to him." On the contrary, the judge must act as the director of the proceeding, guaranteeing that the general principles of rebuttal and publicity of evidence are respected, as well as the specific procedural requirements for each type of means of conviction.

The court here invokes the consolidated jurisprudence of the Constitutional Court (Judgment T-504 of 1998), which specifies that "the judge as director of the proceeding must guarantee, in the interest of the parties' right to defense, the general principles of rebuttal and publicity of evidence, and in this sense, must comply with the requirements established in the procedure for each of the evidence requested."

From this follows a conclusion the court formulates with precision: although the legal system permits innovation in evidentiary means, that innovation must be subjected to scrutiny of the minimum guarantees that due process of evidence requires. Evidentiary freedom is not a gap through which any technology may slip unchecked; it is a space regulated by constitutional principles of superior rank.

## The Contemporary Technological Challenge: The Reconsideration of Evidence Law

The court acknowledges, without ambiguity, that "technological advances have significantly influenced the evolution of judicial proceedings" and that the "rapid development of tools associated with so-called artificial intelligence" obliges us to "reconsider or at least review some of the traditional postulates of evidence law, such as the relevance of evidence, the burden and availability of proof, rebuttal, and judicial control of evidentiary activity."

This acknowledgment is important because it refutes a frequent criticism: that courts reject AI out of pure technological conservatism or ignorance. The court does not deny that AI may be useful in support tasks linked to legal work. What it maintains is that **that usefulness is not unrestricted and its use must not be uncritical**. This is a sophisticated balance: openness to innovation, but subjected to rigorous verification.

### Prior Colombian Jurisprudence: Criteria for the Use of AI in the Judicial Context

The court makes a particularly valuable mention of recent rulings by the Colombia Supreme Court of Justice and the Constitutional Court that have established guiding criteria for the use of artificial intelligence in judicial contexts:

**Auto AC739-2026 of the Supreme Court of Justice:** A lawyer had included in an extraordinary motion for review normative and jurisprudential citations attributed to the use of AI tools. The Court specified that "generative artificial intelligence may constitute a valuable auxiliary if employed with professional judgment and its results are subjected to rigorous verification." This formulation is balanced: it does not reject AI, but subordinates it to two inescapable conditions: (i) professional judgment in its use, and (ii) rigorous verification of results.

**Judgment T-323 of 2024 of the Constitutional Court:** It established a series of guiding criteria for the use of AI in the judicial context, among which the court highlights: transparency in its use, responsibility, verification of generated information, prevention of risks, equality and equity, privacy, effective human control, and—crucially—the non-replacement of human rationality in the adoption of judicial decisions.

**Agreement PCSJA24-1224 of the Superior Council of the Judiciary:** It recognizes the potential of these technologies as support for certain tasks in the administration of justice, but warns about the risks: errors, biases, imprecisions, or technological "hallucinations." It emphasizes that their use cannot substitute the judge's own functions in the assessment of facts, analysis of evidence, interpretation of law, or adoption of judicial decisions, which remain under the exclusive responsibility of the judicial officer.

The court integrates this prior jurisprudence into its analytical framework, thus avoiding accusations of improvisation or ignorance. It establishes, on foundations already constructed by higher jurisdictional bodies, that although AI may play a role in proceedings, that role is necessarily subordinate, verifiable, and transparent.

## The Legal and Technical Difficulties: An Exhaustively Catalogued List of Risks

The judgment dedicates a particularly important section to enumerating, with technical and legal rigor, the problems posed by the incorporation of generative artificial intelligence systems as means of evidence. This is not a pejorative enumeration, but a cartographic analysis of the challenges facing not only Colombia, but any legal system confronting this issue. The court identifies eleven categories of risks:

**(i) Impact on fundamental rights:** The risk of violating guarantees of due process of evidence, especially the right of rebuttal, as well as the security and protection of privacy and personal data (habeas data). These rights could be exposed or accessible to third parties when commercial platforms like OpenAI are used.

**(ii) Replacement of human rationality:** The prohibition on replacing human rationality both in the exercise of jurisdictional function and in the production and formation of evidence, as well as the risk of loss of effective human control over the proceeding. This risk is especially serious because it erodes judicial responsibility.

**(iii) Undue influence on judicial conviction:** The risk that AI systems may unduly influence the formation of judicial conviction or the adoption of decisions, without the required guarantees of transparency, human supervision, and control.

**(iv) Alteration of procedural rules:** The alteration of traditional rules for the production, obtaining, and selection of evidence, insofar as the use of AI tools may displace to algorithmic systems activities properly procedural in nature whose direction and control correspond to the parties and the judge. This displacement would constitute an inadmissible cession of procedural power.

**(v) Algorithmic opacity:** The opacity characterizing the operation of these systems, making it difficult to understand the reasons or processes leading to a given result. This is what specialized literature calls the "black box problem."

**(vi) Traceability and verifiability:** The difficulties in establishing the traceability and verifiability of information sources used by the system. A model trained on millions of texts cannot attribute its assertions to specific sources in a transparent manner.

**(vii) Absence of identifiable responsibility:** The absence of an identifiable author who assumes direct responsibility for generated content. In a judicial proceeding, who is responsible for deficient or biased analysis generated by an AI system?

**(viii) Systemic biases:** The presence of biases in artificial intelligence models, which may produce distortions in generated information or reproduce discriminatory patterns. Language models are not "neutral" spaces: they reflect and amplify biases from the training corpus.

**(ix) Technological hallucinations:** The phenomenon of so-called "hallucinations," consisting of the generation of information lacking factual or legal support, but "presented with an appearance of authority." This is perhaps the most serious risk in a procedural context: a false but linguistically coherent and seemingly trustworthy assertion.

**(x) Chat-chamber effect:** The tendency of the system to return to the user responses that reflect or reinforce their own expectations or hypotheses. A lawyer using ChatGPT to "analyze" their theory of the case will receive responses that tend to validate it, feeding confirmatory biases.

**(xi) Unverifiable reliability:** The reliability problems of generated information, derived from the systemic impossibility of verifying the correspondence between data produced by these language models and reality. Language models cannot distinguish between correct or incorrect assertions; they generate linguistic probabilities.

This catalogue, far from merely illustrative, structures the technical foundation of inadmissibility. It is not that ChatGPT is bad in the abstract; it is that its fundamental architecture generates risks that are incompatible with the guarantees that due process of evidence requires.

## The Conceptual Insufficiency: ChatGPT Is Not "Technical Evidence"

The court then examines the plaintiffs' claim to classify their request as "technical evidence." Here it performs a particularly important normative subsumption analysis.

First, it notes that the request is openly generic, undetermined, and abstract: it contains no specification whatsoever regarding what the object of evidence is, what facts it seeks to establish, what specific aspects of the expert reports or medical history it intends to analyze, nor does it provide any previously elaborated technical analysis. What is intended, the court says, is to "maintain an open or conversational judicial dialogue with a chatbot that the party defines as expert, without a prior questionnaire, without a defined methodology, or any delimitation."

These circumstances are "openly incompatible with the minimum requirements of necessity, relevance, and conduciveness that govern evidentiary activity, as well as the guarantees of rebuttal and control of evidence by the judge."

However, even if the plaintiffs had presented a better-structured request, the court argues that it could not be classified as "technical evidence" in the sense of procedural law. To grant it such treatment, "we would inevitably have to place it within the scope of expert witness evidence, which has precise regulation in procedural law and requires certainly rigorous requirements (article 226 of the C.G.P.)."

Article 226 of the General Procedure Code requires that an expert be a natural person with specialized knowledge in the matter on which the opinion will render. ChatGPT is not a natural person. Although it could be argued that it is an "auxiliary of justice," it does not meet the minimum requirements that make expert evidence admissible: it has no verifiable expertise, it cannot take an oath (a public order ritual element), it cannot be subject to effective cross-examination, and it assumes no personal responsibility whatsoever for its assertions.

The court also refutes the plaintiffs' objection that ChatGPT was not intended to be presented as an "expert witness" but as differentiated "technical evidence." This distinction, the court maintains, is futile: it is either expert evidence (and must comply with its requirements), or it is some other means (testimony, technical report, data message), but in no case does it meet the requirements of any of them.

### The Language Model Argument: Can a Machine Be "Expert"?

The court undertakes a technical-conceptual analysis of considerable depth to explain why it is not plausible to predicate of generative systems that they may be classified as "expert," although it recognizes that they constitute useful tools for research and documentary support.

It invokes the precision of the Colombian Supreme Court itself: "the system 'knows' nothing in the conventional sense. What it possesses is a statistical representation of linguistic regularities, learned from the training corpus—the set of texts it processed—and encoded in terms of tokens."

To illustrate this idea, the court resorts to the famous "Chinese Room" thought experiment, formulated by philosopher John Searle: a person is imagined in a room who completely lacks knowledge of the Chinese language, but has manuals and dictionaries that indicate the rules for relating characters. When questions written in Chinese are introduced from outside, the person consults the manuals and returns a sequence of characters that, to someone outside, constitutes a correct response. However, the person inside does not understand the meaning of what they respond; they simply follow rules to combine symbols.

"Something analogous, although more complex, occurs with generative language models: they collect duly parameterized information through algorithms and produce linguistically coherent responses without actually understanding the content they generate, which explains that their operation is limited to generating probabilistic results from language patterns, without verifiable expert knowledge or semantic comprehension of the material they process."

This explanation is pedagogically valuable because dispels a common confusion: that because an AI system produces coherent and documented responses, it possesses "understanding" or "expertise" in the legal sense of the terms. No. What it possesses is the capacity to generate sequences of tokens that are statistically probable given prior training.

## The Path Forward: Minimum Conditions for Future Incorporation of AI-Derived Evidence

Although the judgment rejects the specific request, the court does not completely close the door to the future incorporation of evidence derived from AI systems. It instead formulates a theoretically rigorous proposal on how such evidence might eventually be incorporated, in the near future, from the use of artificial intelligence tools, **provided minimum budgets are met that ensure its reliability and allow for its rebuttal within the proceeding**.

The court suggests that such evidence might be incorporated as "data messages" within the meaning of Article 247 of the General Procedure Code, that is, as information contained in electronic form. However, for this to be legally admissible and subject to evidentiary assessment, it will be necessary to verify a series of minimum requirements:

**(i) Compliance with principles of regular production and contribution of evidence:** The evidence must be produced in accordance with established procedures, without shortcuts that evade the control of the parties or the judge.

**(ii) Reliability of the technological system:** It must be demonstrated that the system used for generating and preserving information is technically reliable and secure.

**(iii) Integrity of generated content:** It must be accredited that the content has not been altered since its generation.

**(iv) Identification of the initiator or responsible for the message:** The origin of the request must be identifiable and attributable to a specific person or entity.

**(v) Techniques to guarantee unalterability:** Cryptographic or other mechanisms must be employed to guarantee that the document has not been modified.

**(vi) Traceability of the generation process:** Including the instructions or "prompts" employed. This is a crucial requirement: the court demands total transparency about what was asked of the system and under what conditions.

**(vii) Identification of the model or system:** It must be specifically identified what AI model was used, its exact version, and its operating conditions.

**(viii) Reproducibility and audit:** There must be the possibility of reproducing or auditing the procedure by which the result was obtained.

**(ix) Effective guarantee of rebuttal and verification:** The parties and judge must be able to contrast the information, even through technical or expert control when necessary.

These requirements are not merely procedural: they constitute a strategy to domesticate algorithmic opacity, the lack of responsibility, and the manipulation risks that characterize AI systems. If implemented, they would radically transform the profile of any AI-generated evidence: it would cease to be a conversational "black box" and become a traceable, auditable, and verifiable technical document.

## The Cardinal Principle: AI as an Auxiliary Instrument, Never as a Substitute

The judgment closes its argumentation with a constitutional-rank assertion: although in the near future normative and technological development may expand the margin for the use of AI tools in the procedural sphere, **a cardinal principle of the justice system remains inviolate: such tools may only operate as auxiliary instruments of human reasoning, not as substitutes for the critical judgment, responsibility, and decisory function that the legal system has entrusted exclusively to the judge and other legal operators**.

This principle is not an occasional restriction, but a foundation of the rule of law. The administration of justice rests on the individual responsibility of the judge, who acts in the name of the people and under the supremacy of the Constitution. That responsibility cannot be transferred to an algorithm. AI may illuminate, it may process data at scale, it may point out inconsistencies. But judgment—the decision founded on law—remains an intransferable function of the court.

## Conclusions and Doctrinal Projections

### The Core of the Decision

The Cali Superior Court systematically and substantively rejects the incorporation of ChatGPT as a means of evidence. But its value transcends the particular case. It establishes an analytical framework that integrates:

- **Constitutional guarantees** (due process of evidence, rebuttal, publicity)
- **Procedural requirements** (public order ritualism, necessity, relevance, conduciveness)
- **Technical realities** (opacity, lack of verifiable expertise, hallucinations)
- **Guiding principles** (supremacy of human reason, judicial responsibility)

### Implications for Comparative Law

Although the judgment is Colombian, its foundations transcend jurisdiction. A European system applying the General Data Protection Regulation and the Artificial Intelligence Regulation would reach materially similar conclusions: a generative AI system used as means of evidence without safeguards would violate fundamental rights, especially regarding transparency, responsibility, and effective human control.

In fact, the judgment converges with jurisprudential developments in other contexts. Courts in Spain, Italy, and other European States have expressed similar caution regarding the incorporation of AI in judicial proceedings without specific regulation.

### The Minimum Requirements Strategy

Most relevant for future development is that the court does not close the door, but establishes conditions. The requirement for prompt traceability, model identification, procedure reproducibility, and audit possibility constitutes scaffolding that, if respected, would allow certain AI-derived evidence to be incorporated responsibly.

However, this scaffolding is demanding. A company wishing to use an AI system to analyze documents in a proceeding would have to be able to demonstrate exactly what was asked, in what version of the model, under what parameters, with what training sources. Most practical cases would fail to meet these requirements.

### The Outstanding Question: Data Messages or Expert Opinions?

The court's suggestion that AI-derived evidence could be incorporated as "data messages" is intelligent but potentially problematic. A data message is information contained in electronic form. However, if that information is the result of expert analysis on medical opinions or technical matters, should it not be subject to expert evidence requirements?

The judgment seems to suggest not necessarily. If it is systematized analysis produced by an AI model under traceable conditions, with the minimum requirements listed, it could be admitted as a data message subject to subsequent expert verification. This would allow, for example, a report generated by an AI system to be incorporated as evidence, provided the system is auditable and the opposing expert can examine its methods.

### The Risk of Gradual Erosion

A legitimate concern is that, once the first AI-derived evidence is formally admitted under minimum requirements, the door opens to gradual erosion of guarantees. The temptation will be to normalize use, accommodate to routine, reduce requirements. For this reason, the judgment is wise in maintaining the bar high: it is not that AI evidence is impossible to admit, but the conditions are so demanding that only very carefully designed and audited systems could meet them.

## Practical Recommendations for Legal Operators

### For Judges and Magistrates

The judgment offers a clear framework for evaluating future requests involving AI systems. When faced with a request of this type:

- **Require specificity:** Do not admit generic or open requests. Each element of analysis must be delimited.
- **Verify compliance with minimum guarantees:** Apply the list of nine requirements as a checklist.
- **Examine traceability:** Can it be determined exactly what was asked of the system and under what parameters?
- **Allow full rebuttal:** Guarantee that the opposing party may challenge not only the result, but the methodology.
- **Remember human supremacy:** AI-derived analysis is an input, never a decision.

### For Litigating Attorneys

If seeking to incorporate AI-derived analysis as evidence:

- **Do not present the system as "expert":** Present it as technical analysis produced by a tool under human supervision.
- **Document exhaustively:** Record each step, each instruction, each parameter.
- **Facilitate audit:** Allow the opposing party and judge to examine the procedure.
- **Complement with human expertise:** AI may be an auxiliary; responsibility must rest with an identifiable expert.
- **Anticipate challenges:** Recognize the technical limitations of the system and argue how they have been mitigated.

### For AI System Designers in Legal Contexts

The judgment offers technical criteria that developers should take seriously:

- **Radical transparency:** Systems must allow complete audit.
- **Traceability:** Record all instructions and parameters.
- **Identifiable responsibility:** There must be a person or entity that assumes responsibility for the analysis.
- **Source verifiability:** To the extent possible, systems must be able to indicate from what training corpus their assertions derive.
- **Bias testing:** Before using the system in a legal context, perform bias and discrimination audits.

## Conclusions and Final Reflection

The judgment of the Cali Superior Court of March 17, 2026, is a document of great legal maturity. Far from rejecting artificial intelligence out of fear or ignorance, it examines it with technical sophistication, placing it in its proper dimension: a potentially useful instrument but profoundly problematic if incorporated without constitutional and procedural safeguards.

The court rejects both naive technophobia and uncritical technoutopianism. It recognizes that AI will continue to develop, that its presence in the administration of justice is likely, and that normative and technological regulation may expand margins in the future. But it affirms, with constitutional clarity, that that future cannot dispense with two inviolable pillars: identifiable human responsibility and effective judicial control.

In a global context where the temptation is to incorporate disruptive technology without waiting for legal guarantees to be ready, a judgment like this is an act of judicial prudence. It does not say "never." It says "not like this, not now, not without conditions." And it establishes what conditions should be met.

For jurists, system designers, judges, and attorneys, reading this judgment should be mandatory. Because it is, simultaneously, a reminder that procedural law has reasons that technology should not ignore, and an invitation to carefully consider how to incorporate technological change without eroding the foundations of the rule of law.
