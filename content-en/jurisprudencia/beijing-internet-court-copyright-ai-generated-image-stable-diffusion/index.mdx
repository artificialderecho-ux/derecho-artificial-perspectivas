# Beijing Internet Court Recognizes Copyright in AI-Generated Images: Analysis of the Li v. Liu (2023) Decision

On November 27, 2023, the Beijing Internet Court issued a decision that warrants attention beyond its jurisdictional borders: for the first time, a court recognized a user's copyright ownership over an image generated through Stable Diffusion, ordering the infringer to issue a public apology and pay monetary compensation. The ruling, identified as (2023) Jing 0491 Min Chu No. 11279, resolves not merely a minor dispute between private parties; it establishes doctrine regarding the legal nature of AI-assisted creation that stands in stark contrast to the position previously adopted by the United States Copyright Office and raises questions of universal scope regarding where authorship resides when the brush is a diffusion model.

---

## The Prompt as Creative Act: The Court's Central Thesis

The case arose from a straightforward factual scenario: the plaintiff, Li, generated the image "Spring Breeze Brings Tenderness" using Stable Diffusion on February 24, 2023, published it on the Little Red Book platform with an identifying watermark, and days later found the same image—without the watermark—illustrating a poem by the defendant, Liu, on his Baijiahao account. Liu could not recall whether he had removed the watermark and could not establish the origin of the image.

The core legal issue was not the infringement itself—which the court resolved with relative ease once ownership was established—but rather whether the AI-generated image constituted a "work" protectable under Article 3 of the Copyright Law of the People's Republic of China, which requires works to be "original intellectual achievements" expressed in a certain form in the fields of literature, art, and science.

The court constructs its argument around a premise that warrants careful analysis: originality does not reside in who physically executes the strokes, but in who makes the intellectual decisions that determine the outcome. To reach this conclusion, the ruling examines in detail the image generation process: the selection of the base model (AsiaFacemix), the choice and structuring of positive and negative prompts according to a proprietary conceptual scheme (art type + subject + environment + composition + style), the configuration of technical parameters such as the CFG scale, the number of iteration steps, and the random seed, and the iterative refinement process until obtaining the desired result. Each of these decisions, the court reasons, reflects the plaintiff's aesthetic taste, artistic judgment, and intellectual investment. The image is therefore not a mechanical result nor reproducible identically by any person starting from the same data: it is the personalized expression of a creative process.

This construction possesses a notable analytical virtue. Rather than confronting the abstract question of whether "AI can create," the court reformulates it in operational terms: who made the choices that determined the work? The answer invariably points to the human user, not the model. The model is, in the ruling's own metaphor, a tool—sophisticated, but a tool nonetheless—comparable in function to a camera: the fact that technology automates execution does not eliminate the authorship of one who directs the gaze and makes compositional decisions.

---

## The Architecture of Authorship Under Chinese Law: Who Falls Outside and Why

The ruling is equally interesting for what it excludes. Three candidates for authorship are systematically dismissed.

The first is the AI model itself. The court notes that, under Article 11 of the Copyright Law, only natural persons, legal entities, and unincorporated organizations can be authors; this enumeration coincides with the civil subjects of the Chinese Civil Code. AI lacks legal personality and therefore cannot be an author. This conclusion is virtually unanimous in comparative law and is not surprising.

The second dismissed candidate is the model designer. The court's reasoning here is more interesting: the designer made a genuine intellectual investment in creating the model, but that investment was directed at building the tool, not at producing the images the model would subsequently generate. The designer neither intended to create the specific image, nor participated in its generation process, nor preconfigured its content. This conclusion is reinforced by the fact that Stable Diffusion's own license (the CreativeML Open RAIL++-M License) expressly states that the licensor does not claim rights over outputs generated by users. The model is a third-party tool; its author is the manufacturer of that tool, not the author of each image the tool produces.

The third, implicitly excluded, is any theory of shared authorship between the user and the model. The court does not contemplate it and its logic would reject it: if the model has no will of its own and cannot be a subject of rights, no co-ownership is possible. Authorship rests entirely with the user.

This three-tier scheme—model, model designer, user—has conceptual elegance that should not be overlooked. It resolves the question of authorship without needing to pronounce on whether AI "creates" in a philosophical sense, which is precisely the question courts must avoid because it has no legally operative answer.

---

## The Watermark Removal as Infringement of the Moral Right of Paternity

Once the plaintiff's authorship is established, the infringement analysis is more straightforward but contains an interesting technical-evidentiary element. The court must rule on two distinct rights: the right of public communication through information networks (the functional equivalent of the making available right in European law) and the right of paternity.

The infringement of the first is clear: Liu published the image without authorization on his public account, making it accessible to third parties at a time and place of his choosing. There is no relevant discussion on this point.

The more interesting question is that of the right of paternity, because the removed watermark was not a handwritten signature of the author but rather the user identifier assigned by the Little Red Book platform. The court resolves this issue pragmatically: although the user ID is technically assigned by the platform and not freely chosen by the author in the conventional sense, the established correspondence between that ID and the actual user serves to indicate authorship of the work. The plaintiff expressly stated in court that this ID was his signature, and the court accepts this. The removal of the watermark therefore equates to the removal of the author's name and constitutes infringement of the moral right of paternity, regardless of the technical nature of the identifier.

This reasoning is relevant because it anticipates a question that will arise with increasing frequency: how is paternity of digital works accredited and protected when signature conventions are platform-dependent and technically manipulable? The ruling opts for a functional interpretation—what matters is the identifying function, not the format of the signature—which appears most coherent with the reality of the digital environment.

---

## The 500 Yuan Compensation and What It Reveals About the Valuation of AI-Generated Works

The ruling sets compensation of 500 yuan (the minimum statutory applicable when actual losses, illicit profits, or reasonable royalties cannot be quantified), versus the 5,000 yuan claimed by the plaintiff. The reduction is significant, and the court's reasoning explains it forthrightly: the defendant provided evidence that AI-generated images are marketed on Chinese platforms at prices ranging from 3 to 40 yuan per image, which precludes equating their market value to that of conventional photographic or pictorial works.

However, the court expressly rejects using these reference prices to calculate royalties, because it cannot be determined that the compared images are equivalent in originality and use. The minimum compensation is, consequently, a closing solution in the face of evidentiary impossibility, not a declaration on the intrinsic value of AI-generated works as a category. This is a detail worth emphasizing: the ruling does not say these works have little value; it says that in this case, it was impossible to establish how much they are worth.

---

## Divergence from the U.S. Position and Its Comparative Implications

The doctrine of the Beijing Internet Court contrasts with the position maintained by the U.S. Copyright Office (USCO), which has systematically denied registration of AI-generated works on the grounds that they lack the "human authorship" required by the Copyright Act of 1976. The USCO has admitted, with qualifications, that works in which the human element is sufficiently predominant—selection and organization of AI-generated elements, for example—may be registrable to the extent they reflect that human contribution; but an image generated entirely by a diffusion model through prompts has been consistently rejected (see Thaler v. Perlmutter, pending appeal before the D.C. Circuit).

The difference is not merely technical: it responds to different conceptions of the foundation of copyright law. Chinese law, like European continental law, anchors protection in "intellectual investment" and "personalized expression," categories that admit of gradation and which the Beijing Internet Court applies to include prompting work within its threshold. U.S. copyright, by contrast, requires human authorship of the concrete expression, and when that expression is generated by the model, the threshold is not met regardless of the effort invested in prompts.

This divergence has immediate practical consequences for any business or creator operating across multiple jurisdictions: the same image generated with Stable Diffusion may be protected by copyright in China and lack that protection in the United States. Legal planning of AI-generated work portfolios must account for these territorial asymmetries, which are likely to persist for years until legislators adopt harmonized frameworks.

---

## Conclusions

- The Beijing Internet Court establishes that AI-generated images may constitute protectable works under Chinese copyright law when the generation process reflects genuine intellectual investment by the user, materialized in the selection of the model, the elaboration of prompts, and the configuration of parameters.
- Authorship rests exclusively with the human user: neither the AI model nor its designer is an author of the generated outputs, for reasons the ruling bases both on the structure of Chinese copyright law and on the provisions of Stable Diffusion's own license.
- The removal of a platform watermark that serves an identifying function for the author constitutes infringement of the moral right of paternity, regardless of its not being a signature in the conventional sense.
- The minimum compensation set (500 yuan) reflects the evidentiary difficulties in establishing the market value of AI-generated works, not a declaration on their intrinsic value as a category.
- The ruling's doctrine diverges from the position adopted by the U.S. Copyright Office, which denies protection to images generated entirely by AI through prompts; this jurisdictional asymmetry has immediate practical consequences for the cross-border management of AI-generated work portfolios.
- The court's reasoning—focused on who makes creative decisions, not on who executes the strokes—offers an analytical framework exportable to other jurisdictions that, like the European, anchor protection in the author's "intellectual expression" rather than in the material execution of the work.
