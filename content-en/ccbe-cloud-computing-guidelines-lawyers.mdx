# CCBE Guidelines on the Use of Cloud Computing by Lawyers

Author: Ricardo Scarpa  
Date: February 13, 2026  
Document: CCBE Guidelines on the Use of Cloud Computing by Lawyers  
Standards: IRAC Methodology - Harvard Law School

---

## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The CCBE Guidelines of 2025 on cloud computing constitute a professional guidance document that updates prior recommendations from 2012 in response to significant legislative changes, technological evolution, and transformation of legal practice. The document, addressed to the member bars and law societies of the CCBE, identifies and addresses the fundamental risks derived from outsourcing data and IT services to third-party providers, particularly when these are located outside the EU/EEA legal space.

Key findings: The analysis reveals that 42.5% of European companies used cloud computing services in 2023, while in the United States, adoption by lawyers increased from 60% to 70% between 2022 and 2023. The Guidelines articulate two fundamental professional obligations—confidentiality and technical competence—as pillars of compliance, establishing a structure of ten operational recommendations that integrate requirements from the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), emerging cybersecurity legislation, and established ethical principles.

The legal relevance of the document is binary: on one hand, it codifies aspirational professional standards that, while not binding, establish the normative benchmark of professional diligence; on the other, it translates scattered legal obligations from different normative instruments (GDPR, NIS2 Directive, AI Act, among others) into operational guidance specifically calibrated for the legal profession.

## II. APPLICABLE LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### A. Primary European Legislation

#### 1. General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

The GDPR (EU Regulation 2016/679) constitutes the cornerstone of the legal regime for personal data processing in the cloud computing context. The CCBE Guidelines invoke it extensively, particularly regarding:

Processing principles (Art. 5 GDPR):
- Lawfulness, fairness, and transparency
- Purpose limitation
- Data minimization
- Accuracy
- Storage limitation
- Integrity and confidentiality
- Accountability

The document recognizes that cloud outsourcing does not dispense the attorney (identified as the data controller) from these principles, but generates reinforced supervision obligations over the processor (the cloud provider).

Legal bases (Art. 6 GDPR): The Guidelines implicitly assume that client data processing by attorneys is based on contract performance (Art. 6.1(b)) or legitimate interest (Art. 6.1(f)), although recognizing the existence of multiple bases according to specific contexts.

International transfers (Chapter V GDPR): This aspect receives special treatment. The Guidelines expressly cite the Schrems I and Schrems II opinions of the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU), recognizing the invalidation of the Privacy Shield and the imposition of additional requirements through Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs).

#### 2. NIS2 Directive

EU Directive 2022/2555 establishes common high-level cybersecurity measures. Although not explicitly mentioned in the CCBE Guidelines, the document alludes to EU-level cybersecurity legislation that generates information security obligations (Recommendation 5).

#### 3. Artificial Intelligence Act

The CCBE Guidelines recognize that cloud computing nowadays is not just about the storage of data but also about the provision of IT services which run in the cloud—a formulation that anticipates the convergence between cloud services and AI systems. This is particularly relevant for services such as drafting assistants, machine translation tools, and predictive analytics.

#### 4. Cyber Resilience Act (CRA)

The CRA, aimed at improving the cybersecurity of products and services with digital components, generates indirect obligations for cloud infrastructure providers.

### B. Spanish Implementing Legislation

LOPDGDD and Professional Regulation

The reference is implicit. The Spanish data protection regime (Organic Law 3/2018) transposes the GDPR and introduces specific provisions. Crucially, Spanish deontological regulation through Professional Association Statutes establishes confidentiality obligations that overlap with those of the GDPR.

### C. Fundamental European Law Principles

The Guidelines anchor the analysis in two fundamental rights from the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union:

- Article 7: Right to private life
- Article 8: Protection of personal data
- Article 47: Right to effective judicial protection (implicit in the right to confidential legal advice)

The recognition that if the right to confidential communications with lawyers is not ensured, clients may lack the trust to fully disclose the information necessary for the lawyer to provide accurate legal advice foundations the proposed security structure as a condition for access to justice.

### D. CJEU Jurisprudence

Schrems II (Judgment C-311/18, July 16, 2020):

The Guidelines expressly cite this opinion as a critical reference point for transfers to third countries. The CJEU invalidated the Privacy Shield and reinforced requirements for Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs), requiring that the level of protection provided for in the SCCs should be supplemented by additional safeguards.

The opinion is especially relevant for attorney obligations when using U.S. providers, requiring: (i) individual analysis of whether the destination country's laws undermine protection (Art. 46 GDPR); (ii) implementation of supplementary measures such as end-to-end encryption or pseudonymization; (iii) decision to suspend the transfer if measures prove insufficient.

Attorney-Client Confidentiality Principle (CJEU Jurisprudence):

The Guidelines invoke the protection of confidentiality of lawyer-client communications is recognised as a general principle of EU law by the European Court of Justice. This principle, although not codified in a single opinion, emerges from consistent CJEU jurisprudence in cases such as Akzo Nobel (C-550/07) and subsequent cases.

## III. CRITICAL ANALYSIS OF CONTENT AND LEGAL RELEVANCE

### A. Structure and General Approach

The Guidelines adopt a guidance structure rather than binding hard law. This architecture is deliberate: the Bars and Law Societies (formal addressees) must adapt the Guidelines to their specific national contexts. However, the content generates a de facto standard that will be invoked in professional, disciplinary, and compliance liability contexts.

Legal relevance: The Guidelines function as highest-authority soft law within the European legal ecosystem. Although not legally binding, non-compliance could evidence professional negligence or breach of fundamental ethical obligations.

### B. The Two Fundamental Professional Obligations

#### 1. Confidentiality

The Guidelines' analysis on this point is correct but incomplete. The document correctly articulates that:

- Confidentiality is the attorney's duty and right
- It applies to client information, documents, and communications
- It protects any and all information about a client or a client matter
- It applies irrespective of the source of such information

Identified gap: The Guidelines do not explicitly address whether certain cloud data processing (metadata, access logs, forensic analysis) generates secondary confidentiality obligations on the provider. Recommendation 8 (Knowing how data is processed) touches this issue tangentially but does not resolve it.

Implication: An attorney using a cloud service that collects, analyzes, or uses metadata from client-attorney communications for secondary purposes (e.g., marketing, algorithm improvement, competitive intelligence) could breach confidentiality even if substantive data is encrypted. The Guidelines should be more explicit on this point.

#### 2. Professional Technical Competence

The invocation of technical competence as a fundamental professional duty is innovative and has significant implications. The Guidelines establish that:

- Competence encompasses the continuous rapid change of the law and technological environment
- It implies the obligation to become knowledgeable about a technical product to be used for professional activities
- Risk assessment and mitigation form part of competence obligations

Critical analysis: This formulation invokes a due diligence standard that could be demanding. An attorney who fails to:

- Conduct risk assessment of the cloud provider
- Understand contractual terms of use
- Know where data is processed
- Fail to implement security measures

...could potentially breach not only data protection standards but also fundamental ethical obligations. This is more demanding than the prior standard of using available tools.

### C. The Ten Operational Recommendations Structure

Recommendation 1: Risk Analysis and Assessment

Content: It is important for lawyers to analyse and assess the risks of specific products and services that they intend to use.

Analysis: This recommendation introduces a due diligence standard that, although reasonable, does not specify:

- What level of analysis is required (deep technical vs. documentation review)
- Who should perform it (attorneys, external specialists)
- Required documentation of such analysis
- Frequency of reassessment

Weakness: Implementation in small law firms could be disproportionate. The Guidelines recognize that the sources of risks to be evaluated, the details of such evaluation and the possible measures for mitigating the risks depend on both the type and size of law firm but do not provide specific clarification for small practices.

Recommendation 2: Regulatory Compliance

Content: Combines GDPR, national deontological rules, and international transfer considerations.

Strength: Inclusion of security incident notification obligations is crucial.

Weakness: Reference to applicable deontological rules is national, not harmonized. A Spanish lawyer operating in European cloud could be subject to conflicting rules from multiple jurisdictions without clarity on their hierarchical articulation.

Recommendation 3: Following Existing Guidance

The previously published CCBE Guidelines form a coherent corpus:

- Guide on confidentiality in surveillance (2016)
- Guidance on IT security against surveillance (2018)
- Guide on online legal platforms (2018)
- Guide on AI (2022)
- Guidance on remote work tools (2020)

Analysis: Accumulation of specialized guidance creates both clarity and complexity. An attorney must be familiar with multiple documents for comprehensive compliance. This could require significant internal compliance investment.

Recommendation 4: Information Security

Content: The Guidelines cite ISO/IEC 27001, 27017, 27018, 27036-4, and SOC2 standards.

Technical-legal analysis:

- ISO/IEC 27001:2022 is the fundamental standard for information security management systems
- ISO/IEC 27017:2015 is specific to cloud services
- ISO/IEC 27018:2019 addresses protection of personally identifiable information in public clouds

Standard mention creates a compliance barrier: cloud providers lacking these certifications generate increased compliance risk. For small firms, this may limit available options.

Important specific recommendation: Lawyers must be mindful to periodically review certification status (for ISO) or request new attestations (for SOC2).

This implies ongoing monitoring obligation, not simply trust in historical certifications.

Recommendation 5: Provider Vetting

Recommendation 6 (Guidelines numbering) provides the most detailed due diligence structure:

Factors to consider:

1. Availability and quality of technical support
2. Financial stability of provider
3. Quality and transparency of technical information
4. Transparency on subcontractors
5. Compliance with certifications and codes of conduct
6. Subcontractor identity and service scope
7. Specific contractual terms

Analysis of Recommendation 6:

The document identifies several broad types of information that should be taken into account but does not hierarchize their relative importance. For an attorney evaluating providers, this creates ambiguity: what are rejection criteria, what are merely risk factors?

Key contractual subcomponents articulated:

- Periodic backups with physical and logical security
- Authentication mechanisms
- Encryption of stored data
- Access logging
- Third-party security audits
- Non-use of user data for secondary purposes
- Jurisdiction and dispute resolution
- Liability limitation
- Penalties for SLA non-compliance
- Termination and data portability terms

Critical weakness: Many standard SaaS providers (especially smaller ones) do not offer all these terms. The Guidelines do not explicitly distinguish between indispensable and desirable requirements. This creates risk that attorneys reject providers for not fulfilling the entire checklist, even when a subset of measures would be adequate.

Recommendation 6: Processing Location

Content: Lawyers must know where and how client data is processed.

The Guidelines establish that attorneys must:

- Verify legal permissibility of storage outside the firm
- Understand data protection differences by jurisdiction
- Verify transfer mechanisms used (Schrems II, SCCs, Binding Corporate Rules, certifications)

Critical analysis: Here the Guidelines provide high-value technical-legal guidance but introduce significant complexity. Post-Schrems II, transfers to the U.S. require not simply SCCs but additional measures (end-to-end encryption, pseudonymization) whose technical implementation may require specialization.

Important but underexplored recommendation: Lawyers should regularly monitor the relevant updates on legislation, case law and other information to stay updated on their obligations.

This implies monitoring obligation for failures like the Irish Data Protection Commission or CJEU ruling that could invalidate existing transfer mechanisms. For attorneys without international data privacy specialization, this is demanding.

Recommendation 7: Data Processing

Recommendation 8 (Guidelines numbering) warns that even the simplest apps and solutions can include third-party data processing and further use. These can include text editing assistants, translation, image editing, etc.

Implication: An attorney using ChatGPT for drafting, machine translation tools, or collaborative cloud editors could be outsourcing client data to third parties without explicit evaluation. The Guidelines have not yet resolved whether this constitutes confidentiality breach or simply transfer requiring transparency.

Relevant gap: The Guidelines do not explicitly address the question of LLMs (Large Language Models) trained with client data. If an attorney uses an AI model that processes confidential case data to train or improve the model, what is the legal status of such processing under GDPR?

Recommendation 8: Practice Continuity

Recommendation 9 introduces sophisticated operational resilience analysis:

Components:

1. Critical data categorization: Establish Recovery Point Objective (RPO)—maximum acceptable interval between backups
2. Local access to critical data: Maintain updated copies of most recent data (via IMAP, POP, .OST for email)
3. Alternative connectivity: Alternative internet providers, mobile access, blackout preparedness

Analysis: This recommendation is novel and practically valuable. It recognizes that cloud computing creates technical dependencies that could compromise legal service availability. Specific mention of deleting copies on one device may result in their deletion from other devices linked to the same synchronised folder system evidences detailed operational risk knowledge.

Weakness: Continuity recommendations are not explicitly integrated with professional obligations. Does an attorney who loses access to client data due to cloud failure but lacks local backup breach professional obligations beyond civil/criminal liability?

Recommendation 9: Insurance Coverage

Recommendation 10 introduces cyber insurance coverage as a risk mitigation measure.

Analysis: This creates a new compliance level: attorneys must not simply implement technical measures but also insure against their failures. This is economically significant and disproportionate for small firms.

## IV. FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPTS EXPLAINED

### A. Cloud Computing: Definitions and Models

The Guidelines deliberately avoid providing a new definition of cloud computing, recognizing the fragmentation between technical (NIST) and legal (ISO, Digital Services Directive) definitions.

The NIST definition (2011) that the Guidelines cite establishes:

Cloud computing enables ubiquitous, convenient, on-demand network access to a shared pool of configurable computing resources (e.g., networks, servers, storage, applications, and services) that can be rapidly provisioned and released with minimal management effort or service provider interaction.

Relevance for attorneys: This definition emphasizes:

1. Ubiquitous accessibility: Available from any location
2. Low management effort: Third party manages infrastructure
3. Rapid provisioning: Facilitates scalability but reduces control
4. Shared resources: Poses security and data isolation risks

The Guidelines recognize the recent evolution where cloud is not simply storage but provision of IT services which run in the cloud, instead of local server maintained by the user... including lawyers and law firms' personnel (e.g. messaging services for communication with clients, videoconferencing tools, etc.).
