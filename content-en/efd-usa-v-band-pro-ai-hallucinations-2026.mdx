# AI in Law: When Hallucinations Cost $900

## EFD USA, Inc. v. Band Pro Film and Digital, Inc. — Legal Analysis (Cal. App. 2026)

**Court:** Court of Appeal, Second District of California, Division Three
**Number:** B329314 | **Date:** February 18, 2026
**Justice:** J. Hanasona (with concurrence of Edmon, P.J. and Adams, J.)
**Result:** Appeal dismissed. Sanction of $900 imposed on attorney David E. Azar.
**Citation:** 2026 WL 457265 (Cal. App. 2d Dist. Feb. 18, 2026)
**Publication:** Not published in official reports (Cal. Rules of Court, rules 8.1105, 8.1110, 8.1115)

---

## 1. Introduction: The Problem of AI-Fabricated Citations

The proliferation of generative AI tools in legal practice has generated a phenomenon with increasing disciplinary and procedural consequences: **legal hallucinations**. Large language models (LLMs) can generate plausible but non-existent jurisprudential citations, attribute false propositions to real cases, or distort the holding of authentic decisions.

*EFD USA, Inc. v. Band Pro Film & Digital, Inc.* is, as of February 2026, one of the most detailed precedents on **the responsibility of the attorney who delegates the drafting of judicial briefs to AI systems without adequate supervision**. The California court not only imposes a financial sanction but constructs a professional diligence standard applicable in multiple jurisdictions.

This analysis examines the case from a **comparative law perspective**, drawing consequences for legal practice in Spain and in the scope of European Union Law.

> **Publication note:** The ruling is marked as *not officially published* under the California Rules of Court (rules 8.1105 and 8.1110), which limits its value as binding precedent in California courts. However, its doctrinal value makes it an essential reference of the global debate on AI and forensic ethics.

## 2. Factual Background

### 2.1 The underlying litigation

EFD USA, Inc. (EFD), a film production company, sued Band Pro Film & Digital, Inc. and other entities for fraud, negligent misrepresentation, breach of implied contract, and violation of California's *Unfair Competition Law* (UCL), claiming damages exceeding $2 million derived from cinematographic equipment leasing operations.

After a partial settlement with the so-called "Bisel Defendants" for $375,000, the trial was held against the "Band Defendants." The jury awarded EFD $109,958.17 in damages. However, the trial court applied the setoff mechanism of Article 877 of the California Code of Civil Procedure, reducing the judgment to zero because it considered that the settlement with the Bisel Defendants should be imputed entirely to the liability of the other codefendants.

### 2.2 The use of AI in drafting the appellate briefs

The most legally relevant data appears in the section dedicated to **inaccurate citations in the appellate briefs**.

Attorney David E. Azar hired an **"AI consultant"** to draft a portion of the opening brief. The consultant expressly warned Azar of the need to **verify the citations** to detect possible hallucinations. Azar hired a paralegal for that verification. Later, upon noting that the brief exceeded the word limit by approximately 6,000 words, Azar again turned to an AI system to reduce the length, instructing it to **"maintain all citations to authorities, factual and legal, unless they are duplicated,"** without adopting any subsequent verification measure.

The result was a brief with **multiple fabricated or severely distorted citations**.

## 3. Legal Analysis IRAC

### ISSUE — Legal Question

Does an attorney commit sanctionable disciplinary and procedural liability who, having delegated the drafting of a judicial brief to a generative AI system and having been warned of the risk of hallucinations, omits to verify the citations modified by a second AI process intended to reduce the brief's length?

---

### RULE — Applicable Legal Framework

**California procedural law:**

- California Rules of Court, rule 8.204(a)(1)(B): arguments must be supported by real authorities, not fabricated ones.
- California Rules of Court, rule 8.276: court's authority to impose sanctions ex officio.
- *Noland v. Land of the Free, L.P.* (2025) 114 Cal.App.5th 426, 445-448: standard of diligence in citation of authorities and quantification of sanctions.

**Ethical duty:** The court affirms that it is a fundamental duty of the attorney to read legal authorities before citing them in any judicial brief.

---

### APPLICATION — Application to the Case

The court identified at least **five fabricated or severely distorted citations**:

| Citation invoked | Attributed proposition | Proven reality |
|---|---|---|
| *Lilienthal & Fowler v. Superior Court* (1993) | Criteria to distinguish "same tort" in settlement setoffs | The case does not deal with setoffs; the textual citation does not exist |
| *Riverisland Cold Storage, Inc. v. Fresno-Madera Prod. Credit Assn.* (2013) | Admissibility of extrinsic evidence to interpret mediation agreements with arbitration clause | The case does not mention agreements, mediation, or arbitration; it proposes the contrary rule |
| *Leaf v. City of San Mateo* (1980) | Objective theory of contract interpretation | The case does not contain that language; it is a nuisance suit without contested contract |
| *Rudick v. State Bd. of Optometry* (2019) | Failure to respond to an argument implies waiver | The citation does not exist; the case reasons the opposite |
| *Milstein v. Sartain* (1943) | Default judgment admits all material allegations | The text does not include that language |

Azar's conduct presents the following pattern of proven negligence:

1. **Delegation without complete verification protocol:** He hires AI to draft, performs initial verification, but does not establish re-verification after automated modifications.
2. **Omission of supervision in the critical phase:** The second AI process that modifies the citations was not subjected to any quality control.
3. **Continuation of the pattern in the reply brief:** The same method was repeated in the *reply brief*, producing new fabricated citations.
4. **Failure of the corrective mechanism:** Azar and his co-counsel Keiter prepared an errata and corrected brief, but neither ensured that it was filed with the court.

The court distinguishes Azar's conduct from Keiter's, who formatted the reply brief under severe time constraints (received the document three hours before the deadline). Although Keiter acknowledges the errors are "unacceptable," primary responsibility falls on Azar as the author of the briefs.

---

### CONCLUSION

The court imposes a **sanction of $900** payable to the court clerk, for violating the California Rules of Court by citing fabricated authorities. The moderate amount compared to the usual range of $6,000-$12,500 reflects that the deficiencies, though serious in nature, are less extensive than in *Noland* (2025), and that Azar assumes responsibility expressly.

---

## 4. Diligence Standard: What the Court Establishes

The ruling allows extracting an **implied professional diligence standard** articulated in four principles:

**The duty of verification is personal and non-delegable.** The court underscores that it is a fundamental duty of the attorney to read the legal authorities they cite to determine that they support the propositions for which they are invoked. This duty is not satisfied by delegating verification to a paralegal or to another AI process.

**Each automated modification restarts the risk.** AI can introduce hallucinations at any phase of the editing process, not only in initial drafting. Sequential use of multiple tools multiplies risk points and requires chained verifications.

**Prior warning of risk aggravates responsibility.** The AI consultant warned Azar of the need to verify citations. Azar was, therefore, aware of the specific risk. This circumstance converts the omission into qualified negligence versus a known risk.

**Good faith does not exclude sanction.** Azar attributes the errors to inadvertent causes, and the court accepts this version. However, subjective good faith does not exonerate when the objective standard of diligence has not been observed.

> **Practical rule:** The attorney who uses generative AI to draft or edit judicial briefs must implement a **citation verification protocol that activates after each automated text modification**, regardless of the purpose of said modification.

## 5. Comparative Perspective: Implications for Spain and the EU

### 5.1 Spanish ethical framework

The General Statute of the Spanish Bar (Royal Decree 135/2021) establishes in Article 19 the attorney's duty of diligence. This standard, analogous to the Anglo-American *reasonable attorney* standard, is equally susceptible to violation through careless use of AI tools. The General Council of the Spanish Bar has issued guidance where verification of AI-generated citations is expressly mentioned as the attorney's obligation.

### 5.2 EU Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act)

**Regulation (EU) 2024/1689**, of progressive application since 2025, does not classify generative legal assistance systems as high-risk systems under Annex III, except when used in contexts of administration of justice (Art. 6.2 in relation with Annex III, point 8). However, **GPAI systems** like LLMs are subject to Title IX of the AI Act, with transparency obligations regarding their capabilities and limitations.

The obligation of GPAI providers to document known model limitations (Art. 53 AI Act)—including the tendency toward hallucinations—reinforces the position of the attorney-user who receives explicit warnings and yet omits verification of outputs.

### 5.3 Attorney civil liability under Spanish law

In Spain, attorney civil liability is governed by Article 1101 of the Civil Code. Supreme Court jurisprudence (STS July 14, 2016, rec. 2.530/2014; STS October 22, 2019, rec. 3.530/2017) has consolidated that the attorney is liable for damages derived from their negligence, provided there is a causal relationship between said negligence and the harmful result.

Filing briefs with fabricated citations could generate liability to the client if the procedural strategy is compromised, regardless of disciplinary consequences before the Bar Association.

### 5.4 Procedural liability in Spain

The **Civil Procedure Act** establishes in Article 247 the principle of procedural good faith and the possibility of imposing fines for reckless or bad-faith conduct. Filing non-existent jurisprudential citations could qualify as a violation of this principle, enabling the court to impose economic sanctions.

## 6. The Merits: Art. 877 CPC California and Settlement Setoff

Beyond the AI issue, the ruling resolves a litigation on the application of **Article 877 of the California Code of Civil Procedure** (setoff mechanism for settlement with codefendants in solidary liability). The court confirms that the agreement with the Bisel Defendants should fully offset the judgment against the Band Defendants, and that the absence of express allocation in the settlement prevented the court from limiting the scope of the setoff.

This reasoning has comparative interest from the Spanish perspective of **judicial settlement** (Arts. 1809 et seq. CC) and improper solidarity among codefendants, areas where Spanish jurisprudence is equally demanding with the need to express allocation of responsibilities in the settlement agreement itself.

## 7. Practical Protocol for Law Firms: Use of AI in Judicial Briefs

The case allows formulating the following **operational recommendations**:

**1. Segregate drafting and editing phases.** Treat each intervention of an AI system as an independent event that restarts the citation verification cycle.

**2. Personal verification by signing attorney.** The final review of all jurisprudential citations must be personal and direct, not delegable to paralegals or automated systems.

**3. Document the process.** Maintain records of which AI tools were used, what warnings they issued, and what controls were applied. This documentation may be relevant in disciplinary or civil claims.

**4. Written firm policy on AI use.** Establish protocols regulating the use of generative AI in brief preparation, with clear responsibilities assigned to each attorney.

**5. Primary sources as basis.** Use verified databases (Westlaw, Aranzadi, CURIA) as the primary source, using AI only for assistance in structuring or synthesis, never as an autonomous source of citations.

**6. Continuing education.** Ignorance of the hallucination phenomenon does not exonerate from liability; training on LLM limitations is today part of the required professional diligence standard.

---

## 8. Conclusions

*EFD USA, Inc. v. Band Pro Film & Digital, Inc.* (Cal. App. 2026) represents a milestone in **jurisprudence on attorney responsibility in the use of generative AI**. Its most relevant doctrinal contributions are:

The court clearly establishes that the duty to verify legal citations is personal to the attorney and is not exhausted by a single check if the text is subsequently modified by an automated system.

Prior awareness of the hallucination risk aggravates the characterization of negligent conduct and raises the standard of diligence required.

Although the court imposes a moderate sanction ($900), the sanctioning framework could reach $12,500 in more serious cases, configuring a significant economic and reputational risk for the attorney.

The ruling has indirect normative value for jurisdictions like Spain, where ethical duties of diligence and the principle of procedural good faith are equally applicable, and where the EU AI Act adds a specific regulatory layer on the use of GPAI systems.

The case is, ultimately, a reminder that generative AI is an assistance tool, not a substitute for legal judgment and the attorney's professional responsibility.
