# European Ethical Charter on the Use of Artificial Intelligence in Judicial Systems and Their Environment

[Download original document (PDF)](/fuentes/carta-europea-inteligencia-artificial.pdf)

## 1. Introduction to the Ethical Framework of AI in Justice

Digital transformation in Council of Europe member states does not constitute a mere administrative advance but a **strategic reconfiguration** that requires balancing technological efficiency with the non-negotiable safeguarding of fundamental rights.

According to the guidelines of the **European Commission for the Efficiency of Justice (CEPEJ)**, this deployment must be anchored in the conventionality block constituted by the **European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)** and **Convention 108**.

The integration of **Artificial Intelligence (AI)** in judicial infrastructure promises to optimize procedural speed but faces ontological risks that threaten judicial independence and procedural fairness.

The **European Ethical Charter on the Use of AI in Judicial Systems** acts as a preventive regulatory framework designed to guide the design and deployment of data science tools in the public sector. Its purpose is to transition from reactive regulation toward an *ethical-by-design* and *human-rights-by-design* paradigm.

The **five fundamental principles** of the Charter are:

- **Respect for fundamental rights**  
  Guaranteeing access to court and the right to a fair trial, compatible with the ECHR from the algorithm's conception.

- **Non-discrimination**  
  Actively preventing algorithms from processing sensitive data (ethnic origin, beliefs, sexual orientation) in a discriminatory manner.

- **Quality and security**  
  Use of certified sources, integrity of data, and secure technological environments with multidisciplinary design.

- **Transparency, impartiality, and fairness**  
  Intelligible and externally auditable processing methods, balancing intellectual property and justice interests.

- **Under user control**  
  The judge maintains total autonomy, control over options, and the ability to deviate from any algorithmic suggestion.

## 2. Technical Paradigm: Machine Learning vs. Legal Reasoning

It is key to demystify the "predictive justice" discourse. It is not about clairvoyance but statistical *forecasting* based on historical data.

**Machine learning does not replicate** human legal reasoning (teleological and axiological). It operates inductively: linking lexical groups with statistical results without understanding normative meaning.

## 3. Data Infrastructure: Open Data and Privacy

Judicial data are the "oil of the 21st century." The **open data** policy is a requirement for training AI models but poses serious challenges:

- Real anonymization vs. pseudonymization (almost impossible in Big Data due to re-identification)
- **Concrete risks**:
  1. Judge profiling
  2. Strategic forum shopping
  3. Violation of **Art. 22 GDPR** (automated decisions without human oversight)

A biased database crystallizes historical injustices under the appearance of technical neutrality.

## 4. Sectoral Applications: Civil, Commercial, and Administrative Justice

High potential in:

- Online dispute resolution (**ODR**)
- Automated schedules in repetitive or low-value litigation

**But** there is risk of "digital solutionism" that sacrifices the principle of adversarial proceedings.

**Empirical example** (France – Douai and Rennes courts, 2017):

> The software confused simple lexical occurrences with deep legal causalities → aberrant results.

## 5. Deterministic Risk in Criminal Justice

Maximum reserve because **individual liberty** is at stake.

Europe prioritizes rehabilitation and individualization of sentences (vs. U.S. actuarial approach – e.g., COMPAS with racial biases).

**Non-waivable guarantees**:

- Right to a **natural judge** (final human decision)
- Right to a **reasoned decision** (not replaceable by AI)
- Right to know the **algorithmic logic** (modernized Convention 108)
- **Prohibition** of applying group statistics to individual sentencing

## 6. Conclusions: Research Gaps and Future Directions

AI must **strengthen** the Rule of Law, not become an administrative end.

Studies (e.g., UCL – Aletras et al.) achieve ~79% accuracy in ECHR, but based more on facts than complex legal reasoning.

### Future research directions

1. Independent external audit and certification of algorithms
2. Cyberethics and digital literacy for judges and citizens
3. Analysis of excessive standardization risk that blocks jurisprudential evolution
