# Intellectual Property in the Digital Single Market: What European Case Law (2018-2025) Changes Forever
The digital ecosystem has overflowed the classical categories of intellectual property. Between 2018 and 2025, the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) and the national courts of Member States have issued rulings that redefine who can be an author, what can be protected, who is liable for infringements, and how damages are quantified. This analysis synthesizes the most relevant doctrinal axes from the compilation prepared by the European Union Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO), with particular attention to practical implications for rights holders, digital platforms, and legal practitioners.
## Can AI Be an Author? The New Threshold of Originality in European Law
The CJEU's case law has consolidated the concept of **"work" as an autonomous term of Union law**. For an object to deserve copyright protection, two conditions must be met: it must be identifiable with sufficient precision and objectivity, and it must constitute an intellectual creation of its own author, reflecting its personality through free and creative choices.
This requirement has direct consequences for content generated by artificial intelligence. Under the current regulatory framework, **images, texts, or compositions produced exclusively by AI systems are not "works" in the legal sense**, as they are not the result of the creative activity of a natural person. The use of opaque algorithms to generate results from text prompts does not equate to human creative control over the final expression.
The matter becomes more complex when the human being selects, organizes, or significantly modifies material generated by the machine: in such cases, protection may apply regarding the identifiable human contribution, not the autonomously generated content. This distinction will be central in the litigation ahead on training and exploitation of generative models.
## Applied Art and Industrial Design: Accumulation of Protections Is No Longer Taboo
One of the most relevant changes for the design sector is the consolidation of the **cumulative protection regime**. Union law prescribes that there is no hierarchy between the protection conferred by design law and that derived from copyright: the same object may benefit from both, provided it meets the specific requirements of each regime.
Member States may not require a higher artistic value threshold for applied arts compared to other categories of works. This doctrine, established in cases involving footwear (*Birkenstock*) and luxury goods (*Hermès*, *Longchamp*), requires rethinking the protection strategy of any company whose main asset is product design.
Legal analysis must distinguish which parts of the object are arbitrary—resulting from the designer's creative will—and which are predetermined by technical, ergonomic, or sectorial functions. Only the former activate copyright protection.
## The CDSM Directive and the End of the "Black Box" for Platforms
The entry into force of Article 17 of Directive (EU) 2019/790 has radically transformed the legal position of online content-sharing service providers (OCSSPs). Platforms like YouTube or TikTok can no longer simply rely on the liability exemption of the E-Commerce Directive: they are now presumed to carry out an **act of communication to the public** by giving access to works uploaded by their users.
To mitigate their liability, OCSSPs must demonstrate that they have made their "best efforts" to obtain licenses and to ensure the unavailability of specifically notified works. The CJEU has clarified, however, that automatic content filtering systems (ACR) cannot lead to a general monitoring obligation, under penalty of violating users' fundamental rights and blocking lawful uses covered by quotation, criticism, or caricature.
The boundary between a **passive** intermediary—that merely hosts data in a technical and neutral manner—and an **active** one—that organizes, indexes, or promotes content—determines whether the "safe harbor" clause applies or whether the intermediary assumes direct liability. In hybrid business models, this distinction requires case-by-case analysis.
## Data Mining, NFTs, and the Sui Generis Protection of Databases
The Digital Single Market has introduced specific exceptions for **text and data mining (TDM)**, which allow the reproduction of works for scientific research purposes, even when used to train artificial intelligence systems, provided there is no decisive influence from profit-making entities on research organizations.
For commercial TDM uses, rights holders retain the ability to **reserve their rights through machine-readable means**. A reservation expressed in natural language may be sufficient if there are technologies capable of detecting it, which considerably raises the due diligence duty of platform operators.
In the realm of digital assets, the exhibition of replicas of protected artworks via **NFTs** in virtual environments may constitute an infringement of the author's transformation and communication rights, unless the exhibition has a transformative character, lacks direct commercial purpose, and does not compete with the normal exploitation of the original work.
## Enforcement: Quantifying Damage and Balancing Fundamental Rights
The effective enforcement of intellectual property rights hinges on three cumulative criteria for determining compensation: negative economic consequences (including lost profits), moral damage suffered by the rights holder, and illicit profits obtained by the infringer. The **hypothetical royalty** method—what the infringer would have paid for a license—is valid, but must be calculated on realistic market rates and may be increased to reflect the unauthorized nature of the use.
In digital enforcement matters, dynamic blocking orders against DNS providers (such as Cloudflare or Quad9) are admissible when piracy sites mutate through mirror domains or aliases, provided the measures pass a strict proportionality test and do not affect lawful access to information.
The intersection with Criminal Law requires rigor: the principle of legality prohibits extensive interpretation of criminal types to the detriment of the defendant. The unauthorized retransmission of content that does not strictly qualify as literary, artistic, or scientific works—such as sporting events—cannot be classified as a crime against intellectual property; it must be redirected, if applicable, to crimes against the market and consumers.

## Conclusions and Practical Recommendations
The period 2018-2025 has been the most prolific in European digital intellectual property jurisprudence. Five operational conclusions stand out:
- **The European originality threshold is low but demanding in its subjective dimension**: mere investment of time or effort is insufficient; the author's creative imprint must be proven, which excludes AI-autonomously generated content.
- **Cumulative protections (design + copyright) is the norm, not the exception**: any product design company must audit its rights portfolio in light of this doctrine.
- **Digital platforms must rethink their content moderation systems**: the balance between effective filtering and respect for lawful uses does not admit purely algorithmic solutions.
- **Rights reservation against TDM must be implemented in machine-readable form**: a usage policy published only in natural language may not be sufficient as detection technology matures.
- **Hypothetical royalty calculation requires specific expert justification**: generic market rates are insufficient; the increment for the unauthorized nature of use must be documented.
