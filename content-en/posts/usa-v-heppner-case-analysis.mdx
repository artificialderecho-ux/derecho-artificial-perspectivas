# Generative AI and the Collapse of Legal Privilege: A Doctrinal Analysis of *United States v. Heppner*

[Download Case PDF](/fuentes/usa_vs_heppner.pdf)

## 1. Executive Summary

The integration of large language models (LLMs) into professional and personal practice has generated a frontal collision with the pillars of Criminal Procedure. The case *United States v. Heppner*, 25 Cr. 503 (JSR), before the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York (S.D.N.Y.), serves as a somber reminder that the pursuit of technological assistance by a defendant can irreversibly annul their constitutional protections.

The central thesis of this analysis holds that the use of commercial generative AI platforms under Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) models—whose data governance policies include model training and governmental disclosure authority—constitutes a *de facto* waiver of attorney-client privilege.

In the Second Circuit's judicial ecosystem, the "technological convenience" of delegating legal risk analysis to an algorithm is not an innocuous aid, but an insurmountable procedural vulnerability that exposes the defendant's mental processes to Government scrutiny.

This case is strategic for contemporary criminal defense: it demonstrates that the digital trail generated by client recklessness can dismantle defense confidentiality even before it is formally articulated.

## 2. Factual Framework: The Beneficient Scheme and the Digital Trail

The federal investigation of Bradley Heppner, centered on a sophisticated financial fraud scheme, underscores the importance of digital records in establishing criminal intent (*mens rea*). The charges brought by the prosecution not only describe a massive embezzlement but a series of deliberate maneuvers to deceive investors and auditors through corporate opacity.

Per the *Indictment*, Heppner faces **five federal charges**:

- **Securities Fraud and Wire Fraud**: The core of the scheme consisted of presenting Highland Consolidated Limited Partnership (HCLP) as an independent and unrelated lender, when in reality it was a fictitious entity secretly controlled by Heppner for his personal benefit.

- **Conspiracy to Commit Securities and Wire Fraud**: The orchestration of fraudulent transfers between interconnected entities.

- **False Statements to Auditors and Falsification of Records**: Active efforts to conceal the actual relationship between Beneficient, GWG, and HCLP.

The fraud mechanism enabled the diversion of approximately **$300 million** from the public company GWG. From this sum, Heppner obtained personal benefit exceeding **$150 million**, distributed in ostentatious expenditures that triggered federal suspicion: **$40 million** for the renovation and furnishing of his Dallas mansion, and **$10 million** in private jets and personal credit card liquidation.

The decisive digital trail originated on November 4, 2025, during the search of Heppner's property and his subsequent arrest. The discovery of **31 documents** generated using Claude AI (Anthropic) was devastating. The specificity of Heppner's prompts—inquiring about HCLP's independence and the legality of GWG payments under investigation—invalidates the narrative of "academic research." These documents evidence clear *consciousness of guilt*, demonstrating that the defendant knew the illegality of his actions and was actively seeking to mitigate his legal exposure before arrest. This digital activity directly connects to the collapse of the most sacred protection in litigation: attorney-client privilege.

## 3. The Collapse of Attorney-Client Privilege

Second Circuit jurisprudence requires that attorney-client privilege be interpreted restrictively, as its application is in constant "derogation from the search for truth" (*United States v. Correia*, 468 F. Supp. 3d 618, 621 (S.D.N.Y. 2020)). Heppner's interactions with Claude fail at every link of the legal syllogism required for protection:

- **Premise 1 (Qualified Person)**: Per *United States v. Mejia*, 655 F.3d 126, 132 (2d Cir. 2011), privilege requires communication with a licensed attorney. A machine lacks enabling credentials, bar membership, and crucially, is not subject to ethical duties of loyalty or professional confidentiality. Legally, consulting Claude is equivalent to confiding a secret to an unqualified third party.

- **Premise 2 (Purpose of Obtaining Legal Advice)**: The "predominant purpose" of the communication must be obtaining professional legal advice. However, Claude operates under a "Constitution" (since May 9, 2023) and terms of service that explicitly disclaim providing legal advice, instead suggesting consultation with a qualified attorney. Heppner could not reasonably claim an expectation of legal advice from an entity that contractually rejects it.

- **Premise 3 (Third-Party Doctrine)**: Following *In re von Bulow*, 828 F.2d 94 (2d Cir. 1987) and *In re Richard Roe, Inc.*, 68 F.3d 38 (2d Cir. 1995), voluntary disclosure of information to a third party destroys confidentiality. By entering sensitive data into a commercial SaaS environment, Heppner broke the privacy seal necessary to invoke privilege.

The impact is definitive: the introduction of Anthropic as a commercial intermediary defeats the confidential purpose of the communication, turning defense preparation into corporate data accessible to the State.

## 4. Work Product Immunity

The work product doctrine seeks to preserve a "zone of privacy" for attorneys to develop litigation theories and strategies (*United States v. Adlman*, 134 F.3d 1194, 1196 (2d Cir. 1998)). Nevertheless, this protection is also inapplicable to documents generated by Heppner due to the absence of professional direction.

In comparative analysis with the standard of *In re Grand Jury Subpoenas Dated Mar. 19, 2002 and Aug. 2, 2002*, 318 F.3d 379 (2d Cir. 2003), Heppner's documents lack the element of "attorney direction." The defense admitted that Heppner conducted the searches on his **own initiative**, without his attorneys directing the use of Claude. Under the precedent of *United States v. Buyer*, 2023 WL 1381970 (S.D.N.Y. Jan. 31, 2023), the court clearly distinguishes between an attorney's protected strategy and a "layperson's" independent investigation.

This ruling has a critical effect: the subsequent transmission of these documents to his legal team does not purge their original nature. As established in *United States v. Correia*, 468 F. Supp. 3d 618 (S.D.N.Y. 2020), mere transmission of pre-existing non-privileged materials to an attorney does not retroactively "cloak" them with procedural immunity. Heppner independently created incriminating evidence, and no subsequent delivery to his attorneys could reverse that fact.

## 5. Political and Technological Dimension: Model Training as Waiver

The architectural design of commercial generative AI directly collides with the duty of confidentiality. This is not merely passive data storage, but dynamic processing for commercial purposes. Anthropic's Privacy Policies (in effect at the time of the 2025 searches) explicitly inform the company collects both prompts and outputs to train models and improve services. Additionally, it reserves the right to disclose this information to "government regulatory authorities." This contractual consent reduces the user's "privacy interest" to a legally insufficient level to invoke constitutional or procedural protections (*In re OpenAI, Inc., Copyright Infringement Litig.*, 802 F. Supp. 3d 688 (S.D.N.Y. 2025)).

As Ira P. Robbins maintains in *Against an AI Privilege* (2025), extending legal privilege to AI interactions would be contrary to the public interest, given that technology companies do not possess the fiduciary obligations that sustain the justice system. By accepting these terms to obtain AI utility, the user voluntarily accepts their information will be commercially processed, eliminating any reasonable expectation of secrecy from the State.

## 6. Critical Issues and Procedural Resolution

The fate of evidence in the *Heppner* case was sealed by a series of procedural calculations and technical realities that accelerated the loss of control over information:

- **Voluntary Disclosure as Strategic Error**: It was the defense itself that, following the November 4 search, informed the Government about the use of Claude. Although presented as an act of transparency under the Privilege Protocol, it proved to be a catastrophic strategic decision that placed the digital trail at the center of the legal dispute.

- **Rejection of the Diary Analogy**: The defense attempted to invoke *United States v. DeFonte*, 441 F.3d 92 (2d Cir. 2006), comparing the queries to personal notes or a digital diary. The court dismissed this analogy: while a diary is a static repository of private thought, Claude is a **dynamic third-party processor** that trains its models with supplied data. This interaction eliminates the "unilateral and private" nature of personal notes.

- **December 11, 2025 Privilege Protocol**: The parties agreed to segregate the 31 documents from the prosecution team while their nature was litigated. However, upon confirmation of no privilege, the Government obtained direct access to the defendant's mental preparation before the trial scheduled for April 2026.

## 7. Conclusion: The Heppner Precedent and the Future of Defense

The case *United States v. Heppner* closes the door on ambiguity: commercial AI tools are, under no circumstances, safe spaces for criminal defense preparation. This precedent establishes that technological convenience cannot replace the rigorous standards of confidentiality required by law.

For contemporary forensic practice, an unavoidable warning arises: any legal consultation made to a commercial third party will be treated by courts as a public search. To avoid privilege waiver, defendants and their firms must migrate exclusively to "on-premise" AI tools or private computing environments that contractually guarantee data is not used for training and is not subject to third-party disclosure policies. Ultimately, the search for truth prevails over the careless use of emerging technologies that, by their very commercial nature, are antithetical to due process confidentiality.
