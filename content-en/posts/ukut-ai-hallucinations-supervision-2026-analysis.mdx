# UKUT: Landmark Judgment on AI Hallucinations, Professional Supervision, and Confidentiality Violations

**Reference**: UK and R (Munir) v Secretary of State for the Home Department [2026] UKUT 00081 (IAC)  
**Court**: Upper Tribunal (Immigration and Asylum Chamber)  
**Judges**: UTJ Lindsley, UTJ Keith, UTJ Blundell  
**Hearing Date**: October 15 and November 5, 2025  
**Promulgation**: November 17, 2025  
**Publication**: Crown Copyright 2026

## Executive Summary

The British Upper Tribunal has issued a **landmark judgment** establishing the most comprehensive precedent to date in common law jurisdiction regarding professional liability for the use of artificial intelligence in legal practice. This decision goes significantly beyond prior cases (including the French TA Orléans of December 29, 2025) by:

1. **Adjudicating two cases simultaneously** with different levels of culpability
2. **Establishing that the supervisor is MORE culpable** than the direct author of the hallucinations
3. **Warning about Google AI** as an additional source of hallucinations (not only ChatGPT)
4. **Declaring that uploading documents to ChatGPT constitutes confidentiality breach** and waiver of professional privilege
5. **Reforming court procedures** requiring specific statement of truth regarding citation verification
6. **Establishing clear criteria** for referral to regulatory bodies

This judgment marks a before and after in AI legal practice in Europe and the Commonwealth.

- **First global judicial case** on ChatGPT hallucinations

**2. Park v. Kim (British Columbia Court, Canada, 2023)**
- Second documented international case

- Judicial warning but no specific sanction
- Emphasis on "unverified technologies"

#### 2024-2025: Problem Expansion

**3. Unpublished cases in multiple jurisdictions**
- Exponential increase in detected cases
- Jurisprudence development in Australia, New Zealand, USA
- Creation of working groups in professional associations

#### 2025: European Doctrinal Consolidation

**4. R (Ayinde) v London Borough of Haringey [2025] EWHC 1383 (Admin)**
- **Landmark Divisional Court judgment** (June 6, 2025)

**5. TA Orléans n° 2506461 (France, December 29, 2025)**
- **First continental European judgment** on the subject

- Precedent in European civil law

**6. [2026] UKUT 00081 (IAC) (United Kingdom, November 17, 2025)**
- **Most comprehensive judgment to date**
- Adjudicates two cases simultaneously
- Establishes principles on supervision and confidentiality
- Implements immediate procedural reforms
- **Subject of present analysis**

### B. The Hamid Jurisdiction: Basis for UKUT Action

#### Origin and Development

The UKUT 00081 judgment falls within the so-called **"Hamid jurisdiction"**, a doctrine permitting the Upper Tribunal to supervise the professional conduct of lawyers appearing before it.

**R (Hamid) v SSHD [2012] EWHC 3070 (Admin)**:
- Establishes that the High Court has inherent jurisdiction to supervise professional conduct
- Part of the function of ensuring proper administration of justice
- Does not replace regulatory bodies, but may refer serious cases

**Extension to Upper Tribunal**:
- Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007, s.3(5): Upper Tribunal is a "superior court of record"
- S.25: Has the same powers as the High Court in incidental matters
- R (Shrestha & Ors) v SSHD [2018] UKUT 242 (IAC): Confirms applicability of Hamid
- R (Hoxha & Ors) v SSHD [2019] UKUT 124 (IAC): Develops professional duties

**Justification of Hamid Jurisdiction**:

> "The High Court has an inherent jurisdiction to govern its own procedure and part of that jurisdiction requires it to ensure that the lawyers interacting with the court conduct themselves according to proper professional standards" [§2 UKUT 00081]

This jurisdiction is **essential** in the AI context because:
1. Regulatory bodies (SRA, IAA) may take months/years to investigate
2. The hallucination problem requires immediate response
3. The pedagogical function of the court is crucial for changing practices

## II. ISSUES: LEGAL QUESTIONS PRESENTED

### Principal Issue: Supervision Standards in the AI Era

**What is the standard of diligence required of a supervisor (partner, COLP, senior solicitor) when a junior fee-earner under their responsibility presents documents with false AI-generated references?**

This question involves determining:
- The scope of supervision duty in environments with AI access
- Whether the supervisor is more or equally culpable than the direct author
- What internal control systems are required
- When referral to the regulatory body is appropriate

### Secondary Issues Derived from Case 1 (TMF Immigration Lawyers)

**Issue 1.1**: Does Google AI (not only ChatGPT) constitute a source of hallucinations requiring specific warning to fee-earners?

**Issue 1.2**: Is self-reporting to regulatory bodies sufficient to avoid court referral?

**Issue 1.3**: Does uploading client documents to ChatGPT (open-source AI) constitute breach of confidentiality and professional privilege?

### Secondary Issues Derived from Case 2 (City Law Practice)

**Issue 2.1**: Must a Compliance Officer for Legal Practice (COLP) maintain detailed records of who works on each case for traceability purposes?

**Issue 2.2**: Is it acceptable for a "trainee" or "very junior caseworker" to draft grounds for judicial review without effective supervision?

**Issue 2.3**: Do personal circumstances (family illness) constitute sufficient justification for supervision failures?

**Issue 2.4**: How should the case of multiple files potentially affected by the same corrupted "precedent" with false citations be handled?

### Cross-Cutting Issue: Procedural Reforms

**Issue 3**: Is it appropriate for courts to modify their forms to require specific statements of truth regarding verification of cited authorities?

## III. RULES: APPLICABLE LEGAL AND DOCTRINAL FRAMEWORK

### A. United Kingdom Primary Legislation

#### 1. Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act 2007

**Section 3(5)**:
> "The Upper Tribunal is a superior court of record."

**Section 25 (Tribunal Procedure Rules)**:
Grants the Upper Tribunal powers equivalent to the High Court in matters related to its functions, including professional conduct supervision.

#### 2. Legal Services Act 2007

**Section 176** (Conduct Rules):
Establishes that approved regulatory bodies must maintain rules on professional conduct, including duties toward the court.

**Rule 11(5A) UTIAC**:
The representative must be authorized to litigate in the High Court pursuant to the Legal Services Act 2007.

### B. Soft Law and Deontological Codes

#### 1. SRA Standards and Regulations (Solicitors)

**Principle 1**: Act in a way that maintains public confidence in the profession

**Principle 2**: Act with integrity

**Principle 4**: Act in the best interest of the client

**Principle 5**: Provide competent service

**Code of Conduct Rule 1.4**: Do not deceive or attempt to deceive the court

**Code of Conduct Rule 5.1**: Provide competent service in a reasonable time

#### 2. IAA (Immigration Advice Authority) Code of Standards

Previously OISC (Office of the Immigration Services Commissioner)

**Principle 1**: Act in a way that upholds the Rule of Law and proper administration of justice

**Standard 1.2**: Must not knowingly or recklessly allow courts and tribunals to be misled

**Standard 2**: Provide competent service

#### 3. Law Society Guidance on Generative AI (2025)

Published following Ayinde, establishes:

- **Risk recognition**: Generative AI tools can produce "hallucinations"
- **Duty of verification**: Every output must be verified against primary sources
- **Mandatory training**: Supervisors must train their staff
- **Usage policies**: Firms must have clear policies on which tools to use
- **Confidentiality**: Warning about using open-source tools

### C. Applicable Jurisprudence

#### 1. R (Hamid) v SSHD [2012] EWHC 3070 (Admin)

**Ratio decidendi**: The High Court has inherent jurisdiction to supervise the conduct of lawyers appearing before it, as part of its function of guaranteeing proper administration of justice.

**Principles established**:
- The lawyer's primary duty is to the court, not the client
- Referral to regulatory bodies is appropriate in cases of misconduct
- The court must balance the need to maintain standards with proportionality

#### 2. R (Sathivel) & Ors v SSHD [2018] EWHC 913 (Admin)

**Development of Hamid**: Establishes that referrals are appropriate when:
- There are patterns of misconduct
- The gravity of the fault justifies it
- It is necessary to maintain professional standards
- It is proportionate to the circumstances

#### 3. R (DVP) v SSHD [2021] EWHC 606 (Admin)

**On supervision**: Emphasizes supervisors' responsibility for juniors' work:
- Supervisors have positive duty to establish control systems
- Delegating without effective supervision is not an excuse
- Work pressure does not justify systematic failures

#### 4. R (Ayinde) v London Borough of Haringey [2025] EWHC 1383 (Admin)

**Landmark judgment on AI**. Establishes:

**On AI hallucinations** [§6]:
> "Freely available generative artificial intelligence tools, trained on a large language model such as ChatGPT are not capable of conducting reliable legal research. Such tools can produce apparently coherent and plausible responses to prompts, but those coherent and plausible responses may turn out to be entirely incorrect."

**Factors for determining referral** [§24]:
1. Gravity of conduct
2. Whether it was intentional, reckless, or negligent
3. Impact on administration of justice
4. Pattern of conduct vs. isolated incident
5. Professional's response (admission, remedy, insight)

**On excuses** [§29]:
Lack of access to subscription databases is NOT an excuse—there are free resources like National Archives' caselaw website and Inns of Court libraries.

**On "correct content, wrong label"**:
The court firmly rejected the argument that "the legal principles were correct even if the citations were false," comparing it to a mislabeled can [§67-68 of Ayinde, cited in §39 UKUT 00081].

#### 5. R (Shrestha & Ors) v SSHD [2018] UKUT 242 (IAC)

Confirms that the Upper Tribunal has Hamid jurisdiction and establishes that:
- The purpose is to maintain professional standards
- It does not replace formal disciplinary procedures
- It is a last-resort tool for serious cases

## IV. APPLICATION: DETAILED ANALYSIS OF THE TWO CASES

### A. CASE 1: TMF Immigration Lawyers - Mr Tahir Mohammed

#### Facts of the Case

**Context**:
- Appeal against First-tier Tribunal decision (September 18, 2025)
- Mr Mohammed: Level 3 accredited adviser (IAA)
- Also solicitor with practicing certificate

**The Detected Hallucination**:
- Grounds of appeal cited: **"Horleston v SSHD [2007] EWCA Civ 654"**
- **Reality**: That citation corresponds to "South Tyneside Metropolitan Borough Council v Anderson & Others" (equal pay case, totally irrelevant)
- The case "Horleston v SSHD" **never existed**

**Procedure**:
1. **September 23, 2025**: Upper Tribunal issues show cause notice
2. **Same day**: Mr Mohammed responds:
   - Admits the error
   - Attributes it to "human error"
   - **Denies** using ChatGPT or AI
   - Commits to additional training
3. **October 10, 2025**: Revised witness statement:
   - After reviewing browsing history, cannot explain how the false citation arose
   - **Admits**: "cannot dismiss the fact that the case was an AI creation as there is no other explanation"
   - Recognizes it "occurred unknowingly"
4. **October 14, 2025**: Self-report to IAA and SRA
5. **October 15, 2025**: Hamid hearing

**Additional Revelations at Hearing**:

Mr Mohammed confessed to AI uses **unrelated to the grounds**:
1. **Client emails**: Uploaded drafts to ChatGPT to "improve" them
2. **Home Office decision letters**: Uploaded to ChatGPT to summarize them

**Crucial acknowledgment** [§16]:
> "He informed us that he now realises that this is a data breach and will inform the clients that he has done this, as well as the IAA and the SRA."

#### Court's Investigation into Google AI

The court conducted its own investigation into how "Horleston" could have been generated [§17]:

**Experiment**:
- They asked Google AI questions about asylum cases
- Google AI **invented details of the nonexistent case**:
  - Compositions of the Court of Appeal bench
  - Descriptions that it was about "fairness and asylum applications"
  - Judges who plausibly were at the CoA in 2007, but never judged that case

**Variation in responses**:
> "By asking Google AI the same question in slightly different ways, we were able to elicit various different compositions of the bench which decided that fictitious case."

**Court's conclusion** [§17]:
> "The danger in using Artificial Intelligence for legal research is not confined to generative AI models such as ChatGPT, therefore; the use of Google AI for legal research is equally likely to generate results which are false but which might initially be thought to be accurate."

#### Legal Subsumption: Did Mr Mohammed Violate His Professional Duties?

**Application of IAA Code of Standards**:

**Principle 1**: "Act in a way that upholds the Rule of Law and proper administration of justice"
- **Violation**: Presented false jurisprudence to the court
- **Mitigating factor**: It was inadvertent, not intentional

**Standard 1.2**: "Must not knowingly or recklessly allow the courts and tribunals to be misled"
- **Key question**: Was it "reckless"?
- **Analysis**: Using AI without verification is reckless
- **Conclusion**: Yes, acted recklessly by not verifying

**Application of SRA Code of Conduct** (also applicable):

**Principle 1** (public confidence): Violated by presenting false information

**Principle 2** (integrity): Violated, though without bad faith

**Principle 5** (competence): Violated by lack of digital competence—not understanding AI risks

**Rule 1.4** (not deceiving the court): Technically violated, though without intention

#### Confidentiality and Privilege Violation

**Facts** [§16, §21]:
- Mr Mohammed uploaded:
  1. Client emails (with confidential information)
  2. Home Office decision letters (with client's personal data)
- Platform: ChatGPT (open-source, free version)

**Subsumption**:

**Client confidentiality**:
- **Rule**: Duty of confidentiality (common law + SRA Code 6.3)
- **Application**: ChatGPT free version stores and uses data for training
- **Conclusion**: **Clear violation** of confidentiality

**Professional privilege**:
- **Rule**: Legal professional privilege (lawyer-client)
- **Application**: Uploading privileged document to public platform = waiver of privilege
- **Conclusion**: [§21] **"waive legal privilege"**

**GDPR/UK GDPR**:
- **Rule**: Art. 5(1)(f) GDPR—integrity and confidentiality
- **Application**: Uploading personal data to ChatGPT without consent
- **Conclusion**: **Data breach** that must be reported to ICO

**Court's pronouncement** [§21]:
> "To put client letters and decision letters from the Home Office into an open source AI tool, such as ChatGPT, is to place this information on the internet in the public domain, and thus to breach client confidentiality and waive legal privilege."

**Recommendation**:
> "Any regulated legal professional or firm that does so would, in addition to needing to bring this to the attention of their regulator, be advised to consult with the Information Commissioner's Office."

**Suggested safe alternative** [§21]:
> "Closed source AI tools which do not place information in the public domain, such as Microsoft Copilot, are available for tasks such as summarising without these risks."

#### Court's Decision: No Referral

**Factors considered** [§19]:

**In favor of referral**:
1. Violation of duty to the court
2. Reckless use of AI without verification
3. Confidentiality violation (serious)
4. Need to maintain professional standards

**Against referral**:
1. **Self-reporting**: Mr Mohammed reported himself to IAA and SRA before the hearing
2. **Full admission**: He acknowledged all facts
3. **Remedy**: Concrete commitments to change (prohibition of AI at the firm, verification in BAILII)
4. **Insight**: He demonstrated understanding of the problem
5. **Single case**: No pattern of conduct (only 1 false citation detected)

**Reasoning** [§19]:
> "We make no referral to the IAA or SRA as Mr Mohammed has already done this and there is no need for us to do so."

**Important warning** [§19]:
> "If he had not done this we wish to make it clear that we would have made a referral."

**Application of Ayinde** [§19]:
The court confirms that the case meets Ayinde's criteria [§29] for referral, but self-reporting makes additional referral unnecessary.

#### Lessons from Case 1

**1. Google AI is as dangerous as ChatGPT**

First judgment documenting that Google AI (AI mode in search) also generates convincing jurisprudential hallucinations.

**Practical implication**: AI usage policies must cover ALL tools, not only ChatGPT.

**2. Open-source AI = Automatic confidentiality breach**

Using free ChatGPT with client documents:
- ✗ Violates confidentiality
- ✗ Waives privilege
- ✗ Data breach under UK GDPR
- ✗ Requires notification to ICO

**3. Self-reporting can avoid formal referral**

Important criterion: If the professional self-reports before the Hamid hearing, the court may opt not to make additional referral.

**But**: Only if there is genuine insight, full admission, and credible remedy commitments.

### B. CASE 2: City Law Practice - Zubair Rasheed (COLP)

#### Facts of the Case

**Context**:
- Judicial review on Graduate Route visa rejection
- Applicant: Fiza Munir (Pakistani national)
- Law firm: City Law Practice Solicitors and Advocates (CLP)
- COLP: Zubair Rasheed (solicitor, 15 years of experience)
- Author: Waheed Malik (initially described as "part-time trainee lawyer")

**Timeline**:
- September 9, 2024: Initial visa rejection
- March 26, 2025: Administrative Review rejection
- May 6, 2025: First letter before action (signed by Rasheed and Destiny Hayden)
- June 10, 2025: Second letter before action (signed only by Rasheed)
- June 26, 2025: JR filing in Upper Tribunal

**Documents filed**:
- UTIAC1 (claim form)—Rasheed's name on statement of truth
- Statement of Facts—signed by Rasheed
- Grounds for Judicial Review (20 pages, single-spaced)—**without individual signature**

#### The Four Detected Hallucinations

UTJ Blundell, reviewing the case on August 20, 2025, detected **four false references**:

**1. R (Dzineku-Liggison) v SSHD [2020] EWHC 3386** [§13 initial order]

**Falsehood**:
- NO High Court case exists with that citation
- **Reality**: R (Dzineku-Liggison) v SSHD [2020] UKUT 222 (IAC)
- It was an **Upper Tribunal** decision, not High Court
- Decided by UTJ Blundell (the same judge reviewing this case!)

**False proposition**:
The grounds claimed it established that the fee waiver policy was illegal for "overly restricting access to the application process"

**Reality**:
That was NOT the ratio decidendi of the real case

**2. Patel (mandatory refusal – fairness) [2011] EWCA Civ 811** [§14]

**Falsehood**:
- NO Court of Appeal decision exists with that citation
- Title suggests it could be an Upper Tribunal case with incorrect citation
- The court could find no case supporting the invoked proposition

**3. R (Muhandiramge) v SSHD [2010] EWHC 2568 (Admin)** [§15]

**Falsehood**:
- NO High Court decision exists with that name and citation
- **Reality**: Muhandiramge (section S-LTR 1.7) [2015] UKUT 675 (IAC)
- It was an **Upper Tribunal** decision (2015, not 2010)
- Decided by McCloskey J and UTJ Bruce

**False proposition**:
The grounds claimed it was about Tier 1 Post Study Work Scheme and requirement to obtain qualification by date of application

**Reality**:
The real case does NOT deal with that

**4. OE (Nigeria) [2010] UKUT 35 (IAC)** [§16]

**Falsehood**:
- **NO** Upper Tribunal case exists with that citation
- No reported case called "OE" in 2010
- The citation [2010] UKUT 35 (IAC) simply does not exist in the records

**Pattern detected** [§40]:
Two Upper Tribunal cases (Dzineku-Liggison, Muhandiramge) were given High Court citations.
One Upper Tribunal case (Patel) was given Court of Appeal citation.
One case simply does not exist (OE Nigeria).

#### First Response: The "Trainee's" Explanation

**August 27, 2025**: Mr Rasheed responds [§30]:

**Identification of author**:
- Grounds drafted by **Waheed Malik**
- Described as "part-time trainee lawyer"
- Working "under supervision"

**Offered explanation**:
> "The grounds were drafted by a part-time trainee, Mr Waheed Malik, under my supervision. In preparing them, he relied on an outdated precedent on our system, practitioner blogs and personal notes. Unfortunately, he did not verify these references against official sources."

**Alleged mitigating circumstances**:
> "At the time, my supervision was compromised as I was caring for my mother following her recent brain stroke."

**Position on Ayinde**:
Suggests the case is distinguishable from Ayinde and there was no intent to deceive.

**Attached documents**:
- Signed statements from Rasheed and Malik
- Medical evidence about Rasheed's mother
- "Corrected authorities schedule"

#### The Hamid Hearing: Critical Revelations

**November 5, 2025**: Second hearing (first was postponed due to family mourning and Rasheed's religious pilgrimage)

**Only Rasheed attended** (Malik did not appear)

##### Revelation 1: Waheed Malik Was Not a "Trainee"—He Was Rasheed's Brother

**Court's question**: Who is Waheed Malik?

**First description** [§42]: "Very junior caseworker," not trainee solicitor

**Direct question**: Are you related to Malik?

**Initially evasive response, then admission** [§43]:
- **Waheed Malik is Zubair Rasheed's brother**
- Has a law diploma from Wolverhampton University (15-20 years ago)
- Then went to Pakistan to practice law
- Obtained Certificate of Professional Standing from the SRA
- Supposedly practices at the Bar in Pakistan
- **Rasheed could not name his brother's chambers**

**Court's incredulity** [§43]:
> "We found that unlikely. Whether as his brother or as his former employer, we thought it more likely that Mr Rasheed would have known the name of the chambers from which his brother practises."

##### Revelation 2: Malik Is in the UK, Not in Pakistan

**Evasion about whereabouts** [§44]:
- Rasheed initially said he "believed" Malik was in the UK
- Pressed, confirmed Malik lives in Nottingham
- Has leave to enter as skilled worker
- Works in "legal compliance" for a care homes firm
- Entered the UK in late 2024
- Can do additional work under visa conditions

**Critical question**: Why did he not attend the hearing?

**Response** [§44]:
- He told him and his wife about the hearing
- But his son was sick

**Court's incredulity** [§44]:
> "If Mr Malik is a lawyer in Pakistan, and if he works in legal compliance in the healthcare sector, we considered it likely that he would have wished to attend this hearing, and we found it surprising that he had not."

##### Revelation 3: There Was No Real Supervision

**Timing problem** [§45]:
- Medical evidence about mother: hospitalized in **August** 2025
- Document says "cognitive impairment for the last **two weeks**" (from August)
- But the grounds were drafted and filed in **May-June 2025**

**Court's conclusion** [§45]:
> "There is no documentary evidence to support the suggestion that she had been unwell for much of 2025, as Mr Rasheed sought to suggest in his oral evidence."

**The sick mother excuse does not explain the lack of supervision in May-June.**

##### Revelation 4: Lack of Understanding About AI

**Question**: What measures does the firm have to prevent misuse of AI?

**Rasheed's response** [§46]:
> "He stated that there was no mechanism by which staff at his firm could use AI."

**Court's reaction** [§46]:
> "That is to overlook the fact that anyone with access to Google has access to AI, as we have explained above."

**Conclusion** [§46]:
> "His answer demonstrated to us that he had not, as the COLP of CLP, taken any steps to warn his staff of the pitfalls of using a free public search engine such as Google for legal research."

Rasheed described himself as "not a tech savvy person"

**Court's response** [§46]:
> "We do not consider that to be any explanation at all for his failure to put appropriate checks in place."

##### Revelation 5: Inadequate and Inconsistent Record-Keeping

**Context**: The court wanted to know who else had worked on the case.

**Alleged system** [§47]:
- Spreadsheet with names of fee-earners on each case
- When case is closed, removed from spreadsheet but remains in LEAP (case management) and paper archive

**Problem**: The Munir case **was not** on the spreadsheet [§48]

**Rasheed's explanation** [§49]:
Cases are removed from spreadsheet after closing:
- First version: 6 months after closing
- Second version: 4 months after closing
- Third version: 6 months after opening

**No explanation was coherent** [§50]:
- Permission rejected in August 2025
- Hearing in November 2025
- Why was it already removed?

**Rasheed's final response** [§51]:
Case was removed in **March 2025**

**Obvious problem** [§51]:
- Administrative Review rejected March 26, 2025
- Firm notified of decision in March
- They sent two letters before action (May, June)
- Filed JR in June
- **Why remove from spreadsheet in March when the case was still active?**

There was no coherent response.

##### Revelation 6: Does Not Know How Many Cases Malik Worked On

**Question**: On how many other cases did Malik work? [§52]

**Variable responses**:
- Perhaps two
- Maybe three
- Could be four

**Rasheed's admission** [§52]:
> "Nothing is for sure."

**Court's concern** [§52]:
> "Unfortunately, therefore, there appears to be every likelihood that Mr Malik will have worked on other unidentified files for the firm and may have relied on false citations which were generated by AI."

##### Revelation 7: The "Old Precedent" Mystery

**Explanation in witness statement**: The errors arose from using an "old precedent from previous cases" [§53]

**Court's concern**: If that corrupted precedent exists, other cases could be affected.

**Question**: Have you identified the precedent?

**Response**: Yes, deleted it.

**Question**: What type of document was it?

**Could not answer clearly** [§53]:
- Grounds for JR?
- Grounds for admin review?
- Something else?
- At one point suggested the incorrect citations were in "a piece of case-law" but could not identify it

**Question**: How many other affected cases?

**Evasive response** [§54]:
- "Not many"
- "One or two judicial review cases"
- Has been searching "day and night"

**Court's concern** [§54]:
> "We noted that this was not a process that he had described in any way in his witness statement and we were concerned that he was attempting to tailor his answers to minimise our concerns."

#### Legal Subsumption: Rasheed's Violations

**As COLP (Compliance Officer for Legal Practice)**:

**SRA Code for Solicitors, Rule 8.1**:
- COLP responsible for compliance with SRA Standards at the firm
- **Violation**: Did not implement control systems on AI use
- **Violation**: Did not train staff on AI risks
- **Violation**: Did not verify junior fee-earners' work

**As Solicitor with Conduct of the Matter**:

**Principle 1** (public confidence):
- **Serious violation**: 4 false citations presented to the court
- Court spent time verifying nonexistent citations

**Principle 2** (integrity):
- **Violation**: Evasive and inconsistent responses at hearing
- **Violation**: Attempted to minimize gravity ("distinguishable from Ayinde")

**Principle 5** (competence):
- **Violation**: Delegated drafting of grounds to incompetent person
- **Violation**: Did not supervise the work
- **Violation**: "Not tech savvy" is not an excuse for digital incompetence

**Rule 1.4** (not deceiving the court):
- **Technical violation**: Presented 4 false references
- Although there was no intent to deceive, there was serious recklessness

**As Supervisor**:

**Duty to supervise** (common law + SRA Standards):
- **Violation**: Did not effectively supervise Malik
- **Violation**: Allowed "very junior caseworker" to draft complex grounds
- **Violation**: Did not verify citations before filing

#### Credibility Analysis

The court found multiple problems with Rasheed's credibility:

**1. Contradictions on record-keeping** [§49-51]
**2. Evasion about relationship with Malik** [§43]
**3. Inability to explain why Malik did not attend** [§44]
**4. Problematic timing of sick mother excuse** [§45]
**5. Lack of understanding about AI** [§46]
**6. Inconsistencies about the "old precedent"** [§53-54]

**Implicit conclusion**: Rasheed was not entirely honest with the court.

#### Court's Decision: Referral to SRA

**Factors considered** [§55-56]:

**Mitigating factors**:
1. Solicitor with 15 years of unblemished record
2. Genuine regret for what occurred

**Aggravating factors**:
1. **Four false citations** (vs. one in Case 1)
2. **Lack of effective supervision**
3. **Inadequate record-keeping**
4. **Lack of control systems**
5. **Evasive and inconsistent responses**
6. **Multiple cases potentially affected**
7. **Attempt to minimize gravity**
8. **Unconvincing excuses**

**Application of Ayinde principle** [§29]:
> "Where false citations are placed before the Court because of the lack of proper checks, referral to a regulator is likely to be appropriate."

**Special consideration of supervisor role** [§38]:
> "A supervisor who fails to ensure that the work of a more junior fee-earner does not contain false cases or citations is **likely to be more culpable** than a lawyer who fails to ensure that his own work is free from such 'hallucinations'."

**Reasoning** [§38]:
A supervisor who fails:
- Fails the court
- Fails the public
- Fails the client
- **Additionally, fails in the development of junior lawyers**

**Conclusion** [§56]:
> "We consider that the inclusion of false citations in the grounds for judicial review and Mr Rasheed's failure to supervise the work undertaken by his brother in this and an unknown number of other cases **necessitates a referral to the SRA**."

**Rejection of medical excuse** [§56]:
> "We do not consider their mother's illness to provide an adequate explanation for those failures."

**Credibility impact** [§56]:
> "We consider that our concerns about Mr Rasheed's oral evidence serve to strengthen the case for referral."

<a name="conclusions"></a>
## V. CONCLUSIONS: ESTABLISHED DOCTRINE AND OPERATING PRINCIPLES

### A. Answers to the Presented Issues

#### Principal Issue: Supervision Standards

**CONCLUSION**: A supervisor (partner, COLP, senior solicitor) has **aggravated responsibility** when a junior fee-earner under their supervision presents documents with AI hallucinations.

**Bases**:

1. **Positive duty to establish control systems** [§46]
   - It is not sufficient to trust that junior "will do their job well"
   - Mandatory verification protocols must exist
   - Training on AI risks must be provided

2. **Duty to warn about AI risks** [§2, §46]
   - Supervisors must ensure fee-earners know the dangers of non-specialist AI
   - Clear policies on which tools to use must exist

3. **Duty to verify work before filing** [§37, §45]
   - Supervisor must personally review critical documents (grounds, skeleton arguments)
   - Being busy or distracted is not an excuse
   - Work pressure does NOT justify failures

4. **Greater culpability of supervisor** [§38]
   > "A supervisor who fails to ensure that the work of a more junior fee-earner does not contain false cases or citations is **likely to be more culpable** than a lawyer who fails to ensure that his own work is free from such 'hallucinations'."

**Reason**: The supervisor fails in multiple dimensions (court, public, client, professional development of juniors)

#### Issue 1.1: Google AI Also Generates Hallucinations

**CONCLUSION**: Yes, Google AI (AI Mode in Google Search) is equally capable of generating jurisprudential hallucinations as ChatGPT or other LLMs.

**Evidence** [§17]:
- Court experimented with Google AI
- Generated plausible but false bench compositions
- Described nonexistent ratio decidendi
- Varied responses according to question formulation

**Practical implication**:
> "The danger in using Artificial Intelligence for legal research is not confined to generative AI models such as ChatGPT, therefore; the use of Google AI for legal research is equally likely to generate results which are false but which might initially be thought to be accurate."

**Warning**: Using Google search with AI Mode activated without verification = same risk as using ChatGPT

#### Issue 1.2: Self-Reporting Can Avoid Referral

**CONCLUSION**: Timely self-reporting to regulatory bodies CAN (but not always) make additional court referral unnecessary.

**Conditions** [§19, Case 1 analysis]:

**Necessary**:
1. Self-reporting BEFORE the Hamid hearing
2. Full admission of facts
3. Genuine insight (real understanding of the problem)
4. Concrete and credible remedy commitments
5. Evidence that there is no pattern of conduct

**Not sufficient if**:
- There are multiple violations
- Lack of credibility
- Attempts to minimize gravity
- Affects multiple cases

**Contrast**: Mr Mohammed: self-reporting + full admission + insight = No additional referral
Mr Rasheed: no self-reporting + evasive responses + multiple violations = Referral necessary

#### Issue 1.3: ChatGPT Open-Source = Confidentiality Breach

**CONCLUSION**: Uploading client documents to ChatGPT (free version or other open-source AI tools) constitutes **per se** violation of confidentiality and waiver of professional privilege.

**Bases** [§21]:

**1. Client confidentiality**:
- ChatGPT free stores conversations
- Uses data for model training
- = Information becomes "public domain"
- = **Violation of duty of confidentiality**

**2. Professional privilege**:
- Legal professional privilege protects lawyer-client communications
- Uploading to public platform = voluntary disclosure
- = **Waiver of legal privilege** (irrevocable)

**3. Data protection**:
- Contains clients' personal data
- Uploading without consent and without security measures
- = **Data breach** under UK GDPR
- = **Must be reported to ICO**

**4. Possible additional consequences** [§4, §60]:
> "Uploading confidential documents into an open-source AI tool, such as ChatGPT, is to place this information on the internet in the public domain, and thus to breach client confidentiality and waive legal privilege, and any such conduct **might itself warrant referral to the SRA** and should, in any event, be referred to the Information Commissioner's Office."

**Safe alternative** [§21]:
> "Closed source AI tools which do not place information in the public domain, such as **Microsoft Copilot**, are available for tasks such as summarising without these risks."

**Key distinction**:
- **Open-source AI** (ChatGPT free, Google Gemini free, etc.): Data is stored, used for training → **DO NOT USE WITH CLIENT DATA**
- **Closed-source/Enterprise AI** (Microsoft Copilot for Business, ChatGPT Enterprise, Claude for Enterprise): Data is not used for training → **Potentially acceptable with precautions**

#### Issue 2.1: Mandatory Record-Keeping

**CONCLUSION**: A COLP must maintain accurate and accessible records of who works on each case for traceability and accountability purposes.

**Bases** [§47-52]:

**Why it is essential**:
1. Allows identifying author of errors
2. Facilitates review of other potentially affected cases
3. Demonstrates adequate control systems
4. Protects both the firm and individuals

**Minimum required system**:
- Contemporary record of fee-earners on each matter
- Identification of who drafted which documents
- Supervision exercised and by whom
- NOT arbitrarily deleting records
- Retain while case is "live" + reasonable period after

**Problem at CLP** [§48-51]:
- Spreadsheet with inconsistent records
- Munir case inexplicably removed
- Contradictory explanations (6 months vs. 4 months vs. removal in March when case was still active)
- Inability to determine what other cases Malik worked on

**Court's conclusion** [§52]:
Record-keeping system so deficient that it is impossible to know what other cases may be affected by the same AI hallucinations.

#### Issue 2.2: Limits of Delegation to Juniors

**CONCLUSION**: It is NOT appropriate for a "very junior caseworker" or "trainee" to draft grounds for judicial review without effective supervision.

**Bases**:

**Complexity of the task**:
- Grounds for JR require:
  - Deep knowledge of Administrative Law
  - Ability to identify errors of law
  - Skill to cite jurisprudence correctly
  - Understanding of judicial review standards

**Malik's experience level** [§42-43]:
- Initially described as "part-time trainee"
- Actually was "very junior caseworker"
- Law diploma from 15-20 years ago
- No recent training in UK law
- Worked at the firm only 3-4 months

**Conclusion** [§42]:
> "We cannot understand why it was thought by Mr Rasheed to be appropriate for Waheed Malik to draft grounds for judicial review."

**Applicable principle**:
Delegation must be proportional to the fee-earner's competence and experience level. Complex and critical tasks (grounds, skeleton arguments, written submissions) must be:
- Performed by experienced lawyers, OR
- Performed by juniors under direct and detailed supervision

**In this case**: Malik had neither the experience nor the necessary supervision.

#### Issue 2.3: Personal Circumstances as Mitigating Factor

**CONCLUSION**: Difficult personal circumstances (family illness) may be considered as mitigating, but do NOT completely excuse supervision failures, especially if:
1. Timing does not match the facts
2. No contemporary documentary evidence exists
3. The professional has other resources (partners, associates) to cover

**Analysis of Rasheed's case** [§45, §56]:

**Allegation**: Sick mother in Pakistan caused distraction

**Problems**:
1. **Timing**: Medical evidence is from August; grounds filed in May-June
2. **Duration**: Medical evidence mentions "two weeks"; Rasheed suggested "much of 2023"
3. **Resources**: As COLP of the firm, he had resources to delegate or seek help

**Court's conclusion** [§56]:
> "We do not consider their mother's illness to provide an adequate explanation for those failures."

**Important distinction**:
- Personal circumstances may explain why someone delegated more than usual
- But do NOT excuse the obligation to verify critical work before filing
- Especially when it involves documents going to the court

**Comparison with DVP [2021]**:
Work pressure is NOT an excuse for systematic supervision failures.

#### Issue 3: Procedural Reforms

**CONCLUSION**: YES, it is appropriate and necessary for courts to modify forms to include specific statement of truth regarding verification of authorities.

**Measure adopted** [§9]:

**Claim form (UTIAC1) now modified to include**:
> "A legal representative [must] confirm by a statement of truth that any authority cited within the form or in any documents appended to it:
> (a) exists;
> (b) may be located using the citation provided; and
> (c) supports the proposition of law for which it is cited."

**Extension**:
> "Other forms and directions are to be similarly amended."

**Consequence of false statement** [§3, §59]:
> "A legal representative who signs such a statement in a case in which false authorities are cited should **ordinarily expect to be referred to their regulatory body**."

**Justification** [§6-7]:

1. **Procedural economy**: Prevents judges from wasting time verifying false citations
2. **Public interest**: Maintains confidence in the justice system
3. **Protection of vulnerable clients**: Prevents unnecessary costs
4. **Preventive function**: Requires ex ante verification

**Precedent**:
After Ayinde (June 2025) the problem increased in the second half of 2025 [§8].

**Analogy**:
Similar to statement of truth in Statement of Case (CPR 22.1) but specifically directed at AI hallucination problem.

**Expected impact**:
- Will compel lawyers to verify BEFORE filing
- Creates paper trail of responsibility
- Facilitates referrals when there are falsities
- Likely to be adopted by other courts

### B. The Four Fundamental Principles Established

#### Principle 1: Universal Duty of Verification

**Statement** [§57]:
> "Legal professionals are obliged to ensure that legal arguments which are presented to the First-tier Tribunal or Upper Tribunal are factually and legally accurate."

**Scope**:
- Applies to ALL jurisprudential references
- Independent of source (database, AI, precedent file, memory)
- Independent of professional's experience level
- Independent of time pressure or workload

**Consequence of violation** [§57]:
> "Those who cite false cases fail to comply with that professional obligation and **waste the time of the Tribunal**."

#### Principle 2: Aggravated Supervisor Responsibility

**Statement** [§2, §58]:
> "A solicitor or other legal professional who delegates their work to another fee-earner **remains responsible** for the supervision of their work and for ensuring its accuracy."

**Specific supervisor obligations** [§2, §58]:

1. **Warn about AI risks**:
   > "Supervisors must ensure that fee-earners under their supervision are aware of the dangers of using non-specialist Artificial Intelligence (AI) for legal research and drafting."

2. **Perform appropriate checks**:
   > "Failures to do so, or to undertake appropriate checks on the drafting of fee-earners is likely to result in a referral to the Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA) or other professional body."

3. **Greater culpability**:
   > "A supervisor who fails to ensure that the work of a more junior fee-earner does not contain false cases or citations is **likely to be more culpable** than a lawyer who fails to ensure that his own work is free from such 'hallucinations'."

**Reason for greater culpability** [§38]:
The supervisor fails in:
- Their duty to the court
- Their duty to the public
- Their duty to the client
- **Additionally** their duty to properly develop juniors

#### Principle 3: Confidentiality and Open-Source AI

**Statement** [§4, §60]:
> "Uploading confidential documents into an open-source AI tool, such as ChatGPT, is to place this information on the internet in the public domain, and thus to **breach client confidentiality and waive legal privilege**."

**Consequences**:

1. **Deontological violation**: Breach of duty of confidentiality
2. **Loss of privilege**: Waiver of legal professional privilege (irrevocable)
3. **Data breach**: Violation of UK GDPR → must be reported to ICO
4. **Possible additional referral**: May justify SRA referral on its own

**Required action** [§4, §60]:
> "Any such conduct **might itself warrant referral** to the SRA and should, in any event, be referred to the Information Commissioner's Office."

#### Principle 4: Preventive Procedural Reforms

**Statement** [§3, §59]:
Courts can and should adopt preventive procedural measures to address the AI hallucination problem.

**Specific measure adopted**:
Statement of truth in claim forms and other documents requiring the legal representative to confirm that all cited authorities:
- (a) Exist
- (b) Can be located with the provided citation
- (c) Support the legal proposition for which it is cited

**Consequence of false statement** [§3, §59]:
> "A legal representative who signs such a statement in a case in which false authorities are cited should **ordinarily expect to be referred** to their regulatory body."

### C. Guidance for Regulatory Bodies

#### For IAA (Immigration Advice Authority)

**Court's recommendation** [§20]:
> "We are of the view that the guidance on false AI generated material given in Ayinde applies equally to IAA (previously the OISC) regulated advisers."

**Basis**:

**IAA Code of Standards, Principle 1**:
> "Act in a way that upholds the Rule of Law and proper administration of justice"

**Standard 1.2**:
> "Advisers must not knowingly or recklessly allow the courts and tribunals to be misled"

**Court's suggestion** [§20]:
> "If it is the case that the IAA has not provided specific advice with regards to the use of AI by its accredited advisers then the organisation **may wish to consider whether it may be prudent to do so**."

**Implication**:
The court is urging the IAA to issue specific guidance on AI use, similar to the Law Society's for solicitors.

#### For SRA and Other Regulators

**Clear expectation** [§2, §58]:

**Triggers for disciplinary investigation**:
1. Citing false cases (especially multiple)
2. Failing to supervise fee-earners' work
3. Not warning staff about AI risks
4. Not implementing appropriate checks
5. Uploading confidential documents to open-source AI

**Culpability standard**:
- Intentional → More serious
- Reckless → Serious
- Negligent → Equally sanctionable

**Aggravating circumstances**:
- Multiple false citations
- Supervisor role
- Lack of insight
- Evasive responses
- Affects multiple cases
- Attempts to minimize

**Mitigating circumstances**:
- Timely self-reporting
- Full admission
- Genuine insight
- Remedy commitments
- Prior unblemished record
- Single/isolated case

### B. Doctrinal Evolution: From Ayinde to UKUT 00081

#### What UKUT 00081 Adds to Ayinde Doctrine

**1. Supervisor Responsibility (NOVELTY)**

**Ayinde**: Did not specifically address supervision because the case involved counsel acting in their own name.

**UKUT 00081**: Clearly establishes that:
- Supervisor has aggravated responsibility [§38]
- Must warn about AI risks [§2, §58]
- Must implement checks [§2, §58]
- Is more culpable than the direct author [§38]

**2. Google AI as Source of Hallucinations (NOVELTY)**

**Ayinde**: Only mentions ChatGPT as example of generative AI.

**UKUT 00081**:
- First court to warn about Google AI [§17]
- Conducted empirical experiment with Google AI [§17]
- Confirms it generates equally convincing hallucinations

**3. Confidentiality and Privilege (NOVELTY)**

**Ayinde**: Does not address AI use with confidential documents.

**UKUT 00081**:
- Declares that uploading to open-source ChatGPT = breach [§21, §60]
- Establishes it is waiver of privilege [§21, §60]
- Requires reporting to ICO [§21, §60]
- Suggests alternative (Microsoft Copilot) [§21]

**4. Procedural Reforms (NOVELTY)**

**Ayinde**: Only recommends judicial vigilance.

**UKUT 00081**:
- Implements immediate changes to forms [§9]
- Specific statement of truth on citations [§9, §59]
- Expectation of automatic referral if false statement [§3, §59]

**5. Self-Reporting as Mitigating Factor (DEVELOPMENT)**

**Ayinde**: Mentions self-reporting as mitigating factor [§24].

**UKUT 00081**:
- Develops criterion: self-reporting + insight + remedy = can avoid referral [§19]
- Contrasts two cases: Mohammed (no referral) vs. Rasheed (referral) to illustrate

**6. Record-Keeping and Traceability (NOVELTY)**

**Ayinde**: Does not address firms' internal systems.

**UKUT 00081**:
- Establishes need for records on who works on what [§47-52]
- Inadequate record-keeping is aggravating factor [§52]
- Prevents identifying other affected cases [§52]

### C. Position of TA Orléans in International Context

#### Historical Importance

**First continental European judgment** that:
1. Explicitly uses explicit AI technical terminology
2. Warns about AI hallucinations
3. Detects significant number of false citations (15)

#### Differences from Common Law Tradition

**French system (civil law)**:
- Less emphasis on binding precedent
- Jurisprudence less cited in decisions
- But equally requires precision in citations when used

**Context of Orléans case**:
- Main case was on immigration law (expulsion)
- False references were **secondary** to the principal issue
- Court addressed them in separate section "Sur les décisions juridictionnelles citées"

**Contrast with UKUT**:
- In UKUT, AI hallucinations were the **central issue** (Hamid proceedings)
- Much more extensive doctrinal development
- But Orléans has value as first continental European voice

#### Emerging European Convergence

**Observable trend**:

| Date | Event | Jurisdiction |
|------|-------|--------------|
| Jun 2025 | Ayinde | UK (High Court) |
| Nov 2025 | UKUT 00081 | UK (Upper Tribunal) |
| Dec 2025 | TA Orléans | France |
| 2026+ | **Expected cases** | Germany, Spain, Italy, etc. |

**Emerging common principles**:
1. Duty of citation verification
2. Warning about AI hallucinations
3. Professional responsibility for AI outputs
4. Pedagogical function of courts

**Differences in enforcement**:
- **UK**: Hamid jurisdiction allows immediate referral to SRA
- **France**: Separate disciplinary system (Ordre des avocats)
- **Need**: Coordination at EU level on these matters

## VII. IMPLICATIONS FOR GLOBAL LEGAL PRACTICE

### A. For the United Kingdom and Ireland

#### Immediate Impact (2026)

**1. Modification of All Court Forms**

Upper Tribunal has already modified:
- UTIAC1 (judicial review claim form)
- Soon: "Other forms and directions" [§9]

**Expectation**: Other courts will adopt similar changes:
- First-tier Tribunal (all chambers)
- Employment Tribunal
- Tax Tribunal
- Probably County Courts and Crown Courts

**2. Increase in Referrals to SRA**

Post-UKUT 00081, judges will feel obligated to:
- Scrutinize citations more carefully
- Issue show cause notices more frequently
- Refer cases where there are multiple fake citations

**SRA preparation**:
- Will need to increase resources to investigate AI cases
- Develop specific expertise in hallucinations
- Establish precedents on appropriate sanctions

**3. Crisis in Small Firms**

**Problem**: Small firms that:
- Use AI to compete with big firms
- Do not have resources for enterprise AI tools
- Do not have AI usage policies
- Deficient supervision due to work pressure

**Risk**: Wave of similar CLP cases in next 6-12 months

**Required response**: Law Society must provide:
- Specific practical guides for small firms
- AI policy templates
- Affordable or free training

#### Structural Practice Changes (2026-2027)

**1. Mandatory AI Usage Policies**

**Minimum content**:
- Which tools are authorized (whitelist)
- For what tasks they can be used
- Level of supervision required according to criticality
- Mandatory verification protocol
- Confidentiality warnings

**2. Mandatory AI Training**

**For supervisors** (partners, COLPs, senior solicitors):
- How LLMs work
- What hallucinations are
- How to detect them
- Control systems to implement

**For all fee-earners**:
- Risks of non-specialist AI
- Duty of verification
- How to verify citations (BAILII, case law databases)
- Data protection when using AI

**3. Verification Protocols**

**Recommended model**:

**For any document citing jurisprudence**:
1. Author verifies each citation in primary source (BAILII, WLR, etc.)
2. Completes verification checklist (attached to file)
3. Supervisor (if applicable) spot-checks minimum 20% of citations
4. Document management system marks as "verified"
5. Record kept for audit trail

**For grounds, skeleton arguments, opinions**:
- 100% of citations verified by author
- 100% of citations spot-checked by supervisor
- Certificate of verification attached

- Isolated cases in federal courts

- Each circuit develops its own jurisprudence

**American Bar Association** (ABA):
- Issued "Model Rule on Use of Artificial Intelligence" (2024)
- But it is not binding (each State Bar decides)

#### Lessons from UKUT 00081 for the U.S.

**1. Supervisor Responsibility**

**Currently in the U.S.**:
- Partners are responsible for associates under Rule 5.1 Model Rules
- But no specific guidance on AI

**UKUT contribution**:
The principle that supervisor is **more culpable** than junior could be adopted.

**2. Statement of Truth in Court Filings**

**Currently**:
- Rule 11 FRCP already requires signature = certification of well-foundedness
- But does not require specific verification of case citations

**Possible reform**:
- Amendments to Rule 11 to require specific certification of citations
- Similar to what UKUT did

**3. Confidentiality and Open-Source AI**

**The U.S. is ahead** on this aspect:
- ABA Model Rule 1.6 (confidentiality) already interpreted as prohibiting use of unsecured AI
- Several State Bars have issued ethics opinions on this

**But**: UKUT 00081 adds clarity on waiver of privilege

## VIII. DETAILED OPERATIONAL RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. For Individual Lawyers

#### Level 1: Basic (Mandatory for All)

**NEVER do**:
- ❌ Copy/paste case citations from ChatGPT/Google AI without verifying
- ❌ Assume citation "looks correct" is sufficient
- ❌ Upload client documents to ChatGPT free or other open-source AI
- ❌ Use AI to draft critical sections (grounds, skeleton) without line-by-line verification
- ❌ Delegate to AI the selection of applicable jurisprudence

**ALWAYS do**:
- ✅ Verify EVERY citation in official primary source before citing
- ✅ Use BAILII (UK), Légifrance (FR), CENDOJ (ES), EUR-Lex (EU)
- ✅ Keep record of verification (screenshots, URLs, dates)
- ✅ Use closed-source/enterprise AI if need to process confidential docs
- ✅ Continuously train on new AI risks

**Minimum verification process**:

**For each case cited**:
1. Search in official database (BAILII, etc.)
2. Confirm:
   - ☑ Exact case name
   - ☑ Correct citation
   - ☑ Correct date
   - ☑ Correct court
   - ☑ Correct judges
3. Read the case (at least relevant parts)
4. Confirm it supports the invoked proposition
5. Save evidence of verification

**Time estimate**: 2-5 minutes per case

**Worth it**: Totally—vs. risk of referral to SRA

#### Level 2: Advanced (Recommended for Litigators)

**Professional tools**:

**For research**:
- Westlaw Edge AI (with automatic verification)
- LexisNexis+ AI
- vLex Vincent AI
- CaseText CoCounsel

**Advantages**: 
- They only cite cases they can link
- Lower risk of hallucinations
- But: Still verify spot-check 10-20%

**For document review**:
- Microsoft Copilot for Business (closed-source)
- Harvey AI Enterprise
- Robin AI

**For drafting**:
- Use AI for first draft
- NEVER for final version without exhaustive human review

**For verification**:
- Tools that scan doc and automatically verify citations
- Some in development by legal tech startups

#### Level 3: Expert (For Early Adopters)

**Advanced prompting**:

**When using AI for research, prompt like this**:

❌ BAD:
> "What cases support the proposition that..."

✅ GOOD:
> "I need cases on [topic]. Please suggest search terms I should use in [Westlaw/BAILII/etc.]. Do NOT invent case names or citations. If you're not certain a case exists, say so."

✅ EVEN BETTER:
> "I'm researching [topic]. I will search [database] myself. Help me formulate effective boolean search strings."

**Use AI as research assistant, not as lawyer**

**Chain of custody for AI work**:
- Document which tool you used
- Which prompts you gave
- Which outputs you received
- How you verified
- Contemporary file notes

**Benefit**: If there is disciplinary investigation, you can demonstrate due diligence

### B. For Supervisors (Partners, COLPs, Senior Solicitors)

#### Specific Supervisor Obligations

**1. Create and Implement AI Policy** (urgent if it doesn't exist!)

**Minimum content**:

```markdown
# [Firm Name] - Artificial Intelligence Use Policy

## 1. Approved Tools

### Prohibited (Open-Source AI)
- ❌ ChatGPT free
- ❌ Google Gemini free
- ❌ Claude free
- ❌ Any other free public LLM

### Approved (Closed-Source/Enterprise Only)
- ✅ Microsoft Copilot for Business
- ✅ [List specific subscriptions firm has]

### Conditional Approval (With Safeguards)
- ⚠️ Google AI in search: Only for general info, never for case citations
- ⚠️ ChatGPT Plus/Pro: Only for non-confidential brainstorming

## 2. Permitted Uses

### Allowed
- ✅ Generating search terms for manual research
- ✅ Summarizing non-confidential public documents
- ✅ Drafting routine correspondence (after review)
- ✅ Brainstorming legal arguments (not citing cases)

### Prohibited
- ❌ Uploading client documents to open-source AI
- ❌ Copying case citations without verification
- ❌ Generating final versions of court documents
- ❌ Any use involving privileged communications

## 3. Verification Requirements

### For All Citations
- Every case must be verified in primary source
- Checklist must be completed (Appendix A)
- Supervisor must spot-check minimum 20%

### For Court Documents
- 100% verification by author
- 100% supervisor review
- Certificate of verification attached

## 4. Training

- All staff: Annual training on AI risks
- New joiners: Training within first month
- Supervisors: Enhanced training on supervision duties

## 5. Consequences of Breach

- First breach: Warning + mandatory retraining
- Second breach: Formal disciplinary process
- Serious breach (e.g., confidentiality): Potential referral to SRA

## 6. Reporting

- Any suspected AI hallucination: Report to COLP immediately
- Any data breach: Report to COLP + ICO within 24 hours

---
Approved by: [COLP name]
Date: [Date]
Review date: [Annual review]
```

**2. Implement Control Systems**

**For any document citing cases**:


- Tag docs as "AI-assisted" if AI used
- Tag as "verified" only after checks complete
- Prevent filing until verification confirmed

**3. Mandatory Training**

**For all staff (annual)**:
- What are LLMs and how they work (1 hour)
- What are hallucinations (30 min)
- Firm policy on AI (1 hour)
- Verification procedures (1 hour)
- Case studies: Mata, Ayinde, UKUT 00081 (1 hour)
- Total: 4.5 hours/year

**For supervisors (additional)**:
- Supervision duties under UKUT 00081 (2 hours)
- How to spot-check work (1 hour)
- What to do if hallucination detected (1 hour)
- Total additional: 4 hours/year

**Delivery**: Mix of e-learning, workshops, guest speakers

**4. Regular Audits**

**Monthly**: 
- Review random sample of court documents filed
- Check verification checklists completed
- Identify any concerns

**Quarterly**:
- Review AI use logs (if using enterprise tools)
- Review any incidents reported
- Update policy if needed

**Annually**:
- Full compliance audit
- Staff survey on AI use
- Policy review and update

**5. Clear Allocation of Work**

**Task vs. Experience level**:

| Task | Min. Experience | Supervision Required |
|------|----------------|---------------------|
| Routine correspondence | Trainee/Paralegal | Spot-check |
| Complex letters | Junior Solicitor | Review before sending |
| **Grounds for JR** | **Min. 3 years PQE** | **Senior review before filing** |
| Skeleton arguments | Min. 3-5 years PQE | Senior/Partner review |
| Opinions | Min. 5 years PQE | Senior partner review |

**Never allow**:
- Trainee to draft grounds alone (even under "supervision")
- "Very junior caseworker" to do complex litigation work
- Anyone to file without supervisor review if AI used

#### What to Do If You Detect Hallucination

**Immediate steps**:

1. **Stop**: Do not file document
2. **Identify**: Determine all cases in document
3. **Verify**: Check every single one
4. **Replace**: Substitute false cases with real ones (if they exist)
5. **Investigate**: How did this happen?

**Investigation**:
- Who worked on document?
- What AI tools did they use?
- Have they worked on other files?
- Are other files affected?

**Reporting**:
- If already filed: Report to court immediately
- If involves confidentiality breach: Report to ICO within 72 hours
- If serious: Consider self-reporting to SRA

**Prevention going forward**:
- Retrain staff member(s) involved
- Review and tighten procedures
- Increase supervision level
- Consider whether additional referrals needed

#### Managing Difficult Situations

**Scenario 1: Junior fee-earner admits using ChatGPT for cites**

**Response**:
- Don't panic
- Verify all citations in all their recent files
- Retrain immediately
- Formal warning
- Increase supervision
- Document everything

**Don't**: Fire immediately without investigation

**Scenario 2: You discover multiple files affected**

**Response**:
- Create list of all affected files
- Triage by urgency (filed vs. not filed)
- For filed docs: Assess whether to inform court
- For pending docs: Correct before filing
- Contact clients to explain (duty of candour)
- Consider self-reporting to SRA

**Scenario 3: Client documents were uploaded to ChatGPT**

**Response**:
- **Immediate**: Stop any further uploads
- Notify COLP immediately
- Assess severity:
  - What data was in docs?
  - Is it special category data (GDPR)?
  - Is it privileged?
- Report to ICO within 72 hours if breach
- Notify affected clients
- Consider whether privilege waived
- Self-report to SRA
- Review insurance coverage

## IX. FINAL CONCLUSIONS

### A. Synthesis of Consolidated Doctrine

This judgment, together with Ayinde and TA Orléans, consolidates an international doctrine on AI in legal practice. The principles are:

**1. Verification is absolute and unconditional**
**2. Supervisors have aggravated responsibility**
**3. Open-source AI with client data = per se violation**
**4. Procedural reforms are necessary and appropriate**

### B. The Future: Next 5 Years (2026-2031)

**2026-2027: Consolidation**
- More similar cases in all jurisdictions
- Issuance of guidance by all regulators
- First public disciplinary sanctions
- Development of verification tools

**2027-2029: Maturation**
- Legal AI enterprise becomes standard
- Reduced reliance on free/open-source AI
- AI training becomes mandatory part of CPD
- Jurisprudence establishes consistent sanctions

**2029-2031: New Normal**
- Generation of "AI-native" but risk-aware lawyers
- AI tools with built-in verification
- Fewer hallucination cases (but never zero)
- Focus shift toward other AI risks (bias, transparency, etc.)

### C. Final Message for the Profession

**From UK and R (Munir) v SSHD [2026] UKUT 00081 (IAC)**:

> "Legal professionals are obliged to ensure that legal arguments which are presented to the First-tier Tribunal or Upper Tribunal are factually and legally accurate. Those who cite false cases fail to comply with that professional obligation and waste the time of the Tribunal." [§57]

Artificial intelligence is an extraordinary tool that is transforming the practice of law. It can make us more efficient, more accessible, more competitive.

**But only if we use it with:**
- ✅ **Competence** (we understand how it works and its limitations)
- ✅ **Diligence** (we verify everything before using)
- ✅ **Integrity** (we do not try shortcuts that compromise truth)
- ✅ **Responsibility** (we accept the consequences of our decisions)

Lawyers who fail in these duties do so under their own professional risk—and that of their clients.
