The document analyzes the integration of artificial intelligence in the administration of justice, highlighting its potential for improving operational efficiency against the serious risks it poses to fundamental rights, transparency, and due process. It examines the tensions between automation and constitutional guarantees, with emphasis on the need for ethical controls and independent audits. The author concludes that AI must remain as an auxiliary instrument subordinated to human judgment.

### Context and Scope of the Document

The text addresses the profound transformation posed by the incorporation of artificial intelligence systems—particularly those based on machine learning and deep learning—into the functioning of courts and legal practice. It is presented as a paradigm shift affecting the very nature of evidence, judicial decision-making, and the pillars of the Rule of Law. The analysis is primarily based on European standards, particularly those developed by the European Commission for the Efficiency of Justice (CEPEJ) and the Council of the Bars and Law Societies of Europe (CCBE), and evaluates both opportunities for procedural rationalization and structural threats to democratic guarantees.

### Legal Foundations

The central premise lies in that human rights and fundamental freedoms constitute an unpassable limit to technological deployment. Essential principles of due process are invoked, such as the right to confrontation, the presumption of innocence, equality before the law, and the prohibition of discrimination. The CEPEJ Ethical Charter establishes two core requirements: the prohibition of delegating decision-making authority to automated systems and the requirement for transparency and explainability of algorithmic reasoning. These principles apply with particular strictness in the criminal domain, where personal liberty is at stake.

### Legal and Regulatory Keys

The application of AI differs according to the procedural phase: it is acceptable and recommended for management tasks (case classification, deadline control, electronic filing), automatic transcription, support in case law research, or assistance in drafting resolutions. However, its use as a substitute for judgment in the deliberative and decision-making phase is prohibited. In criminal justice, the risks posed by recidivism prediction tools (e.g., COMPAS, HART) stand out, whose opacity and biases may violate the presumption of innocence and create cycles of over-surveillance in vulnerable groups. The Loomis v. Wisconsin case illustrates the collision between trade secret and the right to defense. Independent audit is postulated as an essential mechanism to verify the absence of biases in training data, reasoning traceability, and effective human intervention.

### Risks and Obligations in Practice

Among the main risks are algorithmic opacity ("black box"), the reproduction and amplification of historical inequalities, the imbalance of arms between prosecution and defense in big data environments, and the creation of accountability gaps for autonomous decisions not explicitly programmed. Solutions are proposed such as strict liability for high-risk systems, the distinction between frontend and backend operators, the reversal of the burden of proof when traceability is lacking, and mandatory detailed logging design (logging by design). In legal practice, updated technological competence is required, reinforced confidentiality protection (GDPR and professional secrecy), and attention to the gap between data-rich languages and small jurisdictions.

### Critical Assessment and Conclusions

Artificial intelligence constitutes an extraordinary opportunity to optimize the administration of justice, but its introduction cannot be subordinated to merely economic or budgetary criteria. Efficiency must reinforce—and never erode—the Rule of Law. The text advocates strictly monitored integration, in which technology remains always as auxiliary support and never as replacement for human agency, empathy, and the value context inherent to legal reasoning. Judges, attorneys, and academics must actively participate in the design, audit, and supervision of these tools to preserve the human character of justice and ensure that due process continues to be an inviolable fundamental right.
