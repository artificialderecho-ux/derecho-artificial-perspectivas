# T-323 Judgment of 2024: Judicial Accountability in the AI Era

## I. Introduction: An Inflection Point in Judicial Jurisprudence on AI

On **October 23, 2024**, the **Constitutional Court of Colombia** issued **Judgment T-323** after reviewing a case in which a **circuit labor judge used ChatGPT 3.5 to "extend the arguments" of a decision that affected fundamental rights of a person diagnosed with autism (ASD)**.

This case is fundamental because it represents **the first in-depth constitutional pronouncement by a Latin American court on AI in justice**. Unlike the French Charte (which is an administrative document of principles) and the European Commission guide (which is regulatory soft law), Colombia issues this from a **supreme judicial power with binding effects**.

The Court did something radical: **it did not annul the judge's decision**, recognizing that the decision was made **before** using ChatGPT. But it did establish that the judge violated fundamental principles of transparency and accountability, deriving **12 binding operational principles** for the entire Colombian magistracy.

> **Primary Source:** This analysis is based on the official ABC of Judgment T-323 of 2024, published by the Constitutional Court of Colombia.

## II. The Concrete Case: ChatGPT as "Extender" of Arguments

### Facts

A labor judge resolved a case on protection of fundamental rights of a person with ASD. After justifying and making the decision, the judge **consulted ChatGPT 3.5** to "extend the arguments" they had already decided.

### What Was Problematic

The judge:
1. **Was not transparent**: Only mentioned they used ChatGPT without explaining how, why, or scope
2. **Included inaccurate data**: Incorporated information from AI that "was not entirely accurate"
3. **Without initial context**: The questions were aimed at resolving the case, without initial context for a tool not specialized in Colombian law
4. **But DID protect privacy**: Did not input personal data of the parties

### What Saved the Decision

**The judge made the decision BEFORE using AI**. This is critical: AI was a post-decision argumentative extension tool, not input for the decision itself.

## III. PHASE 1-2: Legal Map and Conflict Matrix

### Applicable Legal Framework

| Norm | Application | Relevance |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **Political Constitution 1991** | Right to due process (Art. 29) | Main basis |
| **GDPR (if applied)** | Art. 22 (automated decisions) | European comparison |
| **Law 1581/2012** (Data Protection Colombia) | Data processing principles | Personal data protection |
| **Law 1266/2008** | Habeas data | Information rights |
| **CONPES 3975/2019** | National AI policy | National soft law |
| **Colombia AI Ethical Framework 2021** | Ethical recommendations | Soft law guide |
| **Circular 002/2024 SIC** | Personal data treatment in AI | Recent regulation |

### Identified Normative Conflicts

**Conflict 1: Efficiency vs. Due Process**
- **Tension**: AI promises speed (resolve cases faster). But due process requires exhaustive verification.
- **Resolution**: Court rejects efficiency as justification for cutting procedural guarantees.

**Conflict 2: Innovation vs. Caution**
- **Tension**: "We recognize the judge's innovative action" (Court). But simultaneously we demand 12 new operational burdens.
- **Resolution**: Innovation permitted but with extraordinary rigor.

**Conflict 3: Specialization vs. Availability**
- **Tension**: ChatGPT 3.5 is not specialized in Colombian law. But it's the available tool. Use mediocre tool or not use?
- **Resolution**: Court encourages development of its own specialized platform.

## IV. The Substantive Decision: "Due Process Was NOT Violated, BUT..."

### Main Conclusion

"Due process was not violated and the use of AI did not constitute usurpation of judicial function, because the system was used AFTER the decision was justified and made."

### But With Two Critical "Buts"

**But 1: Transparency Violation**
- The judge's disclosure was "only partial"
- The duty of transparency is not exhausted by saying "I used ChatGPT"
- Must explain: use, scope, location, why, training, functioning, limitations, justification, data used, need and suitability

**But 2: Accountability Violation**
- The judge "ran the risk of failing veracity" by including "not entirely accurate" AI data
- The questions lacked appropriate "initial context"
- ChatGPT is not specialized in Colombian law

### What Went Well: Privacy

**The judge adequately protected privacy**: Did not input personal data of the parties into ChatGPT.

## V. The Three Due Process Guarantees Threatened

The Court identified **three procedural guarantees that indiscriminate AI use can violate**:

### 1. Natural Judge Guarantee

**Risk 1a: Substitution by AI**
- That a machine replace human decision

**Risk 1b: Biases affecting independence**
- That AI induce the judge to violate impartiality due to tool biases

### 2. Decision Reasoning Guarantee

**Specific Risk: Hallucinations**
- AI produces "fictional information" that the judge does not notice
- These hallucinations can falsely ground a decision
- Non-existent jurisprudence, invented norms, fictional facts

### 3. Evidentiary Due Process Guarantee

**Risk: Evidence decree and evaluation by AI**
- Evidence decree must be in the hands of the natural judge
- Risks: irregular exclusion, privacy violation, intimacy

## VI. The Three Operational Burdens on the Judge

If a judge decides to use AI in a judicial decision, they must comply with **three constitutional burdens**:

### Burden 1: Non-Substitution of Human Rationality

**Rule**: AI can never substitute the judge's logical and human reasoning.

**Scope**:
- AI CANNOT: Interpret facts, assess evidence, justify decision, apply norm to case
- AI CAN: Administrative management, case law research, text summarization

**Non-compliance**: Decision will be **invalid** and there will be due process violation.

### Burden 2: Transparency

**Rule**: Obligation to **clearly** expose AI use, scope, and location of results.

**Is fulfilled when the judge**:
1. Notifies parties they used AI during the process
2. Explains reasons why they are competent (training, specialized studies)
3. Specifies system functioning, capabilities AND limitations (critical)
4. Exposes justification in a understandable, convincing, complete, and specific manner
5. Discloses data used and location in decision
6. Establishes analysis of need and suitability of use

**Non-compliance in T-323 case**: The judge only mentioned using ChatGPT without explaining any of the above.

### Burden 3: Accountability

**Rule**: Judge must be trained, understand risks, verify information.

**Specifically**:
- Verify information is real (against hallucinations)
- Verify it is appropriate for the matter
- Verify it respects factual and legal assumptions
- Ensure system is trained with recent, sufficient, relevant data for Colombian context
- Expressly manifest any inconsistency in the decision

**Non-compliance in case**: The judge included "not entirely accurate" AI data without exhaustive verification.

---

## VII. The Four Additional Burdens (Implicit in Judgment)

### Burden 4: Privacy

Do not input personal or sensitive data into external AI tools.

**Mandatory risk assessment** before supplying data.

Special restriction for tools "not expressly authorized for judicial function".

**The T-323 case**: Judge DID comply with this burden.

### Burden 5: Required Specialization

Judge must consider whether tool is specialized in Colombian law.

**Implication**: ChatGPT 3.5 "does not guarantee updated information, relevant for national context, is not licensed for state justice, is not specialized in Colombian law."

If using non-specialized tool: transparency and accountability burdens are **intensified**.

### Burden 6: Hallucination Verification

Must perform "rigorous verification" of reliability of information supporting justification.

The Court recognizes AI can produce:
- Non-existent jurisprudence
- Invented norms
- False references
- Uncontrolled and non-transparent biases

### Burden 7: Permanent Human Control

"Effective scrutiny" must always be permitted on AI actions.

Access to information and resources to review AI interventions.

---

## VIII. The 12 Binding Operational Principles

The Court established **12 principles that EVERY judge must appropriate** if using AI:

| # | Principle | Definition |
|---|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | **Transparency** | Obligation to clearly evidence use, scope, location, enabling full knowledge and contradiction |
| 2 | **Accountability** | Obligation to be trained, understand impacts, account for origin, suitability, need |
| 3 | **Privacy** | Duty to guard and protect reserve of personal and sensitive data |
| 4 | **Non-substitution** | Ethical and legal impossibility of substituting human action and responsibility |
| 5 | **Seriousness and verification** | Obligation of strict scrutiny of sources, scope, restrictions, flaws, risks |
| 6 | **Risk prevention** | Apply adequate control standards: inaccuracies, outdated information, hallucinations, biases |
| 7 | **Equality and equity** | Eradicate discriminations from AI biases |
| 8 | **Human control** | Permit effective scrutiny of AI actions by human authorities |
| 9 | **Ethical regulation** | Development of individual conduct standards adequate to superior mandates |
| 10 | **Good practices** | Apply reasonable schemes defined by Judicial Branch |
| 11 | **Continuous monitoring** | Adapt to legal, sociological, technological advances |
| 12 | **Idoneity** | Use of technology must be adequate to facilitate access to justice |

## IX. Limitations of the T-323 Decision

### Limitation 1: Does Not Define "Hallucination"

The Court mentions hallucinations but **does not provide a clear technical definition** or detection protocol.

**Consequence**: Judges may not identify them in practice.

### Limitation 2: Does Not Explicitly Prohibit External Tools

The Court encourages development of its own platform but does NOT explicitly prohibit ChatGPT or external tools.

**Consequence**: ChatGPT use will continue to be common, but under more demanding principles.

### Limitation 3: Accountability Falls Entirely on Judge

The accountability system is **unilateral judicial**: Judge must verify, train, understand, evaluate risks.

**Problem**: What incentives does OpenAI have to improve ChatGPT for judicial use? Court does not address this.

### Limitation 4: No Clear Consequences for Non-Compliance

What happens if judge does not comply with the 12 burdens? Is it automatic nullity? Disciplinary liability? Judgment does not specify explicitly.

---

## X. Conflict Matrix Resolved by Court

### Conflict: Judicial Autonomy vs. External Control

**Tension**: Court imposes 12 operational principles. But judges have constitutional autonomy.

**Resolution**: Court justifies this in "potential to generate significant social and legal impact with inter communis effects" (decisions that transcend the specific case).

|--------|---|---|---|

| **Specialization** | Requires consideration | Criticized for lack of specialization | Not required |

## XII. Conclusion: A Jurisprudential Model for Latin America

Judgment T-323 represents an **inflection point**: It establishes that **AI can be a judicial tool, but under an extraordinary constitutional accountability regime**.

### For Latin American Judges

- AI is permitted as a tool, prohibited as a substitute
- 12 principles are the minimum operating framework
- Hallucinations and biases are concrete risks, not theoretical
- Transparency is not optional, it is constitutional

### For AI Developers

- Judicial market demands specialized tools
- Generic tools face regulatory resistance
- User accountability is clear, but where is the provider's?

### For Judicial Authorities

- Mandatory AI training is necessary
- Own specialized platform is desirable (though costly)
- Internal regulatory framework is urgent
