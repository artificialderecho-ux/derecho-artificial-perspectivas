## Abstract

The Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI, developed by the High-Level Expert Group on Artificial Intelligence created by the European Commission, constitute the first systematic attempt to provide legal-operational content to the concept of ethical governance of artificial intelligence in the European space. This article analyzes its tripartite normative structure—lawfulness, ethics, and robustness—examines the ius-fundamentalist genealogy of its four guiding principles, and deconstructs the seven operational requirements that articulate its applied dimension. It is argued that the document operates as a hermeneutic bridge between ethical soft law and emerging positive regulation, assuming an anticipatory function regarding the Artificial Intelligence Regulation. The structural tensions of the framework are identified, and resolution criteria based on the principle of proportionality enshrined in the text itself are proposed.

## I. Introduction: The Emergence of the Trustworthiness Standard

The incorporation of artificial intelligence systems into social, economic, and institutional life presents an unprecedented challenge for traditional legal categories. Regulatory frameworks for civil liability, data protection, and product safety—designed for entities with predictable behavior—are insufficient against systems that learn, adapt, and decide in dynamic environments. In this context, the High-Level Expert Group on AI (HLEG AI), created by the European Commission in June 2018, was tasked with articulating an operational ethical framework that could guide both sector operators and public authorities.

The result, the Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI, published in April 2019 following a public consultation process with over five hundred participants (§ 8), is not a legally binding instrument. Its value, however, is of the first order: it articulates a shared technical-normative language, identifies the fundamental rights at stake with AI, and establishes a requirements architecture that—as will be argued—has anticipated in structure and content the positive regulatory framework that Europe has subsequently constructed.

## II. The Tripartite Structure of Trustworthy AI

The central concept of the document is "trustworthy AI," defined through three components that must be satisfied throughout the entire system lifecycle (§ 15). This structure deserves detailed analysis because it is not accidental: it responds to a deliberate conceptual architecture that articulates the three dimensions of modern regulation.

**A. Lawful AI: The Imperative of Legality**

The first component, lawfulness, requires compliance with all applicable laws and regulations. The Guidelines themselves recognize that they do not explicitly address this component (§ 24), as their purpose is to complement—not replace—the existing legal framework. However, the enumeration of sources they provide (§ 22) has systematic value: the document recognizes the existence of a block of legality applicable to AI systems, comprising EU primary law, GDPR, anti-discrimination directives, international human rights treaties, and sector-specific regulations. This block preexists and operates independently of the voluntary adoption of the Guidelines.

**B. Ethical AI: The Conduct Standard**

The second component, ethics, is the core of the document. It starts from a fundamental premise: laws do not always advance at the same pace as technological evolution and may be inconsistent with ethical norms (§ 26). Ethical AI therefore requires operating in the normative space situated between what is legally permitted and what is ethically demanding. The Guidelines identify four principles that articulate this space: respect for human autonomy, prevention of harm, fairness, and explicability (§ 48). These principles are anchored in the fundamental rights of the EU Charter: autonomy in Arts. 1 and 6, harm prevention in Art. 3, fairness in Arts. 21 et seq., and explicability in Art. 47 (§ 48, note 25).

**C. Robust AI: The Technical-Social Imperative**

The third component, robustness, introduces an engineering dimension into legal analysis. A system may have impeccable ethical intentions and still cause accidental harm if its technical architecture is deficient (§ 27). Robustness, in the Guidelines, is not merely a matter of computer security: it also includes social robustness, i.e., the system's ability to operate appropriately in the sociocultural context in which it is deployed. This social dimension of robustness is especially relevant for systems that interact directly with people, where the effects of inadequate functioning can be psychological, cultural, or democratic, and not just material (§§ 85-86).

## III. The Ius-Fundamentalist Genealogy of the Ethical Framework

One of the most relevant methodological decisions of the document is its choice of a fundamental rights-based approach, rather than any of the great traditions of moral philosophy (utilitarianism, Kantian deontology, virtue ethics). This choice is not neutral: it has direct consequences on the conflict resolution method and the degree of protection offered to certain interests.

Fundamental rights, as conceived in the Guidelines, operate on two simultaneous planes (§ 39). In their legal dimension, they are enforceable rights that form part of the lawfulness component. In their moral dimension, they are rights that every person possesses by virtue of their human condition, regardless of their legal strength: they are, in this sense, the foundation of the ethical component. This double nature allows the document to construct an ethical standard that transcends formal compliance with positive law, without thereby detaching from it.

The tension between the absolutism of certain fundamental rights—human dignity does not admit balancing, as expressly recognized in § 54—and the need to balance conflicting interests in most cases is one of the richest doctrinal problems raised by the document. The adopted solution—a process of reasoned reflection based on empirical evidence (§ 54)—is methodologically coherent but requires operators to develop ethical reasoning capabilities that the educational and professional systems have rarely cultivated until now.

## IV. The Seven Requirements: From Ethics to Engineering

Chapter II of the Guidelines performs an operation of remarkable technical value: it translates the four abstract ethical principles into seven concrete requirements that AI systems must fulfill throughout their entire lifecycle. This translation is not mechanical or univocal: the same principle may give rise to multiple requirements, and the same requirement may serve several principles simultaneously.

The human action and oversight requirement (Req. 1) is the most political of the set: it requires AI systems to preserve people's autonomous decision-making capacity and establishes three graduated control mechanisms—human participation, human control, and human command—depending on the level of risk (§ 65). The transparency requirement (Req. 4) deserves special mention for its operational complexity: it distinguishes three dimensions—traceability, explainability, and communication—that are not always simultaneously achievable and may conflict with intellectual property protection (§ 88).

The accountability requirement (Req. 7) closes the system and operates as a meta-requirement: without mechanisms for auditability, negative effect notification, and access to compensation, the other requirements lack practical effectiveness. The Guidelines expressly establish that when it is not possible to identify ethically acceptable balances, development should not continue in the intended form (§ 90). This assertion has far-reaching practical consequences: it introduces an ethical feasibility criterion as a necessary condition for deployment.

## V. The Document as an Anticipatory Governance Instrument

The legal value of the Guidelines transcends their soft law nature. In the period since their publication, they have operated as an implicit reference framework in European AI normative development. The conceptual structure of the Artificial Intelligence Regulation—with its risk level classification, transparency and human oversight requirements, system lifecycle focus, and emphasis on provider and implementer responsibility—parallels significantly with the content of the Guidelines.

This anticipatory function is not accidental: it responds to the Commission's deliberate strategy of building doctrinal consensus before advancing in positive regulation. The Guidelines have been, in this sense, a legal laboratory: they have allowed concepts to be tested, resistances identified, and the technical language that subsequent legislation would need to be constructed. The document itself recognizes that it intends to be a starting point for debate and should be considered a living document that will need to be reviewed and updated over time (§ 5).

## VI. Conclusions

The Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI represent a milestone in the construction of European artificial intelligence law. Their main contribution does not lie in the creation of new legal obligations but in the articulation of a conduct standard—tripartite trustworthiness—that has provided operational content to the ethical imperative of human-centered AI.

The doctrinal analysis allows three conclusions of legal relevance to be drawn. First: the ius-fundamentalist approach adopted by the document allows precise identification of rights at risk and establishes a protection hierarchy that distinguishes between absolute rights and weighable rights. Second: the seven operational requirements architecture offers a conformity assessment framework susceptible to integration into corporate due diligence processes, internal audits, and litigation. Third: the anticipatory function of the Guidelines regarding the AI Regulation consolidates their hermeneutic relevance as an instrument for teleological interpretation of emerging positive regulation.
