The Superior Court of Justice of the Canary Islands has agreed to form a separate file to investigate the disciplinary liability of an attorney who incorporated multiple citations of non-existent or spurious Supreme Court judgments in a criminal appeal, presumably generated by a generative artificial intelligence tool without any verification. The Criminal Chamber considers that this repeated conduct constitutes a serious violation of the duty of truthfulness and procedural good faith (Art. 247.3 LEC and Art. 542.3 LOPJ), differentiating it from a mere isolated error. The case, resolved in a judgment notified in early 2026, represents one of the first Spanish judicial pronouncements that explicitly links negligent AI use with professional attorney liability.

### Context and Scope of the Document

Judgment 126/2025 from the Criminal Chamber of the TSJ of Canary Islands (ponente: president Juan Luis Lorenzo Bragado) confirms the acquittal issued by the Provincial Court of Santa Cruz de Tenerife in a procedure for sexual assault on a minor. Upon examining the appeal filed by the particular prosecution, the court detects numerous false jurisprudential citations: at least seven Supreme Court rulings (with specific numbers, dates, and ponentes) and a supposed CGPJ report from 2019 on child testimony credibility, none of which exist in official databases (CENDOJ, CURIA, etc.).

The court infers that the citations, though stylistically coherent, are the product of artificial generation ("what the algorithm proposed to him"), and qualifies the verification omission as "glaring negligence." Instead of imposing a direct sanction, it agrees to form a separate file (Art. 554 LOPJ) to investigate possible disciplinary infractions, guaranteeing the attorney a hearing.

### Legal and Regulatory Keys

The pronouncement is based on the framework of procedural good faith and attorney deontological duties:

Procedural good faith (Art. 247.3 and 4 LEC): Requires diligence to avoid inducing error to the court; the repeated incorporation of false citations violates this principle, even without malice.
Duty of good faith before judicial bodies (Art. 542.3 LOPJ): Encompasses the truthfulness of legal allegations; gross negligence is sufficient for disciplinary reproach.
Disciplinary regime (Arts. 552-554 LOPJ): Allows warning, fine (300-3,000 €), or suspension; the separate file ensures contradistinction and proportionality.
European regulations and soft law: The need for effective human supervision is implicitly mentioned (Art. 14 Regulation EU 2024/1689 – AI Act) and the main principles of the CGAE-ICAV White Paper 2023 (personal accountability, mandatory verification, transparency).

The TSJ clearly distinguishes between an isolated venial error and aggravated systematic negligence due to blind trust in AI, aligning with international precedents such as Mata v. Avianca (USA, 2023).

### Risks and Obligations in Practice

For attorneys incorporating generative AI tools (LLMs like ChatGPT, Claude, etc.) in drafting procedural briefs, the case imposes reinforced obligations:

Exhaustively verify all jurisprudential, normative, or factual citations in official sources before including them.
Know the technical limitations of the models (tendency to "hallucinate" plausible but false citations, outdated information, lack of real-time access to legal databases).
Maintain documented critical human supervision (screenshots, query logs, prompt and output records).
Assume full personal accountability: The signature of the brief implies authorship and truthfulness, with no excuse for "algorithm error."
Inform the client about AI use when it may affect confidentiality or outcome (uploading sensitive data to cloud platforms).
Incorporate continuous training on AI risks and best practices in legal practice.

Failure to comply may result in:

Disciplinary sanctions (fine, suspension).
Procedural fines (180-600 €, Art. 247.4 LEC).
Civil liability (malpractice) or, in extreme cases, criminal liability.

### Critical Assessment and Conclusions

The TSJ of Canary Islands judgment constitutes a relevant and necessary precedent in Spanish jurisprudence on AI in the legal profession: It demonstrates that technology does not exempt from professional diligence and that repetition transforms a known technical risk into sanctionable negligence. However, it leaves open practical questions (required review thresholds, uniform collegiate protocols, permissible use of AI as merely an auxiliary tool) that will require development by professional associations and future resolutions.

Key ideas to remember:

Generative AI is a useful tool, but never a definitive source without exhaustive human verification.
The attorney is personally responsible for all content they sign, regardless of technological origin.
Systematic omission of source verification in briefs affecting fundamental rights justifies opening disciplinary proceedings.

This case invites the Spanish legal profession to accelerate the adoption of specific ethical and training protocols on AI, before new episodes further erode the profession's credibility before the courts.
