# Direct Amparo 6/2025 - Supreme Court of Justice of the Nation

## Legal Analysis: Artificial Intelligence and Copyright


**Case:** Gerald García Báez v. Resolution of the Specialized Chamber in Intellectual Property Matters of the TFJA (August 30, 2024)

**Judgment:** Second Chamber of the SCJN, February 26, 2025

**Presiding Justice:** Minister Lenia Batres Guadarrama

---

## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

> This ruling marks a milestone in Latin American law: the SCJN categorically rejects that artificial intelligence can be the author of protectable works. The decision affirms that only natural persons generate original creativity, that AI produces extensions of human creativity, and that AI products enter the public domain for failing to meet the originality requirement of the LFDA.

**Primary Holding:** The Union's Justice **does not grant** relief to Gerald García Báez in his lawsuit against INDAUTOR's refusal to register his work "Avatar Virtual: Gerald García Báez" generated using the Leonardo AI platform.

**Central Implications:**
- Rejection of AI's legal personality
- Reaffirmation of the anthropocentric basis of Mexican copyright law
- Classification of AI products as public domain
- Territorial application of the LFDA

---

## II. CASE STRUCTURE AND PROCEDURE

### A. Relevant Facts

Gerald García Báez requested registration with the National Copyright Institute (INDAUTOR) of a work entitled "Avatar Virtual: Gerald García Báez," described as a two-dimensional and three-dimensional graphical representation associated with the plaintiff for identification in virtual, augmented, immersive, or mixed reality forums with body movement, voice, and audio.

**Plaintiff's Involvement:** The plaintiff expressly confessed that:
- He used his own photographs as parameters
- He gave instructions to the Leonardo platform (generative AI)
- **"The artificial intelligence was the one who had the capability to make the decision to carry out the creative activity"**
- He expressly requested moral rights protection **in favor of the artificial intelligence**

### B. Procedural Pathway

| Stage | Authority | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **Registration Request** | INDAUTOR | Dismissed (artificially generated content, not human creation) |
| **Nullity Action** | TFJA (Specialized Chamber) | Validity of resolution upheld |
| **Direct Amparo** | Fifth Collegiate Court in Administrative Matters | Admitted |
| **Attraction Jurisdiction** | Second Chamber SCJN (January 29, 2025) | Exercised unanimously |
| **Amparo Judgment** | Second Chamber SCJN (February 26, 2025) | **Amparo denied** |

### C. Human Rights Invoked

The plaintiff founded his claim on alleged violations of:
- Articles 1, 3, 6, 14, 16, 17 of the Mexican Constitution
- Articles 1.1, 3, 25, 29, 31 of the ACHR

He alleged normative formal discrimination due to limiting authorship to natural persons only.

---

## III. LEGAL ISSUES

The SCJN delimited five fundamental legal issues:

### Issue 1: Transgression of Constitutional Rights (Arts. 3, 6, 14, 16, 17 CPEUM)

**Question:** Does the refusal to register AI-generated work violate rights to technological innovation, due basis, and motivation?

**Subtopics:**
- Hermeneutic interpretation of the socio-technological context
- Balance between the right to technology and innovation vs. fundamental human rights
- Pro homine principle in the AI context

### Issue 2: Registration of Work Created by Artificial Intelligence

**Question:** Can AI be recognized as the author and generator of works eligible for protection under the LFDA?

**Components:**
- Legal requirements: natural person vs. synthetic entity
- Originality as an essential element
- Differentiation between human creativity and algorithmic processing

### Issue 3: Moral Rights for AI Platform

**Question:** Can moral rights be recognized in favor of an AI system?

**Complexities:**
- Human nature of moral rights
- Impossibility of AI experiencing personal connection to a work
- Fundamental rights as exclusively human attributes

### Issue 4: Application of International Treaties

**Question:** Does the registration refusal breach obligations under the USMCA, Berne Convention, and precedents from other States?

**Analysis:**
- Principle of territoriality in intellectual property
- State normative autonomy vs. international harmonization
- Inconsistency of criteria in comparative jurisdictions

### Issue 5: Unconstitutionality of Articles 12 and 18 of the LFDA

**Question:** Do articles reserving authorship to natural persons constitute unconstitutional discrimination?

**Scope:**
- Objective and reasonable differentiation vs. prohibited discrimination
- Legitimate legislative objective
- Inapplicability of equality rights to non-human entities

## IV. APPLICABLE RULES

### A. Constitutional and Conventional Legal Framework

#### Political Constitution of the United Mexican States (CPEUM)

**Relevant Articles:**

**Article 1** - Human Rights:
> "In the United Mexican States, all persons shall enjoy the human rights recognized in this Constitution... The provisions concerning human rights shall be interpreted in accordance with this Constitution and with international treaties, at all times favoring persons the broadest protection."

**Article 3, Section V** - Technological Innovation:
> "All persons have the right to enjoy the benefits of technological innovation development."

**Article 3, Third Paragraph** - Access to Technology:
> "The State shall guarantee the right of access to information and communication technologies, as well as to broadcasting and telecommunications services."

**Articles 14, 16, 17** - Legal Certainty Guarantees:
> "Due basis, motivation, and legality of administrative acts."

#### American Convention on Human Rights (ACHR)

**Articles 21, 29, 31** - Property Rights and Limitations:
> "The protection of rights does not include the suppression of other rights. Interpretation in accordance with fundamental human rights."

#### Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)

**Article 27** - Right to Protection of Authorship:
> "Everyone has the right to the protection of the moral and material interests resulting from any scientific, literary, or artistic production of which he is the author."

**Critical Interpretation:** The UN Human Rights Council has established that this protection applies exclusively to "authors who are **natural persons** whose creative vision gave expression to the work" (Special Rapporteur on Cultural Rights, Farida Shaheed, para. 99).

### B. Federal Copyright Act (LFDA)

#### Article 3 - Definition of Work

> "The works protected by this Act are those of original creation that may be disclosed or reproduced in any form or medium."

**Structural Requirements:**
1. **Original** creation
2. Expression of the author's individuality and personality
3. Capability of disclosure or reproduction

#### Article 12 - Legal Definition of Author

> "Author is the natural person who has created a literary and artistic work."

**Key Element:** "Natural person" - requirement of human nature.

#### Article 18 - Moral Rights Ownership

> "The author is the sole, primitive, and perpetual holder of moral rights over the works of their creation."

**Moral Rights Include:** Right of disclosure, paternity, integrity of work, retraction, and repentance.

#### Article 2 - Territorial Applicability

> "The provisions of this Act are of public order, of social interest, and of general observance throughout the national territory."

**Implication:** Principle of territoriality - each State regulates its own intellectual property systems.

### C. Applicable International Framework

#### Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works (1886)

**Article 7.1** - Duration of Protection:
> "The protection granted by this Convention shall extend for the life of the author and fifty years after his death."

**SCJN Analysis:** Provision expressly oriented toward human beings (requires "life" and "death"), implicitly excluding artificial entities.

**Articles 3 and 5** - Author Nationality:
> Nationality requirements applicable only to natural persons.

#### USMCA (Agreement among the United States, Mexico, and Canada)

**Articles 19.1, 20.2, 20.4, 20.5** - Intellectual Property Objectives:
> "Promotion of innovation and creativity through their respective intellectual property systems." Flexibility for each State in implementation.

**SCJN Conclusion:** The USMCA does not establish specific guidelines for registration of AI-generated works. Application according to national systems.

### D. Established Jurisprudence

#### First Chamber SCJN (1a./J. 15/2022)

**Criterion:** "Copyright. Article 12 of the Federal Law Respects the Right to Equality and Non-Discrimination, by Limiting that Only Natural Persons Can Be Authors."

**Justification:** Objective and reasonable distinction based on a legitimate objective (exclusive recognition of rights) that only natural persons can fulfill.

#### Second Chamber SCJN in AD 6/2025

**Jurisprudence Consolidation:** Reaffirms that 2022 criteria apply to artificial entities with greater force (AI lacks even the partial legal personality that legal entities possess).

## V. LEGAL ANALYSIS (APPLICATION)

### A. Analysis of the Originality Requirement

#### 1. Conceptualization of "Original Creation"

The SCJN establishes textual subsumption: Article 3 LFDA requires "original **creation**" as a product of "human intellectual creation."

**Constitutive elements of originality:**
- Individual creativity
- Expression of the author's personality
- Distinctive character regarding pre-existing material
- Capacity for autonomous creative decision-making

#### 2. Differentiation Between Human, Animal, and Artificial Intelligence

The SCJN develops epistemological analysis distinguishing three modes of apprehension:

**Animal Intelligence** - Stimulus-based apprehension:
> "The animal apprehends things as response signs... heat as **heating**, water as **wetting**..."

Operation: Quantitative stimulus-response, lacks capacity to apprehend "reality" in a qualitative sense.

**Human Intelligence** - "Real" apprehension (qualitative):
> "In the case of human beings, things are not apprehended only stimulus-based, but also **really** (formality of reality)... the human being apprehends heat as **being** a reality..."

Essential characteristic: Possibility of indeterminate apprehension, open to multiple meanings, generator of original meaning.

**Artificial Intelligence** - Algorithmic apprehension (quantitative):
> "This is **algorithmic apprehension**... the sign-response vector is not only inorganic, but is **quantitative and not qualitative**... through the use of algorithms, a computer system can **simulate** certain reasonings..."

Legal conclusion: AI lacks capacity for qualitative or real apprehension that defines human creativity.

#### 3. Subsumption: The Leonardo Platform Does NOT Generate Protectable Originality

**Minor Premise (Confessed Facts):**
- Leonardo AI executes algorithms based on supplied data
- García Báez provided photographs and parameters
- The system processed information through statistical calculation
- Generated graphical representation through mathematical iterations

**Major Premise (LFDA Art. 3 Norm):**
- Work must be "original creation"
- Requires expression of the author's "individuality" and "personality"
- Presupposes capacity for autonomous creative decision-making

**Conclusion:** The product complies with algorithmic operation, NOT with originality in the legal sense.

### B. Analysis of Authorship

#### 1. Legal Requirement of Natural Person

**Legal Subsumption (Art. 12 LFDA):**

| Normative Element | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **"Natural person"** | Leonardo AI is not a person (neither natural nor legal) |
| **"Has created"** | Leonardo executes algorithms, does not create in the legal sense |
| **"Literary and artistic work"** | Algorithms generate extension of pre-existing creativity |

#### 2. Constitutional and Conventional Basis of the Restriction

**Profound Justification:** The right to authorship protection (Art. 27 UDHR) is fundamentally a **human right**.

**UN Human Rights Council Citation:**
> "The right to protection of authorship is the right of the author or authors who are **natural persons whose creative vision gave expression to the work**."

**Legal Consequence:** Protection is not fungible among entities; inherent to humanity.

#### 3. Inapplicability of Comparison with Legal Entities

The plaintiff argued that if legal entities cannot be authors (established jurisprudence), why should AI be?

**SCJN Counterargument:** The comparison is **logically insufficient** because:
- Legal entities possess at least legal personality (legal fiction)
- AI lacks even such fiction
- More importantly: legal entities are composed of natural persons
- AI is completely foreign to human nature

**Standard Establishment:** The requirement of "natural person" is **objectively reasonable** because only human beings possess:
- Capacity for qualitative apprehension of reality
- Personal experiences, emotions, vital context
- Autonomous creative freedom
- Faculty to make independent creative decisions

### C. Analysis of Moral Rights

#### 1. Nature of Moral Rights

Moral rights constitute a **personal and inalienable** relationship between creator and work.

**Essential Components:**
- **Paternity:** Right to be recognized as author
- **Integrity:** Right that work not be mutilated or modified
- **Disclosure:** Right to decide publication
- **Retraction:** Right to withdraw work from circulation

#### 2. Ontological Impossibility

The SCJN argues that moral rights presuppose:
- Capacity to experience emotional connection with one's own creation
- Interest in preserving integrity as expression of self
- Autonomy for decisions regarding the work's fate

**Application to AI:**
> "AI does not have the creativity, experience, perception of the moment's environment, nor the feelings of the human being... even if AI could at some point acquire a sort of consciousness, it could not embody feelings, a characteristic that corresponds to humanity."

**Conclusion:** Moral rights are **inseparable from human nature**.

### D. Analysis of the Non-Discrimination Requirement

#### 1. Equality Test Formulated by SCJN

To establish a violation of equality, the plaintiff must demonstrate:

1. **Suitable comparison term:** Relevant similarity between compared groups
2. **Differential treatment:** Diverse application of the norm
3. **Lack of objective justification:** Absence of legitimate objective or adequate means

#### 2. Application: Inadequate Comparison

**First defect:** Comparison between natural person and artificial entity

> "The differentiation parameter... is not suitable because the complainant intends, first, to equate a human being with an artificial entity, which is impossible since both have a completely different origin."

**Second defect:** Equating different creativities

> "Secondly, it attempts to equate human creativity with that of artificial intelligence, which possess distinct characteristics, while AI is basically nourished by data... unlike human beings who incorporate experiences, feelings, and emotions."

**Conclusion:** No suitable comparison term exists. The right to equality cannot be used to extend human rights to non-human entities.

### E. Analysis of International Treaties

#### 1. USMCA: Specific Inapplicability

**Plaintiff's Argument:** USMCA mandates promotion of technological innovation.

**SCJN Response:**
- USMCAflexibilizes implementation methods ("each Party may freely establish the method")
- It contains no specific guidelines on registrability of AI works
- Must be applied according to national IP systems

**Principle of Territoriality:** Each State regulates internally according to its own constitutional values.

#### 2. Berne Convention: Implicit Interpretation

**Literal Analysis:**
> "The protection granted by this Convention shall extend for the life of the author and fifty years after his death."

**Teleological Interpretation:** Provision expressly directed at human beings (requires "life" and "death").

**Comparative Precedents:**

| Jurisdiction | Criterion | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **United Kingdom** | "DABUS is a machine, not a person" | Rejected AI authorship (December 20, 2023) |
| **United States** | "Copyright was designed to reach human creators" | Rejected AI authorship (August 18, 2023) |
| **Australia** | Partially recognized AI invention | Anomalous case not binding in Mexico |
| **Mexico (SCJN)** | Authorship is a fundamental human right | Most rigorous position |

**Conclusion:** SCJN position is the most defender of legal anthropocentrism.

### F. Analysis of Public Domain

#### 1. Central Thesis: AI Products are Public Domain

The SCJN formulates an innovative thesis with broad implications:

> "AI products are in the public domain for not being an original work and, consequently, are not registrable since they are based on prior knowledge that humanity collectively possesses."

**Grounding:**
- AI "feeds on" information resulting from pre-existing human creativity
- Information is "public good" (citing Stiglitz)
- Transfer of information does not reduce quantity available to others
- Efficiency requires free dissemination

#### 2. Exception: Software and Computer Programs

The SCJN clarifies that the thesis does not affect:
> "This does not mean that software or computer programs through which AI is created and improved cannot be registered."

**Logic:** Algorithms, code, neural architectures CAN be registered because they represent human creativity of programmers/designers.

**Critical Distinction:**
- **AI Tool** (software): Protectable, human creation
- **AI Product** (image, text): Public domain, not original

#### 3. Economic Implication

> "The fact of paying for this [subscription] does not mean that the 'products' obtained as a result of their use become the property of the subscriber, but rather remain in the public domain."

**Impact:** Users of AI platforms pay for access to tools, not for ownership of outputs.

## VI. CONCLUSIONS AND FINAL HOLDING (CONCLUSION)

### A. SCJN Decision

**Unique Ruling:**
> "The Union's Justice **does not grant** relief or protection to Gerald García Báez, against the resolution of August thirty, two thousand twenty-four, issued by the Specialized Chamber in Intellectual Property Matters of the Federal Administrative Justice Court."

**Vote:** Unanimously (exact vote count not completed in judgment draft).

### B. Determining Arguments

The SCJN rejected the claim on five fronts:

#### 1. Constitutional Rights (Art. 3, 6 CPEUM)

**Conclusion:** Rights to technological innovation and access to technology **cannot be interpreted** in a way that undermines fundamental human rights.

> "The right to enjoy the benefits of technological innovation development is not justifiable if it infringes human rights or if, derived from its practice, the suppression of persons' rights occurs."

#### 2. Authorship and Originality

**Conclusion:** Art. 12 LFDA is **constitutional**. Restriction to natural persons is "objective and reasonable" because:
- Only human beings can deploy "creative action"
- Legitimate objective is to protect human creators
- It does not constitute conventionally prohibited differentiation

#### 3. Moral Rights

**Conclusion:** **Impossible** to grant moral rights to AI because:
- They are inherent to human nature (UDHR Art. 27)
- They require an intimate author-work personal connection
- AI lacks consciousness, emotions, moral integrity

#### 4. International Treaties

**Conclusion:** Berne Convention and USMCA:
- Are directed at natural persons
- Allow national flexibility in implementation
- Principle of territoriality governs IP

**Comparative precedents** (Australia, South Africa, United Kingdom, U.S.) do not bind Mexico; each State decides according to constitutional values.

#### 5. Non-Discrimination

**Conclusion:** Art. 12 LFDA **respects equality** because:
- Comparison parameter between human being and machine is logically invalid
- No suitable comparison term exists
- Right to equality does not apply to non-human entities
- Creativities (human vs. algorithmic) are essentially different

### C. Principles Synthesis

The SCJN establishes as holdings:

1. **Legal Anthropocentrism Principle:** Copyright is fundamentally a human right, not transferable to artificial entities.

2. **Material Originality Principle:** "Original creation" under LFDA requires qualitative apprehension of reality (exclusively human characteristic), not quantitative/algorithmic processing.

3. **AI Products Public Domain Principle:** Outputs of AI systems, for not being original works, automatically enter the public domain, regardless of payment for platform use.

4. **Territory Principle:** Intellectual property is a territorial matter. Each State regulates according to national system. Criteria from other States (UK, U.S., Australia) do not bind.

5. **Human Creativity Protection Principle:** Restriction of authorship to natural persons is a **valid constitutional limitation** that protects the human right to earn a living from one's own creation (UDHR Art. 27).

## VII. IMPLICATIONS AND PROJECTIONS

### A. Immediate Consequences

**For INDAUTOR:**
- Policy of denial of AI works registrations confirmed as legal
- Administrative criteria endorsed by highest court
- Strength to deny future applications

**For AI Developers:**
- Generator software/algorithms: Protectable under LFDA
- Generated outputs: Automatically public domain
- Implications for models like Leonardo, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion

**For AI Users:**
- Cannot acquire copyright over outputs
- Access to tool does not confer intellectual property
- Freedom to use AI products according to public domain

### B. Dialogue with International Systems

#### Convergence with U.S. and United Kingdom

The SCJN aligns with most recent judicial decisions (2023-2024):
- U.S. District Court for D.C. (Thaler v. Perlmutter, August 2023)
- UK Supreme Court (Thaler v. Comptroller General of Patents, December 2023)

**Differentiated Emphasis:** While U.S. emphasizes necessity of incentives (copyright exists to motivate human creators) and UK underlines required "personhood," Mexico emphasizes fundamental human rights.

#### Divergence from Australian Precedents

Australia partially recognized DABUS as inventor in some categories. The SCJN rejects such approach due to:
- Lack of mandatory international linkage
- Differences in constitutional foundations
- Greater protection of human creativity in Mexico

### C. Impact on Future Legislation

#### Confirmed Legislative Gap

The ruling **does not create new law**, but interprets the 1956 LFDA in the 2025 digital-AI context.

**Open questions for the legislator:**
- Should a specific AI regime be created in the LFDA?
- How to protect investments in model training?
- What rights over training data?
- Responsibility for harmful AI outputs?

#### Interpretive Recommendation

The SCJN implicitly suggests:
> "This does not mean that software or computer programs cannot be registered..."

This opens the door for the legislator to specifically distinguish between:
- **Tier 1:** AI tools (protectable)
- **Tier 2:** Foundation models (protectable?)
- **Tier 3:** Generated outputs (public domain)

## VIII. OBITER DICTA AND SECONDARY ARGUMENTS

### A. Analysis of Intelligences Compared

The SCJN develops epistemological analysis (not legally determinative but argumentatively important) on three modes of intelligence:

**Animal Intelligence:** Stimulus-based apprehension
- The animal sees "heat as heating"
- Cannot apprehend reality indefinitely
- Quantitative and automated operation

**Human Intelligence:** Real apprehension
- The human sees "heat as being a reality"
- Open to multiple meanings
- Can attribute meaning and intent
- **Basis of original creativity**

**Artificial Intelligence:** Algorithmic apprehension
- Quantitative sign-response vector
- Simulates reasoning (simul, not genuine)
- Can process rapidly and precisely
- Cannot apprehend "reality" in a qualitative sense

**Implication:** AI is essentially **simulacrum**, not creativity.

### B. AI Learning Cycles

The SCJN describes four machine learning modes:

| Type | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Supervised** | Previously labeled data + human intervention | Teaching cat photos |
| **Unsupervised** | Self-discovery of patterns without labels | System categorizes itself |
| **Reinforcement** | Positive and negative rewards | "Prize" for correct answers |
| **Deep** | Neural networks in layers | Complex massive processing |

**Legal Conclusion:** All these cycles share a characteristic: they are **designed and directed by humans**. Even "unsupervised learning" occurs within parameters established by human creators.

### C. Information as "Public Good"

The SCJN cites Joseph Stiglitz:
> "Efficiency requires that information be disseminated freely" (information is a public good because supplying it to one person does not reduce quantity available to others).

**Application to AI:** Data used to train AI, being information, has the nature of a public good and cannot be privatized in outputs.

## IX. CRITICAL ANALYSIS AND PERSPECTIVES

### A. Strengths of SCJN Argumentation

1. **Constitutional Rigor:** Anchors decision in fundamental human rights (UDHR Art. 27), not only in positive rules.

2. **Legal Epistemology:** Analysis of the difference between intelligences (animal, human, artificial) provides profound grounding.

3. **Systematic Coherence:** Integrates CPEUM, ACHR, LFDA, Berne Convention without apparent contradictions.

4. **Justified Territoriality:** Rejection of application of Australian precedents is based on legitimate state autonomy.

5. **Prospective Clarity:** Distinguishes between protectable software and non-protectable outputs, leaving space for future legal innovation.

### B. Points of Tension or Potential Weakness

#### 1. What if AI Acquires Consciousness?

The SCJN expressly discards:
> "Even if AI could at some point acquire a sort of consciousness, it could not embody feelings, a characteristic that corresponds to humanity."

**Potential criticism:** Argument seems to close the door to future AI evolution. What if AI "feels"?

**Defense:** SCJN would likely interpret that "consciousness" without biological substrate does not have equivalent human meaning. Argumentation could be extended.

#### 2. Public Domain vs. Innovation Incentives

Declaring AI products as public domain could:
- Disincentivize investment in AI platforms
- Create asymmetry: user pays for platform, outputs do not belong to them

**SCJN Response:** Users obtain "better tools" through subscription, not property. Model comparable to licensed software.

#### 3. Difference Between Originality in Technical vs. Legal Sense

AI can generate images/texts that **technically** do not exist in training (they are not exact copies).

**SCJN Response:** Legal originality requires autonomous creative act (qualitative apprehension), not mere technical novelty.

### C. Projections of Legal Development

#### 1. Foundation Models

What happens with GPT-4, Claude, Gemini? Are they protectable as software?

**Probable SCJN interpretation:** Yes, as computer programs (Art. 15 LFDA). But not outputs.

#### 2. Fine-tuning and Personalization

If user trains personalized model with their own data, do they acquire rights?

**Possible analysis:** Method could qualify as "assisted creativity" (similar to software-assisted photography). But without substantial creative intervention, it would remain public domain.

#### 3. Watermarks and Attribution

Can developers include watermarks in outputs for identification?

**Probable position:** Watermark identifies technical origin (tool used), not authorship. Does not confer copyright.

---

## X. CONCLUSIONS FOR LEGAL PROFESSIONALS

### For Attorneys Advising AI Developers

> **Recommendation:** Direct strategic protection toward software/architectures (protectable), not toward generated outputs. Consider business models based on tool subscriptions, not sale of generated content.

### For Copyright Professionals

> **Implication:** Paradigmatic change. The LFDA, under SCJN interpretation, is fundamentally anthropocentric. Future AI legislation must be explicit if they intend to extend protections to artificial systems.

### For AI Regulators

> **Gap Detected:** The 1956 LFDA did not contemplate AI. Specific legislation should address: (a) rights in training data, (b) responsibility for outputs, (c) distinction between tools and products.

### For Users of AI Platforms

> **Legal Clarity:** AI outputs (images, texts) are automatically in the public domain. They do not acquire rights through subscription payment. They can be used freely (including commercially), but cannot be registered.

> **Final Note:** This ruling represents a milestone in Latin American artificial intelligence law. The SCJN consolidates a firm anthropocentric position that, contrasting with some more flexible international precedents (Australia), reaffirms that copyright is fundamentally a human right not extendable to artificial entities. The clarity of the decision provides legal certainty to developers, users, and creators, although it raises questions about how to legislate future forms of assisted or hybrid creativity.
