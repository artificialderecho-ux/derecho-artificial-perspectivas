# Orleans Administrative Tribunal: First European Judicial Warning on AI "Hallucinations" in Case Law References

**Reference:** TA Orleans, December 29, 2025, No. 2506461  
**Presiding Magistrate:** G. Girard-Ratrenaharimanga  
**Date:** December 29, 2025  
**Publication:** January 8, 2026

---

## Executive Summary

The Orleans Administrative Tribunal has issued a historic ruling that sets a precedent in Europe by explicitly warning about the risk of "hallucinations" and "confabulations" from artificial intelligence tools in legal practice. Although the main case concerned immigration law, the court dedicated a specific section to harshly criticize the citation of **15 non-existent case references**, presumably generated by AI.

This ruling establishes a crucial precedent on attorney professional responsibility for verifying references when using generative AI tools, and raises fundamental questions about effective human supervision in the use of these technologies.

---

## I. LEGAL ISSUES PRESENTED (ISSUES)

### Main Issue: Professional Responsibility in AI Use

**What is the standard of due diligence required of attorneys in verifying case law references when using generative AI tools?**

This issue involves determining:
- The duty to verify cited references before the court
- Liability for presenting non-existent jurisprudence
- The scope of the duty of human supervision over AI outputs
- The ethical consequences of negligent use of AI tools

### Secondary Issues

1. **Does the presentation of AI-generated false citations constitute a sanctionable ethical violation?**

2. **Should the court adopt specific measures when it detects inappropriate use of AI by attorneys?**

3. **What standard of "effective human supervision" is required in the context of AI use for legal research?**

## II. APPLICABLE LEGAL AND DOCTRINAL FRAMEWORK (RULES)

### A. Regulations on AI and Professional Responsibility

#### 1. Artificial Intelligence Regulation (EU) 2024/1689

**Article 14 - Human Oversight:**
> "High-risk AI systems shall be designed and developed in such that they can be effectively overseen by natural persons during their use."

**Applicability:** Although generative AI systems like LLMs are not classified as "high-risk" in Annex III, principles of human oversight are extendable by analogy to professional use.

**Recital 48 of the AI Regulation:**
> "Users should be able to interpret the results of AI systems and use them appropriately. Persons overseeing the systems must have the necessary competence, training, and authority."

#### 2. European Code of Conduct for Lawyers

**Article 2.4 - Diligence and Competence:**
Lawyers must provide their services with the necessary diligence and competence, which includes staying updated on technological developments and their risks.

**Article 3.1 - Duty to Inform the Court:**
Lawyers have the duty not to deceive the court and to present truthful and verified information.

### B. Prior Case Law on AI Use in Legal Practice

#### Precedent Cases

**1. Mata v. Avianca (S.D.N.Y., 2023)**
- Paradigmatic case in the United States where an attorney cited 6 invented cases by ChatGPT
- Sanction: $5,000 fine and public reprimand
- Established standard: Unconditional duty of source verification

**2. Park v. Kim (British Columbia Court, Canada, 2023)**
- Detection of AI hallucinations in memorials
- Warning about the use of "unverified technologies"
- No specific sanction but judicial reprimand

**3. Spanish preliminary rulings**
- Although there is no consolidated case law in Spain, the AEPD has warned about the need for human supervision in AI system use in professional environments

### C. Soft Law and Professional Guidelines

#### European Bar Association Guidelines on AI (2024)

The Association of European Bars published preliminary guidelines in 2024 on AI use, establishing that:

1. **Verification Principle:** All AI output must be verified by the professional
2. **Transparency Principle:** Clients must be informed of AI tool use
3. **Responsibility Principle:** The attorney is ultimately responsible for presented content

#### CCBE Recommendations (Council of Bars and Law Societies of Europe)

Document "Artificial Intelligence and the Legal Profession" (2024):
- Emphasizes the duty of technological competence
- Warns about hallucination risks in LLMs
- Recommends internal verification protocols

### A. Factual Context of the Case

#### The Main Case: Immigration Law

The underlying case involved **M. B...**, a Russian citizen, subject to:
- Order to leave French territory without delay (December 3, 2025)

### B. The Explicit Judicial Warning on AI "Hallucinations"

The court used specific technical terminology from the AI field:

> "Il y a donc lieu d'inviter le conseil du requérant à vérifier à l'avenir que les références trouvées **par quelque moyen que ce soit** ne constituent pas une **«hallucination»** ou une **«confabulation»**."

**Semantic analysis of the expression:**

1. **"Par quelque moyen que ce soit"**: Deliberately broad reference that includes:
   - Traditional legal databases
   - Web search engines
   - **Generative AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, etc.)**
   - Any other technology

2. **"Hallucination"**: Technical term in AI field referring to when a language model generates plausible but completely false information. It is significant that the court uses this term in quotes, recognizing its origin in AI vocabulary.

3. **"Confabulation"**: Psychological term that the AI field has adopted to describe when models "invent" information with the appearance of coherence but without factual basis.

### C. Legal Subsumption: Was the Duty of Diligence Violated?

#### Proportionality Test and Human Oversight

Applying principles from Art. 14 of the AI Regulation on effective human oversight:

**1. Suitability:**
- Was the AI tool suitable for the task of case law research?
- **Analysis:** Current LLMs (GPT-4, Claude, etc.) are NOT suitable for accurate case retrieval due to their tendency toward hallucinations
- **Conclusion:** Unsuitable use without verification

**2. Necessity:**
- Was it necessary to use AI without subsequent verification?
- **Analysis:** Reliable legal databases exist (Légifrance, Doctrine.fr) that allow direct verification
- **Conclusion:** There was no need to rely exclusively on unverified outputs

**3. Proportionality stricto sensu:**
- Do efficiency benefits justify the risk of presenting false case law?
- **Analysis:** The risk of discrediting the professional and harming the client is disproportionate to time savings
- **Conclusion:** Disproportionate

#### Analysis of the Required Human Oversight Standard

The AI Regulation establishes that human oversight must allow for:

a) **Fully understanding the capabilities and limitations of the AI system**
- In this case: Knowing that LLMs can generate false references with the appearance of truthfulness

b) **Remaining aware of the possible tendency to rely on or automatically trust the output**
- In this case: Avoiding "automation bias" that leads to blindly trusting AI output

c) **Correctly interpreting the AI system's output**
- In this case: Understanding that an AI-generated case reference requires direct verification in primary sources

d) **Deciding not to use the AI system or ignoring, overriding, or reversing its output**
- In this case: Having decided to verify each reference before including it in the brief

**Conclusion of analysis:** Human oversight was **manifestly insufficient** or **nonexistent**.

### D. Comparison with International Precedents

#### Similarities with Mata v. Avianca (2023)

| Aspect | Mata v. Avianca | TA Orleans 2025 |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Number of false citations** | 6 cases | 15 references |
| **Presumed tool** | ChatGPT confirmed | AI not expressly confirmed |
| **Judicial reaction** | Monetary sanction | Public warning in ruling |
| **Attorney acknowledgment** | Admitted ChatGPT use | No admission on record |
| **Term used** | "Non-existent cases" | "Hallucination", "Confabulation" |

#### Significant Differences

1. **Technical language:** TA Orleans is the first European court to explicitly use AI technical terminology of "hallucination"

2. **Preventive approach:** Instead of imposing immediate sanction, the court opts for a pedagogical warning for the future

3. **Systemic context:** The ruling recognizes this problem can arise from "quelque moyen que ce soit," suggesting a broader understanding of the technological ecosystem

### E. Ethical Implications

#### Possible Code of Conduct Violations

**1. Lack of Diligence (Art. 2.4):**
- Not verifying references before citing them constitutes serious professional negligence
- Aggravating factor: The 15 false references were not produced "without more"—many have case numbers and dates that appear real, suggesting a systematic pattern

**2. Duty Toward the Court (Art. 3.1):**
- Presenting false references, even if by error, may be considered an attempt to mislead the court
- Mitigating factor: No evidence of deliberate bad faith, but rather negligence in verification

**3. Professional Competence (Art. 1.2):**
- An attorney must have the necessary technological competence to use AI tools responsibly
- Digital incompetence is no longer excusable in 2025

#### Possible Disciplinary Sanctions

According to the French disciplinary regime (and by extension, European), sanctions could include:

- **Warning** (least serious)
- **Reprimand**
- **Temporary suspension**
- **Disbarment** (only in extremely serious cases)

In this case, given the explicit judicial warning and apparent lack of bad faith, a **reprimand** from the Bar Association would be most likely if disciplinary proceedings are initiated.

## IV. EMERGING DOCTRINE AND CONSEQUENCES (CONCLUSION)

### A. Answers to the Issues Presented

#### Main Issue: Required Standard of Diligence

**CONCLUSION:** Attorneys have an **absolute duty of direct verification** of all case law references before citing them before a court, regardless of the source used to locate them (including AI tools).

**Foundations:**
1. Art. 14 of the AI Regulation requires effective human oversight
2. Code of ethics requires diligence and truthfulness
3. Comparative case law (Mata v. Avianca) establishes unconditional responsibility
4. The Orleans court has explicitly warned about this risk

#### Secondary Issues

**1. Ethical violation:** YES, presenting false references (even through negligence) constitutes a sanctionable ethical violation.

**2. Court measures:** This ruling establishes that courts MUST explicitly warn about AI hallucination risks when detected, creating a precedent of pedagogical function.

**3. Human oversight standard:** Effective supervision requires direct verification in official primary sources (official databases, authorized publications), not merely superficial review of AI output.

### B. Emerging Doctrine: The Three Duties of Attorneys in the AI Era

This ruling allows extracting three fundamental duties:

#### 1. Duty of Digital Competence

Attorneys must:
- Understand how the AI tools they use work
- Know their specific limitations (especially hallucinations in LLMs)
- Maintain ongoing training on legal technologies

**Practical implication:** Bar Associations must implement mandatory training on responsible AI use.

#### 2. Duty of Independent Verification

Attorneys must:
- Verify ALL AI-generated information in primary sources
- Maintain records of verification performed
- Not rely on the "appearance of truthfulness" of AI output

**Practical implication:** Law firms must establish internal reference verification protocols.

#### 3. Duty of Transparency on AI Use

Although this ruling does not directly address it, doctrinal trends suggest attorneys should:
- Inform clients of AI tool use
- Potentially inform the court when AI is used for substantive tasks
- Document what tools were used and how

**Practical implication:** Development of contractual clauses and procedural declarations on AI use.

### C. Impact on European Legal Practice

#### Short Term (2026-2027)

1. **Increased judicial vigilance:** European courts will begin more strictly scrutinizing cited references

2. **Verification protocols:** Law firms will implement mandatory checklists to verify AI outputs

3. **Professional training:** Bars will offer courses on "responsible AI use in law"

#### Medium Term (2027-2030)

1. **Consolidated case law:** More similar cases will establish more precise standards

#### Long Term (2030+)

1. **ISO standards:** Possible development of international standards for legal AI

### D. Operational Recommendations for the Legal Profession

#### For Individual Attorneys

**NEVER:**
- ❌ Copy case references directly from ChatGPT/Claude without verifying
- ❌ Assume a citation "looks real" is sufficient
- ❌ Use AI to draft case law sections without supervision

**ALWAYS:**
- ✅ Verify each reference in official databases (Légifrance, CENDOJ, EUR-Lex)

- ✅ Use AI as initial research assistant, not as final source

2. **AI Use Policy:**

## V. PROSPECTIVE ANALYSIS: THE FUTURE OF AI IN LEGAL PRACTICE

### A. The AI Trust Paradox

This ruling exposes a fundamental paradox:

**Paradox:** The more sophisticated LLMs become, the more convincing their hallucinations, and therefore the more dangerous they are for non-expert users.

**Previous models (GPT-3.5):**
- More obvious hallucinations
- Less internal coherence
- Easier to detect

**Current models (GPT-4, Claude 3.5, etc.):**
- Highly convincing hallucinations
- Perfectly formatted references
- Plausible case numbers
- Dates consistent with actual chronology

**Implication:** Verification becomes MORE necessary, not less, as AI improves.

### B. Necessary Technological Developments

To reduce this risk, the legal AI industry needs to develop:

#### 1. Legal AI with "Grounding" (Anchoring in Real Sources)

**Concept:** AI systems that ONLY cite case law that can link to a verifiable source.

**Emerging examples:**
- Westlaw AI (with direct links to cases)
- LexisNexis+ AI (automatic citation verification)
- RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) tools with legal databases

**Current limitations:**
- Only available in some jurisdictions
- Expensive for small firms
- Can still have errors in edge cases

#### 2. AI Output Certification

**Proposal:** Digital "chain of custody" system certifying:
- What tool generated the output
- What sources it actually consulted
- Confidence level in each assertion
- Generation timestamp

**Benefit:** Would allow subsequent audits and traceable liability.

#### 3. Automatic Citation Verifiers

**Concept:** Specialized tools that:
1. Scan a document for case references
2. Verify each against official databases
3. Flag those that don't exist or are incorrect

**Current state:** In development by several legaltech startups.

### C. Expected Regulatory Changes

#### 1. At European Level (EU)

**Possible AI Regulation modification:**
- Inclusion of legal AI systems in higher risk categories
- Specific traceability requirements for legal AI
- Obligation to warn about error rates in legal tasks

**Likely timeline:** 2027-2028 (first review of AI Regulation)

#### 2. At Member State Level

**France:** Already leading with this ruling; likely development of official guidelines for courts on how to detect AI hallucinations

**Spain:** AEPD could issue guidelines on AI use in regulated professions, including law

**Germany:** Development of technical standards (DIN) for legal AI

#### 3. At Bar Association Level

All European Bar Associations will need to:
- Update codes of ethics (2026-2027)
- Establish digital ethics committees (2026)
- Create technology supervision protocols (2027-2028)

### D. AI Provider Responsibility

#### Open Question: Should LLM Providers Warn About Legal Use?

**Arguments in favor:**
1. **Duty to inform:** Providers know their models' limitations
2. **Damage prevention:** Warnings could prevent cases like this
3. **Product liability:** Analogy with pharmaceutical warnings

**Arguments against:**
1. **General-purpose tool:** LLMs are not specifically designed for legal use
2. **User responsibility:** Professionals must know their tools' limits
3. **Practical impossibility:** Cannot warn about every possible misuse

**Current state:**
- OpenAI, Anthropic, Google have general disclaimers
- But NO specific warnings about legal use
- Trend: Development of "professional" versions with greater guarantees

#### Toward a Shared Liability Regime

The future will likely see a **shared liability model**:

| Actor | Responsibility |
|-------|----------------|
| **AI Provider** | Warn about known limitations; Implement technical safeguards; Document capabilities and limits |
| **Legal tool developer** | Adapt general AI to legal context; Implement citation verification; Certify accuracy in specific tasks |
| **Firm/Organization** | Establish use protocols; Train staff; Monitor compliance |
| **Individual attorney** | Verify outputs; Maintain digital competence; Ultimate responsibility for filed work |

## VI. FINAL CONCLUSIONS AND KEY LESSONS

### A. Fundamental Lessons from the TA Orleans Ruling

#### 1. AI Is Not Infallible — And Never Will Be

Hallucinations are not a "bug" that will be fixed soon, but an intrinsic characteristic of how current language models work. Even with future improvements, some error risk will always exist.

**Implication:** Human verification is and will always be necessary.

#### 2. Technology Does Not Replace Professional Competence

AI is a powerful tool, but it does not replace attorney judgment, experience, and responsibility.

**Implication:** Legal education must include digital competencies, while maintaining emphasis on critical analysis.

#### 3. Judicial Transparency Is Essential

This court chose to make its warning about AI hallucinations public, creating a valuable pedagogical precedent.

**Implication:** Courts have an active role in educating the profession about new technologies.

### B. Operating Principles for Legal Practice with AI

#### Principle 1: "Trust, but Verify" → "Verify, then Trust"

**Traditional:** Trust a tool but verify occasionally  
**New standard:** Systematically verify before trusting

#### Principle 2: AI as Junior Assistant, Not Senior Partner

**Useful analogy:** Treat AI output like a junior associate's draft:
- Useful as a starting point
- Requires exhaustive review
- Ultimate responsibility lies with supervisor

#### Principle 3: Documentation and Traceability

**Recommended practice:** Maintain records of:
- What AI tools were used
- For what tasks
- How results were verified
- Who performed verification

**Benefit:** Protection in case of audit or disciplinary complaint.

#### Principle 4: Mandatory Continuing Education

**Reality:** Technology evolves faster than regulation.

**Implication:** Attorneys must take personal responsibility for staying updated on:
- New AI tools
- Their specific limitations
- Cases like this that set precedents
- Emerging best practices

### C. The Historic Precedent of TA Orleans

This ruling will be remembered as:

1. **First explicit European judicial warning on AI hallucinations**
2. **First use of technical term "hallucination" in European case law**
3. **First case establishing mandatory reference verification standard in the AI era**

**Prediction:** This ruling will be extensively cited in:
- Future similar cases throughout Europe
- Academic articles on AI and law
- Bar Association ethics guidelines
- Legal AI training programs

### D. Final Message for the Profession

The TA Orleans ruling No. 2506461 is not simply an anecdote about an attorney who made an error. It is a **systemic wake-up call** for the entire European legal profession:

> **Artificial intelligence is a transformative tool that can greatly improve the practice of law — but only if used with competence, diligence, and effective human oversight.**

Attorneys who ignore this warning do so under their own professional risk.
