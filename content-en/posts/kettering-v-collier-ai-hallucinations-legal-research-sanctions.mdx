# Kettering Adventist Healthcare v. Collier: Sanctions for Misuse of Artificial Intelligence in Legal Research

## Definitive Legal Analysis on Attorney Liability for AI-Generated False Citations

**Case:** Kettering Adventist Healthcare d/b/a Kettering Health Network v. Sandra Collier, et al.  
**Court:** United States District Court, Southern District of Ohio, Western Division in Dayton  
**Case Number:** 3:25-cv-273  
**Magistrate Judge:** Hon. Walter H. Rice  
**Order Date:** February 25, 2026  
**Citation:** 348 F.R.D. (forthcoming)

---

## I. Substantial Facts and Procedure

### A. Procedural Context

In this complex action involving Kettering Adventist Healthcare as plaintiff/counter-defendant, attorneys Mary T. Scott, Esq. (both pro se and as former representative of counter-defendant Sandra Lee Collier) and H. Leon Hewitt, Esq. (Collier's current attorney) were subject to disciplinary investigation following the filing of motions containing severely defective case citations.

On January 2, 2026, the Court issued an extensive Decision and Entry (Doc. #113, PAGEID 2020-2026) documenting twelve or more fraudulent citations. These citations appeared in:

1. **Motion to Dismiss** (Doc. #13)
2. **Response to Motion** (Doc. #69)
3. **Memorandum in Opposition** to the Motion to Dismiss by Third-Party Defendants Epstein Becker & Green, P.C., et al. (Doc. #93)

### B. AI-Generated False Citations

The most notable element of this case is that Scott admitted to using **generative artificial intelligence** for "preliminary case identification." As stated in her Response dated January 15, 2026:

> "Regardless of the tool used, the duty to verify citations rests entirely with counsel. I failed to satisfy that duty here."

The AI-generated false citations—known as "hallucinations"—included:

- Cases that do not exist in the cited formats
- Cases completely unrelated to the legal propositions invoked
- Cases that exaggerate or misapply the law

### C. Chronology of Non-Compliance

| Date | Event |
|-------|--------|
| August 2025 | Motion to Dismiss filed with false citations |
| September 15, 2025 | Kettering notifies Scott/Collier of at least 3 inaccurate citations |
| October 2025 | Scott reiterates the SAME false citations in Response (Reply) |
| November 13, 2025 | Third-Party Defendants file memorandum detailing the fraud |
| January 2, 2026 | Show Cause Order issued |
| January 15-16, 2026 | Scott and Hewitt Responses acknowledging conduct |
| January 21, 2026 | Mediation statements filed with new false citations |
| January 28, 2026 | Mediation cancelled by Magistrate Judge Silvain |
| February 25, 2026 | Sanctions Order |

## II. Applicable Legal Framework

### A. Rule 11(b) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure

Rule 11(b)(2) requires that an attorney signing a filing certify that:

> "the claims, defenses, and other legal contentions are warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for extending, modifying, or reversing existing law or for establishing new law."

**Objective Standard:** Non-compliance is measured according to an "inquiry reasonable under the circumstances," reflecting the due diligence expected of a competent attorney in the jurisdiction.

**Critical Case Law Application:**

- *Mata v. Avianca, Inc.*, 678 F. Supp. 3d 443 (S.D.N.Y. 2023) — Seminal case on AI hallucinations; cited in 205 cases per Westlaw
- *Wadsworth v. Walmart, Inc.*, 348 F.R.D. 489 (D. Wyo. 2025) — Monetary sanctions of $3,000 and $1,000 to attorneys for AI-fabricated citations; cited in 61 cases

### B. Ohio Rule of Professional Conduct 3.3(a)(1)

This rule prohibits an attorney from lying to the court or failing to disclose a material fact when there is a duty to disclose. Scott and Hewitt were found guilty of violating this rule by not notifying the court about the false citations after being put on notice by opposing parties.

### C. Contempt of Court

The Court concluded that Scott and Hewitt's continued misrepresentations to Magistrate Judge Silvain in mediation statements (filed just three weeks after the January 2, 2026 Order) constituted contempt of court, although they were not monetarily sanctioned under this specific basis.

## III. IRAC Legal Analysis

### A. **Issue Presented**

Does the use of generative artificial intelligence for legal research without independent citation verification violate Rule 11(b) of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure and constitute contempt of court, particularly when the attorney is notified of inaccuracies but fails to correct them in subsequent filings?

### B. **Applicable Rule**

#### 1. Rule 11(b)(2) — Attorney Certification

**Elements:**
- Signing of a filing by an attorney
- Lack of certification after reasonable investigation
- Violation regarding legal contentions (claims, defenses, legal contentions)

**Reasonableness Standard:** Although the Rule does not prohibit the use of technological tools, competence requires independent verification, especially when:
- There are public warnings about the tool's reliability
- The attorney receives notice of specific errors
- The tool has a known history of producing fictional information

#### 2. Duty of Candor under Ohio Rule 3.3(a)(1)

Once an attorney is put on notice that they have filed inaccurate citations, an affirmative duty arises to:
- Immediately notify the court
- Correct the record
- Not reiterate the same errors in subsequent filings

#### 3. Contempt of Court — Civil Contempt

The filing of mediation statements containing "patent misstatements and mischaracterizations of the law" after promising the court that citations would be verified constitutes a deliberate affront to judicial authority.

### C. **Application of Facts**

#### 1. Rule 11(b) Violation — Mary T. Scott

**Initial Conduct:**
- Scott used generative AI for "preliminary case identification"
- At least 12 citations did not exist or were completely unrelated
- The volume of false citations made it impossible to discern what contained valid legal propositions

**Aggravating Factors:**
- **Prior knowledge:** By February 2025, the legal community was aware that AI resources generate fictional cases (*Wadsworth* cited in 61 cases by that date)
- **Lack of due diligence:** Scott admitted "mistakenly believed those tools were sufficiently reliable"
- **Persistent non-compliance:** Despite Kettering's notice on September 15, 2025, Scott reiterated three of the SAME false citations in her Response
- **Kenty case reference:** The Court specifically noted that Scott cited *Kenty v. Transamerica Prem. Ins. Co.* for a proposition ("settlement demands are privileged") that the Court had already determined "nothing resembling the purported language quoted by Collier and Scott appears in" the opinion. **Incredibly, Scott cited Kenty again for the SAME proposition in her mediation statement.**

**Conclusion:** Egregious violation of Rule 11(b)(2). The Court concluded this was "the most egregious, inexplicable, and repeated violations of Rule 11(b) by any attorneys that have ever appeared before the undersigned" in 46 years on the federal bench.

#### 2. Rule 11(b) Violation — H. Leon Hewitt

**Conduct:**
- Hewitt joined the case later, after Scott had filed motions with false citations
- However, he signed the "memorandum in opposition" (Doc. #93) without independently verifying Scott's citations
- He did not read Kettering's memorandum that had been filed, which would have revealed the errors
- He violated his nondelegable duty to ensure filings are supported by existing law

**Mitigating Circumstances (but insufficient):**
- He was "forthcoming, honest, and apologetic" in his response to the Show Cause Order
- He proposed remediation through "additional experienced litigators for independent review"
- He was less culpable than Scott regarding the initial generation of false citations

**Aggravating Factors:**
- After acknowledging guilt on January 16, he filed mediation statements with new false citations just 5 days later
- The Court concluded that Hewitt "could not even be bothered to check whether the cases he cited existed"

**Conclusion:** Substantial violation of Rule 11(b)(2). Although less egregious than Scott's, Hewitt's conduct was compounded by his inability to verify after promising reform.

#### 3. Violation of Ohio Rule 3.3(a)(1)

Both Scott and Hewitt violated the duty of candor by not notifying the court of their own volition once put on notice of the false citations. The duty was not simply not to reiterate errors; it was to actively notify the court.

#### 4. Contempt of Court

Although not the primary basis for sanction, the Court found contempt based on:
- The filing of mediation statements with "patent misstatements" just 3 weeks after the January 2 Order
- Scott and Hewitt's promises to the court being deliberately violated in real-time
- The conduct pattern suggesting attorneys did not take their obligations to the court seriously

### D. **Defense Rebuttal — "Unreliable Tool"**

Scott argued that she simply "mistakenly believed those tools were sufficiently reliable." The Court rejected this defense:

> "By the time Collier and Scott filed the Motion to Dismiss in August 2025, every licensed attorney knew or should have reasonably known that relying on generative AI for research would likely result in a filing containing AI hallucinations."

**Precedent:** *Wadsworth* (published February 24, 2025) established public notoriety in the legal community regarding AI hallucinations. There is no excuse for ignorance after this date.

## IV. Sanctions Imposed

### A. Monetary Sanctions

**Mary T. Scott, Esq.:** $5,000 (payable to the Clerk of Court within 30 days)

**Basis:**
- Author of false citations (12+ cases)
- Lack of voluntary notification
- Reiteration of errors after being put on notice
- Four occasions of being caught committing legal fraud: by Kettering, by EBG Defendants, by the Court, and by Magistrate Judge Silvain

**H. Leon Hewitt, Esq.:** $2,500 (payable to the Clerk of Court within 30 days)

**Basis:**
- Co-signer without independent verification
- Violation of nondelegable duty
- Less culpable than Scott but still intolerably negligent
- The combined $7,500 ($100/hour) represents reasonable compensation for the 75+ hours the Court spent

### B. Procedural Sanctions

**Motion to Dismiss:** Stricken without prejudice to refiling

**Basis:** Although serious, this sanction was not imposed "with prejudice" because it would harm Collier, who was not found guilty of misconduct. The sanction was designed to punish the attorneys, not the client.

### C. Disciplinary Referral

**Destination:** Ohio Disciplinary Counsel (ODC) through the Court

**Enclosed Documentation:**
1. January 2, 2026 Decision
2. Magistrate Judge Silvain's Order of February 19, 2026
3. This Sanctions Order

**Additional Concern:** The Court expressed "grave concerns as to whether either Scott or Hewitt should remain active members in good standing in the bar of this Court and of the Supreme Court of Ohio."

### D. Recusal of Judge Rice

Judge Walter H. Rice recused himself from further proceedings in the case, requesting reassignment to another District Judge in Cincinnati or Columbus seats.

## V. Comparative Law: Critical Precedents

### A. *Mata v. Avianca, Inc.*, 678 F. Supp. 3d 443 (S.D.N.Y. 2023)

**Importance:** Seminal case establishing that AI hallucinations do not provide an excuse for Rule 11 non-compliance.

**Recognized Harms:**
1. Waste of opposing party's time and money
2. Diversion of judicial resources
3. Deprivation of arguments based on authentic precedents
4. Reputational damage to judges and courts
5. Promotion of cynicism about the legal profession
6. Possible future challenge of authentic rulings based on seeded doubt

**Application in Kettering:** These harms were amplified, given the Court spent 75+ hours addressing the conduct.

### B. *Wadsworth v. Walmart, Inc.*, 348 F.R.D. 489 (D. Wyo. 2025)

**Importance:** Establishes that by February 2025, the legal community had well-established knowledge that "AI resources generate fake cases."

**Sanctions Imposed:**
- Drafting attorney: $3,000
- Co-signing attorney: $1,000

**Factors Considered:**
- Number of false vs. real citations
- Access to reliable research tools (Lexis/Westlaw)
- Attorneys' honesty and apologies
- Prior remediation actions

**Comparison in Kettering:**
- Scott was sanctioned $5,000 (greater than the drafting attorney in Wadsworth) for:
  - Lack of voluntary notification
  - Persistent reiteration
  - Lack of genuine remorse/reform

### C. *Mezu v. Mezu*, 267 Md. App. 354 (Md. Ct. App. 2025)

**Principle:** Judicial time cost is legitimate damage justifying sanctions regardless of whether they were requested.

**Application:** The Court spent 75+ hours; total sanctions of $7,500 ($100/hour) are reasonable.

## VI. Systemic Implications: AI in Legal Research

### A. **Emerging Professional Crisis**

The Court characterized the problem as "one of the biggest threats currently facing federal civil litigation":

1. **Proliferation:** Mata was cited in 205 cases; Wadsworth in 61 cases
2. **Scale:** Attorneys are routinely integrating AI into research workflows
3. **Lack of Verification:** Many attorneys treat AI outputs as quasi-reliable if they seem plausible

### B. **Evolving Standard of Care**

**Post-Kettering Conclusion:**
- The use of generative AI for legal research requires exhaustive independent verification
- "Preliminary identification" is not sufficient purpose; verification must be comprehensive
- Knowledge of AI hallucinations is universally attributable after February 2025
- Duty of candor requires voluntary notification once errors are discovered

### C. **Firm Liability**

The Court considered the attorneys' resource level (access to Lexis/Westlaw). Expectations are higher for:
- Attorneys at prominent firms
- Attorneys with access to professional legal research
- Attorneys working in jurisdictions where AI use is common

## VII. Practical Lessons for the Legal Profession

### **Best Practices Post-Kettering:**

1. **Prohibition of Generative AI for Citations**
   - Do not use ChatGPT, Claude, Bard, or other generative models for case identification
   - If used, it must be solely for contextualization, followed by comprehensive manual verification

2. **Mandatory Verification**
   - Access Lexis/Westlaw for every citation presented
   - Verify the opinion exists in the cited formats
   - Read the relevant sections to ensure they support the proposition invoked

3. **Signing Protocols**
   - Comprehensively review all filings before signing
   - Especially if the filing was drafted by co-counsel
   - Do not assume citation reliability without personal verification

4. **Voluntary Notification**
   - If an error is discovered, notify the court immediately
   - Do not wait for the opposing party to point it out
   - Silence aggravates culpability under Rule 11 and duty of candor

5. **Due Diligence Documentation**
   - Maintain records of citation verification
   - Document sources used
   - Be transparent about any uncertainty regarding a proposition

## VIII. Procedural Precedent

### A. Requirements for Sanction under Rule 11(c)

The Court has discretion to impose:
- Strike of the offensive filing
- Warning, reprimand, or censure
- Requirement to participate in educational seminars
- Fine payable to the court
- Referral to disciplinary authorities

**Limitation:** Sanctions must be "limited to what suffices to deter repetition of the conduct or comparable conduct by others similarly situated."

### B. Burden of Proof

**Procedure:** Show Cause Order under Rule 11 follows an ex parte evaluation by the court of the challenged filing.

**Standard:** Preponderance of the evidence in the Rule 11 context (more than usual civil standard, less than beyond reasonable doubt criminal).

## IX. Critical Analysis: Strengths and Weaknesses of the Order

### **Strengths:**

1. **Exhaustive Documentation:** The Court meticulously documented each defective citation, establishing an irrefutable record
2. **Consistent Precedent Application:** Wadsworth and Mata were appropriately applied
3. **Proportionality:** Sanctions ($5,000 and $2,500) were appropriately calibrated to the harm
4. **Equity Consideration:** The Court protected Collier (innocent client) by not imposing sanctions "with prejudice"

### **Areas of Possible Challenge:**

1. **Contempt Finding:** The basis for the contempt finding was less developed than the Rule 11 analysis
2. **Severity for Hewitt:** Some would argue $2,500 is excessive given his later joinder to the case
3. **Absence of Quantified Damages:** The Court estimated 75 hours but did not break down specific activities by hour

## X. Legal Conclusions

### **Established Principles:**

1. **Generative AI is a tool, not a researcher:** AI outputs require comprehensive independent verification
2. **"Reasonable inquiry" now includes verification of AI outputs:** Post-Wadsworth, negligence in blindly trusting AI is indefensible
3. **Duty of candor is affirmative:** It is not sufficient to refrain from citing erroneously; attorneys must voluntarily notify of discovered errors
4. **Rule 11 sanctions can be severe:** Referral to state disciplinary counsel, combined with monetary fines and possibility of contempt, creates significant exposure

### **Expected Impact:**

- **Short term:** Greater caution in adopting AI for legal research among federal attorneys
- **Medium term:** Development of AI verification standards by bar associations
- **Long term:** Possible state regulation regarding AI use in practice

## XII. Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q: Can attorneys use ChatGPT for legal research after Kettering?**

A: Technically yes, but with extreme caution. Exhaustive independent verification is mandatory. Most firms have adopted de facto prohibitions.

**Q: What is the difference between Rule 11 and contempt of court?**

A: Rule 11 is a procedural sanction mechanism for certification violations in filings. Contempt is a direct affront to court authority. Both can be imposed simultaneously.

**Q: Should I notify the court if I discover a citation error?**

A: Yes, immediately. The duty of candor requires voluntary notification. Waiting for the opposing party to find it aggravates culpability.

**Q: What is "reasonable inquiry" under Rule 11(b)?**

A: An investigation consistent with professional practice in the specific jurisdiction. In 2026, this includes verification on Westlaw/Lexis of ALL cited cases.

**Q: Can attorneys be referred to disciplinary counsel for Rule 11 violations?**

A: Yes. The Court can refer, although most Rule 11 violations do not result in referral. Kettering was exceptional due to egregious, repeated misconduct.
