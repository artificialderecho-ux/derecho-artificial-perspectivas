# EU AI Act: The 8 Prohibited Artificial Intelligence Practices in Europe

## Introduction: A Regulatory Paradigm Shift

On August 1, 2024, the European Union adopted the **Artificial Intelligence Regulation** (EU AI Act, Regulation 2024/1689), the world's first comprehensive regulatory framework establishing binding legal norms for AI systems. Since **February 2, 2025**, a critical element of this regulation is in force: the **absolute prohibition of 8 specific AI practices** considered of "unacceptable risk."

These are not transparency requirements, they are not flexible compliance regulations, and they are not recommendations. They are **direct prohibitions** carrying sanctions of up to **6% of annual global revenue** for companies in violation.

This analysis examines what these 8 prohibitions mean conceptually, why Europe considered them fundamental for protecting rights, and how they will transform legal practice and technology development.

---

## I. The European Approach: "Unacceptable Risk" as a Foundational Concept

Before analyzing the 8 specific prohibitions, it is essential to understand the **regulatory logic** underlying them.

The EU AI Act adopts a **risk-based approach** that categorizes systems into four levels:

1. **Unacceptable Risk:** Systems that fundamentally violate rights. → **PROHIBITED**
2. **High Risk:** Systems that significantly affect rights. → **Strict requirements**
3. **Transparency Risk:** Systems with limited risks. → **Transparency obligations**
4. **Minimal Risk:** Most systems. → **Not regulated** (but voluntary codes possible)

### What "Unacceptable Risk" Means

An AI system presents "unacceptable risk" when its very nature, regardless of context or intention, violates **fundamental rights enshrined in the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights:**

- Human dignity (Article 1)
- Equality (Article 20)
- Non-discrimination (Article 21)
- Data protection (Article 8)
- Privacy (Article 7)
- Freedom of expression (Article 10)
- Freedom of thought, conscience, and religion (Article 11)
- Presumption of innocence (Article 48)

**The critical distinction:** It is not "Can this system be misused?" but "Is the very nature of the system incompatible with fundamental rights?"

## II. The 8 Prohibited Practices: Conceptual Analysis

### Prohibition 1 & 2: Harmful Manipulation and Exploitation of Vulnerabilities (Art. 5.1.a-b)

#### The Line Between Lawful Persuasion and Prohibited Manipulation

Europe conceptually distinguishes between:

**Lawful persuasion:**
- Information clearly presented in understandable way
- Presentation of options (even biased)
- Marketing, advertising, argumentation
- Allows person to exercise judgment

**Prohibited manipulation:**
- Subliminal techniques (operate below conscious threshold)
- Information deliberately designed to deceive
- Presentation that impedes rational judgment
- Alters behavior in substantial way
- With probable significant harm

**Conceptual example:**
```
LEGAL:
E-commerce platform shows product "A" first
because user bought similar. User sees alternatives.

PROHIBITED:
AI algorithm manipulates visual presentation so
user does not see option "B" cheaper. Or presents
false information about product.
```

#### Exploitation of Vulnerabilities: Specific Protection

The second prohibition recognizes that certain groups—minors, elderly, persons with disabilities, socioeconomically vulnerable population—require additional protection.

It is not that decisions about these groups are "prohibited." It is that systems specifically designed to **exploit their vulnerability** are unacceptable.

**Example:**
```
Allowed: Rigorous but fair credit evaluation system
for elderly, same as any applicant.

Prohibited: System that deliberately exploits
isolation/loneliness of elderly to manipulate
financial decision.
```

### Prohibition 3: Citizen Scoring (Art. 5.1.c)

#### The Most Geopolitically Significant Concept

This prohibition—more than any other—defines a **fundamental difference between the European and Chinese regulatory model.**

**What exactly does it prohibit?**

Systems that:
1. **Evaluate/classify** social behavior OR personal/personality characteristics
2. Over a determined period
3. **Generate consequences** in contexts without direct connection
4. Or result in **unjustified/disproportionate** treatment with gravity

**Why it is prohibited:**

The official guide explains that citizen scoring violates:
- Human dignity (reducing person to "score")
- Equality (differentiated treatment without justification)
- Freedom of thought (pressure to conform to score)
- Rule of Law principles (punishment without due process)

**What it does NOT prohibit:**

- Credit scoring (financial evaluation with direct relation to credit)
- Performance reviews (evaluation with direct relation to work)
- Academic evaluations (evaluation with direct relation to performance)

**Key distinction:**
```
Credit Scoring = Lawful (direct relationship between
financial history and payment capacity)

Social Scoring = Prohibited (social behavior evaluation
generates consequences in housing, work, services access—
contexts without relationship)
```

### Prohibition 4: Individual Crime Prediction Based on Profiling (Art. 5.1.d)

#### Presumption of Innocence in the AI Era

This prohibition operationalizes a fundamental principle: **someone cannot be condemned (or restricted) for who they are, only for what they have done.**

**Prohibited:**
- Prediction that "John will likely commit a crime" based on:
  - Demographic profile
  - Personality characteristics
  - Family history
  - Socioeconomic status
  - Psychological profiling

**Allowed:**
- Prediction based on verifiable objective facts:
  - "John was arrested 3 times for theft" = fact
  - Analysis of that fact = allowed
  - But: final decision requires human evaluation

**Deep implication:**

The prohibition recognizes that AI profiling, even if "statistically correlated" with crime, violates presumption of innocence. It is not sufficient that the algorithm is "accurate"—it is incompatible with fundamental rights.

### Prohibition 5: Non-Selective Facial Data Extraction (Art. 5.1.e)

#### Right to Privacy vs. Mass Surveillance

This prohibition specifically blocks the **creation of facial recognition databases** through mass/non-selective extraction from:
- Internet (social media, Google, etc.)
- CCTV circuits (public space surveillance)

**Why it is prohibited:**

It is not facial recognition per se (it has legitimate uses). It is **massive accumulation of facial data without consent** that enables extensive surveillance.

**Prohibited:**
```
Company extracts all faces from Instagram
→ Creates database of millions of people
→ Without consent
→ For recognition
PROHIBITED
```

**Allowed:**
```
Police specifically searches for face of missing person
→ In city CCTV
→ With targeted/oriented search
→ To locate
ALLOWED
```

### Prohibition 6: Emotion Recognition in Workplaces and Schools (Art. 5.1.f)

#### Human Dignity in Dependent Spaces

This prohibition recognizes specific vulnerability in two contexts:
1. **Workplaces:** Employee is economically dependent; asymmetric power
2. **Schools:** Minors; cognitive development still in progress

**Prohibited:**
- AI system that deduces emotions of employee/student
- To evaluate performance/aptitude
- Even with consent (considered coercive situation)

**Allowed:**
- Emotion recognition in medical context (diagnosis)
- Emotion recognition in security context (detecting crisis)
- With real informed consent

**Conceptual rationale:**

The guide explains that in workplaces/schools, there is "structural power" that makes consent less free. Additionally, inferring emotions is deeply invasive of mental privacy.

### Prohibition 7: Biometric Categorization of Sensitive Characteristics (Art. 5.1.g)

#### Categorical Prohibition of Inference on Protected Identities

This prohibition is absolutely clear: **Biometric data cannot be used to deduce/classify according to:**
- Race
- Political opinions
- Union affiliation
- Religious/philosophical convictions
- Sexual life
- Sexual orientation

**There are no exceptions. Not even for "inclusion" or "research."**

**The prohibition covers:**
- Even if there is no discriminatory intent
- Even if for inclusive purpose
- Even if person consents (consent does not cure)

**Conceptual rationale:**

These are foundational identities of a person. Reducing them to "categories" inferred from biometric data is inherently contrary to dignity. It is not about risk—it is about conceptual incompatibility.

### Prohibition 8: Real-Time Remote Biometric Identification (Art. 5.1.h)

#### The Most Complicated Prohibition (with Limited Exceptions)

This is the only one of the 8 prohibitions with **explicit exceptions.** This suggests gravity: the prohibition is the rule; exceptions are very limited.

**Prohibited:**
Remote biometric identification (facial recognition) in **real time** in **public spaces** for **law enforcement purposes** (e.g., police control)

**Why?**

Capability to massively identify citizens in motion, without them knowing they are being identified, for state control purposes = mass surveillance fundamentally incompatible with freedom and privacy.

**Allowed exceptions (with strict safeguards):**

1. **Targeted search for victims/missing persons**
   - Specific serious crimes (kidnapping, trafficking, sexual exploitation of minors)
   - Deliberate search for known victims OR missing persons
   - Requires: Judicial authorization, usage records, impact assessment

2. **Imminent serious threat**
   - Serious/imminent risk to life
   - Terrorist attacks
   - Requires: Prior authorization, impact assessment

3. **Suspects of serious crimes**
   - Location/identification of specific suspects
   - Of serious crimes (not minor offenses)
   - Requires: Prior authorization, impact assessment, notification

**Fundamental condition:**
All exceptions require **PRIOR authorization** (judicial OR independent administrative). It cannot be used "post-hoc" demonstrating it was necessary.

## III. Implications for the Legal Profession

### A. What's Prohibited in Legal Practice

**Prohibited under EU AI Act:**

1. ❌ Using AI for "client scoring"
   - System that evaluates client and generates restrictions
   - Example: "Doubtful client, reject" based on profiling

2. ❌ Emotional recognition of clients/adversaries
   - Example: System that detects if client is "lying" by analyzing emotions

3. ❌ Prediction that client "will commit crime" without objective facts
   - Based only on profiling/characteristics
   - Even if statistically correlated

4. ❌ Mass facial data extraction for database
   - Example: LinkedIn scraping to create database

5. ❌ Categorization of clients by race/religion/sexual orientation
   - Even inferred from biometric/behavioral data

### B. What's ALLOWED

**Allowed under EU AI Act:**

1. ✅ AI for legal analysis (research)
2. ✅ AI for document organization/categorization
3. ✅ AI for legal decision support—IF based on verifiable objective facts
4. ✅ AI for predicting precedents/case law
5. ✅ AI for client evaluation—IF based on verifiable information (payment capacity, actual record, etc.)

### C. Immediate Practical Changes

**Right now (effective Feb 2, 2025):**

**Recommended internal audit:**
- Review if firm uses AI in any way
- For each use: Does it comply with Art. 5 prohibitions?
- If non-compliant: Cease immediately

**Governance:**
- Establish policy on AI in firm
- Document permitted vs. prohibited uses
- Training for lawyers on compliance

**Procurement:**
- If firm uses third-party AI tools: Verify provider compliance
- Obtain certification from provider

## IV. Implications for Technology and AI Companies

### Prohibitions Shape Innovation

EU AI Act prohibitions have the effect of **completely eliminating certain business models:**

**Business models that die:**
- Social/citizen scoring
- Mass facial data extraction for surveillance
- Emotion recognition in HR/education
- Criminality prediction by profiling

**Business models requiring redesign:**
- Facial recognition → Only allowed with specific/consent-based selection
- Person profiling → Must be based on objective facts, not inferences
- Predictive AI in justice → Requires explicit human oversight

### Mandatory Compliance

**This is not optional. Companies in violation face:**
- Fines up to 6% of annual global revenue
- Prohibition to operate in EU
- Civil liability
- System confiscation

---

## V. Implications for Public Authorities

### Surveillance Restrictions

The EU AI Act **restricts state surveillance capabilities** more than any previous regime:

- Mass facial recognition: **PROHIBITED**
- Citizen scoring: **PROHIBITED**
- Crime prediction by profiling: **PROHIBITED**

**Very limited exceptions:**
- Only with PRIOR judicial authorization
- Only for specific serious crimes
- With notification, records, oversight

This is a **regulatory revolution** vs. existing surveillance capabilities.

## VI. Where It's Heading: Expected Evolution

### Next 12-24 Months

**Expected:**
1. **Enforcement:** EU authorities will begin compliance investigations
2. **Case law:** First cases before courts
3. **Clarification:** CJEU will offer interpretation of complex concepts
4. **Global convergence:** Other countries (UK, Canada, Australia) will adopt similar prohibitions

### Long Term (3-5 Years)

**Trend:**
- Prohibitions will become more specific (not just conceptual)
- New practices will be identified as unacceptable risk
- European standard will become de facto global (Brussels effect)

---

## VII. Conclusion: A Moment of Rupture

The EU AI Act, particularly its prohibitions in Art. 5, represents **rupture with the previous approach to technology regulation.**

Before: Regulation by post-hoc (we detect harm, we regulate)
Now: Preventive regulation (we directly prohibit if incompatible with rights)

This has profound implications:

For the **legal profession:** Immediate audit of any AI use is required. Compliance is not optional.

For **technology companies:** Certain business models are unrecoverable in EU. Redesign is mandatory.

For **society:** It is a clear affirmation that some AI uses are indefensible—no matter how "efficient" or "accurate" they are technically.

The message: **Fundamental rights are non-negotiable. Innovation must respect human dignity.**
