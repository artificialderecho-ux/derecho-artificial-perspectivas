# Bibliographic Review: The Council of Europe Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence, Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law (CETS No. 225)

[Download original document (PDF)](/fuentes/convenio_europea_ia_democracia.pdf)

## 1. Introduction and Strategic Context of the Convention

The Council of Europe Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence, Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law (CETS No. 225) stands as the first legally binding transverse instrument at the global level. Its adoption represents a milestone in international law architecture, specifically designed to harmonize artificial intelligence (AI) governance with the founding values of the organization. Unlike sectoral or merely ethical instruments, CETS No. 225 establishes a mandatory standard that transcends European borders, consolidating a framework of legal certainty against the disruptive nature of algorithmic technologies (Council of Europe, 2024).

The institutional evolution and text genesis process underscore its strategic relevance:

1.  **Institutional Genesis (2021-2022):** The process began with the Ad Hoc Committee on Artificial Intelligence (CAHAI), whose report on elements of a legal framework (December 2021) provided the basis for the Committee of Ministers to instruct the Committee on Artificial Intelligence (CAI) to draft the Convention.

2.  **Global Opening and Observers:** The process was not exclusive to member states; it counted on the active participation of observer states with shared values, such as Argentina, the United States, Japan, Australia, Canada, Israel, Mexico, Peru, Uruguay, Costa Rica, and the Holy See (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 3).

3.  **Multisectoral Governance:** 68 representatives from civil society and industry participated in the negotiations, along with international organizations such as the OECD, UNESCO, and the OSCE, ensuring a technical and pluralistic vision.

4.  **Formal Adoption:** The final text was adopted on May 17, 2024, at the 133rd Session of the Committee of Ministers in Strasbourg, entering into force under a premise of regulatory universality.

This solid institutional foundation precedes the need to precisely define the technical object and jurisdictional scope in which the Convention will operate.

## 2. Operative Definition and Scope of Application: A Critical Analysis

The legal architecture of the Convention rests on the strategic decision to adopt the OECD definition of an AI system. This alignment is not merely technical, but responds to the need to "facilitate efforts to harmonize AI governance globally" (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 23). By prioritizing normative interoperability over lexical originality, the Convention ensures essential legal certainty for transnational actors, avoiding regulatory fragmentation that typically suffocates safe innovation.

It is imperative to analyze the exclusions from scope. While Article 3.2 excludes activities related to national security, it is inferred from the treaty architecture that this exception is not absolute. The exclusion does not apply to "dual-use" systems when used for purposes unrelated to national security, nor does it automatically exempt ordinary law enforcement or migration control activities, unless essential sovereign interests are specifically at stake (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 32). This technical delimitation prevents "national security" from becoming a zone of legal impunity, requiring that any interference with human rights be necessary and proportionate.

This scope delimitation consequently necessitates the implementation of guiding principles that must permeate the entire lifecycle of AI systems.

## 3. Fundamental Principles in the AI Lifecycle

Chapter III of the Convention constitutes its axiological core, presenting a "human-centric" approach that guides technological governance (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 53). These principles are designed to be "purpose-oriented," which gives them vital flexibility to remain relevant against the unpredictable pace of algorithmic innovation.

- **Human Dignity and Individual Autonomy:** This principle acts as a bastion against dehumanization. It prohibits reducing individuals to mere data points and prevents the use or manipulation of systems that undermine human agency through deception or undue anthropomorphization of the machine.

- **Transparency and Oversight:** The Convention differentiates between "explainability" (the capacity to give understandable reasons for an outcome) and "interpretability" (access to internal functioning). It is critical to note that the text acknowledges current "technological limitations," admitting that the path to an outcome is often not fully accessible even to designers (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 61).

- **Responsibility and Accountability:** The obligation is established that AI actions must be attributable to identifiable natural or legal persons. This traceability is the *sine qua non* condition for remedy of damages and democratic control of technology.

- **Equality and Non-Discrimination:** The Explanatory Report's analysis is rigorous in distinguishing between social bias (reflection of historical inequalities) and **technical bias**, the latter originating from errors in the learning parameters and functioning of the algorithm (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 75).

The transition from axiological theory to legal practice requires that these principles be backed by tangible remedy mechanisms when systemic failures occur.

## 4. Remedy Mechanisms, Safeguards, and Risk Management

The effectiveness of the Convention is measured by its capacity to correct the information asymmetry between the developer and the citizen. Articles 14 and 15 are not mere recommendations, but the "effectiveness guarantee" of the legal framework. However, it is vital to specify that these remedy obligations are activated only when an AI system has a "significant effect" or "significant impact" on the enjoyment of human rights (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 101-102). This threshold prevents the legal system from being saturated with claims about trivial uses of technology.

For systems exceeding this threshold, Article 16 imposes a risk management methodology based on the following evaluation criteria:

- **Severity:** Magnitude of potential harm to the affected right.

- **Probability:** Estimated frequency of risk occurrence.

- **Duration:** Temporal persistence of adverse effects.

- **Reversibility:** Technical and legal possibility of restoring the situation prior to the harm (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 108).

A pillar of sovereign control is Article 16.4, which empowers Parties to impose **moratoria or prohibitions** on AI uses considered "incompatible" with the Convention's values. This incompatibility manifests when a system presents unacceptable risks that cannot be mitigated through technical or procedural safeguards, thereby protecting the integrity of the Rule of Law against repressive technological applications.

## 5. Implementation, Monitoring, and International Cooperation

The Conference of the Parties (COP) constitutes the dynamic supervisory body of the treaty. Its function transcends the administrative, acting as a consultative forum for the interpretation of the Convention and the exchange of best practices. A fundamental detail for international governance is that the COP's rules of procedure must be adopted by **consensus**, ensuring that no decision on treaty implementation lacks the collective endorsement of the Parties (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 133).

The monitoring system is supported by two integrity requirements:

1.  **Reporting Obligations (Article 24):** Parties must periodically report on measures adopted, especially regarding the flexibility granted in the private sector.

2.  **Independence of Oversight (Article 26):** The Convention requires the creation of national mechanisms operating under strict "functional independence."

"Such bodies must exercise their functions with functional independence from the executive and legislative branches, possessing the powers, expertise, and resources necessary to fulfill their tasks effectively and impartially" (Council of Europe, 2024, para. 142).

This structure ensures that compliance does not depend on the political discretion of the incumbent government, establishing a basis for monitoring to be not a mere formality, but a process of continuous evaluation.

## 6. Conclusions, Research Gaps, and Future Directions

CETS No. 225 achieves a sophisticated balance between rights protection and the promotion of "safe innovation." By centering its regulation on effects on human rights rather than prescribing static technical specifications, the Convention demonstrates "future-proof" architecture. However, critical review identifies areas of normative tension requiring immediate attention.

**Identified Gaps:**

1.  **Fragmentation in the Private Sector:** The flexibility of Article 3.1.b, while facilitating diplomatic consensus, creates a risk of "regulatory arbitrage," where the level of citizen protection depends on the specific declarations of each State, weakening uniformity against global technological actors.

2.  **Opacity in National Security Exclusions:** Despite the Explanatory Report's clarifications, the general exclusion of Article 3.2 leaves a concerning margin of discretion regarding how to independently monitor AI systems in intelligence contexts, where "national interest" is usually invoked expansively.

3.  **Standardization of Risk Indicators:** The Convention delegates to Parties the definition of criteria such as "severity" and "reversibility" (para. 108). There is a critical need for academic and normative research to standardize these indicators, preventing risk assessment from being subjective or disparate across jurisdictions.

Ultimately, CETS No. 225 reaffirms a fundamental principle of contemporary international law: technological progress is a means, not an end, and its legitimacy resides exclusively in its capacity to operate under the primacy of human dignity and the strengthening of democracy.
