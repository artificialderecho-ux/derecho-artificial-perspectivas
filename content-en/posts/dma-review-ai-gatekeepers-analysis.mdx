## I. FUNDAMENTAL LEGAL QUESTION

How should the regulatory framework of the European Union's Digital Markets Act (DMA) evolve to address emerging competitive and governance risks from frontier artificial intelligence models, generative services, and vertically integrated gatekeeper platform ecosystems, while balancing competition protection, user rights, and innovation capacity?

---

## II. INSTITUTIONAL AND PROCEDURAL CONTEXT

### A. First DMA Review Framework

**Legal Obligation (Article 53 DMA):**

The European Commission must review and evaluate the DMA and report its findings to the European Parliament, Council, and European Economic and Social Committee **every three years**, with the first report mandatory before **May 3, 2026**.

**Implementation Status at Consultation Time:**

- **Seven gatekeepers designated** (Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Booking, Meta, Microsoft, ByteDance)
- **23 Core Platform Services (CPS) designated**
- **Applicable obligations since March 7, 2024** (less than 2 years of implementation)

**Critical Legal Implication:** Contributions reflect experiences of **less than two years** of active implementation, limiting empirical evidence of real impact.

### B. Structured Consultation Process

**Three consultative components:**

1. **Targeted Consultation (July 3 - August 26, 12 weeks)**
   - 12 specific questions
   - Possibility of extensive written submissions
   - 319 responses received

2. **Call for Evidence (August 26 - ?, 4 weeks)**
   - Qualitative and quantitative input
   - 63 responses received

3. **AI-Specific Consultation (August 26 - ?, 4 weeks)**
   - Questions on AI services, challenges, industry evolution
   - Potential gatekeeper advantages in AI
   - 96 responses received

**Respondent Population (478 total):**

- ✓ All designated gatekeepers except one (Microsoft did not formally respond)
- ✓ Business users, SMEs, and trade associations (majority)
- ✓ Civil society organizations (significant number)
- ✓ Academics and lawyers
- ✓ Consumer and citizen groups

**Anonymity:** Slightly over 15% responded anonymously.

## III. LEGAL ISSUES IDENTIFIED BY RESPONDENTS

### A. Expansion of Core Platform Service List

#### Position 1: "Existing CPSs"

**Advocates:**

Academics, SMEs, civil society, some business users

**Main Legal Argument:**

AI and Large Language Model functionalities may already be considered as covered by existing CPS categories:
- Search engines (with integrated AI functionalities)
- App stores (distributing AI services)
- Operating systems (hosting AI virtual assistants)
- Intermediation services
- Virtual assistants

**Regulatory Implication:** No DMA amendment required, only interpretive clarification.

#### Position 2: "New Standalone CPS(s)"

**Advocates:**

Various academics, consultancies, some trade associations

**Legal Argument:**

New CPS categories specifically required for:
- Generative AI models as services
- AI chatbots
- Content synthesis services

**Proposal:** Potential harmonization with AI Act definitions.

**Regulatory Implication:** Requires formal DMA amendment to create new CPS categories.

#### Position 3: Caution ("Wait and See")

**Advocates:**

Gatekeepers and affiliated entities

**Legal Argument:**

- Lack of clear evidence of "gatekeeping effects" in AI markets
- Risk of innovation stranglehold
- Risk of market distortion
- Risk of administrative overload
- AI markets still **immature** (too early to expand regulation)

**Regulatory Implication:** Status quo; do not expand DMA to AI without empirical evidence of anticompetitive effects.

#### Legal Convergence Analysis

**Key Interpretive Question:** Do generative AI services (e.g., ChatGPT embedded in Google Search) constitute a "natural extension" of existing CPS or a **sui generis** CPS requiring separate designation?

**Legal Resolution Criteria:**

1. **Functionality:** Does AI service have distinctive functionality from host CPS?
2. **Gatekeeping effects:** Does it create different abusive exploitation opportunities from base CPS?
3. **Market tipping:** Is there risk of irreversible consolidation?
4. **User lock-in:** Does it generate AI-specific user blocking effects?

### B. Cloud Services: The Most Significant Regulatory Gap

#### Identified Problem

**Broad coalition of respondents (SMEs, business users, civil society):**

Cloud services (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS) are **listed as potential CPS** but **none have been designated** despite:

1. **Critical importance for AI:** Training and deployment of models require massive computational capacity
2. **Dominant hyperscale providers:** Amazon AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud
3. **Lock-in problems:** Technological dependencies difficult to break
4. **Data portability challenges:** Practical impossibility of migration

#### Respondents' Argument for Cloud Designation

**Competitive Rationale:**

AI developers are "locked in" to specific cloud providers, preventing true contestability in AI markets.

**Regulatory Argument:**

Without cloud obligations (interoperability, data access, non-discrimination), DMA protections for AI will be **ineffective**.

#### Gatekeepers' Argument Against Cloud Designation

**Position:** Market dynamics substantially differ between:
- IaaS (infrastructure, commoditized)
- PaaS (platform, moderate competition)
- SaaS (software, more competition)

**Duplication Risk:** DMA should not duplicate Data Act, GDPR, DSA protections.

#### Legal Analysis: Critical Regulatory Gap

WITHOUT cloud designation, DMA obligations for AI remain limited to:
- OS interoperability with AI services (Art. 6.7)
- Search data access for AI models (Art. 6.11)

BUT: Training and AI deployment require cloud → gap unclosed.

**Implicit Recommendation from Respondents:** Commission must decide if cloud is a **separate CPS** or extend obligations to cloud as an **essential input** for AI markets.

## IV. DMA OBLIGATIONS AND IDENTIFIED DEFICIENCIES FOR AI

### A. Interoperability: Cross-Cutting Priority

#### Specific Problem in AI Context

**Respondents emphasize:**

"Interoperability by design" is **critical** to ensure that:

1. **Third-party AI services** can effectively integrate with CPS (OS, search, etc.)
2. **No privileged vertical integration** of gatekeeper's own AI assistants
3. **Real competition** in generative services markets

**Identified Deficiency Examples:**

- AI assistant deeply integrated into OS (default, no friction)
- Third-party AI services require multiple steps or experience technical limitations
- APIs for third-party AI services have poor documentation or discriminatory conditions

#### Position on Technical Standards

**Respondent Consensus:**
- Well-defined technical standards are **necessary**
- "Soft" interoperability (bilateral agreements) is insufficient
- **Public standards** required to ensure scalability

#### Applicable Legal Obligation

**Article 6.7 DMA (Operating systems interoperability):**

"large gatekeepers shall implement, in a transparent, non-discriminatory and secure manner, effective procedures to enable providers of services in the digital sector to directly request, on an individual basis, the interoperability of important features of the core platform service with the software and hardware provided by such service provider"

**Interpretation for AI:**

Does an AI chatbot integrated into OS constitute an "important feature"? Ambiguity allows variation in interpretation.

### B. Self-Preferencing in Generative AI Context

#### Identified Problem

**Position of SMEs, business users, civil society:**

Gatekeepers with their own generative models are privileging their AI services over competitors:

1. **Deep integration** into user experiences
2. **Default settings** privileging own services
3. **Privileged data access** for training own models
4. **Vertical bundling:** Forcing use of own AI service with access to other services

#### Gatekeepers' Position

Design options reflect **legitimate considerations** of:
- Privacy
- Security
- User experience
- Functional co-integration

#### Legal Analysis: Self-Preferencing Extension

**Article 6.5 DMA (Self-preferencing):**

"Gatekeepers shall not treat the goods, services or content offered by the gatekeeper itself or a third party differently from other third parties' goods, services or content"

**Interpretive Question for AI:**

Does Art. 6.5 apply when gatekeeper vertically integrates AI service **within** the OS, creating friction for competitors?

**Expected Likely Jurisprudence:**

- ✓ Yes, it applies (expansive approach)
- If gatekeeper integrates its own AI assistant into OS without allowing competitors interoperable access
- This constitutes discriminatory treatment

- ✗ No, does not directly apply (restrictive approach)
- Integration of own service into OS is "quasi-monopoly" of gatekeeper over OS
- Art. 6.5 applies to "goods, services or content" distributed on the platform, not to OS features themselves

### C. Data Access for AI Model Training

#### Articulated Problem

**Respondents (AI developers, SMEs, academics):**

Access to high-quality data is a **critical input** for training competitive models. Gatekeepers with positions in key data services (search, social networks, e-commerce) have **structural advantage**:

1. **Access to own user data**
2. **Proprietary datasets** not available to competitors
3. **Cumulative advantage:** Own data → better model → more users → more data

#### Applicable DMA Obligation

**Article 6.11 DMA (Data access):**

"Gatekeepers shall, on request of a business user, provide the business user, in a timely and effective manner, upon request with access to data generated through the activity of the business user, including in particular those generated by the users of that business user, in a usable, portable, high-quality and machine-readable format"

**Interpretation for AI:**

Should "access to training data" be included so third parties can develop competitive AI models?

**Respondents' Position:**
- ✓ Yes, should be expanded to include data for ML/AI
- User-generated data on platform must be accessible for model training
- Could include "model parameters" or derived "embeddings"

**Gatekeepers' Position:**
- ✗ No, Art. 6.11 applies to individual user data
- Should not require access to aggregated training data
- Security and privacy risks

#### Legal Analysis: Insufficiency of Art. 6.11 for AI

Article 6.11 **protects access to specific individual user data**, not **aggregated training data**.

This creates a **gap:** Gatekeeper can legally:
- Deny access to aggregated historical datasets
- Deny access to trained embeddings
- Deny access to model parameters derived from user data

**Implication:** Respondents argue Art. 6.11 requires amendment to address AI.

### D. Cloud and Hyperscaler Dependence

#### Convergent Problem

Most AI developers are **locked in** to one or few hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, GCP).

**Identified Anticompetitive Effects:**

1. **Vendor lock-in:** Prohibitive switching costs
2. **Coercive bundling:** Bundling of AI models with cloud services
3. **Data dependencies:** Training data resides in hyperscaler cloud
4. **Pricing power:** Hyperscalers can impose premium prices for AI capacity

#### Respondents' Legal Conclusion

**Without cloud designation as CPS**, DMA protections for AI will be **fundamentally limited**.

## V. ANALYSIS OF POSITIONS ON WHISTLEBLOWER AND ENFORCEMENT

### A. Demand for Robust Reporting Mechanisms

#### Respondents' Observation

**Massive asymmetric information** between Commission and gatekeepers:
- Gatekeepers have complete information about their systems
- Commission must detect non-compliance without real visibility
- Business users and gatekeeper employees have valuable information but no protections

#### Respondents' Proposals

1. **Structured and confidential channels** for reporting issues
2. **Whistleblower-type protections** for employees reporting non-compliance
3. **Standardized templates** for evidence submissions
4. **Complaint mechanism** similar to antitrust procedures

#### Legal Analysis: Parallel with California SB 53

Note parallel with **California SB 53** (previously examined):
- California requires whistleblower protections for employees of frontier AI developers
- DMA Review suggests similar mechanisms for DMA enforcement

**Legal Implication:** Global convergence toward whistleblower protections in AI/platform regulation.

## VI. REAL VS. THEORETICAL IMPACT ASSESSMENT

### A. Positive Results Identified

**Respondents recognize successful implementations:**

1. **Browser and app choice:** More consistent choice opportunities
2. **Uninstallability:** Ability to uninstall default applications
3. **Alternative app marketplaces:** Emergence of alternatives (e.g., on Apple's iOS)
4. **Freedom in app distribution:** More free contracts and in-app purchases
5. **Data portability solutions:** New solutions implemented

### B. Persistent Deficiencies

**But respondents also report:**

1. **Gatekeeper influence persists:** Despite regulation, gatekeeper advantage continues
2. **Complex consent flows:** UI design makes exercising rights difficult
3. **Limited interoperability:** In practice, interoperability works poorly
4. **Restrictive app store conditions:** Limitations continue despite regulation
5. **Persistent preferential treatment:** Gatekeeper's own services receive unjust advantage

#### Analytical Conclusion

**DMA effectiveness depends primarily on ENFORCEMENT**, not norms themselves.

Respondents converge: Without robust enforcement, DMA will be **symbolic**.

---

## VII. AI-SPECIFIC CONSULTATION: KEY THEMES

### A. AI-Powered Services Interoperability

#### Respondents' Consensus

Interoperability between:
- **Operating systems** (OS host)
- **Third-party AI services** (chatbots, generative models)

Is **critical** for contestability.

#### Vertical Integration Risks

Gatekeepers with:
- Their own OS
- Their own AI model deeply integrated into OS
- Vertical ecosystem

Create **lock-in effects** preventing effective competition.

### B. Proprietary Datasets and Differentiated Access

#### Articulated Problem

Gatekeepers with access to:
- User data on their platforms
- Unique training datasets

Obtain **structural advantage** in AI that is **unassailable** for competitors without that data position.

#### Proposed Solutions

1. **Expansion of Art. 6.11** to training data
2. **Fair licensing obligations** for critical datasets
3. **Data interoperability** for AI models

### C. Cloud Hyperscaler Dependence

#### Critical Problem

AI training and deployment require massive cloud capacity.

**Lock-in to hyperscalers** = **blockade to competitive AI development**.

Without cloud designation as CPS, DMA cannot address this.

---

## VIII. GATEKEEPERS' POSITION IN CONSULTATION: DMA CRITIQUE

### A. Gatekeepers' Arguments

1. **Innovation chilling:**
   - DMA obligations inhibit risk-taking, R&D investment
   - Regulatory uncertainty damages global competitiveness

2. **Proportionality concerns:**
   - Obligations are too broad, inflexible
   - Compliance costs not justified by benefits

3. **Technical legitimacy:**
   - Many design choices reflect legitimate privacy, security
   - DMA should not intervene in technical decisions

4. **Clarity needed, not enforcement:**
   - Problem is interpretative uncertainty
   - Solution is regulatory guidance, not more enforcement
   - Flexible dialogue preferred over formal procedures

### B. Academic Critique of Gatekeeper Position

**Implicit response from other respondents:**

- ✗ Gatekeepers have had **voluntary opportunity** to conform
- ✗ Documented non-compliance suggests **non-voluntary compliance**
- ✗ "Uncertainty" is pretext for resisting regulation
- ✓ Vigorous enforcement is the only means to obtain real compliance

## IX. COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS: DMA vs. AI ACT vs. GDPR

### A. Regulatory Overlap in AI Context

**Three relevant regulatory frameworks:**

| Aspect | GDPR | AI Act | DMA |
|--------|------|--------|-----|
| **Scope** | Personal data | AI systems (risk) | Digital markets |
| **Mechanism** | Privacy by design | Risk-based regulation | Competition |
| **Enforcement** | National DPAs | Competent authorities | European Commission |
| **AI applicable?** | Yes (data use) | Yes (all) | Partially (AI CPS) |

### B. Coherence vs. Conflict

**Potential conflict:**

- GDPR requires **data minimization**
- AI Act requires **dataset documentation**
- DMA requires **data access for competition**

Respondents request **coherence** to avoid operational impossibilities.

### C. Data Act (in development)

**Data Act** also requires **data access under certain terms**.

**Overlap:** Data Act + DMA data access requirements = accumulation of obligations.

**Respondents request:** Clarity on interplay between Data Act and DMA.

## X. LEGAL CONCLUSIONS AND IMPLICIT RECOMMENDATIONS

### A. DMA Review Compass

**Four possible directions:**

#### Option 1: Aggressive Expansion ("Pro-competition" Position)

- Designate cloud as CPS
- Create new CPS categories for generative AI
- Expand Art. 6.11 to training data
- Strengthen enforcement

**Supported by:** SMEs, civil society, academics, some business users

#### Option 2: Operational Clarification ("Pragmatic" Position)

- Keep DMA fundamentally the same
- Issue detailed guidance on AI application
- Improve enforcement mechanisms
- Increase resources

**Supported by:** Some academics, some business users

#### Option 3: Minimalist Approach ("Pro-innovation" Position)

- DMA is sufficient; do not expand
- Wait for AI markets to mature
- Rely on "interplay" with AI Act
- Reduce prescriptiveness

**Supported by:** Gatekeepers, affiliated entities

#### Option 4: Regulatory Coordination ("Coherence" Position)

- Not necessarily expand DMA
- But clarify interplay with AI Act, Data Act, DSA, GDPR
- Create coherent framework for regulatory ecosystem
- Ensure non-conflict

**Supported by:** Various stakeholders

### B. Decision Urgency on AI

**Critical question for Commission:**

Are DMA protections **sufficient** for AI as an emerging field, or is **specific expansion** required?

**Critical legal timing:**

- AI markets are **currently forming**
- Next DMA review is in **2029**
- If waited, gatekeeper position consolidation may be **irreversible**

### C. Mandatory Report (May 3, 2026)

Commission must decide if DMA, as currently written, is **fit-for-purpose** for the AI Era, or requires legislative update.

**Options are not neutral:** Inaction allows gatekeeper consolidation in AI.
