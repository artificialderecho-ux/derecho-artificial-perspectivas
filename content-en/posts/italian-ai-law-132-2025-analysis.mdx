# Italian Law 132/2025 on Artificial Intelligence: Legal Analysis

## Introduction

Law 23 September 2025, no. 132 represents a fundamental milestone in the regulation of artificial intelligence at the Italian national level. This norm establishes a comprehensive regulatory framework that harmonizes domestic law with Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act). This legal analysis examines the ontological foundations, institutional architecture, transparency standards and fundamental rights protection, as well as sectoral regulation and the liability regime established by the norm.

---

## CHAPTER I: ONTOLOGICAL FOUNDATIONS AND HARMONIZATION FRAMEWORK

### 1.1. Legal Definitions and the Technical-Legal Bridge

Law no. 132/2025 constitutes the cornerstone of the internal regulatory system, establishing a relationship of subordination and complementarity with Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act). Article 2, paragraph 1, acts as a technical-legal bridge by delegating the conceptualization of AI systems and AI models directly to the definitions contained in Article 3, points 1) and 63) of the European Regulation, in order to safeguard legal certainty in an accelerated innovation environment.

This dynamic reference technique ensures that any technical update in the Union framework is automatically integrated into the Italian legal system, avoiding premature obsolescence of the norm before technological progress. However, doctrine warns that this structural dependence forces operators to perform a stratified reading that distinguishes between system architecture and model functionality, especially in general-purpose systems that can be integrated into various applications.

### 1.2. The Nature of Data and Fragmentation Risks

Unlike the previous technical reference, the Italian legislator has chosen to introduce an autonomous definition of data in Article 2, comma 1, letter b), understanding it as any digital representation of acts, facts, or information. This all-encompassing construction generates significant regulatory tension with the architecture of the AI Act, which avoids generic definitions in favor of highly specialized categories, such as training, validation, and testing data.

The introduction of a divergent national notion could derive in opaque algorithms if representativeness and accuracy criteria are not aligned with Union quality metrics. Data governance must ensure that training dataset traceability respects the accountability principle, mitigating structural biases before the AI system is put into service.

### 1.3. The Anthropocentrism Principle and Technological Sovereignty

Article 1 of Law no. 132/2025 establishes as a primary purpose the promotion of AI in a human-centered dimension, placing human self-determination as an insurmountable limit to computational development. This principle materializes in Article 3 as a Grundnorm requiring that AI system use respect fundamental rights and freedoms enshrined in the Constitution and the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights.

The law links national competitiveness and technological sovereignty to ethical development that is not limited to market efficiency, but guarantees transparency and human oversight at each phase of the system lifecycle. This vision seeks to transform meta-legal and ethical principles into technical legality parameters, requiring that processes be verifiable and appropriate to detected risk levels.

## CHAPTER II: GOVERNANCE ARCHITECTURE AND NATIONAL STRATEGY

### 2.1. National Authorities for AI: The AgID and ACN Binomial

The institutional framework established by Law no. 132/2025 for managing artificial intelligence in Italy is fundamentally articulated through Article 20, which designates the Agency for Digital Italy (AgID) and the Agency for National Cybersecurity (ACN) as the National Authorities for AI. This institutional binomial distributes technical and operational supervision competencies: AgID assumes the functions of notifying body, handling the evaluation and accreditation of conformity assessment bodies, while ACN is invested with market surveillance and cybersecurity powers.

However, this centralized governance structure has sparked doctrinal debates regarding its full autonomy from the Executive, given that European law (AI Act) requires rigorous functional independence to avoid "regulatory capture" and contingent political pressures. Likewise, the law integrates into this ecosystem pre-existing sectoral authorities, such as Bank of Italy, CONSOB, and IVASS, for the supervision of AI systems in financial and insurance sectors, ensuring that surveillance is adjusted to specific risk levels of each sector.

### 2.2. Interministerial Coordination Committee and Strategic Management

National governance is complemented with a high-level political coordination dimension located in the Presidency of the Council of Ministers. According to Article 19, comma 6, the Interministerial Coordination Committee is established, chaired by the President of the Council or the delegated Authority for technological innovation. This body, of choral and multidisciplinary nature, integrates holders of strategic portfolios such as Economy, Business, University, Health, and Justice, among others.

Its primary function is the exercise of strategic direction that transcends mere technical management, orienting AI implementation toward public interest and the common good. This centripetal drive toward the Presidency seeks to harmonize sectoral policies and ensure that algorithmic development does not occur in sealed compartments, but under a unitary vision that guarantees system coherence and fundamental rights protection.

### 2.3. National AI Strategy (2024-2026): The Italian "Third Way"

Article 19, paragraph 1 mandates the biennial elaboration and updating of the National Artificial Intelligence Strategy (2024-2026), which is configured as the roadmap for technological sovereignty and innovation promotion. This strategy is doctrinally positioned as a "third" regulatory and economic way, seeking to distance itself both from extreme American liberalism, often criticized for its opacity, and the centralized surveillance characteristic of the Chinese model.

The Italian approach, aligned with the European Union's human-centered perspective, prioritizes collaborative research promotion among companies, public bodies, and the scientific community, especially through the creation of experimentation spaces or sandboxes. The objective is to promote an equitable and competitive digital market that reduces informational asymmetries and guarantees that opaque algorithms do not erode democratic debate or citizen autonomy of will.

## CHAPTER III: TRANSPARENCY, DATA, AND PERSON PROTECTION

### 3.1. Transparency and Language: The Qualified Information Duty

The legal transparency regime for the use of artificial intelligence systems is articulated on the imperative communication obligation to the end user, according to Article 4 of Law no. 132/2025. This provision requires that all information related to data processing through AI be provided using clear, simple, and exhaustive language, with the objective of guaranteeing effective cognoscibility of risks and enabling the user to exercise the right to object.

This national framework integrates with Article 50 of Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act), which imposes on providers the duty to design systems so that natural persons are informed they are interacting with an AI, except in public security scenarios or when the nature of the service makes it evident. Doctrine warns that this transparency must not be merely formalistic or technocratic, restricted to experts, but must ensure substantial intelligibility of the decision-making process to safeguard the right to effective judicial protection.

### 3.2. Data Governance and Quality: Standards for High-Risk Systems

The integrity of algorithmic development is subordinated, according to Article 3, paragraph 2 of the Italian regulation, to the guarantee and supervision of correctness, reliability, and quality of data and processes used. This requirement is directly connected with Article 10 of the AI Act, which regulates data governance for systems classified at elevated risk levels.

Providers have the obligation to use training, validation, and testing datasets that are pertinent, representative, and, to the extent technically possible, free from errors. Governance must include prior identification and mitigation of structural biases (bias) that may result in algorithmic discrimination, especially when special categories of personal data are processed.

### 3.3. Vulnerable Groups Protection: Disability and Minors

Article 7 of Law no. 132/2025 establishes reinforced guarantees for persons with disabilities, demanding full access to AI systems and their functionalities under equal conditions, following the parameters of the United Nations Convention of 2006. The legislator explicitly prohibits any form of discrimination or harm derived from technological barriers, promoting AI as an instrument of autonomy and not exclusion.

In parallel, protection of minors is structured in Article 4, paragraph 4, limiting access to AI technologies for minors under fourteen years to consent from those exercising parental responsibility. These protections align with the absolute prohibition established in Article 5, paragraph 1, letter b) of the AI Act.

## CHAPTER IV: SECTORAL REGULATION: HEALTH, WORK, AND EDUCATION

### 4.1. AI in the Healthcare System: Guarantees and Clinical Challenges

The application of artificial intelligence in the health sector is governed by a logic of technical subordination to medical judgment, according to Article 7 of Law no. 132/2025. AI systems operate exclusively as support instruments in prevention, diagnosis, and treatment processes, prohibiting any automation that displaces the final decision from the health professional.

The legislator imposes a rigorous quality standard (Art. 7.6), requiring periodic verification of model reliability to mitigate errors that compromise patient safety. Article 8 enables processing of special categories data for scientific research under public interest regime, always guaranteeing anonymization or use of synthetic data.

Systematic integration is completed with Article 10, which incorporates AI into the Electronic Health Record (FSE) infrastructure, under technical supervision of the National Agency for Regional Health Services (AGENAS) and cybersecurity control by ACN.

### 4.2. The Labor Market: Transparency and Algorithmic Power

The regulation of AI in the work environment, articulated in Article 11 of the national law, integrates with the protective framework of the Workers' Statute and the so-called "Transparency Decree" (d.lgs. 152/1997). The employer or client has the imperative obligation to inform workers about the use of automated decision-making or monitoring systems, guaranteeing that these do not violate human dignity or the service provider's privacy.

Article 11.3 emphasizes that AI use must ensure respect for fundamental rights, expressly prohibiting any type of algorithmic discrimination based on gender, ethnic origin, or political convictions. Article 12 establishes the Observatory on AI systems adoption in the labor world, a multidisciplinary body tasked with defining training strategies and monitoring risk levels.

### 4.3. Intellectual Professions and the Qualified Information Duty

The exercise of intellectual professions under the aid of algorithmic technologies is subject to the "prevalence of intellectual work" clause established in Article 13 of Law no. 132/2025. AI systems may only be used for instrumental and support activities, maintaining the professional as the sole legal responsible for the service.

In the legal profession, the attorney cannot delegate to the system the interpretation of law or the evaluation of evidence, must verify the accuracy of any output. Article 13.2 introduces a new deontological duty: mandatory communication to the client about AI system use through clear, simple, and exhaustive language.

## CHAPTER V: AI IN PUBLIC ADMINISTRATION AND JUSTICE

### 5.1. Administrative Efficiency: The "Human in the Loop" Principle and Financial Invariance

The deployment of artificial intelligence systems in Italian Public Administration is governed by the imperative of increasing operational efficiency, according to Article 14 of Law no. 132/2025. Technological integration must guarantee cognoscibility of system functioning and traceability of its use, enabling interested parties to understand the logic of decision-making processes.

The norm prescribes that AI use be strictly instrumental and supportive of providential activity, safeguarding the autonomy and decision-making power of the human official. However, implementation is subject to a financial invariance clause (Art. 14.4), which obliges administrations to adapt their technical and human resources without additional budget allocations.

### 5.2. The Magistrate and Algorithmic Decision: The Exclusive Humanity Reserve

Article 15 of the national law establishes an insurmountable humanity reserve: the interpretation of law, evaluation of facts and evidence, and adoption of all judicial measures are exclusive competencies of the magistrate. This regime aligns with the classification of judicial AI systems as high-risk in Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act).

While legal analytics and predictive justice tools can reinforce legal predictability, their stochastic nature carries the risk of jurisprudence "crystallization." There is risk of the "pungolo gentile" (gentle nudge), where algorithmic output cognitively conditions the judge.

### 5.3. CSM and Training: Toward a Digital Jurisdiction Culture

AI governance in justice requires institutional synergy between the Ministry of Justice and the Superior Council of the Judiciary (CSM). Article 15.4 mandates the Minister to promote didactic activities aimed at basic and advanced digital training of magistrates.

Training must not be limited to technical skill, but must integrate ethical and legal literacy that enables the magistrate to audit support systems and detect possible "hallucinations" generated by large language models.

## CHAPTER VI: INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY, LIABILITY, AND SANCTIONING REGIME

### 6.1. Copyright in the Generative Era: The Humanity Reserve

Law no. 132/2025 introduces substantial modifications to intellectual property architecture to respond to the generative artificial intelligence challenge. Article 25 modifies Law no. 633/1941, specifying that copyright protection is exclusively reserved for works of creative character that constitute a result of human intellectual work.

This provision reinforces the anthropocentrism principle, preventing algorithms from acquiring ownership of moral or economic rights. Article 70-septies regulates text and data mining (TDM) activities, allowing reproduction and extraction of data from legitimately accessible works for AI training.

### 6.2. AI as "Instrumentum Sceleris": Criminal Protection and Aggravating Circumstances

The Italian legislator has configured AI use in the criminal field as a potential instrument for committing crimes. Article 26 introduces Article 612-quater to the Criminal Code, typifying the illicit dissemination of AI-falsified content (especially deepfakes) that induces error about its genuineness.

A new common aggravating circumstance is established in Article 61, number 11-decies, for crimes committed through AI systems. In financial markets, Article 26 significantly aggravates penalties for market manipulation when the act is performed through AI systems.

### 6.3. Civil Liability and Burden of Proof: Legislative Delegation

The technical complexity of AI systems has led the legislator to delegate to the Government the adaptation of the civil liability regime. According to Article 24, paragraph 5, letter d), the Executive must define a discipline regulating the distribution of the burden of proof in cases of damages caused by AI systems.

Doctrine warns of the risk that algorithmic opacity derives in "positional" objective liability for the programmer or provider. Delegated legislation must balance damaged party protection with operator legal certainty.

### 6.4. Final Summary

Law no. 132/2025 presents itself as the closing norm of the national system, seeking to harmonize technological development with fundamental rights protection. The effectiveness of this framework will depend on the capacity of national authorities (AgID and ACN) to supervise risk levels and the adequacy of future legislative decrees to the reality of stochastic and constantly evolving technology.

---

## Conclusion

Italian Law 132/2025 represents a comprehensive and balanced approach to artificial intelligence regulation, establishing robust guarantees for fundamental rights protection while promoting responsible technological innovation. Its pyramidal structure reflects a sophisticated legal vision that recognizes both the opportunities and risks of algorithmic technologies.

The harmonization with the European AI Act, mediated by dynamic reference techniques, demonstrates a legislative strategy that seeks to maintain coherence within the community framework while safeguarding national regulatory autonomy. The successful application of this norm will depend on the capacity of institutions to develop legislative decrees that adapt general principles to the technical complexity of stochastic and opaque systems.
