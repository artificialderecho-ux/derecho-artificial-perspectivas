# Danish Copyright Reform Bill: Protection Against Deepfakes, Digital Simulacra, and AI

<Alert className="mb-6 bg-blue-50 border-blue-200">
  <AlertDescription className="text-blue-900">
    **Document:** Forslag (Bill) to amend the Copyright Act (Ophavsret) | **Effective:** March 31, 2026 | **Political Agreement:** June 26, 2025 | **Ministry:** Kulturministeriet (Ministry of Culture of Denmark)
  </AlertDescription>
</Alert>

## I. FUNDAMENTAL LEGAL QUESTION

What is the Danish and international regulatory framework governing legal protection against AI-generated digital simulacra of public figures, artists, and ordinary citizens, and what are the obligations and rights emerging for digital platforms, creators, and society at large?

---

## II. CONTEXT: REGULATORY AND TECHNOLOGICAL CRISIS

### A. Problem Diagnosis: The Deepfake Technology Acceleration

The Danish bill identifies a **contemporary digital governance crisis** characterized by:

**1. Massive Availability of Deepfake Generation Tools**

The Danish legislator recognizes that "easy-to-use tools available on the internet, including applications that can be downloaded on phones, can quickly create high-quality manipulated content." This accessibility has transformed what was previously a marginal risk into a **systemic threat**.

**2. Growing Impossibility of Distinguishing Authentic from Fake Content**

With "the rapid evolution of AI and video, audio, and image manipulation capabilities, it is almost impossible to distinguish authentic material from manipulated." This "practical impossibility" of differentiation generates **public trust erosion** in information.

**3. Viral Propagation Speed in Digital Ecosystems**

Images and videos "quickly become established in people's consciousness," especially when spread through social media. Viralization speed far exceeds the capacity to correct falsehoods.

**4. Democratization of Identity Impersonation Capability**

The technology now allows "most people" to copy and use "personal characteristics of artists, including voice and appearance, in manipulated representations of their performances." This represents a **democratization of harmful capability**.

## III. APPLICABLE LAW: PRE-EXISTING DANISH LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### A. Inadequate Protection Under Existing Danish Law

#### 1. Copyright Act (Ophavsretsloven)

**Current Limitation:** The Act only protects "fremførelse af litterære og kunstneriske værker" (performance of literary and artistic works). This limitation means:

- Artists whose performances do not constitute "literary or artistic work" in technical-legal sense are unprotected
- There is no **likeness protection** comparable to that of works
- Digital simulations are not considered "copies" in traditional legal sense

**Protection Gap:** The 1961 law (Romkonventionen) required "performing artists" to perform works. Modern artists who create performances without basis in pre-existing literary/artistic work lack protection.

#### 2. Digital Services Act (DSA)

While the EU's DSA imposes obligations on platforms, **it lacks specific civil legal basis** for deepfake victims to demand content removal.

#### 3. Criminal Code (Straffeloven)

**Relevant but limited sections:**

- **§ 264e (Identity Misuse):** Covers "manipulation of appearance" but requires element of **"utilbørlig" (impropriety)** filtering many legitimate uses
- **§ 267 (Defamation):** Protects against slander but requires content be "false" and "egnet til at krænke ære" (apt to injure honor)
- Both are **"privat påtale"** (private prosecution) crimes with low processing rates

#### 4. Data Protection Act (Databeskyttelsesforordningen - GDPR)

While applicable to biometric data, GDPR:
- Only protects when there is practical identifiability of specific individual
- Requires specific legal basis for processing
- **Does not criminalize distribution** directly, only unjustified processing

#### 5. Marketing Act (Markedsføringsloven)

Offers protection **only in commercial contexts**, not against political or democratic manipulations.

#### 6. Unwritten Danish Common Law Principles

Two judicial principles have evolved:
- Prohibition on commercial use of person's image without consent
- Right to compensation for tort if publication constitutes **retsstridig** (unlawful) violation of privacy

**Limitations:** These principles require costly civil litigation and lack statutory basis.

### B. Applicable European Regulatory Framework

#### 1. Copyright in the Digital Single Market Directive (2019/790)

- Article 17: Platform liability for user content
- **Gap:** Does not specify obligations regarding deepfakes
- **Implication:** Danish proposal goes **beyond** European minimum

#### 2. Artificial Intelligence Regulation (AI Act - EU 2024)

- Article 50: Mandatory transparency on synthetic content-generating systems
- Article 50.4: Identification of deepfakes as "AI-generated or manipulated"
- **Gap:** Focus on transparency, not distribution prohibition

#### 3. European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR)

- Article 10: Freedom of expression (with exceptions)
- Article 8: Right to private life
- **Legal Tension:** Proposal must balance freedom of expression with rights protection

## IV. DANISH LEGISLATIVE PROPOSAL: STRUCTURE AND INNOVATION

### A. General Regulatory Architecture

The proposal introduces **two main new protections** in the Copyright Act:

#### 1. Protection of Artists Against Simulacra of Their Performances (§ 65a)

**Protected Subjects:** "Udøvende kunstnere eller artister" (performing artists or artists)

**Definition of "artist":** Person who "scenisk fremstiller, synger, reciterer, deklamerer, spiller eller på anden måde optræder med kunstneriske præstationer" (scenically performs, sings, recites, declaims, plays, or otherwise performs artistic performances) without requirement that performance be of pre-existing literary/artistic work.

**Prohibited Act:** "Virkelighedsnære digitalt genererede efterligninger" (realistically digitally generated simulacra) of an artistic performance **may not be made available to the public** without consent.

**Definition of "virkelighednær" (realistically):**
- Simulacrum must be "egnet til at fremkalde en forvekslingsrisiko" (apt to create confusion risk) with genuine performance
- "Realism" requirement excludes clearly fantastical art
- Explicit exclusion: Simulacra **explicitly marked as artificial**

**Duration of Protection:** 50 years from artist's death

**Scope:** Artists who are nationals or residents of European Economic Area (EEA)

#### 2. Protection of Citizens Against Simulacra of Personal Characteristics (§ 73a)

**Protected Subjects:** "Alle fysiske personer" (all natural persons) - including foreigners

**Definition of "personlige, fysiske kendetegn" (personal physical characteristics):**
- Appearance, voice, movements
- Any element that together makes person recognizable
- Individual parts if, by themselves, create confusion risk (e.g., distinctive voice)

**Prohibited Act:** Digital generated and realistic simulacrum of personal characteristics **may not be made available to the public** without consent, even in non-commercial contexts.

**Duration of Protection:** 50 years from person's death

**Constitutional Express Exceptions (§ 73a.2):**

Protection **DOES NOT APPLY** to simulacra that are "primarily expression of caricature, satire, parody, pastiche, power criticism, social criticism, etc." **EXCEPT** if simulacrum constitutes misinformation that may cause:
- Serious damage to third-party rights
- Serious damage to essential interests (life, health, privacy, reputation, property)

**Evaluation Standard:**
- Analysis of "clarity" that content is caricature/satire
- Analysis of concrete misinformation risk
- Analysis of subject identity (public figures may tolerate more criticism)
- **Three-part test:** Clarity + Content + Context

### C. Operational Protection Mechanisms

#### 1. Civil Liability (Not Criminal)

**Rejected criminalization:** Unlike other offenses (§ 264e), the new protections **do not include criminal penalties**.

**Legislator's Rationale:**
- Criminalization would be too restrictive for freedom of expression
- Civil route is more proportional for artistic/political simulacra cases
- Digital platform systems can implement the law without criminal risk

**Available Remedy:** Compensation for "vederlag" (exploitation compensation) + "erstatning" (additional damages) + "godtgørelse" (moral damages for non-pecuniary harm)

#### 2. Removal and De-indexing (§ 84.1.4)

**New Judicial Power:** Courts may order that copies "fjernes fra det medie, det er tilgængeliggjort på" (be removed from the medium on which they were made available)

**Interpretation:** This creates legal basis to sue platforms for deepfake removal under copyright law, not just under DSA

**Operational Implementation:**
- Victim may sue platform for violation of § 65a or § 73a
- Court orders removal as civil remedy
- Platform must remove under court order or face contempt sanctions

#### 3. Collective License Explicitly Excluded

**Significant Political Decision:** Legislator rejected allowing "aftalelicens" (collective license) for these rights.

**Rationale:** "Personlige kendetegn er tæt forbundet med ens person" (personal characteristics are intimately connected with the person). They cannot be collectively commercialized.

**Implication:** Each simulacrum requires **individual explicit consent**, not laundering through mass licenses.

## V. ARTICULATION WITH EUROPEAN LAW: TENSIONS AND COMPATIBILITY

### A. Compliance with ECHR Article 10: Freedom of Expression

#### Proportionality Analysis

**Three-step test according to European Court of Human Rights jurisprudence:**

**Step 1: Is there a legitimate restriction?**

The Danish proposal identifies legitimate objectives:
- Prevention of disorder or crime
- Protection of reputation and third-party rights
- Protection of basic democratic interests

**Step 2: Is it prescribed by law?**

Yes: Both new protections and exceptions are clearly codified.

**Step 3: Is it necessary in a democratic society?**

Arguments in favor:
- Deepfake technology has transformed marginal threats into systemic ones
- Existing means (criminal, civil, marketing) are insufficient
- Proposal includes explicit exceptions for caricature/satire/criticism

Arguments to question:
- "Realism" requirement is vague (how realistic is necessary?)
- Misinformation test may be subjective
- 50-year post-mortem duration is extensive

#### Incorporated Safeguard Mechanism

The Danish legislator included §73a.2 as **internal constitutional defense**, not merely an exception. This is significant because:
- Demonstrates awareness of ECHR tensions
- Creates proportional defense structure
- Requires political critics to demonstrate clarity vs. misinformation

### B. Compatibility with AI Act (EU 2024)

#### Complementary Relationship

The Danish proposal goes **beyond** the AI Act:

| Aspect | AI Act | Danish Law |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Deepfake transparency | Art. 50: Mandatory marking | Yes, included |
| Distribution prohibition | No | Yes (without consent) |
| Platform liability | Only under DSA | Additionally under copyright |
| Artist protection | Not specific | § 65a complete |
| Citizen protection | Indirect | § 73a direct |

#### Regulatory Synergy

The two frameworks reinforce each other:
1. AI Act requires transparency
2. Danish law prohibits distribution without consent
3. Combined, they create **effective control** over deepfakes

### C. Relationship with Copyright Directive (2019/790)

#### Extending Beyond European Minimum

**Article 17 (Platform Liability):**
- Requires platforms to create mechanisms to detect copyright violations
- Danish proposal creates **specific rights** platforms must respect under Art. 17

**Danish Innovation:**
- Creates removal obligation not only under DSA, but under copyright
- Allows multiple enforcement avenues
- More robust than DSA-only approach

## VI. DETAILED ANALYSIS: ARTIST PROTECTION (§ 65a)

### A. Protected Subjects: Definition of "Artist"

#### Extending Traditional Concept

Traditional copyright law distinguished:
- **Udøvende kunstnere:** Perform pre-existing literary/artistic works
- **Artister:** Any other performing artists

**Historical Problem:** This excluded:
- Singers of songs composed by others
- Dancers with original choreography not "artistic work" in technical sense
- Comedians with improvised monologues
- Musicians with improvisations

**Legislative Solution:** Definitional extension:

> "Artister skal forstås som skuespillere, sangere, musikere, dansere og andre personer som scenisk fremstiller, synger, reciterer, deklamerer, spiller eller på anden måde optræder med kunstneriske præstationer."

**Technical Translation:** "Artists shall be understood as actors, singers, musicians, dancers and other persons who perform, sing, recite, declaim, play, or otherwise perform artistic performances."

**Key Element:** "Kunstneriske præstationer" (artistic performances) without requirement of pre-existing work.

#### Determination Criteria

According to proposal, requires analysis of:

1. **Careful planning and deliberation** of the performance
2. **Artistic or aesthetic nature** of the performance
3. **Weight in competitive context** or professional evaluation

**Explicit Exclusions:**
- Purely sporting performances (e.g., soccer goal)
- Accidental performances without artistic element
- Pure technical performance without aesthetic component

**Example Analysis:**
- ✓ Singer with distinctive voice performing new song
- ✓ Comedian with original comedy monologue
- ✗ Athlete executing technical soccer movement
- ✗ News presenter reading script without artistic interpretation

### B. The Protected Simulacrum: "Virkelighedsnær Digitalt Genereret Efterligning"

#### Element 1: "Digitalt Genereret" (Digitally Generated)

**Broad Definition:**

> "Begrebet digitalt genereret skal forstås bredt som alt, der er genereret ved brug af teknologiske hjælpemidler."

**Inclusions:**
- Sophisticated AI systems (neural network-based deepfake)
- Less sophisticated editing tools
- Video, audio, or image manipulation with software
- AI-based voice synthesis

**The Crucial Point:** Not the sophistication of the tool, but that there is **technological manipulation**.

#### Element 2: "Virkelighedsnær" (Realistic)

**Definitional Standard:**

The simulation must be "egnet til at fremkalde en forvekslingsrisiko" (apt to create confusion risk) with the genuine performance.

**Case-by-Case Analysis:**

| Type | Analysis | Protection |
|------|----------|-----------|
| AI-synthesized singer voice | High confusion risk | ✓ Yes |
| Video of actor saying line never said | High confusion risk | ✓ Yes |
| Exaggerated caricature of comedian | Low confusion risk (clearly exaggerated) | ✗ No |
| Parody of political figure using synthesized voice | Depends on parodic clarity | ↔ Case-by-case |
| Clearly fantastical avatar with artist's voice | No confusion risk | ✗ No |

**Vagueness Test:** Legislator is aware that "virkelighedsnær" is an indeterminate legal term requiring **jurisprudential development**.

#### Element 3: "Uden samtykke" (Without Consent)

**Legal Definition of Consent:**

> "Samtykke vil foreligge, når der er tale om en frivillig, specifik, informeret og utvetydig viljeserklæring"

**Cumulative Requirements:**
1. **Voluntary:** Non-coercive
2. **Specific:** Not blanket/general
3. **Informed:** Person understands what is authorized
4. **Unambiguous:** Clear expression of will

**Revocation:**

Consent may be withdrawn without special requirements. The retraction:
- **Prospective:** Affects only future uses
- **Not retroactive:** Does not affect already distributed simulacra
- **On redistribution:** Prohibits new sharing/post-retraction sharing

**Burden of Proof:**

> "Det er den, der tilgængeliggør den virkelighedsnære digitalt genererede efterligning for almenheden, der skal bevise, at der foreligger samtykke fra den efterlignede."

**Implication:** Burden of proof on whoever publishes the simulacrum.

### C. Duration: 50 Years Post Mortem

#### Legislative Justification

**Duration Distinction:**

| Right | Duration | Calculation | Justification |
|-------|----------|-------------|---------------|
| § 65 (Traditional performance) | 50 years | From performance act | Protects specific act |
| § 65a (Simulacrum) | 50 years | From death | Protects personal identity |

**Rationale for Changing Basis:**

The proposal recognizes that simulacra:
- Are not based on any specific identifiable performance
- Are typically constructed from **multiple performances** over the artist's career
- Require post-mortem access to artistic reputation and identity

**Duration Limit:**

Legislator rejected "perpetual" protection considering:
- Would be "for indgribende" (too invasive) of expression rights
- Need for societies to historically reference public figures
- Risk of indefinite blocking of historical criticism/satire

**International Comparison:**
- EU: Generally 70 years post-mortem for copyright
- Danish proposal: 50 years is **intermediate** (less than authors, more than short-term)

### D. Exceptions and Application of § 24b: Parody, Caricature, and Pastiche

#### Exception Mechanism

The proposal will explicitly apply § 24b of ophavsretsloven (which already allows parody, caricature, and pastiche of works) to artist simulacra.

**Implication:**

Even realistic simulacra may be legal if they constitute "parodi, karikatur eller pastiche" (parody, caricature, or pastiche).

**Three-step test (implemented through ECHR Art. 10):**

1. Does it constitute unreasonable damage to the artist?
2. Are the artist's legitimate interests respected?
3. Is there purpose of criticism or creative humor?

**Practical Application:**

- ✓ Satirical video where politician speaks in exaggerated parodic style = protected parody
- ✗ Realistic video where actor says false line to embarrass = not parody
- ↔ Ambiguous satirical deepfake where it's not clearly indicated as satire = requires case-by-case evaluation

## VII. DETAILED ANALYSIS: CITIZEN PROTECTION (§ 73a)

### A. Universality of Protection

#### Unlimited Territorial Scope

**Significant Innovation:**

> "Bestemmelsen i § 73 a gælder for efterligninger af alle fysiske personer, herunder udenlandske statsborgere."

**Translation:** "The provision in § 73a applies to simulacra of all natural persons, including foreign citizens."

**Contrast with § 65a:**

| Protection | Scope | Justification |
|------------|-------|---------------|
| § 65a (Artists) | EEA citizens | Harmonization with reciprocal European treatment |
| § 73a (Citizens) | All/Unlimited | Universal right to privacy/identity |

**Rationale:** Fundamental human dignity cannot depend on nationality.

### B. Definition of "Personal Physical Characteristics"

#### Constitutive Elements

"Personlige, fysiske kendetegn" includes:

1. **Udseende** (Appearance)
   - Facial features
   - Body build
   - Distinctive marks
   - Scars or physical peculiarities

2. **Stemme** (Voice)
   - Characteristic timbre
   - Speech patterns
   - Accents or idiolects
   - Distinctive sounds

3. **Bevægelser** (Movements)
   - Gait
   - Characteristic gestures
   - Habitual postures
   - Body movement patterns

**Recognizability Element:**

The characteristics must, "tilsammen udgør den personlige identitet, som indebærer at en person genkendes" (together constitute personal identity that allows person recognition).

**Part Protection:**

"Enkeltdelene af de personlige kendetegn foreslås beskyttet forudsat, at en efterligning indeholdende enkeltdelene i sig selv vil være egnet til at skabe forvekslingsrisiko" (Individual parts of personal characteristics are protected provided that a simulacrum containing the individual parts would itself be apt to create confusion risk).

**Example:** A very distinctive voice of a famous politician, even without showing face, would be protected if their voice alone is recognizable.

### C. Misinformation Test: Freedom-Protection Balance

#### Three-Part Structure of § 73a.2

The norm establishes that protection DOES NOT apply to simulacra that are "primarily" (hovedsageligt) expression of:

1. **Caricature** (Karikaturer)
2. **Satire** (Satire)
3. **Parody** (Parodi)
4. **Pastiche** (Pastiche)
5. **Power criticism** (Magtkritik)
6. **Social criticism** (Samfundskritik)
7. **"etc."** (Open-ended)

**BUT** (Critical Exception):

> "Medmindre efterligningen udgør misinformation, som konkret kan medføre alvorlig fare for andres rettigheder eller væsentlige interesser."

**Cumulative Requirements of the Exception:**

1. **Misinformation** = Information "usande, vildledende eller fejlagtige" (false, misleading, or erroneous)
2. **Konkret fare** (not abstract/theoretical) = Identifiable concrete risk
3. **Alvorlig** (Serious) = Significant potential damage
4. **Til andres rettigheder eller væsentlige interesser** = Affecting:
   - Liv (life)
   - Helbred (health)
   - Privatliv (privacy)
   - Renommé (reputation)
   - Ejendom (property)

#### Legislative Examples of Prohibited Misinformation

**Case 1: Deepfake of Police Chief**

Realistic parody where police chief appears inciting "selvtægt" (vigilantism) against specific group.

**Analysis:**
- Clarity: Is it obvious it's satire? If explicitly parody = exception
- Content: Incites violence against specific group = serious misinformation
- Context: If distributed without satire marks = prohibited

**Case 2: Deepfake of Politician Accusing Minority**

Realistic parody where political figure accuses minority of specific crime, without clarity it's parody.

**Analysis:**
- Content: False accusation of crime = misinformation
- Risk: Discrimination against minority, damage to reputation = serious interests
- Clarity of parody: Is it obvious? If NO = prohibited

**Case 3: Deepfake of Businessperson in Financial Transaction**

Video where realistic businessperson appears authorizing million-dollar transaction.

**Analysis:**
- Content: False financial authorization = serious misinformation
- Risk: Real property loss
- Clarity: If parodic = exception; if realistic = prohibited

#### Proposed Judicial Evaluation Test

The legislator offers weighting criteria for judges:

**1. Clarity Test**

How evident is it that it's caricature/satire/parody?

Factors:
- Graphic quality (too perfect sounds fake?)
- Words, actions, objects in simulacrum (incongruous/absurd?)
- Settings and context (fantastical or realistic?)
- Explicit marking (says "parody/satire"?)
- For videos: Clarity in BOTH audio AND video? (Critical since many watch without sound)

**2. Misinformation Risk Test**

Can content create concrete risk of:
- False belief
- Serious reputation damage
- Incitement to violence
- Economic damage

**3. Subject Identity Test**

Who is the subject?

- **Public figures** (politicians, celebrities): Can tolerate more criticism per ECHR Court
- **Private persons**: Deserve more robust protection
- **Public agents:** Intermediate position

**Legislative Purpose:** The proposal recognizes that public figures who sought prominence must tolerate greater "degree of criticism" per ECHR jurisprudence.

### D. Duration: 50 Years Post Mortem

#### Justification: Protection of Deceased and Relatives

**Key Argument:**

> "Det kan anses som krænkende for den efterlignedes efterladte, at der i nær tidsmæssig sammenhæng med den efterlignedes død tilgængeliggøres en digitalt generede, virkelighedsnær efterligning af den afdøde"

**Translation:** "It may be considered an infringement on the bereaved that, in close temporal proximity to the deceased's death, a digitally generated, realistic simulacrum of the deceased is made available without consent."

**Balance:**

- **50 years:** Sufficient to protect deceased dignity and relatives' feelings
- **Limit (not perpetual):** Prevents indefinite blocking of historical references
- **Comparison:** Coincides with post-mortem copyright durations

**Special Post-Mortem Dignity Protection:**

Legislator recognizes that using deceased's appearance in simulacra can:
- Offend family sensitivities
- Distort historical legacy
- Be particularly invasive (deceased cannot consent)

## VIII. ENFORCEMENT MECHANISMS AND LIABILITY

### A. Civil vs. Criminal Route: Proportionality

#### Rejection of Criminalization

**Significant Political Decision:**

By amending § 76 and § 77 of ophavsretsloven, the proposal explicitly **excludes** new protections from criminal penalties.

**Comparison with § 264e (Existing Criminal Offense):**

| Aspect | § 264e Criminal | § 65a/§ 73a Civil |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Penalty | Fine or jail up to 6 months | No penalties |
| Intent requirement | Yes (forsætligt) | Yes and also negligence (uagtsomt) |
| Responsibility | Criminal | Civil |
| Victim | May be private accuser | Must sue |
| Proof | Beyond reasonable doubt | Preponderance of evidence |

**Legislative Justification:**

Criminalization would be "for indgribende" (too invasive) regarding:
- Artistic expression freedom
- Humor and satire
- Political criticism

**Implication:** Deepfakes of politicians in satire would not expose creator to criminal penalty, only civil suit.

### B. Civil Remedies: Compensation

#### Compensation Structure (§ 83)

**Three Components:**

1. **Vederlag** (Exploitation compensation)
   - Reasonable amount for use of performance/characteristics
   - Similar to royalties
   - Independent of actual damage

2. **Erstatning** (Additional damages)
   - Actual economic damage (e.g., lost commercial opportunities)
   - Consider:
     - Fortjeneste tabt (lost profits)
     - Uberettiget fortjeneste (unlawful gain of violator)

3. **Godtgørelse** (Moral damages)
   - Compensation for non-pecuniary damage
   - Injury to dignity, privacy, reputation
   - **Important Innovation:** § 83 is rarely invoked in copyright; proposal makes it available for deepfakes

### C. Removal and De-indexing (§ 84.1.4)

#### New Power: Removal Order

**Significant Addition:**

Proposal adds new numeral to § 84:

> "4) fjernes fra det medie, det er tilgængeliggjort på"
> "Be removed from the medium on which they were made available"

**Practical Operation:**

1. Victim sues for § 65a/§ 73a violation
2. Court finds violation
3. Court orders:
   - Retraction of simulacrum
   - Removal from specific platform
   - Eventually, tilintetgørelse (destruction of copies)
4. Order is enforceable against platform under Civil Procedure Code

#### Relationship with EU DSA

**Synergistic Effect:**

| Framework | Obligation |
|-----------|-----------|
| DSA Art. 17 | Platform must create mechanism for removal of illegal content |
| Danish Law § 84 | Defines "illegal" under copyright: includes deepfakes |

**Implication:** EU platforms now face double obligation:
- Respond to DSA notifications
- Respond to court orders under § 84

## IX. FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS PROTECTION: ECHR ANALYSIS

### A. Alleged Compliance with ECHR Article 10 (Freedom of Expression)

#### Incorporated Proportionality Test

The Danish legislator explicitly affirms:

> "Det er regeringens opfattelse, at det foreslåede generelle forbud ikke går videre, end hvad der er nødvendigt for at varetage disse hensyn"

**Translation:** "It is the government's opinion that the proposed general prohibition does not go further than necessary to serve these objectives."

#### Three Legitimate Objectives Identified

According to § 3 of preamble:

**1. Prevention of Disorder or Crime**

- Deepfakes can incite vigilantism
- Can incite crime against specific groups
- Can facilitate fraud or identity impersonation

**2. Protection of Reputation and Third-Party Rights**

- Simulacra can irreversibly damage reputation
- Right to private life (ECHR Art. 8)
- Artists' economic rights

**3. Protection of Democratic Interests**

- Public trust in information
- Integrity of democratic debate
- Differentiation between facts and fiction

#### Proportionality Components

**Element 1: Limitation to "Virkelighedsnære" Simulacra**

Proposal limits protection only to realistic simulacra, not clearly fantastical ones. This:
- Respects artistic creation of clearly false content
- Allows exaggerated satirical criticism
- Does not limit obviously identifiable parodies

**Element 2: Explicit Exceptions for Caricature/Satire**

Proposal **directly incorporates** ECHR exceptions, establishing that caricature, satire, parody, social criticism are **not prohibited**, even if realistic, if they do not constitute serious misinformation.

**Element 3: Case-by-Case Misinformation Analysis**

Proposal does not prohibit broad categories, but requires judges to analyze:
- Clarity that it is satire
- Concrete misinformation risk
- Subject identity
- Interests at stake

**Element 4: Limited Duration**

50-year post-mortem protection, not perpetual. This:
- Allows eventual historical references
- Does not indefinitely prevent criticism
- Balances post-mortem dignity with future freedom

#### Position of Public vs. Private Figures

**ECHR Principle (per European Court):**

Public figures (politicians, celebrities) must tolerate greater degree of criticism because:
- They voluntarily sought prominence
- They can defend themselves publicly
- Democratic interest in being able to criticize them

**Danish Implementation:**

Proposal requires that "misinformation" evaluation consider "hvem efterligningen retter sig imod" (who is the target of the simulacrum) and "hvem der er genstand for eventuel kritik" (who is subject of eventual criticism).

**Legislative Specification:**

> "Det vil indgå i vurderingen af, om en efterligning er omfattet af den foreslåede § 73 a, stk. 2, om det er tydeligt, at der er tale om en karikatur mv. Er der tydeligt tale om en karikatur mv., må det som det klare udgangspunkt antages, at efterligningen ikke vil være egnet til at udgøre en generel fare"

**Translation:** "It will be part of the evaluation whether it is clear that it is a caricature, etc. If it is evidently a caricature, it must as a clear starting point be assumed that the simulacrum will not be apt to constitute a general danger."

### B. Compliance with ECHR Article 8 (Private Life)

#### Protection of Privacy Right

The proposal justifies new protections as:

> "Hastigheden i den teknologiske udvikling og den lettede adgang til deling øger ligeledes behovet for at beskytte borgerne mod, at deres personlige fysiske kendetegn benyttes til at gøre dem til afsendere på budskaber uden deres samtykke"

**Translation:** "The speed of technological change and facilitated access to sharing likewise increase the need to protect citizens against their personal physical characteristics being used to make them senders of messages without their consent."

**ECHR Article 8 Application:**

European Court has recognized that:
- Image and voice are part of personal identity (private life)
- Image/voice manipulation interferes with right not to be falsely represented
- Deepfake distribution constitutes interference with privacy

**Proportionality element for Art. 8:**

Unlike Art. 8, interferences with Art. 8 may be broader if:
- Prescribed by law (✓ New law)
- Pursue legitimate aim (✓ Privacy/dignity protection)
- Necessary in democratic society (✓ Recognized deepfake crisis)

## X. UNRESOLVED ISSUES AND FUTURE DEVELOPMENTS

### A. Vagueness of "Virkelighedsnær"

#### Problem

Term "virkelighedsnær" (realistic) is legally indeterminate. Edge case examples:

- 95% realistic voice synthesis?
- Low-resolution video that could be real?
- Photorealistic but clearly CGI animation?
- Slightly pixelated deepfake?

#### Expected Resolution

Danish legislator anticipates **jurisprudence will develop standards** through:
- Test case litigation
- Progressive court guidance
- Platform dialogues

### B. Definition of "Kunstneriske Præstationer"

#### Problem

Term "artistic performances" lacks clear line between:
- Legitimately protected art
- Mere technical acts without artistic element
- Sports vs. expressive movements

#### Expected Litigation Examples

- Is a soccer goal an "artistic performance"?
- Is hyper-realistic cabaret an artistic performance?
- Is unscripted stand-up monologue protected?

### C. Application of Satire Exceptions to Political Deepfakes

#### Problem

Satire/criticism exceptions are deliberately broad but depend on:
- Clarity (how obvious must satire be?)
- Intent (how is satirical intent proven?)
- Context (does distribution venue matter?)

#### Expected Litigation

- Satirical deepfake of politician distributed without clear marks
- Deepfake mixing satire with real misinformation
- Deepfake shared outside original parodic context

## XI. COMPARISON WITH OTHER REGULATORY FRAMEWORKS

### A. Danish Position in European Context

#### Denmark is a **Pioneer**

No other European country has specifically legislated on deepfakes of artists and citizens in this manner.

**Comparison with:**

| Country | Approach | Status |
|---------|----------|--------|
| France | Bill in development | Proposed, not enacted |
| Spain | LSSI-CE reform with deepfake mentions | In development |
| Germany | Copyright Act changes | Proposed |
| EU | AI Act + DSA | Enacted (transparency approach) |
| Denmark | Comprehensive ophavsretten reform § 65a/§ 73a | **Complete proposal 2025** |

#### Denmark's Innovations

1. Protection of "non-traditional" artists without pre-existing work
2. Universal citizen protection without nationality limitation
3. Complete civil mechanism (compensation + removal) integrated in ophavsretsloven
4. Internal ECHR exceptions (caricature/satire/parody) directly codified in law
5. Platform liability under copyright, not just DSA

### B. Relationship with EU AI Act Approach

**AI Act (Transparency/Marking):**
- Requires AI systems to mark synthetic content
- Permissive approach (allows with transparency)

**Danish Law (Consent/Prohibition):**
- Requires consent for distribution
- Restrictive approach (prohibits without consent)

**Complementarity:**
The two frameworks reinforce each other. AI Act requires transparency; Danish law requires consent.

## XII. LEGAL CONCLUSIONS

### A. Innovative Character of the Proposal

The Danish proposal represents **deliberate exceeding** of existing European frameworks:

1. **Goes beyond ECHR minimum:** Although ECHR allows interferences in freedom of expression, Denmark chooses more robust protections
2. **Goes beyond AI Act:** Although AI Act requires transparency, Denmark requires consent
3. **Goes beyond Copyright Directive:** Although directive creates platform liability under DSA, Denmark adds copyright liability

### B. Legal Balance Achieved

The proposal incorporates **multiple ECHR protection levels:**

**Level 1: Freedom of Expression Protection**
- Explicit exceptions for caricature/satire/criticism
- Case-by-case "clarity" analysis
- Consideration of public vs. private figures

**Level 2: Private Life Protection**
- Prohibition of realistic simulacra of personal characteristics
- No requirement of commercial element or damage
- Protection for all (citizens and artists)

**Level 3: Democratic Protection**
- Recognition of public trust in information role
- Tools against serious misinformation
- Balance between protected political criticism and prohibited misinformation

### C. Pragmatic Enforcement Mechanisms

The proposal chooses **civil (not criminal) enforcement** recognizing:

- Severity of criminalization for freedom of expression
- Proportionality of civil remedies
- Civil court flexibility vs. categorical criminal

### D. Identified Risks and Challenges

The proposal is **aware of risks:**

1. **Legal vagueness:** "Virkelighedsnær" requires jurisprudential development
2. **Over-enforcement risk:** Platforms could remove legitimate content
3. **Under-enforcement risk:** Victims may lack resources to litigate
4. **ECHR tension:** Although balanced, could face constitutional challenges
