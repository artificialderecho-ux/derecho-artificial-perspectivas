Executive Summary

This article provides an in-depth analysis of the Spanish Data Protection Agency (AEPD) Guide from January 2026 on the use of third-party images in artificial intelligence systems. This document constitutes a fundamental reference for understanding the legal risks, both visible and invisible, derived from personal data processing in the form of images through generative AI systems.

The increasing use of AI-powered image generation and manipulation tools has generated new risk scenarios that go beyond immediate apparent damages, affecting crucial aspects of personal data protection that remain hidden from users and affected individuals.

The AEPD guide provides an essential technical and regulatory framework for evaluating these risks from a data protection and fundamental rights perspective. The analytical criteria it proposes do not constitute changes in data protection doctrine but rather a sophisticated application of well-established principles to the specific context of generative artificial intelligence.

I. Introduction: The Regulatory and Factual Context

In the current context of generative AI tool proliferation, the use of third-party images or videos has acquired a dimension that transcends trivial uses. A photograph, video, or visual representation in which a person is identified or identifiable constitutes personal data pursuant to the terms of the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the Organic Law on Data Protection (LOPDGDD).

Person identifiability is not limited to one's face. According to criteria from specialized jurisprudence and international regulatory practice, a person may be identifiable through their voice, body, gestures, clothing, tattoos, relational environment, or through the combination of several elements. This circumstance persists even when the image has been altered through artificial intelligence systems.

The generalization of thoughtless use of AI tools to generate or modify images has normalized practices that, from a data protection perspective, present considerable risks. These seemingly trivial uses (filters, avatars, caricatures, animations) generate real impacts, both visible and invisible.

II. Visible Risks: Applicable Evaluation Criteria

The so-called visible risks are those appreciated when an AI-generated or modified image or video is shared, published, or disseminated through any medium. The AEPD establishes eight fundamental criteria that remain fully applicable even though the content was generated or modified by artificial intelligence systems.

A) Reasonable Expectation and Lawful Basis for the Specific Use

The fact that a photograph was available in a private messaging group, on a social network, or was sent occasionally does not confer general authorization to incorporate it into AI tools, transform it, generate variants, or disseminate the result. The greater the distance from the original context, the greater the requirement for lawful basis.

B) Scope and Ease of Dissemination

The potential impact depends both on the number of recipients and the technical ease for forwarding and copying. On social networks, algorithmic amplification mechanisms exponentially accelerate impact, regardless of whether the content is AI-generated or original material.

C) Persistence and Real Possibility of Removing Content

risk increases when it is impossible to effectively remove the content and its subsequent copies. Removal measures do not always guarantee total elimination, so practical reversibility constitutes a central element in risk assessment.

D) Sexualization and Synthetic Intimate Content

This category presents an extremely high-intensity risk signal. It refers to added nudity, eroticization, sexual insinuations, or intimate scenes generated from neutral images. The potential harm is considerable, facilitating blackmail and harassment.

E) Attribution of Non-Real Facts and Reputational Effects

The risk arises when content attributes to a person facts or conduct that did not occur and are plausible. This significantly affects reputation, personal relationships, and professional position. The harm can be serious and requires rigorous assessment.

F) Decontextualization and Reinterpretation

An image or video can cause harm if presented outside context or accompanied by text that alters its meaning. This risk is not neutralized by the provider's generation filters.

G) Vulnerability of the Affected Person

The threshold of prudence must be maximum with minors, elderly persons, persons with disabilities, or other vulnerable groups. Even seemingly innocent uses can trigger harassment or stigmatization.

H) Special Impact on the Person

It must be assessed whether there is quantifiable harm in personal relationships, physical integrity, employment scope, psychological damage, educational context, identity impersonation, or any significant negative legal effect.

III. Invisible Risks: Hidden Dimensions of Processing

There are real risks affecting the person whose image is uploaded to an AI system, simply by doing so, even when the use is trivial and the result is not published. These risks derive from normal service operation and usually go unnoticed by users and affected individuals.

A) Effective Loss of Control

When uploading an image to an AI system, the content ceases to be under user control and is processed by an external provider. For the affected person, this means real loss of control over where their image is and what happens to it.

B) Technical Retention and Non-Visible Copies

Many AI systems temporarily retain images for processing, error management, or backup. This retention is usually invisible and unverifiable, making it difficult to know if the image was actually deleted.

C) Involvement of Multiple Actors

Processing usually involves cloud infrastructure, storage services, security tools, and technical personnel. This expands the number of systems and persons who can access the image, significantly increasing risk.

D) Provider's Own and Extended Purposes

In addition to the requested result, the provider may process images to ensure security, detect abuse, evaluate service quality, or improve functionality. This implies retaining examples longer or reusing them internally.

E) Metadata Generation and Internal Inferences

During processing, systems automatically analyze content to detect faces, bodies, or characteristics, generating technical metadata. Although these analyses have functional purposes, they constitute additional processing that leaves traces.

F) Persistent Identification Risk

Some tools are designed so that a person appears consistently across multiple images generated from a single photograph. The system reuses traits that allow recognizing the subject, facilitating re-identification and loss of control.

G) Information Asymmetry and Difficulty Exercising Rights

The affected person usually does not know which system was used, what happened to their image, how long it was retained, or whom to contact for deletion. This asymmetry practically limits the exercise of access, deletion, or objection rights.

H) Exposure Risk from Security Incidents

AI systems may suffer technical failures, unauthorized access, or security breaches. Uploaded images may be exposed without voluntary dissemination, with high impact for the affected person.

I) Multiplier Effect and Connection with Subsequent Damages

Once uploaded, generating multiple variants in a short time is easy. This low repetition cost increases the probability of harmful results in subsequent iterations, explaining why many visible damages are supported by prior technical risks.

**Uploading third-party images to AI systems is not a neutral act, even without subsequent dissemination or intent to cause harm. Risks arise from loss of control, technical opacity, extended purposes, and difficulty for the affected person to react—factors that must be considered even in seemingly trivial uses.**

IV. Situations of Special Relevance for the AEPD

In many cases, uses fall outside the scope of data protection regulations when carried out strictly for personal or domestic purposes. Similarly, processing of deceased persons is not within the scope of GDPR application.

However, this does not exclude that other fundamental rights such as honor, privacy, or image may be affected, nor that other legal provisions may apply, including the Criminal Code. When there are indications of crime, action corresponds to police authorities, the Prosecutor's Office, and judicial bodies.

Cases Requiring Special Attention

The AEPD pays special attention when use significantly increases risks for the affected person:

**1. Effective loss of control over the image with no knowledge of its use.**

**2. Generation of plausible content attributing non-occurred facts.**

**3. Involvement of minors or vulnerable persons.**

**4. Introduction of sexualization, humiliation, or discrediting elements.**

**5. Dissemination in environments where personal, social, or professional impact is especially intense.**

V. Conclusions and Legal Perspective

The AEPD Guide from January 2026 represents an important milestone in the development of data protection jurisprudence applicable to artificial intelligence systems. Its contribution lies in the clear distinction between visible risks (appreciable in dissemination) and invisible risks (derived from the mere act of uploading an image).

This categorization represents a conceptual advance because it addresses the operational reality of current AI systems, many of which generate impacts regardless of whether the user publishes or shares the result. Technical opacity, actor multiplication, persistent reuse, and purpose breadth operate inevitably.

From a legal analysis perspective, the Guide reinforces that any personal data processing, including that carried out by AI systems, requires sufficient lawful basis pursuant to GDPR. The apparent triviality of a use does not exempt compliance with these constitutional principles.

**The responsibility of AI providers is reinforced: they must implement robust technical controls, communicate what happens with images, how long they are retained, and for what purposes. Users must develop sophisticated understanding of real risks, beyond tool familiarity.**
