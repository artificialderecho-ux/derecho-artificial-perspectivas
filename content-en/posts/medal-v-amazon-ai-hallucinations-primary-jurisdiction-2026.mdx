# AI Hallucinations in Federal Litigation and Primary Jurisdiction Doctrine: *Medal v. Amazon.com Services, LLC*

The order issued on February 27, 2026, by Judge John H. Chun of the United States District Court for the Western District of Washington, in the matter *Medal v. Amazon.com Services, LLC*, Case No. 2:23-cv-01975-JHC, presents two legal issues of increasing practical relevance. The first concerns the unsupervised use of large language models (LLMs) in the preparation of court filings and the professional and procedural responsibilities arising therefrom. The second concerns the limits of the primary jurisdiction doctrine as a mechanism for staying consumer protection litigation pending future administrative regulation. The court denied Amazon's motion to stay, concluding that neither the *Syntek* test factors nor efficiency considerations justified deference to the FDA. Simultaneously, the court warned—without yet imposing any sanction—that it would address in a separate order the inclusion in the complaint of a nonexistent legal citation generated by an LLM.

## AI-Generated Fabricated Citation: Professional Responsibility and Rule 11

### The Facts

Plaintiffs' counsel cited "21 U.S.C. § 343(r)(6)(D)" as the legal basis for the requirement that DSHEA disclaimers appear "prominently" on dietary supplement labels. The court found that this subsection does not exist. In a separate filing (Dkt. # 125), counsel acknowledged that the nonexistent citation was the result of "insufficiently supervised use of a large language model." The court characterized the inclusion of this apparent *confabulation* as "unacceptable" and stated that it would address the matter in a separate order.

This incident is part of a growing pattern of LLM-generated hallucinations appearing in filings before federal courts. The most cited precedent is *Mata v. Avianca, Inc.*, 22-cv-1461 (S.D.N.Y. 2023), in which two attorneys were sanctioned under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 11 for incorporating into their brief jurisprudential citations entirely fabricated by ChatGPT. The *Medal* order does not yet impose a sanction, but the court's express reservation indicates that the issue warrants formal attention beyond a footnote.

### Applicable Legal Framework: Professional Responsibility and Rule 11

Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 11(b) provides that the attorney signing a filing certifies that, "to the extent known after reasonable inquiry," the legal allegations contained therein "are warranted by existing law." This obligation of diligent verification is not satisfied by merely delegating research to an artificial intelligence tool without subsequently corroborating the results against primary sources.

Formal Opinion 512 of the American Bar Association (2023) on the use of generative artificial intelligence tools addresses this issue directly. While the opinion does not prohibit the use of LLMs, it establishes that the professional competence required by Model Rule 1.1 obligates the attorney to understand the material risks of any technology utilized, including the known tendency of generative AI systems to produce plausible but factually incorrect legal citations. Supervision of AI-generated results is therefore not merely a recommended best practice but an integral component of the duty of competence.

In *Medal*, the violation was aggravated by the fact that the nonexistent subsection—§ 343(r)(6)(D)—was cited repeatedly in the operative complaint. The court noted, with some indulgence, that the error did not affect its analysis of the merits. This case-specific mitigating circumstance should not be interpreted as a signal that LLM hallucinations in court filings are harmless. Courts have shown increasing willingness to impose monetary sanctions, disclosure obligations, and, in serious cases, referrals to bar associations.

### Practical Consequences for Legal Practice

The *Medal* order, together with *Mata* and emerging deontological standards, consolidates several principles of immediate application:

First, verification of all legal or jurisprudential citations generated with AI assistance against authorized primary sources—whether Westlaw, Lexis, or the official U.S. Code repository—constitutes a non-delegable minimum before any court filing. The LLM output is a starting point for research, not a deliverable ready for incorporation into the brief.

Second, law firms that have adopted AI-assisted research workflows must implement formal quality control protocols requiring validation by a licensed attorney—not a paralegal or unsupervised associate—of the existence and accuracy of all citations before filing.

Third, the court's announcement of a separate order on this matter suggests that it may impose a disclosure obligation, require corrective measures, or explore sanctions under Rule 11, 28 U.S.C. § 1927—which sanctions unreasonable and vexatious multiplication of proceedings—or the court's inherent authority. Plaintiffs' counsel must be prepared for such proceedings.

## Primary Jurisdiction Doctrine: Limits of Administrative Deference in Food Labeling Litigation

### Legal Issue

The central procedural issue was whether the primary jurisdiction doctrine justified staying the litigation pending the FDA's anticipated regulatory reform of 21 C.F.R. § 101.93(d)—the provision requiring that DSHEA notices appear "on each panel or page" of a dietary supplement label containing a health claim.

### Applicable Framework: The *Syntek* Test and Efficiency as Decisive Factor

Primary jurisdiction is a prudential doctrine permitting federal courts to refer questions to an administrative agency with specialized regulatory authority when doing so better serves the objectives of uniform interpretation and expert analysis. *Syntek Semiconductor Co. v. Microchip Tech. Inc.*, 307 F.3d 775 (9th Cir. 2002), identifies four relevant factors: (1) the need to resolve a question (2) committed by Congress to the agency's jurisdiction (3) within a comprehensive regulatory regime (4) requiring technical knowledge or uniformity in application. *Astiana v. Hain Celestial Grp., Inc.*, 783 F.3d 753, 760 (9th Cir. 2015), adds that efficiency is the decisive factor: the doctrine should not be applied where it "would unnecessarily delay resolution of the claims."

### Application of *Syntek* Factors

**First and second factors: absence of novel question.** The court concluded that the first two *Syntek* factors counsel against a stay. The central task of the litigation—comparing the text of § 101.93(d) with the labels as they appear on Amazon's platform—is an ordinary judicial function. Ninth Circuit courts routinely perform this comparison without FDA intervention. *Kosta v. Del Monte Corp.*, 2013 WL 2147413 (N.D. Cal. 2023). There is no question of first impression: the regulation's text is clear, and no ambiguity requires the agency to interpret it in the first instance.

The court distinguished the present matter from the so-called "evaporated cane juice" (ECJ) cases, in which courts applied the primary jurisdiction doctrine because the scope of the term "natural" presented genuine interpretive uncertainty requiring agency expertise. *Kane v. Chobani, LLC*, 645 F. App'x 593 (9th Cir. 2016). Similarly, cannabidiol (CBD) cases justified a stay because Congress had not yet established a regulatory regime for such products. *Colette v. CV Scis., Inc.*, 2020 WL 2739861 (C.D. Cal. 2020). None of these analogies applies to the present case: § 101.93(d) has been in effect since DSHEA's passage in 1993, and its operative language—"on each panel or page"—is unambiguous.

**Third factor: granted.** The court considered, and plaintiffs did not dispute in their opposition, that dietary supplement labeling falls within the FDA's comprehensive regulatory regime for food labeling. This factor favors a stay, but alone is insufficient to impose one.

**Fourth factor: presumption against retroactivity precludes stay.** The analytically most significant portion of the order concerns the fourth *Syntek* factor—whether the imminent reform of § 101.93(d) would create a conflict requiring agency intervention. Amazon argued that enforcing the "each panel" requirement while the FDA prepares its elimination would produce inconsistent results.

The court rejected this argument based on the established presumption against retroactivity. Under *Landgraf v. USI Film Prods.*, 511 U.S. 244, 272 (1994), congressional enactments and administrative rules do not apply retroactively unless there is express statutory authorization. Neither § 101.93 nor its enabling statute, 21 U.S.C. § 343(r)(6), contains any provision for retroactive effect. The FDA's December 2025 letter announcing its intention to initiate rulemaking likewise indicates that any new regulation would be applied prospectively.

The court distinguished the cases cited by Amazon—*Dasilva*, *Colette*, and *Gitson*—by noting that those cases involved genuinely novel regulatory contexts (CBD and ECJ products) where retroactivity was, at least, plausible. In the present case, § 101.93(d) has been in effect for over three decades, and DSHEA itself has never been subject to a substantive review of this nature. The inference that a future prospective modification of the regulation would retroactively reach past conduct is, in the court's view, mere speculation unsupported by the record.

### Efficiency Considerations

The proceeding has been pending since 2023. In the absence of a precise timeline for the FDA's rulemaking process—and considering that notice-and-comment rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act typically extends for years—a stay would impose substantial delay on plaintiffs' already-accrued claims. *Samson v. United Healthcare Servs., Inc.*, 2019 WL 2173454, at *5 (W.D. Wash. 2019). The efficiency criterion therefore independently counsels against applying the primary jurisdiction doctrine.

### The Preemption Argument

Amazon's subsidiary argument—that a prospective reform of § 101.93(d) might "inform" a preemption defense under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act—was likewise denied. Under *Davidson v. Sprout Foods, Inc.*, 106 F.4th 842 (9th Cir. 2024), claims under the Sherman Law that merely reproduce federal food labeling requirements are not implicitly displaced by federal law precisely because they do not require the court to resolve questions "reserved to the FDA." Since any prospective modification of § 101.93(d) would have prospective effect, plaintiffs' claims—relating to the labeling regime in effect during the class period—would continue to be governed by the regulation then in force. The preemption issue therefore does not arise.

The FMCSA line of cases (*Connell v. Heartland Express*; *Ayala v. U.S. Xpress*)—in which courts found that certain agency orders had retroactive effect displacing state tort claims—is distinguishable: those orders involved a federal order that explicitly displaced an identified provision of California labor law, not a prospective modification of a federal regulation that might hypothetically affect past conduct.

## Systemic Scope: AI Governance in Courts and Administrative Deference in a Deregulatory Context

### Judicialization of AI Governance in Proceedings

Courts across the country are addressing the question of how to regulate lawyers' use of AI. Several districts have issued standing orders requiring disclosure of when AI tools were used in preparing filings (including N.D. Tex. and D. Colo.). The Western District of Washington has not yet issued a standing order of this kind, but Judge Chun's announcement of a separate order in *Medal* may give rise to one. The emerging trend points toward: (a) disclosure obligations regarding the use of AI-assisted legal research tools; (b) certification by the signing attorney of independent verification of all citations; and (c) potential monetary sanctions for supervision failures.

### Primary Jurisdiction and the Current Deregulatory Context

The FDA's letter announcing its intention to reform § 101.93(d) appears, in appearance, to be the product of the deregulatory process initiated by the Department of Health and Human Services' May 2025 Request for Information. The *Medal* order implicitly resists the use of the primary jurisdiction doctrine as a shield for defendants against liability based on anticipated but not yet materialized regulatory changes. This doctrinal signal is significant: courts will not stay proceedings merely because an agency has expressed its intention to deregulate, particularly when there is no precise timeline and no indication that future rules will be applied retroactively.

## Conclusions and Practical Recommendations

- **On the use of LLMs in legal practice:** Any legal or jurisprudential citation generated with AI assistance must be independently verified against authorized primary sources before filing. Failure to do so exposes the attorney to Rule 11 sanctions, disciplinary referral, and reputational harm. Law firms should adopt formal AI-assisted research protocols with mandatory validation by a licensed attorney.

- **On the primary jurisdiction doctrine:** The doctrine does not operate as a preventive shield against litigation merely because an agency has announced its intention to review a regulation. The *Syntek* factors require a genuinely novel question or technical ambiguity, not the mere possibility of future regulatory change. Courts will insist on efficiency and will not tolerate delays of indeterminate duration.

- **On the presumption against retroactivity:** The presumption against retroactive application of administrative rules is robust. Defendants seeking a stay on the argument that future reforms will affect already-accrued claims bear a demanding evidentiary burden that is not satisfied by mere agency signaling.

- **On the DSHEA disclaimer requirement:** Until § 101.93(d) is formally modified through the notice-and-comment rulemaking process, the "on each panel or page" requirement remains enforceable. Platforms and supplement manufacturers should not interpret the FDA letter as a compliance exemption.

- **On Sherman Law claims:** As confirmed by *Davidson v. Sprout Foods*, claims under the Sherman Law that reproduce federal food labeling standards are not displaced by federal law. Defendants cannot evade liability by appealing to prospective federal regulatory changes.
