# AI Voice Cloning and the Right to One's Own Image: Analysis of the Landgericht Berlin II Judgment (2 O 202/24)
<Badge variant="outline">Jurisprudence · Germany · 2025</Badge>
<Callout type="info">
**Judicial Reference:** Landgericht Berlin II, Zivilkammer 2, Judgment of August 20, 2025, Case No. 2 O 202/24. Presiding Judge: Dr. Hagemeister, Vorsitzender Richter am Landgericht.
</Callout>
## Introduction: Why This Judgment Matters

On August 20, 2025, Landgericht Berlin II issued what may be considered one of the most significant rulings in Europe regarding the unauthorized commercial use of AI-generated voices. The court ordered the defendant—operator of a YouTube channel with 190,000 subscribers and an online store—to pay **€4,000 in fictitious license fees** (*fiktive Lizenzgebühr*) and an additional **€1,155.80 in out-of-court costs**, having used an AI-cloned voice that mimicked the claimant's voice without consent.

The judgment is significant for several reasons: it articulates for the first time—with dogmatic rigor—the legal equivalence between human voice imitation and AI cloning; it applies the right to reputation and one's own voice as a component of the *allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht* (general personality right under § 823 I BGB); and it expressly rules that neither the satirical format nor the acquisition of usage licenses from the AI provider constitute valid legal shields against the voice holder.

This analysis examines the judgment from a structured legal perspective using the IRAC methodology, draws its implications for Spanish and European law, and reflects on its projection within the framework of the Artificial Intelligence Act (RIA) and the GDPR.
## 1. Relevant Facts

The claimant is a well-known German dubbing actor who provides the voice for a famous Hollywood actor in the German versions of his films. The defendant operates a YouTube channel with satirical-political content and an online store linked to it. In two videos published on said channel, the defendant used as narration a voice generated by an AI-based voice cloning tool, whose similarity to the claimant's voice was sufficiently marked that commentators and viewers identified it as the voice of the dubbing actor.

After being sent a demand letter by the claimant's lawyers (September 2023), the defendant signed a cease-and-desist declaration (*Unterlassungserklärung*), but refused to pay the claimed compensation of €2,000 per video or the legal fees.

The claimant requested from the court:
1. €4,000 (€2,000 × 2 videos) for the unauthorized use of his voice.
2. €1,088.60 in out-of-court legal costs from the first attorneys.
3. €67.20 additional for the second out-of-court action.

The court fully upheld claims 1 and 3, and slightly reduced claim 2 to the verified taxable base, only dismissing the excess interest rate requested (9% versus the 5% recognized).
## 2. Legal Analysis (IRAC Methodology)
### 2.1. Principal Legal Issue (*Issue*)
> Does the commercial use of an AI-generated voice that mimics the voice of a known dubbing actor—without their consent—violate said actor's general personality right, and give rise to an unjust enrichment obligation under §§ 812 I S. 1 Fall 2 and 818 II BGB?
### 2.2. Applicable Legal Framework (*Rule*)
The court based its decision on the following norms and instruments:
**German Civil Law:**
- § 823 I BGB (*Schadensersatzpflicht*): Liability for violation of absolute rights, including the *allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht*.
- §§ 812 I S. 1 Fall 2 and 818 II BGB: Unjust enrichment action for interference in legally protected spheres (*Eingriffskondiktion*); the defendant must restore the objective value of the benefit.
- § 287 II ZPO: Judge's power to equitably quantify the fictitious license fee.
- § 308 ZPO: Judge cannot grant more than requested (*ne ultra petita*).
**Image and Personality Legislation:**
- §§ 22 and 23 KUG (*Kunsturhebergesetz*): Protection of the right to one's own image, applied analogously to the right to one's own voice, given that no specific regulation (*lex specialis*) exists for voice, unlike portraiture.
**Fundamental Right:**
- Art. 2 I in conjunction with Art. 1 I GG (German Basic Law): Human dignity and free development of personality as the constitutional foundation of the *allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht*.
**European Data Protection Law:**
- Art. 6 I GDPR: Legal bases for processing.
- Art. 6 I f) GDPR: Legitimate interest as a possible authorization, dismissed by the court as failing the balancing of interests test.
- Art. 85 GDPR: Journalistic or artistic exception, also dismissed.
**Leading Precedent:**
- BGH, 01.12.1999, I ZR 49/97 (*Marlene Dietrich*): The general personality right also protects proprietary interests linked to identity traits.
- BGH, 26.10.2006, I ZR 182/04: Satirical use of image or name of famous person.
- BGH, 21.01.2021, I ZR 120/19: Methodology for calculating fictitious license fees.
- OLG Hamburg, 08.05.1989, 3 W 45/89: Voice cloning of deceased comedian by an imitator; equivalence between imitation and voice reproduction.
**Scholarship:**
- Ellenberger, *Persönlichkeits- und Urheberrechte beim Voice Cloning*, RDi 2024, 599.
- Engel-Bunas, *Recht an der eigenen Stimme in Zeiten von Deepfakes*, RDi 2025, 292.
- Götting/Schertz/Seitz, *Handbuch Persönlichkeitsrecht*, 2nd ed. 2019, § 16 Rn. 20 ss.
### 2.3. Application (*Application*)
#### A) Interference with the Proprietary Content of the Right to One's Own Voice
The court starts from the established BGH doctrine according to which the *allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht* protects not only the moral-ideal dimension of the person, but also their proprietary interests (*vermögenswerte Interessen*): the name, the portrait, and—relevant here—the voice of a known person have economic value over which only the holder may decide.

The dogmatic key to the judgment lies in the following equivalence: **for legal purposes, it makes no difference whether the imitation of a voice is made by a human imitator or an AI tool**. What is decisive is not the technical means employed, but the effect on the public and on the holder's interests. The court notes that a non-negligible segment of viewers associated the video voice with the claimant as the dubbing actor, which establishes sufficient attribution confusion (*Zuordnungsverwirrng*) to affirm the interference.

In this regard, the judgment correctly notes that the average viewer did not identify the voice as that of the Hollywood actor himself (who is not German), but rather as that of the German dubber, whose voice is the relevant identification element for the German audience. This factual finding—corroborated by video comments expressly mentioning the claimant—is decisive.
#### B) Absence of Justifying Cause
The defendant raised several lines of defense, all rejected:
**First line: The voice is different.** The defendant argued that they had chosen, from the options offered by the AI software, a synthetic voice that resembled the actor's vocal range, but that it was not "their" voice. The court dismisses this argument by noting that what is legally relevant is sufficient similarity to create confusion, not absolute identity. The same logic applies to human imitators.
**Second line: License acquired from the AI provider.** The defendant argued having acquired usage rights (*Nutzungsrechte*) over the voice from the voice cloning tool's platform provider. The court rejects this thesis with an argument of significant practical impact: the AI provider cannot license rights it does not own. If the original voice holder did not consent to the provider creating a derived voice for commercial exploitation by third parties, no link in the chain can cure that absence of consent.
**Third line: Satirical use protected under freedom of expression (Art. 5 GG).** The court acknowledges that the videos contain a satirical component—political criticism of government management—but distinguishes between the *purpose* of satire and the *means* used to convey it. Political satire does not require using, without authorization, the voice of an identifiable third party; such use is not oriented toward satirizing the claimant himself or the Hollywood actor, but rather toward exploiting the voice's notoriety to attract traffic to the channel and, ultimately, to the online store. The commercial purpose is therefore predominant. The court applies § 23 II KUG analogously to conclude that, even in the realm of artistic freedom, the affected party's legitimate interests—neither to donate their voice for commercial purposes—prevail.
**Fourth line: GDPR route not applicable.** The judgment also examines the possibility of framing the conduct under biometric data processing (voice as personal data) under Art. 6 GDPR. It dismisses both the legitimate interest of Art. 6 I f) GDPR—which fails the balancing of interests test—and the journalistic or artistic exception of Art. 85 GDPR.
#### C) Quantification of the Fictitious License Fee
The quantification methodology follows BGH doctrine on fictitious license fees (*fiktive Lizenzgebühr*): it asks what price reasonable parties would have agreed upon in a hypothetical negotiation for the use in question. The court relies on testimonial evidence from an advertising agent who declared that the claimant is the highest-paid advertising voice in Germany and that his minimum fees for image advertising (functional equivalent to video) start at €1,800 for limited uses. Weighing the channel's size (190,000 subscribers), the absence of time limits on use, and the voice's notoriety, the court sets €2,000 per video, the amount requested by the claimant and supported by the evidence.
### 2.4. Conclusion (*Conclusion*)
The court concludes that the defendant violated the claimant's *allgemeines Persönlichkeitsrecht* in its proprietary dimension by using an AI-generated voice that mimicked the actor's voice without consent and for commercial purposes. This generates an unjust enrichment obligation quantified at €4,000 (§§ 812 I S. 1 Fall 2, 818 II BGB), as well as an obligation to repair damages through tort liability regarding legal costs (§ 823 I BGB i.V.m. Art. 2 I, 1 I GG).
## 3. Implications for Spanish and European Law
### 3.1. The Right to One's Own Voice in Spain
Spanish law lacks a specific norm equivalent to §§ 22 et seq. KUG for voice, but the right to one's own voice is implicitly recognized in Art. 7.6 of Organic Law 1/1982, on civil protection of the right to honor, personal and family privacy, and one's own image, which sanctions the capture, reproduction, or publication of a person's image or voice without their consent. The Supreme Court jurisprudence has extended the protection of Art. 18 CE (right to honor, privacy, and image) to voice as an element of personal identity (STS of May 11, 1989; STS of October 28, 2014).

The doctrine of the Berlin judgment is transferable to Spanish law without major dogmatic difficulties: AI voice cloning that generates confusion about attribution or endorsement of the holder may constitute illegitimate interference with the rights under Art. 7.6 LO 1/1982 when it has a commercial purpose.
### 3.2. Framing under the Artificial Intelligence Act (RIA)
The RIA (Regulation EU 2024/1689) does not directly address *voice cloning* with commercial purpose as a specific risk category, but it introduces two relevant vectors:
- **Transparency on synthetic content (Art. 50 RIA):** Providers of AI systems that generate synthetic audio content are required to ensure said content is marked as artificially generated in a machine-detectable manner. This obligation, whose non-compliance may aggravate liability, would have been relevant in the analyzed case: the court notes that the videos did not indicate the voice was AI-generated.
- **General-purpose AI systems (GPAI, Arts. 51-56 RIA):** AI models serving as the basis for voice cloning tools may be subject to transparency obligations on training data and safeguards against copyright and personality rights violations.
### 3.3. Voice as Biometric Data under the GDPR
Voice is biometric data when processed to uniquely identify a person (Art. 9 GDPR). Training voice cloning models from actor recordings without their consent could constitute processing of special categories of biometric data, requiring an enhanced legal basis. The LG Berlin II judgment points in this direction, although the GDPR is not the primary basis for the conviction.
## 4. Practical Takeaways

<Callout type="warning">
**For platform operators and content creators** using voice cloning or voice synthesis tools:

1. Acquiring usage licenses from the AI provider **does not exempt** obtaining consent from the original voice holder.

2. Satirical or entertainment format **does not automatically protect** against the holder if the use has a predominant commercial purpose.

3. Absence of marking as AI-generated content may aggravate liability.

4. Economic exposure is not limited to compensation: it includes out-of-court and procedural legal costs.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info">
**For rights holders** (actors, announcers, dubbers):

1. Voice is a protected proprietary asset, regardless of whether an image contract exists.

2. The fictitious license fee is quantified based on the market value of the voice, not actual proven losses.

3. The burden of proof on attribution confusion can be satisfied with user comments on digital platforms.
</Callout>
## 5. Critical Assessment
The judgment deserves an overall positive assessment from the standpoint of personality rights protection against generative AI. Its main dogmatic contribution is the **technological neutrality of the analysis**: the court refuses to create a special or more permissive regime for AI-generated voices compared to human-imitated ones. This position is consistent with the principle that technology should not alter the level of protection of fundamental rights.

However, the judgment raises questions that higher courts will need to resolve:
- What is the exact threshold of similarity required to establish interference? The court speaks of "a non-negligible segment" of the public that associates the voice, but does not quantify the percentage.
- Can the AI provider be jointly liable alongside the user if they trained their model with recordings without consent? The judgment does not address liability in the AI supply chain.
- What happens when the cloned voice does not belong to a professional with verifiable fees? The fictitious license fee methodology will need to be adapted to cases where the holder is not a public or commercial figure.
## Conclusion
The Landgericht Berlin II judgment of August 20, 2025 establishes a precedent of primary importance: **the use of an AI-cloned voice for commercial purposes, without the holder's consent, violates the general personality right and generates an obligation to restore unjust enrichment**. The equivalence between human imitation and AI cloning, the dismissal of the license argument against the AI provider, and the rejection of the satirical exemption when a commercial purpose is involved are the three pillars of a solid legal reasoning exportable to other European legal systems, including Spanish.

In a context where voice cloning tools are increasingly accessible and precise, this ruling sends a clear signal to content creators, platforms, and AI providers: technology is not a license to dispense with the consent of persons whose voice—or vocal identity—is exploited for profit.
