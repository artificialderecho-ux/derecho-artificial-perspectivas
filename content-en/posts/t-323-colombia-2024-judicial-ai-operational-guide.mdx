# T-323 Judgment of 2024: Operational Guide for Colombian Judges

## Situation: Colombia Issues First Constitutional Judgment on AI in Justice

On **October 23, 2024**, the **Constitutional Court of Colombia** issued **Judgment T-323**, the first in-depth constitutional ruling in Latin America on the use of AI by judges in decision-making.

**The case**: A labor judge used ChatGPT 3.5 to "extend arguments" of a decision regarding the rights of a person with autism.

**What happened**: The Court said "the decision was not annulled (because it was made BEFORE using AI), BUT the judge violated transparency and accountability."

---

## The Verdict: "Permitted, But Under 12 Principles"

### Can a Judge Use ChatGPT?

**Yes, BUT:**
- ✅ For administrative management, case law research, text synthesis
- ✅ NEVER to make the decision
- ✅ Permitted AFTER justifying the decision
- ❌ NOT permitted BEFORE or AS THE BASIS of decision

### The Golden Rule

"AI is a tool, it never decides"

If the decision is made by AI without a judge, it will be **invalid** and there will be a violation of due process.

---

## The 12 Binding Operational Principles

The Court established **12 principles that EVERY judge must comply with** if using AI:

### 1. Transparency (CRITICAL)
**Judge MUST explain:**
- That they used AI
- Why they used it
- How they used it
- What capabilities it has
- What limitations it has
- What data they input
- Where results appear in the decision

**NOT sufficient:** Saying "I used ChatGPT"

**T-323 case:** The judge failed here. They only mentioned they used AI.

### 2. Accountability
**Judge MUST:**
- Be trained
- Understand risks
- Verify AI information
- Ensure information is real, appropriate for the matter, respectful of facts
- Verify AI is trained with recent Colombian data

### 3. Privacy
**PROHIBITED:**
- ❌ Input personal data into ChatGPT
- ❌ Input sensitive data (health, sexual orientation, ethnicity)
- ❌ Use tools not authorized for justice

**T-323 case:** The judge DID comply with this.

### 4. Non-Substitution
**PROHIBITED:**
- ❌ Let AI interpret facts
- ❌ Let AI assess evidence
- ❌ Let AI justify the decision
- ❌ Let AI apply the norm to the case

### 5-12. Additional Principles
5. **Seriousness and verification** - Exhaustively review sources and risks
6. **Risk prevention** - Identify hallucinations, biases, outdated information
7. **Equality and equity** - Not allow AI biases to cause discrimination
8. **Human control** - Human authorities always have the final word
9. **Ethical regulation** - Apply ethical standards of conduct
10. **Good practices** - Follow Judicial Branch standards
11. **Continuous monitoring** - Adapt to technological and legal changes
12. **Idoneity** - AI should facilitate access to justice, not hinder it

---

## The 3 Threats to Due Process

The Court identified **3 procedural guarantees** that AI can violate:

### 1. Natural Judge
**Risk:** AI replaces or biases the judge

**Protection:** Judge must be human, pre-established, independent, impartial

### 2. Decision Reasoning
**Risk:** AI produces "hallucinations" (fictional information)
- Non-existent jurisprudence
- Non-existent laws
- Invented facts

**Protection:** Judge must exhaustively verify everything

### 3. Evidentiary Due Process
**Risk:** AI decides which evidence to admit/reject

**Protection:** Evidence decree and evaluation is the EXCLUSIVE competence of the judge

---

## What Went Wrong in the T-323 Case

The judge:

| ❌ FAILED | ✅ COMPLIED |
|---------|-----------|
| Was not transparent about use | Did not input personal data |
| Did not explain why they used AI | Used AI AFTER deciding |
| Included "not entirely accurate" AI data | Acknowledged AI is not specialized |
| Did not verify AI information | Did not use AI to make decision |
| Did not consider lack of specialization in Colombian law | - |

---

## What the Court Requires If You Use AI

### Step 1: Justify and Make the Decision
Resolve the case without AI. Define thesis, applicable norms, jurisprudence.

### Step 2: (OPTIONAL) Use AI to Extend
If you want to use AI after deciding, you can:
- Expand arguments
- Search for additional jurisprudence
- Summarize texts
- Correct writing

### Step 3: Verify EVERYTHING
Before including anything from AI in the decision:
- Verify each reference exists
- Verify jurisprudence says what AI says
- Explicitly identify inconsistencies

### Step 4: Be Transparent
In the judicial decision, include:
- That you used AI
- Why you used it
- How you used it
- What limitations it has
- Where its results appear
- That you verified everything

### Step 5: Identify Responsible Person
There must be "an identifiable and identifiable person" whom parties can address regarding AI.

---

## What the Court Explicitly PROHIBITS

### PROHIBITED: 5 Uses

❌ **Use AI to interpret facts**
- You cannot delegate to AI the evaluation of what happened in the case

❌ **Use AI to assess evidence**
- You cannot delegate to AI what evidence is relevant

❌ **Use AI to apply norm to case**
- You cannot delegate to AI the subsumption "norm X applies to case Y"

❌ **Use AI as basis for decision** (without judge above)
- If AI is the source of your decision (even if you edit it later), it is invalid

❌ **Use external tools for personal or sensitive data**
- You cannot pour briefs, dossiers, expert reports, health data into ChatGPT

---

## What Happens If You Don't Comply

### Implicit Consequences

1. **Nullity of decision** - Judge must have properly justified it
2. **Disciplinary liability** - Non-compliance with official duties
3. **Successful tutela action** - Parties can allege due process violation
4. **Extraordinary appeal** - Enhanced challengeability

**Note:** Judgment does not specify explicitly, but the logic is clear.

## T-323's Unanswered Questions

### Question 1: What Exactly Is "Hallucination"?
Judgment does not give a clear technical definition. How does a judge identify one?

### Question 2: Is ChatGPT Prohibited or Not?
The Court does not explicitly prohibit it, but imposes so many burdens that use becomes impractical.

### Question 3: What Responsibility Does OpenAI Have?
The Court says "OpenAI warned users to verify." Is that sufficient?

### Question 4: Is Own Platform Mandatory?
The Court encourages it, but does not order it. Cost would be enormous.

## Conclusion: A Clear But Demanding Framework

The T-323 Judgment says:

"AI is welcome in justice, but as a tool under extraordinary judicial watch. Not as a substitute. Not as an oracle. Not as decision automation."

For Colombian judges (and Latin American judges who adopt this framework):
- AI is permitted, but under 12 binding principles
- Transparency is constitutional, not optional
- Hallucinations and biases are real risks, not theoretical
- Accountability is personal: You cannot blame OpenAI
- Specialization matters: ChatGPT is not specialized in Colombian law
- Verification is mandatory: Every AI data point must be cross-checked
