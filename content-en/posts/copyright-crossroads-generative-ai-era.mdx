The document analyzes the impact of generative artificial intelligence on the traditional pillars of copyright. It examines the tensions between massive training of models on protected content and the exclusive rights of rights holders, as well as the text and data mining (TDM) exceptions introduced by Directive (EU) 2019/790. The author highlights that the conflict is not limited to the training moment, but extends to the model output, where substantial fragments of others' works or high-fidelity imitative styles may emerge.

### Fundamental Axes of Analysis

1. **Learning Model and Black Box:** It explains how generative AI models, through deep neural networks, transform protected works into high-dimensional mathematical representations. Although the model weights are not readable copies of the works, the opacity of the black box complicates the verification of unauthorized uses and the detection of biases, which increases the evidentiary burden in infringement litigation.

2. **TDM Exceptions and Opt-out:** The text compares the data mining regime in the EU with the American fair use approach. It emphasizes that Articles 3 and 4 of the DSM Directive allow TDM, but grant rights holders the faculty to reserve this right through machine-readable exclusion mechanisms. Ignoring these reservations places developers in a position of significant legal vulnerability.

3. **Originality and Human Authorship:** International decisions on computer-generated works (United Kingdom, India, South Africa) and paradigmatic cases such as DABUS are reviewed. The essay defends that the core of protection must remain anchored in human creative contribution, shifting protection toward hybrid models where AI functions as an instrument of experimentation, but not as a subject of rights.

### Practical Risks and Shielding Proposals

The text identifies several operational risks: regurgitation of specific works, involuntary activation of copyleft licenses, deepfakes and creative identity impersonation, as well as the economic hollowing out of copyright if models are fed on catalogs without compensation. As a counterweight, shielding measures are proposed such as:

- Documenting human intervention (human-in-the-loop) in prompts and editing.
- Establishing dataset traceability systems and exclusion of reserved works.
- Creating collective compensation mechanisms for rights holders whose catalogs feed high-commercial-impact models.

### Conclusion

The piece concludes that the crossroads of copyright in the era of generative AI requires a careful rebalancing between innovation incentives and safeguarding the creative ecosystem. The law cannot limit itself to reacting to each isolated controversy, but must anticipate normative architectures that recognize the systemic character of AI. Ultimately, the challenge is to ensure that algorithms do not erode the economic and moral dignity of creators, but rather operate as tools in service of strengthened and fairly rewarded human creativity.
