# Judicial Protocol for Artificial Intelligence Implementation

## Essential Guide for Judges on AI Systems in Courts

> **Note:** 
    <strong>Regulatory basis:</strong> This protocol integrates provisions from the UNESCO Recommendation on AI Ethics (2021), the EU AI Act (2024), GDPR, and LOPDGDD, articulating frameworks for responsible AI use in judicial decisions and proceedings.
  

---

## I. FUNDAMENTAL DEFINITIONS AND CONCEPTS

### 1.1 What is Artificial Intelligence in the Judicial Context?

**Operational definition:** Computational system that processes data and information in a way that simulates intelligent behavior, including aspects of reasoning, learning, prediction, and control.

**Applications in justice:**
- **Administrative support:** Automation of routine tasks (calendar management, hearing transcription, document translation)
- **Document analysis:** Keyword search, pattern analysis, document synthesis, relevant case law search
- **Decision support:** Fact summary, data analysis, case law review, judgment drafting assistance

**Applicable regulatory framework:**
- Articles 3 and 4 of the AI Act (AI system definitions and risk classification)
- Principles of the UNESCO Recommendation on AI Ethics
- UNESCO Guidelines for AI Use in Courts

### 1.2 Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI)

**Definition:** Systems capable of generating original content (text, images, code) from large volumes of data through specific instructions ('prompts').

**Specific risks in judicial context:**
- Hallucinations: Generation of fictitious information or non-existent legal citations
- Confidentiality: Exposure of personal data trained into the model
- Bias: Reproduction of prejudices present in training data
- Lack of traceability: Inability to audit the decision-making process

**Permitted applications:** Exploratory analysis, preliminary drafting (under strict judicial oversight)

**Restricted applications:** Participation in definitive decisions, sensitive data analysis without validation

### 3.2 Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment (FRIA)

**Analysis matrix for AI systems in judicial decisions:**

| Fundamental right | Potential risks | Safeguard measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Right to impartial judge** | Magistrate profiling; systemic model biases | Judicial identity anonymization; impartiality auditing |
| **Right to defense** | Lack of transparency on AI involvement; impossibility to counter-argument | Detailed report on AI role; right to request human review |
| **Equality before law** | Indirect discrimination from training data biases | Differential impact analysis; non-discrimination testing |
| **Access to justice** | Excessive automation closes review opportunities | Guarantee of appeal to human judge; clear procedures |
| **Privacy** | Sensitive data leaks; litigant profiling | Encryption; data minimization; access auditing |
| **Judicial independence** | Algorithmic pressure on decisions; public trust | Significant human oversight; AI criteria publicity |

## IV. USE PROTOCOL: OPERATIONAL BEST PRACTICES

### 4.1 Control and Validation Chain

**Step 1: Anticipatory vigilance**
- Maintain critical attention during system use
- Verify relevance of result to specific case
- Do not assume automaticity of recommendation

**Step 2: Output verification**
- Actively review legal citations (detect GenAI hallucinations)
- Compare system summaries with original documents
- Validate factual data before incorporating into decision

**Step 3: Reasonableness analysis**
- Evaluate whether system conclusion is consistent with existing case law
- Identify potential biases in analysis
- Consider exceptions or peculiar circumstances not captured by AI

**Step 4: Participation documentation**
- Record which AI modules were used
- Document how they influenced judicial reasoning
- Maintain evidence of human review and validation

### 4.2 Sensitive Data Handling

**Strict prohibitions:**
- ❌ Do not share personal data or case files with public GenAI platforms
- ❌ Do not use public Wi-Fi to access judicial AI systems
- ❌ Do not process minor data without robust anonymization measures
- ❌ Do not use unaudited systems for gender-based violence, trafficking, terrorism cases

**Mandatory practices:**
- ✓ Use only devices with updated operating systems
- ✓ Connect via VPN in secure private networks
- ✓ Apply minimization principle: only data essential for specific function
- ✓ Maintain audit log of who accesses what data and when
- ✓ Anonymize litigant data in exploratory analysis

### 4.3 Hallucination and Error Management

**Definition:** Generation of fictitious information, non-existent citations, or invented case law by AI systems, especially GenAI.

**Detection protocol:**

| Warning indicator | Recommended action |
|---|---|
| Citation to ruling without date or identifiable court | Verification in case law portal (Constitutional Court, CGPJ, CENDOJ) |
| Reference to legal article not found in Official Gazette | Direct search in current legislation |
| Case law synthesis contradicts known rulings | Re-reading of original sources |
| Factual summary omits relevant case facts | Systematic comparison with procedural documents |
| Analysis applies another jurisdiction's regulations | Verification of territorial scope |

**Correction protocol:**
1. Do not incorporate error into judicial decision
2. Document in internal records: error type, AI module, date
3. Report to system administrator for retraining
4. Notify AEPD if error affected already-issued decision

## V. RESTRICTIONS AND PROHIBITED APPLICATIONS

### 5.1 Judicial Decision Prediction Systems

**Legal problem:** Some providers offer systems that "predict" how a specific judge would rule based on decision history.

**Legal status:**
- **Prohibited under GDPR Art. 22** (solely automated decision-making with profiling)
- **Prohibited under AI Act Art. 5** (social profiling restricting rights)
- **Violates judicial independence** (Bangalore Principles of Judicial Conduct)

**Protection measures:**
- Ruling anonymization (France: Art. L111-13 Judicial Organization Code)
- Prohibition of judge profiling system commercialization
- Administrative sanctions by AEPD in case of violation

### 5.2 Remote Biometric Identification in Hearings

**Restriction:** Real-time facial identification during trial proceedings.

**Very limited exceptions:**
- Public security: search for persons convicted of terrorism/serious crime
- Must be previously notified to parties
- Requires specific judicial resolution
- Subject to court supervision

### 5.3 Automated Witness Credibility Analysis

**Problem:** Systems that analyze body language, voice patterns, facial expression to "detect" lies.

**Restriction reason:**
- Contested scientific reliability
- Violates right to defense (witness cannot respond to technical accusations)
- Replaces judicial evaluation of testimony
- High risk of cultural/ethnic bias

**Conclusion:** Not permissible in judicial decision.

## VI. TRANSPARENCY AND ACCOUNTABILITY

### 6.1 Duty to Inform Litigants

**When to inform:**
- ✓ AI system participated in case instruction/analysis
- ✓ AI recommendation influenced decision
- ✓ Personal data processed by system

**How to inform:**
- Clear reference in ruling: "Document analysis was assisted by AI system [name/type]"
- Identification of specific tasks: "Search for related case law"
- Clarification: "The decision in all aspects was made by the judge, who validated/rejected system recommendations"

**Language model:**
> "In the instruction of this proceeding, a document analysis system based on artificial intelligence was used to locate relevant case law. Said system identified [X] rulings, which were analyzed by the court. This resolution is the exclusive result of judicial reasoning, having critically validated technical results and discarded those not applicable to the specific case."

### 6.2 Appeal and Review Mechanisms

**Fundamental right:** Pursuant to Art. 22 GDPR and Art. 86 AI Act, parties must be able to request:

1. **Significant explanation:** How AI participated in decision, what data it analyzed, what recommendations it generated
2. **Human review:** Right to obtain reconsideration by judge without AI involvement
3. **Appeal:** Access to appeal before higher court (guarantee of appellate review)

**Procedure:**
- Litigant requests "Review of decision based on automated systems" within deadline
- Judge conducts de novo analysis without using AI system outputs
- Decision based on pure judicial reasoning
- Documentation of both reviews in case file

## VII. SIGNIFICANT HUMAN OVERSIGHT

### 7.1 "Significance" Standard

**Regulatory definition (AI Act Art. 14):** Human capability to:
- **Understand** system logic and limitations
- **Monitor** outputs in real-time
- **Intervene** before result impacts the data subject
- **Act** based on their intervention (not merely confirmatory)

### 7.2 Incompatibility of Oversight

**NOT significant oversight:**
- ❌ Automatically reviewing outputs without carefully reading them
- ❌ "Clicking accept" on system results without analysis
- ❌ Merely formal oversight (fulfills requirement without substance)
- ❌ Supervisor cognitive overload (too many cases to adequately review)

**IS significant oversight:**
- ✓ Careful reasonableness analysis before incorporating into decision
- ✓ Active validation of data/citations generated by system
- ✓ Real possibility of rejecting recommendation if unconvincing
- ✓ Documentation of critical thinking applied
- ✓ Sufficient dedicated time and resources

## IX. SPECIFIC RISK ASSESSMENT

### 9.1 Risk Matrix by Case Type

| Procedure type | Bias risk | Technical error risk | Confidentiality risk | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Low-value civil (property)** | Low | Low-Medium | Medium | Assisted document analysis permitted |
| **Family civil** | HIGH | Low | HIGH | Very strict oversight; robust anonymization |
| **Criminal (minors)** | HIGH | Medium | CRITICAL | Very limited use; prior judicial authorization |
| **Gender-based violence** | HIGH | Low | CRITICAL | Specialized oversight; highly sensitive data |
| **Human trafficking** | HIGH | Low | CRITICAL | Do not use public GenAI; closed judicial system |
| **Terrorism** | Medium | Low | CRITICAL | Maximum data protection; external auditing |

### 9.2 Early Warning Indicators

**If you observe these patterns, reconsider system use:**

- 🚨 System results consistently harsher with identifiable demographic group
- 🚨 Frequent hallucinations or incorrect case law citations
- 🚨 System suggests punishments/decisions outside typical range for similar case
- 🚨 Litigants report lack of transparency or misunderstanding of how AI influenced
- 🚨 Successful appeals because system made factual/legal error
- 🚨 Third parties (AEPD, ombudsman) express concern about system

## X. RESPONSIBILITY AND SANCTIONS

### 10.1 Judge Responsibility

**Civil liability:**
- Use of AI resulting in discrimination/violation of rights
- Negligence in human oversight (not verifying outputs)
- Lack of transparency toward litigants

**Disciplinary liability:**
- Violation of judicial independence (being "governed" by AI recommendation)
- Failure to comply with impartiality duty
- Violation of right to defense due to lack of information

**Criminal liability (in serious cases):**
- Participation in discrimination by race/gender (crime, if intentional)
- Prevarication (issuing AI decision knowing it lacks factual basis)

### 10.2 Judicial Body Responsibility

**Obligations:**
- Evaluate systems before deployment
- Provide adequate training
- Supervise and audit ongoing use
- Implement complaint mechanisms

**Sanctions (AEPD):**
- Fine up to €10 million or 2% annual revenue (GDPR)
- Fine up to €30 million or 4% annual revenue (AI Act for prohibited systems)
- Orders to cease system operation
- External auditing obligation

## XI. PRACTICAL CASE: RULING WITH AI SUPPORT

### 11.1 Example of Responsible Drafting

```
FACTUAL BACKGROUND
[... facts...]

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND
[... procedure...]

LEGAL GROUNDS

I. PARTICIPATION OF ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE SYSTEMS IN THIS RESOLUTION

In order to guarantee transparency pursuant to Article 22 GDPR and Article 86 
of the EU AI Act, the following is informed:

In the instruction of this matter, a document analysis system based on 
machine learning, specifically [system name/type], was used to locate 
case law related to the contested legal issues. Said system identified 
a total of [X] potentially relevant rulings from the Supreme Court and 
Provincial Courts.

The court proceeded to critically and actively review each of the identified 
rulings. Of the [X] references, the court considered [Y] applicable to the 
specific case, after analyzing:
  - Factual similarity to the present matter
  - Case law doctrine developed in series of rulings
  - Evolution of case law criteria
  - Possible distinctions regarding case circumstances

The court expressly rejected [Z] of the references because [specific for each 
rejection], considering them inapplicable or insufficiently persuasive.

II. LEGAL ANALYSIS (BASED ON JUDICIAL REASONING)

[... substantive legal analysis, where reasoning is entirely judicial, 
though it may be informed by AI-assisted document analysis...]

CONCLUSIONS

The court, in exercising its decisory power and after autonomous and 
independent analysis of facts and law, ORDERS...
```

### 11.2 Errors to Avoid

❌ **Incorrect:**
> "The AI system determined that the facts of the case are X"
> [Suggests AI made the decision]

✓ **Correct:**
> "After document analysis, the court determines that the proven facts are X"
> [AI was the tool, not the decision-maker]

## XIII. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### 13.1 Synthesis of Fundamental Principles

1. **AI is a tool, not a decision-maker:** The judge retains full authority and responsibility
2. **Significant human oversight is mandatory:** Not merely confirmatory
3. **Total transparency:** Litigants must know how AI participated
4. **Appeal rights preserved:** Access to human review without AI
5. **Enhanced data protection:** Judicial data are a special category
6. **Prior risk assessment:** Before deployment, not after

### 13.2 Final Checklist for Judges

Before each AI use in a case:

- [ ] Have I verified the system is authorized by my judicial body?
- [ ] Have I explicitly identified what tasks I delegated to AI?
- [ ] Have I critically reviewed each output before using it?
- [ ] Have I detected potential biases or errors?
- [ ] Will I transparently inform the parties?
- [ ] Can I ground this decision without relying on AI?
- [ ] Have I documented my independent reasoning?
- [ ] Does this resolution reflect my judicial discretion?
