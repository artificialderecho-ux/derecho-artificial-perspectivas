## Introduction: From the AI Act to Professional Liability

The integration of artificial intelligence in legal practice has transcended the technical experimentation phase to become an imperative of **professional responsibility and due diligence**. Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act) establishes a uniform regulatory framework that obligates law firms to critically evaluate the risks arising from the deployment of AI systems.

This guide provides a multidimensional analysis of AI Act compliance in the legal profession, encompassing:

- **Legal and technical foundations**: key definitions, risk classification
- **Technology ecosystem**: LegalTech tools, AI solution lifecycle
- **Deontological risks**: opaque algorithms, hallucinations, algorithmic bias
- **Regulatory framework**: AI Act as a product safety standard
- **Technical mitigation**: reliable architectures, RAG, XAI, post-hoc verification
- **Governance**: internal protocols, universal human review, training

---

## Chapter 1: Foundations - The Challenge of Truthfulness in Digital Legal Practice

### 1.1 Purpose and Scope of the Guide

Technological competence is an **unavoidable requirement** for modern professional practice. The AI Act encompasses the entire lifecycle of AI systems employed in law firms: from selection and training to post-market supervision and auditing.

### 1.2 Key Definitions under the AI Act

**AI System**: a machine designed to operate with **varying levels of autonomy**, capable of exhibiting adaptation after deployment.

**Inference Capability**: the technical faculty of deducing models or algorithms from data, enabling learning, reasoning, or factual modeling.

**General-Purpose AI Models (GPAi)**: architectures with a significant degree of **generality**, capable of performing a wide variety of differentiated tasks.

**Deployer**: a natural or legal person who uses an AI system under their own authority in the course of professional activity.

### 1.3 The Fundamental Dichotomy: Generative AI vs. Consultive AI

**Generative AI (General Purpose)**: operates through probabilistic prediction of the next token, optimizing fluency and conversational coherence. It is **inherently prone to hallucinations**—generation of plausible but false information—due to its design that prioritizes avoiding silence.

**Consultive AI (Specialized)**: based on Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architectures, acts as an **expert assistant** that cites verifiable sources, enabling traceability and human verification.

### 1.4 Strategic Adaptation Methodology

Adaptation requires multidimensional evaluation of:

1. **Risk Levels**: classification of tools into "High Risk" vs. "Limited Risk" categories
2. **Non-Substitution Principle**: technology as an instrumental complement; legal decision-making remains inalienably human
3. **Opacity Management**: implementation of technical measures against "black box" effects
4. **AI Literacy**: obligation to ensure firm personnel understand risks and limitations

## Chapter 2: Technology Ecosystem - Classification and Lifecycle

### 2.1 AI Functionalities in the Firm

Implementation is not monolithic but bifurcated into two functional categories:

- **Analytical/Predictive AI**: pattern detection in court decisions, information classification, risk assessment
- **Generative AI**: assisted drafting, content synthesis, contractual document generation

### 2.2 Relevant Tools Map

**Analytics Solutions**: vLex Analytics, Jurimetría, Tirant Analytics

**Management and Transcription**: Copilot, Fireflies.ai, DigalawX

**Assisted Research**: Vincent AI (vLex), Iberley IA, CoCounsel (Thomson Reuters), Justicio

**General-Purpose AI**: ChatGPT, Claude (with elevated risk levels without retrieval integration)

### 2.3 Legal AI Solution Lifecycle

```
1. Conception and Analysis → functional requirements and regulatory constraints
2. Development → ML training, data validation, testing
3. Operation → deployment, inference, evolutionary maintenance, recalibration
4. Retirement → data suppression per GDPR
```

### 2.4 Technical Integration: LLM and Deep Learning

The predominant architecture in digital legal practice is based on **Deep Learning**, emulating artificial neural networks. **Large Language Models (LLM)** probabilistically predict the next token in a sequence.

The most reliable integration is **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)**: a retrieval module identifies relevant fragments in an authorized legal corpus; this data enriches the prompt injected into the LLM to ensure responses are **strictly grounded** in verifiable sources.

## Chapter 3: Multidimensional Risk Analysis and Deontological Impact

### 3.1 Systemic Risks: Opacity, Biases, Hallucinations

**Opaque Algorithms ("Black Box")**: architectures where the underlying logic and decision-making process are incomprehensible to humans, compromising the traceability required by the AI Act.

**Hallucinations**: an endemic pathology of LLMs that generates linguistically coherent but factually false or legally unfounded information. They are classified as:
- *Factual/Extrinsic*: fabrication of non-existent authorities or precedents
- *Grounding (Misgrounding)*: citation of real sources with interpretations those sources do not support or contradict
- *Algorithmic Bias*: violation of fundamental rights arising from non-representative training data that encode historical prejudices

### 3.2 Judicial Precedents on Negligence

**Case Mata v. Avianca (S.D.N.Y. 2023)**: attorneys were sanctioned for filing briefs with six fabricated court citations from a chatbot, demonstrating that asking the AI itself about its veracity is **manifestly insufficient**.

**Spanish Constitutional Court Sanction (Spain, Note 90/2024)**: it declared that **attorney liability is absolute and independent of the tool**, maintaining the obligation to exhaustively review all content before court filing.

### 3.3 Technological Competence Obligation

Established as a new deontological pillar: attorneys must possess **sufficient AI literacy** to critically evaluate the risks of systems employed.

Professional due diligence requires not only *ex ante* reliability analysis but mandatory *ex post* examination of each result, based on the **principle of human reserve**: professional judgment is irreducible and cannot be substituted by automation.

### 3.4 Professional Secrecy and Privacy: Cloud Computing Risks

**External Government Access**: foreign regulations (Cloud Act) may enable access to data stored on servers outside national/European jurisdiction.

**Data Leaks**: risk that sensitive client information may be reused by models to generate responses for other users.

**Impact Assessment (EIPD)**: mandatory before deploying AI systems that process data of natural persons, ensuring integrity and confidentiality per Art. 35 GDPR.

## Chapter 4: Regulatory Framework - The AI Act as Product Safety Standard

### 4.1 Risk Classification under the AI Act

Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 establishes a **layered risk approach**:

1. **Unacceptable Risk**: systems employing subliminal or manipulative techniques that alter human behavior or informed decision-making capacity. Prohibited.

2. **High Risk**: subject to mandatory security requirements and prior conformity assessment. Includes AI systems for judicial authorities or assistance in investigating/interpreting facts and law.

3. **Limited Risk**: systems subject to transparency obligations (must be labeled as AI-generated).

4. **Minimal or No Risk**: exempt from specific obligations beyond general regulatory compliance.

### 4.2 The Legal Profession and "High Risk" Systems

Legal practice is directly implicated by the **High Risk** category. Systems intended for judicial assistance or legal interpretation have **potential impact on effective judicial protection and impartial judge**.

The regulation prescribes that use of these tools must be limited to **support functions**, establishing that **final decision-making is an irreducably human activity** that cannot be substituted by AI. Analytical tools intended to influence judicial strategy or predict case outcomes must be audited under this standard, ensuring opacity does not limit the right to defense.

### 4.3 Roles and Responsibilities: Deployer vs. Provider

**Deployer**: a natural or legal person who uses an AI system under their own authority. Assumes **subjective liability for lack of due diligence** in supervising results, being responsible for validating, correcting, and, where applicable, correcting hallucinations or system errors before incorporation into legal services.

**Provider**: developer of an AI system intended for introduction onto the market under their own name or trademark.

### 4.4 AI Act - GDPR Synergy: Data Protection by Design

The AI Act complements (does not repeal) the General Data Protection Regulation. Operational integration:

**Impact Assessment (EIPD)**: deployers use the provider's technical documentation (Art. 13 AI Act) to fulfill data protection impact obligations (Art. 35 GDPR).

**Data Quality and Governance**: training datasets must be relevant, representative, and free of errors. Exceptionally, processing of **special categories of data** (Art. 9 GDPR) is permitted only when strictly necessary for detecting and correcting algorithmic bias, always applying enhanced safeguards and avoiding re-identification.

**Transparency and Data Subject Rights**: the right to **clear and meaningful explanation** about the role of AI in automated decisions producing legal effects or significantly affecting individuals is guaranteed.

## Chapter 5: Technical Mitigation - RAG and Reliable AI Architectures

### 5.1 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

Prescribed abandonment of the **general-purpose generative AI** paradigm ("creative oracle") in favor of **specialized consultive AI** ("expert file").

**RAG Mechanism**:
1. **Retrieval**: identification of chunks from an external, curated, and authorized document corpus
2. **Generation**: enrichment of the prompt with retrieved context to ensure **strictly grounded** response

Empirically, RAG reduces hallucinations compared to base models, although it maintains residual error rates (17%-34%), requiring continuous optimization of the knowledge base.

### 5.2 Normative Hierarchy in Legal AI Agents

Legal AI agent architectures must integrate **Hans Kelsen's normative hierarchy** to ensure legal validity of inferences. A system lacking hierarchical consciousness is inherently prone to "invalidity hallucination" by grounding responses on lower-ranking norms invalidated by superior norms (laws by Constitution).

**Mitigation**:
1. **Structured knowledge bases**: tagging documents with metadata reflecting hierarchical rank
2. **Hierarchy-sensitive retrieval algorithms**: systematic prioritization of higher-authority sources
3. **Kelsenian validation modules**: mandatory verification that all proposed interpretations are coherent with higher-ranking norms

### 5.3 Explainability and Interpretability (XAI)

In response to opaque algorithms, **Explainable AI (XAI)** is prescribed as a prerequisite for effective judicial protection. Progressive development of technical maturity:

- **Grounded Responses**: ability to anchor and cite each assertion in a verifiable source (basic traceability)
- **Reasoned Responses**: externalization of logical inference steps, demonstrating a coherent reasoning chain
- **Reasoned Interpretation**: advanced level where AI justifies why it chooses a specific interpretation over other plausible alternatives, weighing matrices and ambiguities

### 5.4 Post-Hoc Verification: Automated Factual Checking Modules

Given the technical impossibility of completely eliminating hallucinations during the generation phase, implementing **post-hoc verification mechanisms** as the "last line of defense" is mandatory.

**Automated Fact-Checking**: comparison of generated assertions against canonical knowledge bases (case law, official doctrine, public registers) with derogation metadata.

**Syntactic and Logical Validation**: use of deterministic heuristics to verify citation format and temporal coherence of cited decisions.

**Transparent Uncertainty Communication**: systems present **calibrated confidence scores** and employ "strategic silence" (justified abstention) when retrieved information is ambiguous or insufficient, avoiding automation bias propensity.

**Fragment-Level Citation**: unambiguous linkage of each conclusion to the exact passage from the source that supports it, facilitating expert human supervision.

## Chapter 6: Governance and Action Plan - Toward a "Safe-by-Design" Firm

### 6.1 Internal Control Protocols

Imperative implementation of effective **information governance** based on **proactive accountability**.

**Internal Control Protocols**:
- (i) Taxative definition of authorized technological tools and their specific purposes
- (ii) Designation of supervisors with authority and technical competence
- (iii) Traceability through documentary records of each relevant AI-assisted decision

Records must include in detail: algorithms employed, training data (when the firm acts as provider), result evaluation criteria.

### 6.2 "Human Reserve" Principle: Universal Human Review Mandate

**Human supervision constitutes the vertebral axis** of responsible AI integration in the legal profession, being an **indispensable safeguard** to prevent or minimize risks to fundamental rights.

**Universal Human Review Mandate** is prescribed: all information or draft generated by an AI system must be reviewed, validated, and, where applicable, corrected by a legal professional before incorporation into legal services or filing with courts.

The "Human Reserve" principle establishes that final legal decision-making is an **irreducible and irreplaceable human activity**: technology is an support tool, but cannot replace the attorney's professional judgment. Constitutional jurisprudence prescribes that **attorney liability is absolute and independent of the tool**, maintaining the obligation to exhaustively review all content before court filing.

Delegating the essential part of intellectual work or substantive legal decision-making to AI is prohibited.

### 6.3 Literacy and Continuous Training

**AI literacy** has ceased to be a technical recommendation to become a **legal obligation** (Art. 4 EU Regulation 2024/1689).

**Continuous and Updated Training Plans** will enable personnel to:
- (i) Understand basic functioning and capabilities of employed systems
- (ii) Identify systemic risks (hallucinations, algorithmic biases)
- (iii) Correctly interpret results to avoid automation bias

Technological competence is an unavoidable requirement for professional practice, oriented toward promoting a learning culture that reinforces legal service quality.

### 6.4 Audit and Accountability

**Exhaustive Technical Documentation**: firms maintain complete documentation of processes and evaluations linked to AI use.

**Compliance Preservation**: conformity certificates for a **period of ten years** from the system's commissioning.

**Post-Market Surveillance**: implementation of an active system for collecting and analyzing data on the actual functioning of tools to detect risks, serious incidents, or accuracy failures. Audits must be **dynamic**, evaluating divergences between expected and obtained results, to adopt immediate corrective measures, including information removal or system deactivation if security standards or data subject rights are violated.

## Conclusions: The Legal Profession as a Guarantee of Effective Judicial Protection

The integration of AI in law firms is not an option but an unavoidable professional imperative. However, such integration **must reinforce, not weaken, the pillars of Law**: truthfulness, responsibility, due diligence, and protection of fundamental rights.

The AI Act provides the regulatory framework; reliable technical architecture (RAG, XAI, post-hoc verification) offers mitigation tools; responsible governance ensures that innovation does not weaken, but reinforces effective judicial protection.

The legal profession, as a guarantee of effective judicial protection, must lead the digital transformation ensuring that innovation does not weaken, but reinforces the pillars of Law.
