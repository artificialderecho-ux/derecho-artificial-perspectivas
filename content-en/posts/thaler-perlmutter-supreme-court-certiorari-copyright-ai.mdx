# *Thaler v. Perlmutter* Before the U.S. Supreme Court: Can AI Be the Author of a Copyright-Protected Work?

On October 9, 2025, Dr. Stephen Thaler filed a certiorari petition before the United States Supreme Court that obliges the nation's highest judicial body to finally resolve the question that has been fracturing the intellectual property and technology debate for years: Can works autonomously generated by an artificial intelligence system receive copyright protection without any creative contribution from a human being?

The petition provisionally closes—seven years of litigation that traversed the U.S. Copyright Office, the District Court for the District of Columbia (*Thaler v. Perlmutter*, 687 F. Supp. 3d 140 (D.D.C. 2023)), and the D.C. Circuit Court of Appeals (*Thaler v. Perlmutter*, 130 F.4th 1039 (D.C. Cir. 2025)), which on March 18, 2025 confirmed the denial of registration with an opinion authored by Judge Millett.

*Thaler v. Perlmutter* is not just another AI and intellectual property litigation. It is the cleanest case the U.S. legal system has produced to force a doctrinal decision on non-human authorship: there is no factual dispute, no partial human contribution to weigh, and no user claiming co-authorship. Only a machine, its owner, and the question that no Congress and no Supreme Court has wanted to answer explicitly.

This article analyzes the certiorari petition applying IRAC methodology, examines Thaler's central arguments and weighs them against the D.C. Circuit's position, and draws implications for the European copyright and artificial intelligence legal framework.

---

## I. Facts, Procedure, and Question Presented

### 1.1 The *Creativity Machine* and «A Recent Entrance to Paradise»

Dr. Stephen Thaler is a computer scientist specializing in artificial intelligence who developed a system called the *Creativity Machine*. Said system autonomously generated a two-dimensional artwork titled «A Recent Entrance to Paradise», without direct creative contribution from any human being in the specific creative process of the work.

On November 3, 2018, Thaler filed a registration application with the U.S. Copyright Office. In the application, he expressly declared that the author was the *Creativity Machine*, that the work had been "autonomously created by a computer algorithm running on a machine," and that he, as the owner of the system, should be considered the copyright holder. He included the work under the category of *work made for hire*.

This procedural transparency is decisive: Thaler did not attempt to claim personal authorship or hide the autonomous nature of the generative process. That choice makes the case an extraordinarily clean legal vehicle, because the only question the court must resolve is exactly what the petitioner raised from the beginning.

### 1.3 The Question Presented

The petition frames the question with clinical precision:

> "Whether works produced by an AI system without direct and traditional authorship contribution from a human person can be the subject of copyright."

This question unfolds into three sub-issues that structure the argumentation: (i) whether the human authorship requirement contradicts the text and structure of the Copyright Act; (ii) whether that requirement frustrates the constitutional objectives of the Intellectual Property Clause; and (iii) whether the case offers a suitable procedural vehicle for definitive resolution of the question.

## II. IRAC Analysis

### 2.1 Issue—Principal Legal Question

*Does Section 102(a) of the Copyright Act (17 U.S.C. § 102(a)), interpreted pursuant to the Intellectual Property Clause (Art. I, § 8, cl. 8 of the U.S. Constitution), require that the author of a work be a human being for said work to obtain copyright protection?*

### 2.2 Rule—Legal Framework and Precedent

#### 2.2.1 Key Statutory Text

Section 102(a) of the *Copyright Act* provides that copyright protection subsists in "original works of authorship fixed in any tangible medium of expression." The provision does not contain the expressions "natural person," "human being," or any equivalent. Section 102(b) exhaustively enumerates what cannot receive protection—ideas, procedures, processes, systems, methods of operation, concepts, principles, or discoveries—without including non-human authored works in that catalog.

Section 201(b) expressly provides that "the employer or other person for whom the work was prepared" is considered the author in the case of works made for hire. Section 302(c) provides a specific duration—95 years from publication or 120 years from creation—for this type of work, precisely because the holder may be an entity without biological life.

#### 2.2.2 Copyright Office Administrative Doctrine

The *Compendium of U.S. Copyright Office Practices* (3rd ed. 2021), § 306, establishes that "the Copyright Office will register an original work of authorship provided it was created by a human being" and § 313.2 that "the Office will not register works produced by a machine or merely mechanical process that operates randomly or automatically without any creative contribution or intervention from a human author."

The D.C. Circuit expressly recognized that this human authorship requirement does not come from any law or any Supreme Court ruling, but from an observation by the former Register of Copyrights in the 1965 annual report. Its historical consolidation in successive Compendia (1973, 1984, 2021) is, for Thaler, demonstration that this is extralegal policymaking not subject to the rulemaking procedures required by the Administrative Procedure Act (5 U.S.C. § 706(2)).

#### 2.2.3 U.S. Supreme Court Precedent

**Burrow-Giles Lithographic Co. v. Sarony, 111 U.S. 53 (1884).** The Court extended protection to photography, then non-existent technology for the legislator, interpreting "author" as the "inventive or master mind" that originates and produces the work. Thaler invokes this precedent to argue that AI deserves the same expansive treatment the Court gave to the camera.

**Feist Publications, Inc. v. Rural Telephone Service Co., 499 U.S. 340 (1991).** Justice O'Connor established that the only constitutional requirement for copyright protection is originality, not human effort. The *sweat of the brow* doctrine was expressly rejected.

**Star Athletica, L.L.C. v. Varsity Brands, Inc., 580 U.S. 405 (2017).** The Court declared that the existence of copyright in a work cannot depend on analysis of the creator's "methods of design, purposes and reasons." Thaler argues that the Office falls exactly into that fallacy by denying registration based on the autonomous methodology by which the work was generated.

**Fortnightly Corp. v. United Artists Television, Inc., 392 U.S. 390 (1968).** "We must read the statutory language of sixty years ago in light of drastic technological change." This dictum is the contextual anchor of the petition: the 1976 Act cannot be interpreted to exclude technologies whose existence was impossible to foresee at the time of its enactment.

#### 2.2.4 Position of the D.C. Circuit

Judge Millett built the Circuit's decision on seven structural provisions of the Copyright Act that, in her reading, only make sense if the author is a human being: (i) immediate ownership of the work requires legal capacity to possess property, which a machine lacks; (ii) copyright duration is tied to the author's life; (iii) termination rights are structured around surviving spouse, children, and grandchildren; (iv) transfer requires signature; (v) protection of unpublished works is conditioned on the author's nationality or domicile; (vi) joint work requires the "intent" of the parties to merge their contributions; and (vii) whenever the Act refers to machines, it does so as tools in service of authors, never as authors themselves.

<Callout type="warning">
**Determinative procedural issue.** The Circuit declined to rule on whether Thaler could be considered an author in his capacity as owner, user, and programmer of the *Creativity Machine*, because Thaler did not raise that theory before the Copyright Office and thus deemed it unpreserved for judicial review. This *waiver* makes the case an absolutely pure test: the only theory before the Court is whether the machine can be an author by itself, without any human mediation.
</Callout>

### 2.3 Application—Argumentative Analysis

#### 2.3.1 The Textualist Argument: *Expressio Unius*

The petition builds its central argument on the absence of any explicit textual limitation. If Congress had wanted to limit protection to works of human authorship, it would have used the same normative technique it employed to exclude other content in Section 102(b), which enumerates what is not protectable in detail. The deliberate omission of the "human authorship" restriction in Section 102(a) constitutes, in textualist reading, a legislative choice, not a gap.

This *expressio unius est exclusio alterius* argument is especially pertinent in the Roberts Court's context, which has shown sustained preference for textual interpretation over legislative intent. The Circuit's reply, based on structural inferences about the Act's pronouns and the duration system tied to the author's life, rests on less firm interpretive ground from this methodological perspective.

#### 2.3.2 Non-Human Authorship Already Exists in the Law: Corporations and Legal Entities

Thaler points out that corporations and other non-human entities have been authors of registered works for over a century without controversy, precisely under Section 201(b) (*work made for hire*). Both the Second Circuit (*Marvel Characters, Inc. v. Kirby*, 726 F.3d 119, 143 (2d Cir. 2013)) and the Ninth Circuit (*Warren v. Fox Family Worldwide, Inc.*, 328 F.3d 1136, 1140–41 (9th Cir. 2003)) have recognized non-human entities as authors without requiring identification of the natural person who executed the traditional elements of authorship.

If the Act already contemplates non-human authorship in its architecture for legal persons, the Office's argument—that it requires a natural person to execute the "traditional elements of authorship"—lacks consistent statutory basis.

#### 2.3.3 Subsidiary Theories of Ownership

The petition develops two alternative theories for the case where the Court does not recognize direct authorship by the machine.

**First: *Work made for hire*.** Section 201(b) allows the employer or person for whom the work was prepared to be considered the author. Thaler argues that the *Creativity Machine*, under his absolute control as owner, programmer, and sole operator, functioned as an employee *sui generis* solely for purposes of this provision. The Office rejected this theory for absence of contract between Thaler and the machine; the Circuit additionally dismissed it on the grounds that the provision presupposes the existence of a protectable interest that never arose, given that the work was not registerable from its origin.

**Second: The accession doctrine.** This property doctrine, of Roman roots and broad presence in Anglo-American jurisprudence, establishes that the owner of a thing is the owner of the fruits that thing produces. The petition cites from the 6th century Irish principle—"to each cow belongs its calf, then to each book belongs its copy"—to *Webb's Fabric Pharmacies, Inc. v. Beckwith*, 449 U.S. 155 (1980), where the Supreme Court recognized that interest on a principal fund belongs to the owner of the fund. If the *Creativity Machine* is Thaler's property, its intellectual production—the work—should belong to him by the same principle.

#### 2.3.4 The Photographic Analogy and Control Over the Creative Process

One of the strongest arguments of the petition is the internal inconsistency of the Office's position regarding photography. Courts have never denied protection to a photograph arguing that the photographer did not exercise "sufficient control" over the result or that the random element was excessive. In *Chamberlin v. Uris Sales Corp.*, 150 F.2d 512 (2d Cir. 1945), the Second Circuit ruled that copyright could not distinguish between the intentional and the unintentional in the creative process. In *Alfred Bell & Co. v. Catalda Fine Arts*, 191 F.2d 99, 105 (2d Cir. 1951), Judge Frank observed that even inadvertent variations—the copyist's poor eyesight, an unexpected thunder—are protectable if the author adopts them as their own.

If Thaler had taken random photographs during the day with no artistic criterion, he would be the recognized author of each of them. But by employing AI instead to generate works with identical randomness, the Office denies him all protection. This asymmetry, the petition argues, lacks any legal principle to support it and cannot be derived from the text of the Act.

#### 2.3.5 Constitutional Objectives as Weighty Argument

The Intellectual Property Clause (Art. I, § 8, cl. 8 of the Constitution) authorizes Congress to "promote the Progress of Science and the useful Arts." The Supreme Court has emphasized in multiple precedents that the benefit to the public—not the reward to the individual author—is the primary objective of the system (*Twentieth Century Music Corp. v. Aiken*, 422 U.S. 151 (1975); *Eldred v. Ashcroft*, 537 U.S. 186 (2003)).

If AI-generated works cannot receive protection, no one will have economic incentives to invest in developing and disseminating them. Generative AI is already integrated into the most widely used creative tools in the world—Adobe Photoshop, Canva, DALL-E, Midjourney, Microsoft, Google Stable Diffusion—and the absence of protection for autonomously generated works will create legal uncertainty that will disincentivize exactly the investment that the Constitutional Clause seeks to stimulate.

### 2.4 Conclusion—Analysis Assessment

Thaler's petition is legally sound in its central textualist argument: Section 102(a) of the Copyright Act does not contain the human authorship requirement that the Copyright Office applies. That requirement comes from a 1965 administrative observation, without normative rank, and was never subjected to the rulemaking procedures required by the Administrative Procedure Act.

The D.C. Circuit's position, though historically backed by centuries of administrative practice, rests on structural inferences rather than an express prohibition. The strength of those inferences—the duration system tied to the author's life, the Act's pronouns, the requirement of signature for transfer—depends on interpreting the Act as a closed code that does not admit scenarios unprovided for by the 1976 legislator, exactly the reading the Supreme Court itself has rejected in the past when addressing then-non-existent technologies (*Fortnightly*, *Goldstein*, *Burrow-Giles*).

However, Thaler's subsidiary theories present their own not-insignificant difficulties. The *work made for hire* doctrine requires that the "employee" be a legal entity with capacity to contract obligations, not a tool; accession operates more naturally in tangible property than in intellectual property, where ownership is a legal construction, not an automatic physical consequence.

The most likely outcome, if the Supreme Court grants certiorari, is a narrow decision resolving only whether the Act requires human authorship, without ruling on whether Thaler could be an author as the owner or user of the machine—issue declared unpreserved. That limited resolution would leave open the more complex and practical questions about generative AI involving substantial human contribution, which is, paradoxically, the scenario affecting the vast majority of the current creative market.

## III. Implications for the European Legal Framework

### 3.1 The Concept of "Intellectual Creation" and Its Incompatibility with Pure AI Authorship

The European copyright framework rests on a personalist conception of authorship rooted in the continental *droit d'auteur* tradition. The Court of Justice of the European Union has defined originality as reflecting the "author's own intellectual creation" in *Infopaq International A/S c. Danske Dagblades Forening* (C-5/08, 2009) and consolidated that doctrine in *Painer c. Standard VerlagsGmbH* (C-145/10, 2011), where it emphasized that a photograph receives protection precisely because it allows the photographer to imprint their "personal mark" on the work.

This requirement of intellectual own creation has been interpreted by European doctrine almost unanimously as a projection of the human creator's personality, which excludes, in the majority reading, AI-generated works without identifiable human creative contribution. Unlike the U.S. system, where the debate centers on whether the Act expressly prohibits non-human authorship, in Europe the obstacle is dogmatic: the very concept of originality presupposes a person.

### 3.2 The AI Act (EU) 2024/1689 and Silence on *Output*

Article 53 of Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, in force since August 2, 2025 for general-purpose AI models, imposes on providers the obligation to publish a sufficiently detailed summary of the training data used, in order to facilitate the exercise of copyright by rights holders whose content was used.

This provision addresses the *input* dimension—can protected works be used to train AI systems?—but remains silent on the *output* dimension that *Thaler* poses: can works generated by those systems receive protection? The questions are distinct, and the European regulatory framework treats them separately, generating notable asymmetries: a system that uses protected works to train without authorization may incur liability toward the original rights holders, and at the same time, the result of that training may not be protectable for the benefit of the system owner.

<Callout type="info">
**Practical relevance for Spain.** Spanish companies contracting AI-generated content services should note that, under current Spanish law (Arts. 5 and 14 of Royal Legislative Decree 1/1996, consolidated text of the Copyright Act), works autonomously generated by AI without identifiable human creative contribution do not obtain copyright protection. A contractual clause attempting to assign rights over such works may be ineffective if there is no right to assign. Assignment of a non-existent right is not opposable against third parties.
</Callout>

### 3.3 Three Vectors of Pressure on the European Legislator

The outcome of *Thaler v. Perlmutter* in the U.S. Supreme Court will have, regardless of its result, a catalyzing effect on the European debate for three structural reasons.

**Competitive vector.** If the Supreme Court recognizes the possibility of protection for autonomously generated AI works, American companies developing and employing generative AI will have a legal asset—copyright in the work—that their European competitors will lack under the dominant personalist interpretation. Pressure to level the normative playing field will intensify immediately.

**Legislative vector.** If the Supreme Court denies certiorari or affirms the denial of registration, pressure for Congress to intervene by statutory route will increase. American legislative reform assigning ownership to the owner or user of the AI system could become the reference model for similar initiatives in Europe, where the European Commission has not yet advanced any specific proposal on AI authorship.

**Dogmatic vector.** The doctrines argued by Thaler—particularly accession and instrumental authorship—are compatible with European intellectual property law principles that associate ownership with investment effort, such as *sui generis* database rights (Directive 96/9/EC) or neighboring rights of phonogram producers. These regimes protect investment without requiring personal creativity from the holder. An evolution extending similar logics to AI system *output* would not require abandoning the European copyright architecture, but expanding its auxiliary categories.

## IV. Why *Thaler v. Perlmutter* Matters Beyond the U.S

*Thaler v. Perlmutter* is, at its core, a case about the limits of administrative interpretation in the absence of express legislative text. The U.S. Copyright Office applied for decades a requirement—human authorship—that never appeared in any law or Supreme Court ruling, and that was consolidated in administrative practice without being subjected to the required normative procedures.

The substantive question— whether autonomously AI-generated works can receive legal protection—is, however, the most important that intellectual property law has faced since *Burrow-Giles* extended copyright to photography in 1884. That precedent took time to resolve an equivalent asymmetry: technology had advanced, and law had not followed. The parallel with the current situation is hard to ignore.

The Supreme Court, if it grants certiorari, must choose between three positions: recognize that the current text of the Act admits protection for non-human authored works, declare that the Act does not permit it and refer the question to the legislator, or—on the more nuanced solution that Thaler's procedural waiver will make difficult—recognize that the owner or user of the system may be the author through other routes that the petitioner himself did not adequately preserve before the agency.

Any of these outcomes will generate ripples in legal systems that, like the Spanish and European, share the same normative silence on AI authorship. The silence is not neutral: in copyright, what is not protected is in the public domain. That is the tension that *Thaler v. Perlmutter* has brought, with unusual clarity, to the door of the world's most important court.
