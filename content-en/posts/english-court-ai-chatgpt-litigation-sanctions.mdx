**English Court Establishes Comprehensive Sanctions Regime for Using ChatGPT in Litigation Without Verification**

**What the Divisional Court has just told every attorney who uses AI to research case law**

**ℹ Note – Summary (TL;DR):** The High Court of England and Wales issued on June 6, 2025, the most comprehensive ruling to date on unverified AI use in litigation. In two consolidated cases, the court systematizes the entire spectrum of available sanctions, from costs to imprisonment. It confirms that the duty to verify AI citations already exists, without the need for specific regulation. And it warns firm leaders: the next Hamid hearing will put them in the spotlight.

## **The Two Cases: What Happened**

Case 1 (Ayinde). A junior barrister drafts a judicial review brief regarding emergency housing. It includes five judgments that do not exist. When the defendant detects them, rather than acknowledging the error, the legal team responds in writing minimizing it as "cosmetic errors" and refusing to explain it. The Divisional Court calls this "a grossly unprofessional categorization." The barrister receives a costs order of £2,000, is referred to the regulator, and it is discovered that the same type of error also occurred in another case before the County Court.

Case 2 (Al-Haroun). A solicitor presents to Judge Dias J a witness statement with case citations. The court's judicial assistant verifies the 45 citations: 18 do not exist; most of the remaining do not contain the quoted texts nor support the propositions for which they are cited. One of the fake citations attributes a decision to Judge Dias J herself. The solicitor admits to having relied on his client's research without verification. The court refers him to the regulator.

## **The Sanctions: From Least to Most Severe**

The court describes a complete spectrum of possible responses. Public admonishment in judgment matters, but the court warns that except in exceptional circumstances it is not sufficient as the sole response. The wasted costs order requires the attorney to pay the unnecessary costs caused to the other party: in the Ayinde case, £2,000 per responsible party. Referral to the regulator (Bar Standards Board for barristers; Solicitors Regulation Authority for solicitors) may result in suspension or disbarment. Strike-out can eliminate a case. Contempt of court can mean up to two years' imprisonment. And in the most serious cases, deliberately placing false material before the court to interfere with the administration of justice is perverting the course of justice, with a maximum penalty of life imprisonment.

## **What This Means for You as an Attorney**

First: the duty to verify AI output does not await specific regulation to exist. It already exists. The court derives it from general ethical rules: do not deceive the court, provide competent service. Second: if you use AI to investigate, you are responsible. Just as you are responsible for the work of a trainee you supervise. There is no difference. Third: the authorized sources for verification are specific: National Archives, BAILII, official Law Reports from ICLR, databases from reputable publishers. Google is not an authorized source. Fourth: if you are a Head of Chambers or Managing Partner, the court warns that in future Hamid hearings it will examine whether you fulfilled your leadership responsibilities. That means: do you have a written protocol? Is your team trained? Do you adequately supervise juniors?

## **What Matters Most in the Ruling: The Institutional Warning**

The Divisional Court does not limit itself to sanctioning the attorneys involved. It sends copies of the judgment to the Bar Council, the Law Society, and the Council of the Inns of Court. Its message is clear: publishing guidance is not sufficient. "More needs to be done" to ensure attorneys comply. And it warns: in future Hamid hearings, the court will ask whether those with leadership responsibilities have fulfilled them. The era of soft self-regulation regarding AI in litigation has ended.

## **The Golden Rule of the Ruling**

The court says it in a single phrase: "The critical safeguard is to check any output by reference to an authoritative source" (§ 87). Not asking the AI if it's sure. Not asking it to explain its limitations. Accessing the source. Reading it. Verifying that it exists, that it says what you think it says, and that it supports the point you're making. Every time.
