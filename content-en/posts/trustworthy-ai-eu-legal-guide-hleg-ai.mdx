## What Does It Mean for AI to Be \"Trustworthy\" and Why Does It Matter for Your Business?

The European Commission tasked a group of 52 independent experts with defining the ethical standards that artificial intelligence systems must meet to be considered trustworthy. The result was the Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI, published in 2019 by the High-Level Expert Group on AI (HLEG AI).

[Read full doctrinal analysis at Firma Scarpa](/firma-scarpa/IAFiable-doctrinal)

Although not legally binding in themselves, these guidelines have become the reference framework that anticipates and explains the European AI Regulation (AI Act). Companies that do not align their systems with these standards face increasing regulatory risks, civil liability, and loss of institutional trust.

## The Three Pillars of Trustworthy AI

The Guidelines define trustworthy AI based on three equally necessary components that must be fulfilled throughout the entire system lifecycle (§ 15):

**Lawful AI.** Complying with all applicable laws and regulations: EU primary law, GDPR, anti-discrimination directives, sector-specific regulations, and international human rights treaties. This component is a necessary minimum, but not sufficient.

**Ethical AI.** Respecting principles and values that go beyond legal compliance. Laws do not always keep pace with technology (§ 26); ethics fills that space. Four principles structure this component.

**Robust AI.** Technical and social robustness to avoid unintentional harm. A system may have impeccable ethical intentions and cause harm if its technical architecture or socio-cultural adaptation is deficient (§ 27).

## The 4 Principles Every AI Must Respect

The Guidelines anchor the four ethical principles in the fundamental rights of the EU Charter (§ 48):

**Respect for Human Autonomy.** AI cannot manipulate, coerce, or deceive. It must augment people's capabilities, not replace their judgment. Basis in Arts. 1 and 6 of the EU Charter.

**Prevention of Harm.** The system must be secure, anticipate its risks, and particularly protect vulnerable groups. Basis in Art. 3 of the EU Charter.

**Fairness.** Prohibition of unjust bias, discrimination, and unequal access. Decisions must be contestable. Basis in Arts. 21 et seq. of the EU Charter.

**Explicability.** Individuals affected by AI decisions have the right to receive a comprehensible explanation of the process. Basis in Art. 47 of the EU Charter.

A crucial nuance: human dignity (Art. 1 EU Charter) operates as an absolute right that does not admit balancing with other interests (§ 54). No commercial, security, or efficiency argument can justify its infringement.

## The 7 Specific Requirements for Businesses

Chapter II of the Guidelines translates the ethical principles into seven operational requirements that AI systems must fulfill throughout their entire lifecycle (§ 58):

**1. Human Oversight.** Someone must be able to control, correct, or stop the system at all times. The Guidelines establish three graduated mechanisms—human participation, human control, and human command—depending on the level of risk (§ 65). The lower the level of oversight, the more demanding the verification and governance requirements.

**2. Technical Robustness and Security.** Resistance to attacks, contingency plans for failures, and analysis of potential malicious uses. The system must be reliable in its intended performance.

**3. Privacy and Data Governance.** Responsible management of personal data with minimal necessary collection. Clear access protocol: who, when, and for what purpose. Priority to public sector data over personal data (§§ 69-73).

**4. Transparency.** Label the system as AI. Document the algorithm's logic. Provide user-adapted explanation. This requirement distinguishes three dimensions—traceability, explainability, and communication—that may conflict with intellectual property protection (§ 88).

**5. Non-Discrimination and Inclusion.** Audit biases in datasets. Verify representativeness of protected groups. Bias reporting channel for users. Identifiable biases must be eliminated at the collection phase (§ 80).

**6. Social and Environmental Well-Being.** Measure the environmental impact of model training. Assess the effect on employment and social ties. Prioritize less harmful options (§ 84).

**7. Accountability.** Internal and external audits. Notification of negative effects. Accessible compensation mechanisms for affected parties. Without this requirement, the other six lack practical effectiveness (§§ 87-91).

## What AI Can Never Do: Absolute Prohibitions

The Guidelines identify five limits that admit no balancing or exceptions (§§ 54, 130-134):

Presenting an AI system as human when the user asks or has the right to know. Creating autonomous weapons systems without effective human control. Conducting mass assessments of citizens' moral character without legal basis and communicated legitimate purpose. Undermining human dignity under any pretext. Identifying or tracking people on a large scale without clear justification in existing legislation.

## Why These Guidelines Matter Even Though They Are Not Mandatory

The Guidelines are soft law: their adoption is voluntary. However, their practical legal relevance is high for three reasons.

First, they anticipate the AI Act. The structure of the European AI Regulation—risk level classification, transparency and human oversight requirements, system lifecycle emphasis—parallels the Guidelines significantly. Knowing them is understanding the AI Act's logic.

Second, they operate as a due diligence standard. In acquisition processes, internal audits, and litigation, the Guidelines constitute the reference criterion for evaluating whether an organization acted with due diligence in developing or deploying an AI system.

Third, they set the limit of permitted deployment. The document itself establishes that when it is not possible to identify ethically acceptable balances between conflicting principles, development should not continue in the intended form (§ 90). This ethical feasibility criterion is already operational, before the AI Act enters into force.

## Conclusions and Next Steps

The Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI are not history: they are the map of the regulatory territory that the AI Act is codifying. Organizations that have already adopted their seven operational requirements have an advantage in the Regulation's compliance process.

The practical starting point is the assessment checklist in Chapter III, designed to be adaptable to any specific use case. It is not exhaustive, but covers the main risk vectors. The document recommends that the review and application of this checklist be institutionalized in the internal governance structure, with defined roles from management to everyday operations (§§ 115-116).

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Are the Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI Mandatory?**

Not directly. They are a soft law instrument without binding legal force in themselves. However, they anticipate the content of the European AI Regulation, operate as an enforceable conduct standard in litigation and due diligence processes, and their voluntary adoption is strategically advantageous given the entry into force of the AI Act.

**What Types of Organizations Do the Guidelines Apply To?**

All stakeholders: developers, implementers, end users, and civil society (§§ 56-57). Not just big tech: any organization that uses, purchases, or develops AI systems, regardless of size or sector, falls within their scope.

**What Happens If My AI System Cannot Fulfill All Requirements Simultaneously?**

The Guidelines expressly recognize that tensions between principles and requirements may arise without a predefined solution (§ 54). The resolution mechanism is reasoned reflection based on empirical evidence and the principle of proportionality. If it is not possible to identify an ethically acceptable balance, the document establishes that development should not continue in the intended form (§ 90).
