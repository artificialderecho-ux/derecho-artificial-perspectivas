# Human Inventor Exclusivity Against Artificial Intelligence (Analysis of Judgment [2023] UKSC 49)

©Ricardo Scarpa (derechoartificial.com)

## 1. Context and Regulatory Framework

The *Thaler v Comptroller-General* judgment stands as a paradigmatic legal dispute in the contemporary debate on the protection of innovations generated through Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems. In the current patent ecosystem, historically designed to incentivize and reward human ingenuity, the emergence of systems capable of producing technical solutions without direct human intervention has provoked a frontal collision with the dogmatic structures of Industrial Property Law. This ruling does not represent a mere administrative resolution, but a cutting-edge test case that tests the elasticity of traditional regulatory frameworks against technological autonomy.

The procedural route originates from patent applications **GB1816909.4** and **GB1818161.0**, filed by Dr. Stephen Thaler, relating to a food container and a light beacon, respectively. The disruptive element lies in that Thaler did not attribute authorship of the inventions to himself, but designated his AI system, **DABUS**, as the autonomous inventor. The central legal question focuses on statutory interpretation of the *Patents Act 1977* (hereinafter, the 1977 Act), specifically in its Sections 7 and 13. Thaler's challenge transcends the registration technique: it seeks to subvert the classical notion of intellectual property to transmute ownership of the creative act to ownership of digital production means, questioning whether the system can admit a genealogy of rights not born from a human subject.

## 2. Judicial Reconstruction: The Legal Personality Syllogism

In statutory interpretation, logical coherence is imperative to safeguard the legislator's will embodied in the 1977 Act. The UK Supreme Court, under Lord Kitchin's opinion, deliberately avoided ontological debates about AI "consciousness" to focus on a strict legality analysis.

The *ratio decidendi* of the judgment can be reconstructed through a legal syllogism revealing the system's rigidity:

1. **Legal Premise:** Section 7(3) of the 1977 Act defines the inventor as the "actual deviser." Under patent law structure, this function presupposes ownership of rights and obligations, a capacity exclusive to those holding legal personality.
2. **Factual Premise:** DABUS is an algorithmic entity lacking legal personality. It is neither a natural person nor a legally recognized entity to be a rights holder.
3. **Conclusion:** Consequently, DABUS cannot be qualified as an "inventor" in the strict sense of the statute.

It is fundamental to distinguish this reasoning from any *obiter dictum* on AI's technical sophistication. The court, assuming hypothetically that DABUS generated the inventions autonomously (Para. 51), determined that the concept of **"actual deviser"** constitutes an insurmountable barrier. Being a category reserved by law for natural persons, DABUS's lack of humanity prevents the machine from acting as the legitimate origin of the ownership chain. Without a human deviser, the process lacks its necessary subjective anchor.

## 3. Analysis of Ownership Hierarchy and the "Inventor" Concept

The patent registration system relies on legal presumptions designed to provide legal certainty in commercial transactions. The ownership hierarchy established in the 1977 Act permits no gaps in right traceability, requiring an uninterrupted chain from the creator to the applicant.

Through rigorous interpretation of Sections 7(2) and 7(4), the following exclusion pillars are identified:

1. **Primacy of Human Inventor:** Primary grant is directed to the inventor (Section 7(2)(a)). Consolidated jurisprudence (e.g., *Yeda Research*) ratifies that the "inventor" is necessarily a **natural person**.
2. **Break in Transfer Chain (Section 13(2)(b)):** For a third party to derive rights, there must be an act of transfer (contract or law) from the original inventor. Given DABUS lacks legal personality, it lacks the capacity to own property and is therefore legally incapable of transferring it. There cannot be a derivation of rights from an object.
3. **Inapplicability of Procedural Presumptions:** The presumption of Section 7(4), assuming the applicant is the legitimate owner, is a procedural tool reserved exclusively for **persons**. It cannot be invoked to remedy the non-existence of a legally recognized inventor.

The systemic consequence is the collapse of the application. Upon not identifying a person under Section 13(2), the application becomes defective *ab initio*. The lack of a "legal inventor" inevitably leads to abandonment of the claim, as the system does not contemplate spontaneous generation of property rights without an author with legal personality.

## 4. Dismantling the Doctrine of Accession in Intangible Goods

A critical point of litigation was Dr. Thaler's attempt to invoke the accession doctrine, seeking to assimilate ownership of natural fruits with that of intellectual creations. The argument sustained that, as the owner of a movable acquires ownership of its offspring, the owner of an AI acquires ownership of its technical emanations.

The court categorically rejected this analogy based on the classic distinction between tangible and intangible property:

- **Restriction of Subject Matter:** The accession doctrine applies restrictively to **tangible property**. As noted in the judgment at **Para. 20**, this doctrine cannot be extended to intangible concepts such as inventions, which are creations of the human intellect regulated by specific statutes.
- **No Legal Basis for Automatic Creation:** At **Para. 88**, the court clarifies that there is no principled basis to apply accession to developments generated by DABUS. The 1977 Act does not contemplate "possession by automatic creation" as a legitimate mode of patent acquisition.

Furthermore, allowing this analogy would generate a systemic risk: the displacement of protection from human creative effort to mere technological capital processing capacity. Thus, ownership would be prevented from deriving automatically from property of the means, protecting the essence of the patent as an incentive to human ingenuity rather than the mere deployment of hardware.

## 5. Systematic Perspective and Limitations of the Ruling

The ruling aligns with international harmonization trends, symmetry with **J8/20 from the European Patent Office (EPO)**. Both institutions agree that inventor designation is a requirement demanding legal personality.

However, from an academic perspective, the ruling reveals "jurisprudential gaps" by omission:

- The court **did not investigate** DABUS's real autonomy, limiting itself to accepting the applicant's factual assumptions to resolve on a strictly literal basis (Para. 51).
- The judgment preserves an **"anthropocentric legal fiction."** By hiding behind the literalness of the 1977 Act, the court avoids ruling on whether the concept of invention must evolve given a reality where the machine is no longer a mere instrument but a generative agent.

While the ruling is robust against appeals due to its adherence to legal text, its dependence on a regulatory framework from almost half a century places it in a reactive position facing imminent legislative reform that seems inevitable.

## 6. Final Assessment: The Future of Autonomous Innovation

Judgment [2023] UKSC 49 ratifies that, in the current state of British law, the patent enclosure remains closed to non-human entities. The gap between technological dynamism and statutory rigidity is ever deeper, posing a direct challenge to the competitiveness of jurisdictions maintaining restrictive criteria.

For AI developers, the message is clear: under the current regime, inventions generated fully autonomously risk falling into a **patentability vacuum**. Coinciding with Justice Elisabeth Laing LJ's observation, the current system is not configured for machines; therefore, any change in this paradigm requires direct parliamentary intervention. Only legislative reform can redefine who—or what—can be the subject of invention in the twenty-first century, preventing technological progress from surpassing the Law's capacity to regulate it.

### Glossary of Key Terms

- **Actual Deviser:** Central legal concept of Section 7(3) of the 1977 Act; interpreted as the natural person who conceives the inventive concept.
- **Accession:** Civil law doctrine granting the owner of a thing ownership of what it produces; the Supreme Court ruled its inapplicability to tangible/intangible intellectual property (Paras. 20 and 88).
- **Ratio Decidendi:** The binding legal rationale of the judgment; in this case, the requirement of legal personality to be considered an inventor.
- **Obiter Dictum:** Comments or opinions from the court not essential to the final decision, such as assessments of AI's technical capacity.
- **Section 13(2)(b):** Statutory requirement demanding non-inventor applicants indicate how they derived their right to the patent, a prerequisite that fails in the absence of a human inventor.
