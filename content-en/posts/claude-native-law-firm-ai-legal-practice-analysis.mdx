## Introduction: A Disruptive Perspective on Legal Technology

Zack Shapiro, Managing Partner at Rains and formerly co-founder and general counsel of BZR, is a Yale Law School graduate who began his legal career as a clerk in federal courts (S.D.N.Y. and 2nd Circuit) and at Davis Polk & Wardwell. His recent publication on X, titled "The Claude-Native Law Firm: How I Actually Practice Law with AI in 2026," represents a paradigm shift in how lawyers can—and should—think about integrating generative AI tools into contemporary legal practice.

Unlike most theoretical analyses of legal AI, Shapiro offers something rarer and more valuable: a detailed operational account of how he uses Claude on real legal problems, with particular emphasis on how general-purpose broad tools outperform specialized legal products.

This article critically analyzes Shapiro's arguments, their implications for professional liability, ethics and data protection challenges, and how his model challenges existing regulatory frameworks in jurisdictions such as Spain and the EU.

---

## I. The Central Argument: Why Claude, Not "Legal AI"

### A. Critique of the Specialized Legal Tech Market

Shapiro's proposal begins with a provocative assertion: specialized legal AI products (Harvey, Spellbook, CoCounsel, Luminance) are "wrappers" built on the same foundation models that power general-purpose tools, and for a small-practice professional, a well-configured general-purpose AI is better.

This argument has profound legal implications. Legal-specific products promise to "train" the system with the firm's clause library and playbooks. But Shapiro identifies the fundamental error: a template library is not a competitive advantage, because every competent firm in a practice area has roughly the same templates. What differentiates a good lawyer from a mediocre one was never the template, but what the lawyer did with it.

**Legal Implication:** Shapiro's argument raises an uncomfortable question for specialized legal-tech providers. If value lies not in templates but in individual professional judgment encoded in instructions, then services charging premiums for "customization" are selling a product whose real differentiator is invisible and transferable. This has consequences for professional liability: if a lawyer uses a system that promises to adhere to their "playbook" but then produces defective analysis, where does liability reside?

### B. The Technical Argument: Code and Document Manipulation Capability

The second pillar of Shapiro's argument is technical but deeply relevant to practice: Claude is optimized for writing code, which means it can write code on the fly to directly manipulate the applications lawyers already use.

This is not merely an efficiency improvement. It implies a break in the legal tool chain. When Claude opens a .docx file at the XML level and writes the exact markup Microsoft Word expects, attributed to the lawyer's name, preserving every formatting detail, it is performing operations that historically required human intervention or specialized software. It is, effectively, supplanting functions that are traditionally considered part of professional services delivery.

**Legal Implication:** This change creates a regulatory gray zone. If Claude generates tracked changes in a contract under lawyer supervision, who is responsible for errors that might result from automation? Are there specific disclosure obligations when the work product is AI-generated before professional review?

## II. Legal Analysis: Attorney-Client Privilege and Data Protection

### ISSUE: How is Attorney-Client Privilege (ACP) Protected When Documents Are Uploaded to Claude?

### RULE: Applicable Legal Framework

#### A. Spanish and European Context

In Spain, communication between lawyer and client under professional secrecy is protected by:

- **Article 24 CE:** Right to defense and legal assistance
- **Article 542.2 LECRIM:** Professional secrecy of lawyers
- **GDPR (Arts. 4.11, 9):** Personal data and special categories
- **Directive 2000/31/EC (E-Commerce):** Liability of technical intermediaries

#### B. Treatment of AI as a "Technical Third Party"

Shapiro notes that the applicable framework is the same as for cloud storage, e-discovery platforms, and online legal research databases. This characterization depends on specific conditions being met:

1. **Client acceptance:** Informed consent regarding AI use
2. **Exclusion from model training:** Zero data retention for model improvement
3. **Processing agreements:** Data Processing Agreements (DPAs) ensuring GDPR-compliant processing
4. **Due diligence documentation:** Records of the provider's data management practices

### APPLICATION: Compliance Analysis

#### 1. Does the "Technical Third Party" Regime Actually Apply?

This is the critical point. Shapiro bases his conclusion on Anthropic offering:

- Zero-data-retention API option
- Business Data Processing Agreements
- Guarantees that client data is not used for model training

**However, there are significant tensions:**

**a) Automated Decision-Making (Art. 22 GDPR)**

If Claude generates legal analysis that the lawyer subsequently adopts without substantive review, this could constitute "solely automated decision-making" under Art. 22 GDPR. The GDPR requires:

> "The data subject shall have the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing, including profiling, which produces legal effects concerning him or similarly significantly affects him."

A legal document directly affecting client rights (settlement agreement, response to demand letter) that is entirely AI-generated with nominal lawyer review could fall into this prohibited category.

**Conclusion on this point:** Shapiro is correct that the applicable regime is that of technical third party, but *only if* the additional condition is met: the lawyer performs substantive, not merely nominal, review and approval. The article is clear on this aspect ("every document that my firm sends out has been reviewed, revised, and approved by a licensed attorney"), but the line between substantive and superficial review is difficult to draw in litigation.

**b) Miniaturization of Confidentiality Through Fragmentation**

Another risk: Shapiro mentions he works with Claude in conversational ("chat") mode and autonomous ("Cowork") mode. In Cowork mode, Claude has access to the lawyer's entire computer folders and can read/edit multiple documents interconnectedly.

If those documents include internal client communications, ongoing case analysis, strategy memos, there is a risk that confidential information disperses across multiple queries, fragmenting privilege coverage. GDPR and Spanish professional secrecy law work better when data is clearly delimited; transfer through a tool that "reads folders" can create exposure points.

**d) Liability in Breach Cases**

What if Anthropic's servers suffer a security incident? Shapiro notes he has required Anthropic to enter into a business data processing agreement, but ultimate liability for breach remains shared. Under GDPR Art. 82, both Anthropic and the lawyer (as data controller) could be potentially liable to the client for damages arising from unauthorized access.

## III. Professional Ethics and Technological Competence

### ISSUE: Does the Professional Competence Obligation Change in the AI Era?

### RULE: Technology Competence Standards

#### A. United States

Most states have adopted or are considering changes to professional conduct rules. ABA Model Rules 1.1 (Competence) is now interpreted by many bars to include "technology competence." Some states (Florida, New York) have added explicit comments on the duty to understand technological tools.

#### B. Spain and EU

In Spain, Law 34/1988 on the Practice of the Legal Profession and the Spanish Bar Association Code of Ethics do not yet include explicit provisions on technological competence. However:

- **Directive (EU) 2019/713** on criminal protection of intellectual property establishes general due diligence standards
- **AEPD Guidelines** on AI (2023) note that providers and users of AI systems have supervisory obligations

#### C. Shapiro's Position

Shapiro argues that "ethics rules now require technological competence in most jurisdictions. We are approaching the point where not using these tools is the most difficult professional liability position to defend."

### APPLICATION: Analysis of the Proposition

This is an clever but potentially risky inversive argument:

**Traditional Position:** Using AI without expert supervision = incompetence

**Shapiro's Position:** NOT using AI when it produces better work product = incompetence

**Evaluation:** Shapiro is correct that technological competence is a growing obligation, but his conclusion assumes an unestablished fact: that Claude *consistently* produces better work product than unaided human lawyers.

The reality is more nuanced:
- Claude is excellent for first pass and high-level analysis
- It tends to "hallucinate" citations in certain contexts (Shapiro mentions this)
- Intensive supervision is required in areas of medical liability, procedural law, where errors are costly

**Correct Conclusion:** It is not that using AI is mandatory. It is that ignoring AI when it can improve efficiency *under appropriate professional supervision* could be negligent. The standard is supervision, not blind adoption.

## IV. The "Skills" System: Encoding Professional Judgment

### Analysis of Conceptual Innovation

Shapiro highlights that instead of using predefined templates, he has created "skills"—persistent instructions that encode his personal analytical framework, preferred formats, voice, and judgment on how to execute specific types of legal work.

**Important Legal Implication:** This transforms the nature of "lawyer's work." Traditionally:

- The lawyer performs intellectual work
- The work is documented and peer-reviewed
- Malpractice liability is assigned to the individual lawyer

With skills, there is a new layer:

- The lawyer encodes their judgment in instructions
- The AI executes according to those instructions
- The lawyer reviews the output

**Where does liability reside if a "skill" produces deficiency?**

Shapiro's practical example: He has a "contract review" skill with "different modes depending on context, severity ratings, missing provisions checklist, market term benchmarking, and seamless handoff to tracked changes skill."

If that skill contains defective logic—for example, it incorrectly classifies limitation of liability provisions, or market term benchmarking is obsolete—is that lawyer malpractice liability, or AI provider liability?

**Current Legal Answer:** Lawyer liability. Although they created the skill, the lawyer is responsible for its content. The skill is, legally, an expression of their professional judgment. But this is unsatisfactory because the skill can mutate and evolve in ways the lawyer does not continuously monitor.

## V. Transferability and Legal Business Models

### ISSUE: What Happens When Skills Are Transferable to Other Lawyers?

### APPLICATION: Regulatory and Liability Implications

Shapiro notes that if he had 50 associates, he could install the plugin on each machine, and each associate would immediately produce contract reviews using his analytical framework, draft communications in his voice, and apply tracked changes in his preferred format.

This is revolutionary but legally problematic:

#### 1. Multiple Liability

If all lawyers at a firm use the same skills, and a skill produces deficiency, is the firm collectively liable? Is the individual lawyer liable for using a skill created by another person?

#### 2. Supervision and Delegation

In Spain, the Code of Ethics requires lawyers to supervise delegated work. If a junior associate uses a skill created by a senior, does that satisfy the supervision obligation or circumvent it?

#### 3. Confidentiality Among Lawyers

If a skill encodes one lawyer's judgment and is used by many, does it inadvertently include privileged information from the lawyer's previous cases?

**Example:** Shapiro created a "legal research" skill containing instructions on how to "launch parallel searches simultaneously" and prioritize primary authority. If that skill was developed while Shapiro represented a client in a regulatory matter, does the skill now encode that client's confidential information, potentially accessible to all firm lawyers?

## VI. Subscription Model and Pricing Transformation

Shapiro offers monthly subscription pricing in addition to traditional hourly billing. Subscription clients receive ongoing counsel, contract review, compliance monitoring, and routine governance for a fixed monthly fee.

**Legal Implication:** This model is permitted in jurisdictions like the U.S., but in Spain requires special attention:

- **Law 34/1988** allows retainers and fixed-fee agreements
- But there must be clarity on what services are included
- **AEPD** (applicable if it includes personal data) requires clarity on who is responsible for data processing

The risk: if the client does not understand that "ongoing supervision" is now partially automated (Claude reviews 80% of the company's privacy policy, the lawyer reviews Claude's analysis), there is a risk of negligent misrepresentation.

## VII. Emerging Regulatory Challenges

### ISSUE: How Does Shapiro's Model Fit Within Frameworks Like the EU AI Act?

### RULE: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act)

The AI Regulation, in force since August 2024 in the EU, classifies AI systems into risk categories. AI systems that summarize legal analysis, generate legal research, or produce decisions on legal questions could qualify as high-risk under:

**Art. 6 (High Risk) + Annex III:**
- "AI systems used for the interpretation and application of law"

If an AI system generates a regulatory compliance analysis or a response to a demand letter, it is being used in "interpretation of law."

### APPLICATION: Compliance Obligations

Under the AI Act, providers and users of high-risk systems must:

1. **Impact assessment:** Document how the system affects fundamental rights
2. **Documentation:** Maintain records of system decisions
3. **Transparency:** Inform affected parties when AI is used
4. **Significant human oversight:** No total automation

**Shapiro's model satisfies these requirements in theory:**
- Documents Claude use
- Maintains professional supervision
- Obtains client consent
- Applies significant human oversight

**But in practice:**
- The concept of "significant human oversight" is not defined in the regulation
- If Claude generates a 30-page research memo and Shapiro reviews it in 2 hours, is that "significant" oversight?
- The AI Act does not distinguish between defensible automation and negligent automation

## VIII. Critical Evaluation: Strengths and Weaknesses of the Model

### Strengths

1. **Honesty about limitations:** Shapiro is explicit about where he trusts Claude and where he maintains critical oversight. It is not "hype." He specifically notes that "research on this is consistent: people who use AI outside its competence, or who trust it without interrogating output, perform worse than people who don't use AI at all"

2. **Professional judgment implications:** The insight that value lies in individual encoded judgment, not templates, is correct and sophisticated

3. **Disciplined accountability:** Every document is reviewed, re-reviewed, and approved. There is no autopilot

4. **Client transparency:** Includes a clause in engagement letters explaining AI use. Clients "sign it without blinking"

### Weaknesses

1. **Underestimation of hallucination risk:** Although Shapiro mentions his skills include auto-review for "hallucinated citations," hallucination evaluation techniques remain incomplete

2. **Transferability and liability:** The argument about transferring skills to 50 associates is legally naive. That would imply complex collective liability

3. **Pricing and disclosure:** The subscription model requires more explicit disclosure about where AI intervenes. "The client loves it" suggests they don't fully understand

4. **Incomplete jurisdiction:** Shapiro's examples are primarily U.S. law. In Spain/EU, where the AI Act and stricter data regulations exist, the model faces more friction

---

## IX. Conclusions and Normative Vision

### What Shapiro Is Getting Right

The core insight is correct: "Experienced lawyers have an enormous advantage in this new world, and most of them don't realize it. If you've spent 10 or 20 years developing judgment in your practice area, you are sitting on exactly the asset that AI makes more valuable, not less."

Generative AI is fundamentally a judgment amplification tool. A good lawyer with AI is much better than a mediocre lawyer with AI, who is just... a mediocre lawyer with an assistant who makes errors on autopilot.

### What Requires Clarification

1. **Automation disclosure:** Clients must understand not that "we use AI," but *which specific parts* of the work are primarily AI-generated vs. lawyer-generated

2. **Supervision standards:** "Review" must be more rigorously defined. Reading the output is insufficient. It requires the ability to detect errors, incompleteness, gaps

3. **Regulatory evolution:** The European AI Act will require specific documentation. EU lawyers need more formal frameworks than Shapiro describes

4. **Tiered liability:** There must be clarity on whether liability rests with the AI provider, the user lawyer, or both, in different scenarios

## X. Spanish and European Perspective

How would Shapiro's model translate to a Spanish law firm?

### GDPR Compliance: ✅ Viable
- Anthropic offers zero-data-retention API in the EU

- Client disclosure and consent are straightforward

### AI Act Compliance: ⚠️ Requires Additional Documentation
- Would need formal high-risk impact assessment
- Documentation of supervision process
- Formal registration of uses

### Professional Ethics Compliance: ✅ Likely

## Practical Takeaways for Lawyers in Spain and the EU

### 1. Adopt AI with Structured Supervision, Not "Hope"
It is not sufficient to use Claude and hope it works. It requires investment in designed prompts, skills (persistent instructions), and validation processes.

### 2. Document Everything
Especially in the European context, maintain records of:
- Which documents were processed by AI
- What human review was performed
- How client consent was obtained

### 3. AI Act Adaptation
If you work in high-risk areas (automated decisions on rights, regulatory compliance analysis), expect formal documentation requirements.

### 4. Client-First Transparency
Instead of Shapiro's "clients sign without blinking," consider more explicit disclosure. Explain:
- Which parts are AI-generated
- How they are supervised
- What potential risks exist

### 5. Investment in Prompts and Skills
If Shapiro spent hours developing and refining skills, you should too. This is where the true competitive value resides.
