# Critical Analysis of the Zapata Vargas Precedent: Professional Liability and the Challenge of AI Hallucinations

[Download PDF of the case](/fuentes/Zapata%20Vargas.pdf)

The integration of generative artificial intelligence (AI) in legal practice has ceased to be an efficiency promise to become a terrain of frontal collision with the most deeply rooted principles of forensic ethics and procedural loyalty. The case of attorney **Jorge Hernán Zapata Vargas**, resolved by the **Civil, Agrarian and Rural Cassation Chamber of the Supreme Court of Justice** through **Decision AC739-2026 (M.P. Martha Patricia Guzmán Álvarez)**, does not represent an isolated event of malpractice, but stands as the first jurisprudential milestone in Colombia that delineates the boundaries of professional liability in the face of so-called algorithmic "hallucinations." This case possesses fundamental strategic importance for the integrity of the judicial system, as it tests the capacity of high courts to preserve public faith and due process amid the irruption of technologies that, under an appearance of technical rigor, have the potential to introduce "pseudo-law" into judicial proceedings, eroding institutional trust.

## 1. Case Context: The Genesis of a Judicial Hallucination

The litigation finds its origin in the sustentation of an extraordinary motion for review filed against a judgment of **June 6, 2025**, issued by the Civil Family Chamber of the **Superior Court of Villavicencio**. When presenting his sustentation brief, the legal representative, Jorge Hernán Zapata Vargas, invoked the grounds for review based on an argumentative architecture that included normative and jurisprudential citations that, after technical review by the Clerk's Office, proved to be nonexistent (**Decision AC739-2026**).

In the initial document, the attorney cited **Article 250 of the General Code of Procedure (CGP)**, attributing to it paragraphs and content relating to the violation of the right to defense and the omission of notifications. In reality, said article regulates the indivisibility and evidentiary scope of documents, completely lacking paragraphs (**Decision AC739-2026**). Accompanying this normative imprecision, the attorney included six references to supposed cassation sentences (e.g., **SC-4061/2020**, **SC-5092/2019**, **SC-1730/2018**, among others) that did not appear in any official repository of the Corporation.

The central legal question lies not only in the falsity of the citations, but in the professional's subsequent justification and his persistence in the error. After being required by the Court to explain the inconsistencies, Zapata Vargas admitted that the imprecisions were "consequences and possibly from an Artificial Intelligence program worked with the purpose of streamlining answers and procedures" (**Decision AC739-2026**). What is most grave, and what determined the severity of the sanction, was the recidivism: in his amendment brief, the attorney included four new equally nonexistent jurisprudential citations (e.g., **SC1295-2019**, **SC3316-2020**), accumulating a total of ten apocryphal references before the highest instance of ordinary jurisdiction. It is imperative to infer that this behavior tensions the minimum standard of procedural loyalty required of any legal operator.

## 2. Prior Legal Doctrine and Referential Framework

The ethical architecture governing the attorney's conduct in Colombia preexisted the AI boom. However, this technology has stressed the compliance standards required by the **General Code of Procedure (CGP)**. Liability in this case is based on a systematic interpretation of Articles 78 to 81 of the CGP. **Article 78** imposes the duty to "proceed with loyalty and good faith" and "act without recklessness." Moreover, **Article 79**, in its paragraph 6, establishes a presumption of recklessness or bad faith when "deliberately inaccurate transcripts or citations are made" (**Decision AC739-2026**).

Prior to this decision, jurisprudence had already drawn warning lines:

1. **Judgment T-323 of 2024 (Constitutional Court):** Established guiding criteria for the use of AI in judicial offices, highlighting the principles of **transparency, responsibility, and non-substitution of human rationality**. It warned that "the falsity of a motivation... can occur when it produces hallucinations that are not noticed by the judicial official" (**Judgment T-323/24**).
2. **Judgment STC17832-2025:** The Supreme Court annulled a provision of the **Superior Court of Sincelejo** that was based on nonexistent citations (STC13560-2023 and STC4734-2025), qualifying it as a " configurative defect of the procedural way" that violated due process (**STC17832-2025**).

This interpretive evolution marks a transition from "simple good faith" toward an **intensified due diligence standard** when using technological tools that, by design, lack commitment to factual truth.

## 3. Judgment Reasoning: The Court's Pedagogy and the Pillars of Verification

The Supreme Court of Justice, in an exercise of high technical density, broke down the functioning of **Large Language Models (LLMs)** to ground the imputation of liability. It explains that systems like **ChatGPT** or **Claude** are not databases, but statistical prediction engines that use the **Transformer** architecture and **attention** mechanisms to predict the most probable token (word or fragment) (**Decision AC739-2026**).

### The Hallucination Phenomenon and "Pseudo-Law"

The Court highlights that the system "knows" nothing; it possesses a statistical representation of linguistic regularities. **Hallucination** or **confabulation** occurs when the model, finding no exact information, completes the sequence with material that is syntactically coherent but factually false. This is critical in law, where the ritual structure (dates, case numbers, rapporteurs) is easily replicable, creating **"pseudo-law"** that simulates normative validity but lacks real existence (**Decision AC739-2026**).

### The Five Pillars of the Duty of Verification

In light of the constitutional canon, the Court grounds the positive obligation of verification on five essential pillars (**Decision AC739-2026**, Section 4.2):

1. **Assertive Nature of Citation:** Citing a source implies an assertion of fact about its existence and validity. The person who cites assumes a commitment to truthfulness that is not exhausted in the mere absence of intent.
2. **Disproportion Between Prevention Cost and Harm:** Verification in official repositories (**Java Consulta**, Clerk's Office) is free and immediate, while the harm of a false citation can vitiate a judgment or distort the litigation.
3. **Structural Institutional Trust:** The adversarial system presupposes that parties act with transparency. Neither the judge nor the opposing party should be obligated to scrutinize every reference to rule out falsehoods.
4. **Normative Framework of Loyalty and Diligence:** "Zealous diligence" (Law 1123 of 2007) and "good faith" (Art. 78 CGP) require verification that the source is real.
5. **Structural Function of Sources:** In law, the source is the law. Invoking a nonexistent precedent is not a weak argument; it is introducing an apocryphal normative foundation.

### The Concept of "Willful Blindness"

The Court links the attorney's conduct to the doctrine of **"willful blindness."** By using a tool with known propensity for error and omitting elementary verification, the professional incurs in conscious avoidance of knowledge. While in criminal matters it is functionally equated with intent, the Court uses it here as a factor for intensifying negligence, qualifying it as a very serious breach of the duty of care (**Decision AC739-2026**).

## 4. Critical Analysis: Between Innovation and Judicial Public Faith

Decision **AC739-2026** is an exercise of necessary balance. Its strength lies in the defense of **technological neutrality**: AI is not intrinsically blameworthy, but its uncritical use is.

### The Principle of Iura Novit Curia

A notable aspect is why the Court decided to rule on the merits despite the apocryphal citations. Under the principle *iura novit curia*, the judge must determine the applicable law regardless of what is alleged by the parties. The Court purged the brief of false sources, and noting that what remained lacked legal support (grounds of Art. 355 CGP), dismissed the appeal (**Decision AC739-2026**). This protected the principal's access to justice, preventing the attorney's malpractice from automatically closing the procedural opportunity, although it ultimately did not succeed.

### Jurisdictional Comparison and Specific Hallucinations

This ruling aligns Colombia with the global trend. In **Mata v. Avianca** (USA, 2023), Judge Kevin Castel sanctioned attorneys for presenting ChatGPT citations. Likewise in **Zhang v. Chen** (Canada, 2024). In the Zapata Vargas case, the gravity is illustrated with the distortion of real precedents:

### Gaps and Challenges: Functional Transparency

Despite its solidity, the decision leaves uncertainty about **"functional transparency."** Although the Court requires warning of AI use, it does not define whether this should be done in a specific section or whether the use of grammar correction tools also requires declaration. There is a risk of generating a negative bias in the judge toward briefs admitting algorithmic assistance.

## 5. Implications for AI Law and Judicial Compliance

This precedent imposes a transformation in the Legal Operations of firms. The concept of **"Human-in-the-loop"** passes from being an ethical recommendation to a legal **compliance obligation** to avoid **referral to the Judicial Discipline Commission**.

### Reinterpretation of Recklessness and Disciplinary Risks

Negligence in the use of digital tools is now formally assimilated to **procedural recklessness**. The sanction of **15 SMLMV** (approximately 26 million pesos or $6,000 USD according to **Diario Libre**) is exemplary due to recidivism. It is imperative that legal departments implement citation audit protocols through mandatory verification against authentic sources before any filing. The attorney's reputation is inextricably linked to their capacity for technological supervision.

### Impact on Professional Liability

The Court clarifies that liability is **non-delegable**. It is not admissible to excuse oneself on the grounds that the brief was drafted by an intern, a clerk, or an AI. The signatory of the document assumes full legal authorship and guarantees the fidelity of their sources (**Decision AC739-2026**).

## 6. Jurisprudential Outlook and Evolution

The Zapata Vargas ruling, in harmony with **Judgment T-323 of 2024**, positions high courts as the architects of AI ethics regulation in Colombia in the absence of a statutory law.

### Mandates to the Superior Council of the Judiciary

The Constitutional Court granted a period of **four months** to the **Superior Council of the Judiciary** to disseminate guides and manuals on generative AI in the Judicial Branch (**Judgment T-323/24**). These manuals must cover the 12 guiding criteria (Transparency, Responsibility, Privacy, Non-substitution, etc.). It is expected that these guidelines will unify the standard of care not only for judges but for litigants as well.

### Legislative Horizon

In Congress, projects like **PL 200 of 2023 Chamber** seek to codify these limits. However, jurisprudence has already established that the attorney must be a critical filter. While technology advances toward systems with **RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** to reduce hallucinations, the burden of verification will remain with the human professional.

**Final Reflection:** The Zapata Vargas precedent reminds us that in the era of automation, the essence of law remains an act of human responsibility. Technology can assist in synthesis and drafting, but it cannot substitute normative validation. As the Court masterfully states: **"The tool assists; the attorney decides, structures, and responds"** (**Decision AC739-2026**). In this line of argument, the integrity of the Colombian judicial system will depend on the capacity of its operators to use innovation without renouncing the ethical rigor that grounds justice.
