# OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible AI: Legal Analysis and Implementation

<div className="guide-header">

**Official Source:** OECD (2026), OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible AI  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1787/41671712-en  
**PDF:** [Download complete document](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-due-diligence-guidance-for-responsible-ai)  
**Approved:** January 26, 2026 (Digital Policy Committee & Investment Committee)

</div>

## Executive Summary

The OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible AI provides a practical framework for companies across the AI value chain to identify, assess, and manage risks of adverse impacts.

**Core Structure:** 6 iterative steps integrating responsible business conduct (RBC) with OECD AI Principles (2024), aligned with the Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (MNE Guidelines).

**Scope:** Applicable to AI input providers (Group 1), developers/deployers (Group 2), and users of AI systems (Group 3).

**Relevance:** International soft law serving as a bridge between divergent regulatory frameworks (European GDPR, AI Act, national legislation).

---

## I. REGULATORY CONTEXT AND LEGAL POSITIONING

### A. Reference Regulatory Framework

The OECD Guidance is structured on three pillars:

#### 1. **OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (MNE Guidelines, 2023)**
- Voluntary RBC (Responsible Business Conduct) principles
- Applicable to multinational companies
- Cover 10 impact areas (human rights, workers, environment, corruption, consumers, taxation, science/technology, competition, disclosure)

#### 2. **OECD Recommendation on Artificial Intelligence (AI Principles, 2024)**
- 5 values-based principles (human-centred, fair, transparent, explainable, robust/secure/safe/accountable)
- 5 recommendations to policymakers
- Comprehensive lifecycle coverage of AI systems

#### 3. **UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs)**
- Alignment with "Protect-Respect-Remedy" framework
- Due diligence obligation as minimum international standard
- Access to remediation for affected persons

**Legal Implication:** The OECD positions due diligence not as a legally binding obligation (it is not legally binding), but as a responsibility standard that governments actively promote. However, this soft law is increasingly being incorporated into binding regulations (European CSDDD, national regulations).

### B. Relationship with Existing Regulatory Frameworks

| Framework | Year | Status | Relationship with OECD |
|-----------|-----|--------|---------------------|
| **GDPR** | 2018 | Binding (EU) | OECD complements with comprehensive due diligence |
| **EU AI Act** | 2024 | Binding (EU) | OECD aligns with high-risk requirements |
| **EU CSDDD** | 2024 | Binding (EU) | Incorporates OECD due diligence into due diligence obligations |
| **NIST AI RMF** | 2023 | Voluntary (US) | Partially aligned on risk management |
| **ISO/IEC 42001** | 2023 | Technical standard | Complementary to OECD for technical governance |

## II. OECD FRAMEWORK: STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS OF THE 6 STEPS

### STEP 1: Embed RBC into policies and management systems

#### ISSUE: How should internal governance be structured to implement AI due diligence?

#### RULE: OECD Standards + MNE Guidelines

The Guidance requires companies to:

1. **Adopt explicit RBC policies** including commitments to OECD AI Principles
2. **Assign responsibilities** to senior management and board level
3. **Integrate management systems** that operationalize due diligence
4. **Communicate expectations** to business relationships

#### APPLICATION: Critical Legal Interpretation

**Identified Weakness:** The Guidance does not specify what constitutes "senior management" nor establishes responsibility thresholds. In European context:

- EU CSDDD (Art. 3.1) requires executives to implement due diligence
- EU AI Act (Art. 9) requires documented risk management systems
- Spanish LOPDGDD requires designation of Data Protection Officer

**Compliance Recommendation:** Explicitly designate:
- **Chief AI Officer** or equivalent for AI governance
- **AI Risk Committee** with cross-functional representation (legal, technical, ethics)
- **Clear documentation** of roles and accountability chain

#### CONCLUSION

Step 1 requires translation into concrete governance structures. The OECD Guidance is relatively flexible, allowing adaptation to different company sizes, but creates risk of insufficiency when binding regulations require more specific measures.

---

### STEP 2: Identify and assess actual and potential adverse impacts

#### ISSUE: What risks should a company evaluate in its AI value chain and how should they be prioritized?

#### RULE: Severity + likelihood framework

The Guidance uses classic risk matrix:

**Severity** = f(Scale, Scope, Irremediability)
- **Scale:** Gravity of individual impact
- **Scope:** Number of affected persons
- **Irremediability:** Ability to restore post-damage

**Likelihood/Foreseeability:** Estimated probability based on:
- Reports from similar systems
- Reasonably foreseeable and improper use
- Geographic and political context

#### APPLICATION: Critical Analysis of Categorization

**Box 2.1 - High-Risk Uses:** The Guidance identifies potentially high-risk uses:

- Credit scoring systems (AI Act Art. 6: high risk)
- Worker monitoring (GDPR Art. 22 intersection)
- Real-time facial recognition (prohibited AI Act Art. 5.1.d)
- Migration/asylum decisions (AI Act Art. 6 + Annex III)

**Identified Tension:** What happens when OECD Guidance (soft law) and European AI Act (hard law) establish different risk levels?

Example: A credit risk analysis system using general AI:
- According to OECD: Could be "high-risk" based on potential severity
- According to AI Act: Definitely "high-risk" (Annex III)
- According to GDPR: Requires DPIA under Art. 35 if processing sensitive data

**The OECD Guidance does not resolve these tensions.** A company must comply with the most demanding framework.

#### CONCLUSION

Step 2 is conceptually sound but requires complementation with:
1. Jurisdiction-specific regulatory analysis
2. Domain expert consultation (not just RBC)
3. Clear escalation mechanisms when there is uncertainty

---

### STEP 3: Cease, prevent and mitigate adverse impacts

#### ISSUE: What specific actions must a company take when it identifies risks?

#### RULE: Responsibility matrix based on level of involvement

The Guidance establishes three responsibility categories (Boxes 2.4-2.5):

| Category | Definition | Required Actions |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| **CAUSED** | Company directly causes impact | Cease, remediate |
| **CONTRIBUTED** | Company substantially facilitates/incentivizes impact | Cease contribution, mitigate, leverage business relationships |
| **DIRECTLY LINKED** | Company linked through business relationship | Use leverage to prevent/mitigate |

**Practical Example (Scenario Box 2.5):**

Company X develops worker surveillance system. Company A uses it to detect and fire union activists.

**Company X's involvement:** CONTRIBUTED if:
- The system was designed for that use
- Company X knew of the risk
- Did not implement adequate safeguards

**If Company X had done due diligence:** Could have avoided contribution through:
- Restrictive contractual clauses
- Restricting access to high-risk clients
- Post-deployment use auditing

#### APPLICATION: Concrete Mitigation Measures

**For Group 2 (Developers/Deployers):**

Boxes 2.8-2.13 provide specific technical actions:

1. **Transparency & Explainability:**
   - Model cards, data sheets
   - Disclosure of limitations
   - Appeal mechanisms

2. **Security & Robustness:**
   - Red teaming (adversarial testing)
   - Drift monitoring (unauthorized changes)
   - Suspension protocols (Box 2.13)

3. **Deployment Safeguards:**
   - Gradient of release (closed → open)
   - Rate limiting, content filters
   - KYC (Know Your Customer) checks

**Identified Legal Tension:** Can a company refuse to serve certain clients based on potential risk of misuse?

- According to OECD: Yes, it is part of due diligence ("use-based restrictions")
- According to competition law: Potential violation of competition law
- According to GDPR: Not discrimination if based on objective risk

**Legal Recommendation:** Explicitly document the risk criteria used (objective, non-discriminatory, proportionate).

#### CONCLUSION

Step 3 provides useful tools but requires case-by-case analysis of:
1. Technical capacity to implement measures
2. Proportionality regarding identified risks
3. Compatibility with other legal obligations (competition, consumer protection)

---

### STEP 4: Track implementation and results

#### ISSUE: How should a company monitor the effectiveness of its due diligence measures?

#### RULE: Continuous monitoring + periodic audits

The Guidance requires:

- **Quantifiable metrics** of system performance
- **Documentation of test sets, evaluations, validations** (TEVV)
- **Incident reporting** to stakeholders
- **Periodic internal/external audits**

#### APPLICATION: Practical Implementation

**Identified Challenge:** The Guidance does not provide specific metrics. In European context:

- **EU AI Act, Art. 72:** Mandatory post-market monitoring
- **ISO/IEC 42001, Annex A.6:** Specifies AI governance metrics
- **NIST RMF:** Provides risk metric examples

**Recommendation:** Develop compliance dashboard with:
1. Security/robustness KPIs (e.g., adversarial robustness score)
2. Fairness KPIs (e.g., disparate impact analysis)
3. Governance KPIs (e.g., % identified incidents vs. total)

#### CONCLUSION

Step 4 is critical but requires technical complementation. The OECD Guidance provides a conceptual framework; implementation requires ML monitoring and governance expertise.

---

### STEP 5: Communicate actions to address impacts

#### ISSUE: What information should a company publicly communicate about AI due diligence?

#### RULE: Transparency requirements (OECD + AI Act)

**OECD requires disclosure of:**
- RBC policies and commitments
- Risk identification processes
- Prioritization criteria
- Actions taken/planned
- Timelines and improvement benchmarks
- System capabilities and limitations

**EU AI Act (Art. 13, 53, 55):** Requires specific technical information:
- Purpose and training information
- Significant development decisions
- Incident reporting information

#### APPLICATION: OECD vs. AI Act Tension

The OECD emphasizes "commercial sensitive information" may be protected (confidentiality concerns). But EU AI Act requires disclosure "sufficient detail" for regulators to verify compliance.

**Discordance Risk:** A company could argue it cannot disclose certain technical details due to trade secret protection, but this could violate AI Act.

**Solution:** Tiered disclosure:
- Public: Policies, principles, risk criteria
- Confidential (to regulators/auditors): Protected technical details under NDA

#### CONCLUSION

Step 5 requires balancing transparency (OECD) with trade secret protection. Emerging practice is disclosure through trusted third parties (auditors) rather than complete publication.

---

### STEP 6: Provide for or cooperate in remediation

#### ISSUE: How to remediate damages caused or contributed by AI systems?

#### RULE: Multiple remediation mechanisms (Box 2.17)

The Guidance identifies options:

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Restoration** | Restore situation prior to damage | If AI discriminated in credit: approval + interest |
| **Compensation** | Financial compensation | Quantifiable economic damages |
| **Rehabilitation** | Support services | Counseling, legal services |
| **Satisfaction** | Recognition of violated rights | Public apology, policy change |
| **Non-repetition** | Future guarantees | System decommissioning, systemic changes |

#### APPLICATION: Legal Feasibility Analysis

**Example: Discriminatory Credit Scoring System**

Identified impact: Women systematically rejected (disparate impact)

**Option 1: Restoration**
- Legally viable in theory
- Practically complex: How many women, what period?
- Requires identification and contact

**Option 2: Compensation**
- European: Clear framework under GDPR + tort liability
- Quantification: Loss of opportunity? Interest?
- CJEU jurisprudence on discrimination (cf. Defrenne v. Sabena)

**Option 3: Satisfaction + Non-repetition**
- Requirement in GDPR Art. 82: "Effective" reparation
- Implies systemic algorithm change
- Post-remediation monitoring

**Legal Challenge:** Who bears responsibility?
- System provider: Causation/Contribution
- AI user (e.g., bank): Responsibility for technical measures
- Both: Co-responsibility (cf. EU CSDDD)

#### CONCLUSION

Step 6 is where OECD soft law must connect with binding legal frameworks. The Guidance recognizes multiple mechanisms but does not establish priorities or sufficiency criteria for remediation. This requires case-by-case analysis under applicable legislation.

## III. APPLICABILITY TO DIFFERENT ACTORS (GROUPS 1-3)

### Group 1: AI Input Providers

**Includes:**
- Data/annotation providers
- Infrastructure providers (cloud, compute)
- Hardware manufacturers (semiconductors)
- Capital providers (VC investors)

**Specific Responsibilities:**

1. **Data providers:**
   - Due diligence on data origin and privacy
   - Verify data was lawfully obtained
   - Risk assessment on dataset biases

2. **Compute/Cloud providers:**
   - Infrastructure security and availability
   - GDPR compliance (Data Processing Agreements)
   - Non-facilitation of prohibited uses

3. **Investors:**
   - Portfolio risk assessment
   - Bilateral engagement with investees
   - Escalation to divesting if RBC inadequate (Box 2.16)

**GDPR/AI Act Compliance:**
- Data providers = data processors (GDPR Art. 4.8)
- Compute providers = sub-processors (GDPR Art. 28)
- Limited responsibility by control chain

### Group 2: Developers and Deployers

**Maximum Responsibilities:**

1. Full lifecycle (design → deployment → monitoring)
2. Implementation of all Step 3 measures
3. Permanent post-market monitoring
4. Responsibility for foreseeable misuse

**AI Act Compliance (most demanding):**
- Exhaustive risk management documentation
- DPIA before deployment
- Conformity assessment (Art. 43-48)
- Registration in national databases

**Legal Vulnerability:**
- Broad malpractice liability
- Liability for "foreseeable" damages
- Regulatory audit risk (AEPD, national AI authorities)

### Group 3: Users of AI Systems

**Moderate but growing responsibilities:**

1. Evaluate risks of systems they use
2. Implement workplace safeguards (if applicable)
3. Worker engagement on monitoring
4. Responsibility for misuse

**Practical Example:**

If financial company uses AI scoring system:
- Due diligence on system (did Group 2 comply?)
- Implementation of controls over decisions (human oversight)
- Communication to clients about AI use
- Appeal mechanisms

## IV. COMPLIANCE WITH KEY REGULATORY FRAMEWORKS

### A. GDPR (2018)

| GDPR Requirement | OECD Relationship | Gap |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----|
| **Art. 5: Principles** (lawful, transparency, minimization) | Aligned | None |
| **Art. 22: Automated decisions** | Complementary | OECD does not specify what constitutes "single decision" |
| **Art. 35: DPIA** | Equivalent to risk assessment | OECD broader (not just privacy) |
| **Art. 28: DPA** | Covered by Step 1 (management systems) | Specific implementation required |

**Comprehensive Compliance Recommendation:**
- Execute GDPR DPIA within OECD framework
- OECD due diligence is broader context for DPIA

### B. EU AI Act (2024)

| AI Act Requirement | OECD Relationship | Compatibility |
|--------------------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Art. 6-7: High-risk classification** | Equivalent to Step 2 risk assessment | ✅ Compatible |
| **Art. 9: Risk management system** | Equivalent to Steps 1-4 | ✅ Compatible |
| **Art. 13: Transparency** | Equivalent to Step 5 | ✅ Compatible |
| **Art. 43-48: Conformity assessment** | Complementary | ⚠️ OECD does not specify audit |
| **Art. 55: Post-market supervision** | Equivalent to Step 4 | ✅ Compatible |
| **Art. 5: Prohibited systems** | Partially covered | ❌ OECD does not address prohibitions |

**Critical Analysis:**
The European AI Act is more prescriptive than OECD. A company fully compliant with AI Act will meet most OECD requirements, but not necessarily vice versa.

**Recommendation for European companies:**
- Use AI Act as "minimum standard"
- OECD as additional "best practices framework"
- Particularly important for Step 6 (remediation)

### C. Spanish LOPDGDD (2018, amended 2023)

| Article | Requirement | OECD Relationship |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Art. 5-7** | Rights of affected persons | Covered by Step 6 (remediation) |
| **Art. 17** | Impact assessment | Equivalent to Step 2 |
| **Art. 35** | Data Protection Officer | Complementary with Due Diligence Officer |

## V. CRITICAL IMPLEMENTATION CHALLENGES

### 1. **Proportionality vs. Standardization**

**Problem:** The Guidance emphasizes that due diligence must be "proportionate" to company size and risk severity (Box 2.6). But:

- SMEs have limited resources
- European regulations require minimum standards without flexibility
- How to reconcile?

**Emerging Solution:** Collaborative approaches
- Industry coalitions for shared standards
- Collective risk assessments
- OECD explicitly recognizes this (Step 1.3, business relationships)

### 2. **Accountability Gap in Complex Supply Chains**

**Problem:** In generative AI systems:
- Multiple data providers
- Third parties do fine-tuning
- End users make adaptations

Who is responsible for what?

**The Guidance responds partially:**
- Involvement = responsibility (Boxes 2.4-2.5)
- But in practice, cause/contribution is difficult to prove

**Recommendation:** Comprehensive documentation of contributions at each lifecycle stage.

### 3. **Remediation in Cross-Border Contexts**

**Problem:** Damage caused by AI in EU used by US company with third-country data.

**Under OECD:**
- Distributed responsibility
- Leverage approach (persuade vs. compel)

**Under GDPR + AI Act:**
- Clear territorial jurisdiction
- But complex enforcement if international actors

**Solution:** Dispute resolution mechanisms:
- International arbitration
- National Contact Points (OECD) as mediators
- Regulatory authority cooperation

### 4. **General-Purpose AI Models (GPAI)**

**Unique Problem:** Base models can be used for hundreds of applications, many unpredictable at training.

**OECD Position:**
- Due diligence must consider "reasonably foreseeable" uses and misuses
- Includes potentially adverse uses
- Requires deployment safeguards

**Practical Tension:**
- How specific must risk assessment be?
- Who bears responsibility for uncontemplated uses?

**Recommendation:**
- GPAI developer: Due diligence on inherent risks
- Deployer: Due diligence on specific application
- Shared responsibility model

## VI. PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION GUIDE BY PHASE

### Phase 1: Initial Diagnosis (Weeks 1-4)

**Checklist:**

- [ ] Map all AI systems used/developedsupplied
- [ ] Identify which "Group" applies (1, 2, or 3)
- [ ] Review existing RBC policies
- [ ] Identify gaps against OECD AI Principles
- [ ] Assign governance responsibilities

**Required Resources:**
- Legal review (RBC specialist)
- Technical audit (ML/AI expertise)
- Stakeholder consultation plan

### Phase 2: Policy Development (Weeks 5-12)

**Key Actions (Step 1):**

1. Adopt/update AI Principles statement
2. Document risk governance framework
3. Establish AI Risk Committee
4. Develop escalation procedures

**Documents to Produce:**
- AI Governance Policy
- Risk Management Framework
- Business Relationship Standards
- Stakeholder Engagement Plan

### Phase 3: Risk Assessment & Scoping (Weeks 13-24)

**Key Actions (Step 2):**

1. Execute risk assessment for each system
2. Categorize as high/medium/low risk
3. Prioritize based on severity + likelihood
4. Document prioritization criteria

**Deliverables:**
- Risk Register (all AI)
- Risk prioritization matrix
- Action roadmap

### Phase 4: Mitigation Planning (Weeks 25-36)

**Key Actions (Step 3):**

1. For high-risk systems: Develop mitigation plans
2. Identify required technical measures
3. Implement deployment controls
4. Establish monitoring protocols

**By system type:**
- Data: Data governance, quality reviews
- Model: Bias audits, robustness testing
- Deployment: Access controls, usage restrictions
- Operation: Incident monitoring, feedback loops

### Phase 5: Monitoring & Reporting (Ongoing)

**Key Actions (Steps 4-5):**

1. Establish KPIs and dashboards
2. Execute periodic audits
3. Document incidents
4. Communicate progress internally
5. Report publicly (transparency)

**Recommended cadence:**
- Quarterly: Risk reviews
- Semi-annual: Audit assessments
- Annual: Public disclosure

### Phase 6: Remediation Mechanisms (As needed, Step 6)

1. Establish grievance mechanisms
2. Define escalation procedures
3. Develop remediation playbooks
4. Train staff on remediation options

## VIII. CONFLICTS AND DISCREPANCIES WITH OTHER FRAMEWORKS

### Comparative Table: OECD vs. Binding Regulations

| Aspect | OECD | GDPR | AI Act | Resolution |
|--------|------|------|--------|-----------|
| **Scope** | All RBC risks | Only privacy/data | Only high-risk AI | Apply all; OECD is broader |
| **Binding** | Voluntary (soft law) | Binding | Binding | Comply with binding as minimum |
| **Assessment method** | Severity + Likelihood | Severity alone | Risk (probability × severity) | Use most rigorous method |
| **Oversight** | Self-regulation | Regulatory authority | National authorities + Regulators | Assume regulatory oversight |
| **Remediation** | Flexible | Damages + compensation | Penalty + prohibition | Broader remediation |

**Conclusion:** For European companies, complying with AI Act and GDPR generally implies complying with OECD, but not necessarily vice versa. Always apply the most demanding standard.

## IX. CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### Strengths of the OECD Guidance

1. **Flexibility:** Allows adaptation to different contexts and company sizes
2. **Holism:** Covers complete value chain, not just data/privacy
3. **Practicality:** Provides concrete examples and playbooks
4. **Alignment:** Integrates multiple frameworks (GDPR, AI Act, UNGPs)
5. **Stakeholder-centric:** Emphasizes engagement with affected persons

### Identified Limitations

1. **Lack of prescription:** Does not specify what constitutes "sufficient mitigation"
2. **Responsibility ambiguity:** In complex chains, accountability can be diluted
3. **Soft law without enforcement:** Compliance depends on corporate good faith
4. **GPAI Gap:** Limited guidance for general-purpose models
5. **Underdeveloped Remediation:** Step 6 is less detailed than other steps

### Recommendations for Implementation

**For AI developers/deployers (Group 2):**

1. **Adopt OECD as foundational framework** but complement with AI Act/GDPR requirements
2. **Establish formal governance** with clear responsibilities
3. **Execute comprehensive risk assessments** not just privacy but all OECD principles
4. **Exhaustively document** all mitigation decisions
5. **Implement permanent post-market monitoring** with specific KPIs
6. **Establish remediation mechanisms** before damages arise

**For input providers (Group 1):**

1. **Implement due diligence** proportionate to role in value chain
2. **Provide transparent information** to business relationships about known risks
3. **Actively collaborate** in risk assessment of downstream actors
4. **Monitor regulatory evolution** in multiple jurisdictions

**For AI users (Group 3):**

1. **Procure systems from providers committed to OECD**
2. **Execute specific risk assessment** on own applications
3. **Implement controls over decisions** (human oversight)
4. **Communicate to stakeholders** about AI use

**For regulators:**

1. The OECD Guidance provides solid basis for international harmonization
2. Recommendation: Explicitly incorporate into national regulations (e.g., LOPDGDD reform)
3. Establish coordinated supervision mechanism (e.g., AI authority network)

## Final Note

This OECD Guidance represents an important milestone in the evolution of corporate responsibility in AI. Although it is soft law, its influence is undeniable—it is being incorporated into binding regulations in multiple jurisdictions.

**For lawyers and compliance officers:** The Guidance should be understood as a directive framework working in conjunction with, not instead of, local regulations. Case-by-case analysis is essential.

**For companies:** Proactive OECD implementation not only fulfills RBC obligations but provides robust defense against litigation or regulatory review.

**For regulators:** The Guidance provides a basis for international convergence in AI governance—currently highly fragmented.
