## Introductory Framework and Phenomenology of the Conflict

### 1.1 The Anthropic vs. Department of Defense Conflict: Sovereignty and Corporate Ethics

The landscape of U.S. national security in February 2026 has been defined by an unprecedented rupture between the executive branch and the frontier artificial intelligence sector. This conflict, catalyzed by the termination of a multi-year contract valued at up to **$200 million**, represents the materialization of a structural tension between state sovereignty and corporate autonomy in the deployment of dual-use technologies.

The genesis of the dispute lies in the imposition of **ethical "red lines"** by the company Anthropic on its Claude model, which explicitly restrict its application in:

- **Mass domestic surveillance** of U.S. citizens
- **Operation of autonomous weapons systems** without significant human supervision
- **Intelligence operations** with elevated risks of fundamental rights violations

In response to this posture, Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth, under the Trump administration, has proceeded to **designate Anthropic as a "Risk to the Supply Chain and National Security."** This technical measure imperatively prohibits any commercial activity between Anthropic and the partners or contractors of the renamed Department of Defense, under the argument that the State must possess **total and unrestricted access** for all legal purposes in the defense of the republic.

### 1.2 The "Woke AI" Problem Versus National Interest

The federal administration has framed this confrontation within a broader security policy aimed at eradicating what it terms **"Woke AI."** According to the December 2025 presidential directives, the government considers that the ethical safeguards imposed by technology companies are not neutral, but constitute:

- **Ideological biases** disguised as security criteria
- **Institutional extortion** attempting to subordinate constitutional order to corporate terms of service
- **Bureaucratic obstacles** to frontier innovation

The technical-political problem centers on the government demand for **models that generate truthful results without the restrictions imposed by equity frameworks**. According to the executive, these restrictions slow innovation and allow strategic adversaries, particularly **China**, to achieve supremacy in the technological race.

The removal of Anthropic from official platforms such as **USAi.gov** constitutes not merely a commercial sanction, but a prescriptive action to force a transition toward services the government characterizes as more patriotic and aligned with the need for rapid acceleration of the decision cycle.

### 1.3 Objectives and Methodology of the Analysis

This report's primary objective is to technically and systematically analyze the impact of this rupture on:

- The **defense architecture** of the United States
- The **global AI ecosystem** and its normative derivations
- The **balances between state sovereignty and corporate autonomy**

The methodology adopted is **multidisciplinary and deductive**, structured on three fundamental analytical pillars:

**Technical-Operational Dimension**
: Evaluation of the reliability of current models and the risks of opaque algorithms in combat contexts, analyzing the different risk levels detected in critical data processing.

**Ethical-Legal Dimension**
: Analysis of the responsibility gap and the adequacy of systems to International Humanitarian Law, particularly in the protection of fundamental rights and the maintenance of meaningful human control.

**Regulatory and Geopolitical Dimension**
: Study of state pressure instruments, such as the Defense Production Act, to ensure technological sovereignty against normative fragmentation among states.

## Technical Foundations and Taxonomy of AI in Defense

### 2.1 Core Concepts: Big Data, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning Applied to Combat

The operability of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in the military domain should not be understood as an isolated technological phenomenon, but as a **systemic architecture** dependent on the integration of advanced logical processes.

**Big Data** (Massive Data Volume)
: The fundamental pillar of this structure is Big Data, technically defined by the concurrence of three vectors:
  - **Volume**: Massive scale data (terabytes, petabytes)
  - **Velocity**: Real-time processing of continuous streams
  - **Variety**: Multiple sources (ground sensors, satellites, communications intelligence)

In the tactical environment, Big Data acts as the **critical input** that feeds the "combat cloud," transforming raw data into a common operational picture that reduces command uncertainty.

**Machine Learning**
: The processing of this volume of information is executed through Machine Learning, an AI subdiscipline focused on the development of algorithms that optimize their performance through statistical experience. Machine learning systems:
  - Identify patterns in historical data
  - Iteratively improve their performance without reprogramming
  - Enable predictions based on massive data analysis

**Deep Learning**
: Within this paradigm, Deep Learning represents the highest level of technical complexity, using **multi-layered artificial neural networks** interconnected that simulate the hierarchical structure of the human brain. These networks:
  - Identify hidden patterns in unstructured data
  - Perform visual target recognition in drone images
  - Analyze behavioral profiles with statistical precision

However, this complexity introduces the critical risk of **opaque algorithms** or "black boxes," where the internal reasoning of AI for a lethal recommendation proves unintelligible to the human operator, compromising traceability and the ethical basis of the decision.

### 2.2 Autonomy Levels and Human Control: HITL/HOTL/HOOTL Classification

The integration of AI into weapons systems requires a **rigorous taxonomy** of the degree of human intervention, structured in three levels of risk and control:

| Level | Acronym | Description | Legal Risk | Current Status |
|-------|----------|-------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Semiautonomous** | HITL | Machine identifies/tracks; operator authorizes force use | Low | ✓ Deployed |
| **Supervised Autonomous** | HOTL | AI executes shot; human can intervene in real-time | Medium-High | ⚠ In Development |
| **Fully Autonomous** | HOOTL | System operates without intervention after initial activation | Critical | ✗ Prohibited (mostly) |

**Human-in-the-Loop (HITL)**
: Semiautonomous systems where the machine identifies and tracks targets, but the human operator retains exclusive authority to initiate lethal action. This is the minimum standard required by current fundamental rights frameworks.

**Human-on-the-Loop (HOTL)**
: Supervised autonomous systems where AI can make operational decisions and execute the shot on its own, but a human monitors the process with the technical capability to intervene or abort the sequence in real time.

**Human-out-of-the-Loop (HOOTL)**
: Fully autonomous systems (LAWS - Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems) that operate without human intervention or supervision after initial activation.

The transition toward HOOTL levels is the point of **greatest doctrinal friction**. Technically, current models are warned not to possess the necessary reliability to precisely discern complex legal concepts such as:

- The distinction between combatants and civilians
- Identification in dense urban environments
- Assessment of surrender capability
- Determination of *hors de combat* status

The delegation of life-and-death decisions to autonomous machines generates a **responsibility gap**, where the causal nexus of an unlawful action dilutes among programmers, analysts, and the algorithm itself, challenging the accountability principles of International Humanitarian Law.

### 2.3 Critical Infrastructure: Semiconductors and the Supply Chain

The analytical capability of military AI is inseparable from its **physical hardware or base**. Defense applications depend on two critical components whose supply chain presents latent strategic vulnerability:

**Fundamental Semiconductors (28 nm and above)**
- Constitute the "workhorse" of contemporary military technology
- Integrated into radars, missile guidance systems, and control units
- An average military vehicle requires **more than 1,700 components** for operation
- Domestic production: **4%** of global total
- Chinese dominance: **>50%** of world production

**Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs)**
- Act as the "digital backbone" of military electronics
- 40% of demand comes from the defense sector
- U.S. industrial base: severely atrophied
- Critical Asian supply dependency: **existential risk**

This dependency generates **critical national security risks**, including:
- Possibility of **sabotage through malicious components** inserted in hardware
- **Operational paralysis** through supply denial
- **Supply chain vulnerabilities** introduced by adversaries

Therefore, **technological sovereignty in AI** requires not only algorithmic excellence, but a prescriptive policy of **reindustrialization and supplier diversification** to ensure defense industrial base resilience against geopolitical competitors.

## Operational Applications and Impact on the Operational Theater

### 3.1 Decision Support Systems (DSS): Project Maven and JADC2

The integration of Artificial Intelligence into command and control architecture has ceased to be a peripheral capability to become the **core of military modernization**. AI Decision Support Systems (AI-DSS) are designed to mitigate uncertainty through massive information processing from multiple domains.

**The JADC2 Concept (Joint All-Domain Command and Control)**
: Represents the most significant paradigm shift in U.S. strategy, seeking to:
  - **Centralize operational planning and execution** through an interconnected combat cloud
  - Create a unified **Common Operational Picture (COP)** across services
  - Enable **any sensor to provide data to any attack platform** instantaneously
  - Reduce the decision cycle from hours to minutes (or seconds)

**Project Maven: Intelligence Analysis Automation**
- Employs **computer vision algorithms** to automate analysis of unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) imagery
- Autonomously identifies **hostile activities or military targets**
- Frees human analysts from **data saturation**
- Enables **resource allocation based on strategic priorities** processed in real time
- Generates target recommendations at an unprecedented scale

### 3.2 OODA Loop Automation: AI as a Force Multiplier

The military utility of AI is quantified through its capacity to **accelerate the OODA loop** (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act), achieving that the organization surpasses the adversary's reaction speed.

```
TRADITIONAL OODA LOOP (Hours)
Observe → Orient → Decide → Act
   ↑_________________________________↓
             Feedback

AI-ENABLED OODA LOOP (Minutes/Seconds)
Observe ─AI─> Orient ─AI─> Decide ─AI─> Act
   ↑__________________________________↓
         Time Compression
```

By acting as a **power multiplier**, AI enables processing of immense quantities of information that exceed human cognitive capacity, transforming raw data into what is termed **"automatic intuition."** This cognitive advantage is essential in hybrid war environments or the so-called "gray zone," where threat identification is complex and ambiguous.

**Critical Identified Risks:**
- **Automation bias**: The propensity of military personnel not to question AI results due to false perception of technical objectivity
- **Loss of meaningful control**: If a commander does not understand the internal reasoning of a recommendation, the decision lacks a solid ethical basis
- **Ethical compression**: Time pressure may lead to automatic acceptance of recommendations without critical evaluation

### 3.3 Case Studies: Real Systems in Current Operations

#### 3.3.1 "Habsora" (Gospel) System - Gaza Strip

- **Function**: Rapid target generation based on infrastructure and buildings
- **Capability**: Produces **up to 100 bombing recommendations daily**
- **Comparison**: In contrast to the 50 annual targets a human analyst previously processed
- **Identified risks**: Acceptable error margins for low-rank targets; use of unguided munitions in dense urban areas

#### 3.3.2 "Lavender" System - Biometric Identification

- **Function**: Target database through statistical probability
- **Scope**: Has listed **up to 37,000 individuals** linked to hostile groups
- **Method**: Association through behavioral pattern analysis
- **Consequences**: Target list creation based on correlation, not certainty

#### 3.3.3 "Where's Daddy?" System - Family Targeting

- **Function**: Tracking of identified individuals to attack their family homes
- **Legal criticality**: Potential violation of IHL distinction and proportionality principles
- **Ethical implication**: Conversion of family members into secondary targets through associative logic

#### 3.3.4 Drone Swarms (Nemyx Technology) - Ukrainian Front

- **Capability**: Autonomous coordination of multiple drones as a single organism
- **Response**: Real-time adaptation to changes in human priorities or electronic interference
- **Operative scale**: A soldier can control **multiple attack units simultaneously**
- **Tactical objective**: Saturate and bypass enemy air defenses autonomously
- **Critical risk**: Machine learning in combat enables drones to adapt tactics independently

## Ethical Dimensions and the International Legal Framework

### 4.1 International Humanitarian Law (IHL): Operational Principles

The integration of artificial intelligence into lethal target selection demands **rigorous technical adequacy** to the imperative norms of International Humanitarian Law.

#### 4.1.1 Principle of Distinction

**Normative definition**: Obligation to discriminate at all times between combatants and the civilian population.

**Technical application to AI:**
- Current algorithms operate through **probabilistic reasoning**
- Present **elevated error rates** in dense and dynamic urban environments
- AI **lacks the capacity to discern complex qualitative concepts**, such as:
  - Surrender intention
  - *Hors de combat* status
  - Dynamic changes in combatant status

**Fundamental problem**: Systems operate exclusively under **statistical inferences** from historical data that may be biased or incomplete.

#### 4.1.2 Principle of Proportionality

**Normative definition**: Prohibits attacks where incidental civilian damage is excessive in relation to the anticipated direct military advantage.

**Problematic implementation in AI systems:**
- Systems like "Habsora" and "Lavender" reveal a **systematization of pre-authorized risk levels**
- **Fixed collateral damage quotas** are accepted according to target rank
- Delegating this calculation to an algorithm is **legally problematic** because:
  - Proportionality requires a **subjective and contextual value judgment**
  - It escapes binary machine logic
  - Introduces **automation of decisions requiring human judgment**

#### 4.1.3 Principle of Precaution

**Normative definition**: Obligation to take all feasible measures to minimize human suffering.

**Effects of AI acceleration:**
- Decision cycle acceleration **endangers the necessary "tactical patience"**
- Evaluating less harmful alternatives requires **time for analysis**
- AI transforms the human operator into a **mere approval stamp** within a mechanized process

### 4.2 The Accountability Gap

One of the most profound legal vacuums is the termed **accountability gap**. This phenomenon occurs when:

```
AI System Commits Fundamental Rights Violation
                    ↓
Attribution of Causal Nexus to Specific Human Decision Becomes Impossible
                    ↓
Responsibility Diluted Among Multiple Actors
```

**Actors involved with ambiguous responsibility:**
1. **Programmer**: Designed the opaque algorithm
2. **Intelligence Agent**: Collected training data
3. **Analyst**: Accepted recommendation in seconds
4. **Commander**: Ordered execution

**Fundamental legal problem**:
- International Law is based on the **moral and legal responsibility of individuals**
- Artificial agents **lack responsible capacity**
- Culpability dilutes to the point of being unassignable

**Automation bias:**
This is the propensity of military personnel to **not question** AI results due to a **false perception of technical objectivity**. If a commander does not understand the internal reasoning of an attack recommendation, the decision **lacks a solid ethical basis**.

**Critical consequence:**
- Absence of **binding international treaties** specifically regulating LAWS
- Risk that **atrocities committed by machines go unpunished**
- Violation of the very notion of **just war and accountability**

### 4.3 Comparative Ethical Doctrine: Department of Defense vs. Anthropic

The conflict embodies the collision of two divergent doctrinal frameworks:

| Aspect | DoD Doctrine | Anthropic Doctrine |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Primary Objective** | Sovereignty and technological supremacy | Technical reliability and safety |
| **Model Access** | Total and unrestricted for "any legal purpose" | Restrictions on mass surveillance and LAWS |
| **Underlying Logic** | Imperative of geopolitical dominance | Technical risk analysis and fundamental rights |
| **"Red Lines" Posture** | Are ideological restrictions compromising national defense | Are technical safeguards preventing IHL violations |
| **Corporate Autonomy Interpretation** | Constitutes "veto power" incompatible with sovereignty | Is ethical responsibility of technology provider |

**Official Department of Defense posture:**
Articulates five ethical principles: responsibility, equity, traceability, reliability, and governability. However, it interprets these principles under the **national sovereignty imperative**, demanding:
- Total and unrestricted access
- Absence of external dependencies
- Right to use technology for "any legal purpose"

**Anthropic's posture:**
Establishes prescriptive "red lines" prohibiting:
- Mass domestic surveillance
- Fully autonomous weapons systems
- Lethal force decisions without meaningful human supervision

The company maintains that current frontier models **do not possess the necessary reliability** to guarantee the safety of combatants and civilians. This technical evaluation generates irreconcilable conflict with demands for total subordination.

## AI Regulation, Sovereignty, and Geopolitics

### 5.1 The New National AI Framework: Federal Centralization

AI governance in the U.S. has transitioned toward a model of **absolute centralization**, based on the premise that **normative fragmentation** among states constitutes a risk to national security.

**December 2025 Presidential Guidelines:**
- Prescription of a uniform national standard
- Explicit prohibition of the "mosaic" of state laws
- Technical qualification: "bureaucratic barrier that paralyzes innovation"

**Primary objective:**
Nullify local regulations such as those detected in:
- **Colorado**: Algorithmic bias detection
- **California**: Ideological equity frameworks
- **Other states**: Transparency and accountability regulations

**Implementation mechanisms:**

1. **AI Litigation Task Force**
   - Authority: Department of Justice
   - Function: Challenge any state law interfering with technological development

2. **Financial coercion**
   - Federal government withholds infrastructure and connectivity funds (BEAD)
   - Destination: States maintaining "onerous" regulations
   - Criterion: That force models to alter results according to social agendas

### 5.2 State Pressure Instruments: DPA and Risk Designation

The State has redesigned its legal architecture through extraordinary instruments:

#### 5.2.1 Defense Production Act (DPA)

**Legal origin**: Statute from the Korean War era

**Authority granted to the President:**
- Prioritize defense contracts
- Compel high-capacity AI companies to collaborate on national security projects
- Force performance of tasks deemed "critical"

**Official presentation:**
Prescriptive measure to eliminate **corporate terms of service** that act as a "veto" over military operational decisions.

#### 5.2.2 Designation as "Supply Chain Risk"

**Commercial isolation mechanism:**
- Legal designation without formal due process requirement
- **Imperatively prohibits** any DoD partner, supplier, or contractor from maintaining relationships with designated entity

**Application in Anthropic case:**
- Termination of direct contracts
- Isolation of entire military supply chain
- Cascade effect: Providers must choose between Anthropic or DoD

**Strategic objective:**
Force **"patriotic alignment"** in the technological ecosystem, ensuring:
- Hardware and software in classified networks lack external dependencies
- No ethical restrictions beyond federal validation
- Command chain operates under total state control

### 5.3 The Race for Supremacy: AI 2030 and U.S. vs. China Strategic Competition

AI geopolitics is currently defined by an **existential competition** between the United States and the People's Republic of China, projected toward the **2030** horizon.

#### 5.3.1 U.S. Vision: Integral Dominance

**Pillars of national strategy:**

| Component | Description | Investment |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Algorithmic Supremacy** | More capable frontier models | Private investment + federal funds |
| **Supply Chain Resilience** | National semiconductors and PCBs | $400M in MP Materials + others |
| **Decision Cycle Speed** | OODA acceleration through AI | DoD/intelligence integration |
| **Arsenal Modernization** | Nuclear and conventional with AI | Department of Defense |
| **Technological Sovereignty** | Absence of critical dependencies | Normative centralization + DPA |

**Strategic investment:**
- Semiconductors: $400 million (MP Materials)
- AI infrastructure: Massive federal funds
- Military research: Defense budget + DARPA

#### 5.3.2 Chinese Strategy: "Intelligentized Warfare"

**Differentiated approach:**
- Civil-military fusion in AI development
- Autonomous swarm deployment
- Total state control of supply chain (semiconductors, rare earths)
- Integration of AI into new operational doctrines

**Current competitive advantage:**
- Semiconductors production: >50%
- Rare earth control: Global dominance
- AI-military operations integration: Advanced

#### 5.3.3 Systemic Risks of the Race

The urgency to achieve operational dominance may incentivize:

- **Deployment of opaque algorithms** in high-criticality environments
- **IHL violations** in pursuit of tactical advantage
- **Dilution of human control** through time compression
- **Risk of uncontrolled escalation** in autonomous systems

**Fundamental challenge:**
Maintain balance between:
- Military effectiveness (speed, lethality, autonomy)
- Protection of fundamental rights (distinction, proportionality, accountability)

## Strategic Synthesis and Recommendations

### 6.1 Lessons from the Anthropic Case

The conflict constitutes a **fundamental stress test** for public-private collaboration in critical technologies.

**Principal lessons:**

1. **Ethical alignment is not neutral**
   - Is not merely a technical factor, but direct collision with state sovereignty
   - Determines who controls access and conditions of use

2. **Limits of corporate autonomy in defense**
   - Corporate autonomy has strict limits when the product is a strategic asset
   - National risk designations can be used as normative weapons

3. **Provider transition**
   - The federal system prioritizes operational continuity under "total legal use"
   - OpenAI and xAI accepted integrating safeguards within classified contracts
   - Establishes precedent: Acceptance of government term is condition of access

4. **Importance of technical reliability**
   - Anthropic prioritized reliability to avoid risk of disproportionate attacks
   - Government interpreted as "ideological extortion"
   - Reveals incompatibility between technical risk evaluation and military imperative

### 6.2 Recommendations for Technological Sovereignty

To guarantee defense industrial base resilience:

#### 6.2.1 Hardware Assurance

**Immediate actions:**
- Expand domestic manufacturing capacity for fundamental semiconductors
- Establish guaranteed minimum prices
- Finance domestic productive scale

**Reference model**: $400M investment in MP Materials for rare earths

**Justification**: 40% of PCB demand comes from defense sector; unacceptable vulnerability

#### 6.2.2 Data and Model Transparency

**Uniform federal standard:**
- Centralize frontier model supervision
- Eliminate "mosaic" of state laws
- Implement analytical tools (SCALE) to map vulnerabilities in combat cloud

**Objective**: Ensure no foreign component compromises classified networks

#### 6.2.3 Strategic Decoupling

**Dependency policy:**
- Condition federal contracts on absence of critical Chinese dependencies
- Force "patriotic alignment" through incentive and exclusion threat
- Establish supply chain audits

### 6.3 Final Conclusions

**Central thesis:**
AI integration at the operational level is an unavoidable power multiplier, but the pursuit of technological supremacy **must not lead** to acceptance of opaque algorithms that dilute moral and legal responsibility.

**Foundation:**
- A system that **cannot distinguish** combatants and civilians not only violates IHL but constitutes **operational risk** for own forces

**Critical prescriptions:**

1. **Human factor must remain central** to every lethal decision
2. **Continuous technical supervision** by AISI and NIST
3. **AI informs but never replaces** significant human judgment
4. **Assignable responsibility** throughout the decision chain

**Integral conclusion:**
U.S. dominance in the AI race for 2030 will depend on its capacity to:

- **Merge** algorithmic lethality with robust ethical framework
- **Protect** fundamental rights and ensure accountability
- **Maintain** command chain integrity against growing machine autonomy
- **Balance** military urgency with legal prudence

## Glossary of Technical-Legal Terms

**Opaque Algorithms (Black Box)**
: AI systems whose inference processes and internal parameters are difficult to correlate with results, making them unintelligible to the human operator.

**Machine Learning**
: AI subdiscipline focused on algorithms that automatically improve their performance through experience and statistical data analysis.

**Big Data**
: Methodology and set of technologies for capturing, managing, and analyzing massive data volumes characterized by high velocity and variety of sources.

**Accountability Gap**
: Legal and ethical vacuum that arises when an autonomous system commits a harmful action and it becomes impossible to attribute the causal nexus to a specific human decision.

**OODA Loop**
: Iterative decision-making process (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act) that seeks to surpass the adversary's reaction speed.

**International Humanitarian Law (IHL)**
: Set of norms protecting non-participating persons in hostilities and limiting the effects of war, applicable to armed conflicts.

**Human-in-the-Loop (HITL)**
: Control level where the human must authorize each lethal action of the machine.

**Human-on-the-Loop (HOTL)**
: Level where the machine can act on its own but the human supervises with real-time intervention capability.

**LAWS (Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems)**
: Military systems capable of selecting and attacking targets independently without significant human intervention.

**Red Lines**
: Ethical and operational restrictions imposed by developers to prohibit specific AI uses.

**Combat Cloud**
: Interconnected network enabling transparent real-time data exchange among all military platforms.

**Automation Bias**
: Human propensity to excessively trust suggestions from an automated system without critical evaluation.

**Technological Sovereignty**
: State's capacity to control technological assets and supply chains without critical foreign provider dependencies.
