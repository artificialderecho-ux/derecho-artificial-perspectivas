# United Nations Resolution A/RES/78/265: Global Legal Framework for Safe and Reliable Artificial Intelligence Systems

<Alert className="mb-6 bg-blue-50 border-blue-200">
  <AlertDescription className="text-blue-900">

## I. FUNDAMENTAL LEGAL QUESTION

What is the international regulatory framework established by the United Nations General Assembly for the governance of safe and reliable artificial intelligence systems, its binding obligations for Member States, and how does it align with compliance with the Sustainable Development Goals?

---

## II. APPLICABLE LAW AND INTERNATIONAL LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### A. Foundations of International Law

Resolution 78/265 is based on several fundamental international legal pillars:

#### 1. United Nations Charter and Universal Declaration of Human Rights

The document expressly reaffirms both the United Nations Charter and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights as overarching frameworks that bind any regulation on technology and artificial intelligence. These instruments establish the principles of respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms that must be preserved throughout the entire lifecycle of AI systems.

#### 2. 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development

The resolution links AI systems governance with achieving the 17 Sustainable Development Goals. This connection establishes that artificial intelligence must be conceived as an instrument for accelerating sustainable development in its three dimensions: economic, social, and environmental.

#### 3. Complementary Resolutions on Technology and Fundamental Rights

The regulatory framework is complemented by multiple resolutions addressing specific aspects:

- **Impact of rapid technological change** on achieving SDGs
- **Information and communications technologies** for sustainable development
- **Science, technology, and innovation** as enablers of development
- **Promotion and protection of human rights** in digital technology contexts
- **Right to privacy in the digital age** as a fundamental safeguard
- **Information Society** as a historical digital governance framework

### B. Legal Definition of "Safe and Reliable Artificial Intelligence Systems"

The Resolution provides a legally binding operational definition of what constitutes "safe and reliable artificial intelligence systems" in non-military contexts:

#### Essential Characteristics (Complete Lifecycle)

Systems include all stages: **pre-design, design, development, evaluation, testing, deployment, use, sale, acquisition, operation, and retirement**.

#### Constitutive Attributes

A system is considered safe and reliable when:

1. **Human-centered** - Preserves human agency and dignity
2. **Reliable** - Functions predictably and consistently
3. **Explainable** - Allows understanding of its functioning and decisions
4. **Ethical** - Aligned with internationally recognized ethical principles
5. **Inclusive** - Accessible and beneficial to diverse populations
6. **Respectful of human rights** - Full protection of rights and freedoms
7. **Respectful of international law** - Compliance with international norms
8. **Privacy-preserving** - Protection of personal data
9. **Sustainable development-oriented** - Contribution to SDGs
10. **Accountable** - Clear accountability and remediation mechanisms

### C. Legally Identified Risks

The Resolution also explicitly lists risks that justify regulation:

- Difficulty in achieving Agenda 2030 and meeting SDGs
- Undermining of three-dimensional sustainable development
- Deepening of digital gaps and inequalities
- Reinforcement of structural biases and discrimination
- Affecting information integrity
- Weakening of human rights protection
- Increased risks of accidents and threats from malicious actors

## III. APPLICATION: LEGAL OBLIGATIONS OF STATES

### A. Mandatory Obligations (Binding Provisions)

#### 1. Bridging the Artificial Intelligence Gap

Operational paragraph 1 **Resolves** that States have the obligation to reduce and eliminate AI gaps between countries and within them. This obligation includes:

- Creation of national technical capacity
- Technology transfer on mutually agreed terms
- Technical and financial assistance to developing countries
- Equitable participation in international AI governance

#### 2. Active Promotion of Safe and Reliable Systems

Operational paragraph 2 **Resolves** that States must actively promote safe and reliable AI systems as SDG accelerators, which implies:

- Removal of unnecessary regulatory barriers for safe systems
- Encouragement of responsible AI investment
- Creation of environments conducive to ethical innovation
- Risk management without hindering digital transformation

**Geographic scope:** This obligation is **especially urgent for developing countries**, recognizing capacity asymmetries.

#### 3. Regulation Through National Regulatory Approaches and Frameworks

Operational paragraph 6 establishes that States are **Encouraged** (flexible obligation modality) to promote safe systems through:

**a) National Regulatory Frameworks**

Compatible with:
- National policies and priorities
- International law obligations
- Different regulatory approaches based on national context

Oriented toward:
- Responsible and inclusive innovation
- Investment in AI
- Safe and reliable systems

**b) Operational Security Measures**

States should encourage:
- Identification, classification, and interoperable assessment of vulnerabilities
- Testing and risk prevention before deployment
- Post-deployment feedback mechanisms
- Public awareness of system capabilities and limitations

**c) Technical and Organizational Safeguards**

- Risk monitoring and management throughout lifecycle
- Personal data protection and privacy policies
- Impact assessments as appropriate
- Physical and system security

**d) Transparency and Traceability**

States must promote:
- Technical tools for content authentication (watermarking, labeling)
- Ability to identify manipulated vs. authentic content
- Media and information literacy
- Human oversight in automated decisions

**e) Non-Discrimination and Rights Protection**

Frameworks for:
- Avoiding biases coded in data
- Combating algorithmic discrimination
- Protecting vulnerable populations
- Differential impact analysis

**f) Intellectual Property Protection and Innovation**

Balance between:
- Copyright and protected content protection
- Encouragement of innovation in AI systems

**g) Privacy and Data Protection**

- Compliance with international, national, and subnational legal frameworks
- Transparency in use of personal data
- Reporting requirements

**h) Significant Human Oversight**

In automated decisions affecting end users:
- Review of automated decisions
- Human decision alternatives
- Effective remedy and accountability means

**i) Linguistic and Cultural Diversity**

Especially relevant for **large language models**:
- Multilingualism in training data
- Diversity preservation throughout lifecycle

**j) Collaborative Research**

- Information exchange among actors
- Evidence-based best practices
- Identification of benefits and risks

**k) Attention to Gender Digital Gap**

- Equitable access to digital technologies including AI
- Digital literacy for women
- Online privacy and security
- Gender perspective and racial equality in policies

**l) Labor Impact Assessment**

- Research on labor market effects
- Mitigation of negative consequences especially in developing countries
- Digital training programs
- Support for inclusive innovation

### B. International Cooperation Obligations

Operational paragraph 4 **Urges** States to cooperate to achieve:

#### 1. Inclusive and Equitable Access

- Expanding participation of all countries in digital transformation
- Effective participation in development, deployment, and use of AI systems
- Technical capacity building
- Knowledge sharing and technology transfer

#### 2. Improving Connectivity and Infrastructure

- Alliances for digital infrastructure
- Access to technological innovations
- Participation throughout AI system lifecycle
- Ensuring global security and reliability

#### 3. Increased Capacity in Least Developed Countries

- Removal of structural obstacles
- Access to scientific sources
- Affordable technology
- Research and development
- Strengthening alliances

#### 4. Mobilizing Financing

- Increased funds for SDG innovation and research
- Research on safe digital technologies and AI systems
- Capacity for contribution from all regions

#### 5. Creating Conducive Environments

- Technology transfer on mutually agreed terms
- Technical and financial assistance
- Regulation compatible with national needs
- Inclusive AI solutions

## IV. INTERNATIONAL ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE GOVERNANCE

### A. Governance Guiding Principles

The Resolution establishes that any governance approach must be:

1. **Appropriate** - Adapted to differentiated contexts and needs
2. **Based on international law** - Coherent with existing regulatory framework
3. **Interoperable** - Compatible across jurisdictions
4. **Agile** - Capable of adapting to rapid technological change
5. **Adaptable** - Flexible for evolving technology and understanding
6. **Inclusive** - Equitable participation of developed and developing countries
7. **Responsive** - Attentive to differentiated needs and capabilities

### B. Multilateral Actors in Governance

Operational paragraph 3 identifies that governance requires participation of:

- **Member States** - Normative leadership
- **Private sector** - Corporate responsibility
- **International and regional organizations** - Coordination and standards
- **Civil society** - Surveillance and citizen participation
- **Media** - Public information
- **Academic circles and research institutions** - Analysis and evidence
- **Technical communities** - Specialized expertise
- **Affected persons and communities** - Direct participation

Operational paragraph 8 particularly emphasizes that **"all communities, particularly in developing countries"** must participate in a **"cohesive, effective, coordinated, and inclusive"** manner.

### C. Role of the United Nations System

Operational paragraph 10 **Urges** specialized agencies, funds, programs, and UN system entities to:

- Assess and improve response to AI opportunities and challenges
- Work collaboratively, coordinatedly, and inclusively
- Conduct research on impact and applications
- Report on progress and difficulties
- Collaborate with developing countries on capacity building
- Facilitate shared access to benefits of safe AI systems
- Contribute to SDG achievement

## V. HUMAN RIGHTS PROTECTION

### A. Fundamental Obligation of Respect and Protection

Operational paragraph 5 establishes a **mandatory mandate** that **"human rights and fundamental freedoms must be respected, protected, and promoted throughout the entire lifecycle of artificial intelligence systems."**

This mandate is **unconditional**:
- Not limited to "high-risk" systems
- Applies to **the entire lifecycle**
- Establishes absolute limit on system operation

### B. Prohibition of Systems Incompatible with International Law

States are **urged to refrain from or cease using** systems that:

1. Are **impossible to operate in accordance with international law**, OR
2. Pose **undue risks** to enjoyment of human rights
3. Especially for **populations in vulnerable situations**

This provision is **clear in its prohibitive content**: it is not guidance, but a direct urging for non-use.

### C. Reaffirmation of Rights on the Internet

It is reaffirmed that **"the rights of persons must also be protected on the Internet"**, including during the AI system lifecycle. This protection includes:

- Freedom of expression
- Privacy
- Dignity
- Non-discrimination
- Due process

## VI. DATA GOVERNANCE AND PRIVACY

### A. Data as a Critical Factor

Operational paragraph 7 recognizes that **"data is fundamental for the development and functioning of artificial intelligence systems"** and that it is essential:

- **Fair, inclusive, responsible, and effective** data governance
- Improvement of data generation, accessibility, and infrastructure
- Use of digital public goods

### B. Cross-Border Data Flows

States are **urged to**:

- Share best practices in data governance
- Promote international cooperation and assistance
- Achieve greater coherence and interoperability
- Promote reliable cross-border flows (where feasible)
- Make development more inclusive, equitable, and beneficial

### C. Privacy as a Safeguard

The Resolution expressly links personal data protection with:
- Legal framework compliance
- Transparency and reporting
- Impact assessments
- User control mechanisms

## VII. IDENTIFIED RISKS AND MITIGATION MEASURES

### A. Technological and Security Risks

The Resolution explicitly identifies that **incorrect or malicious** design, development, or use of AI systems can cause:

#### Structural Risks
- Deepening of digital gaps
- Reinforcement of existing inequalities
- Amplification of structural biases

#### Discrimination Risks
- Leading to discrimination
- Violation of equal treatment
- Disproportionate impact on vulnerable groups

#### Information Integrity Risks
- Weakening of information integrity
- Reduced access to reliable information
- Manipulation of public narratives

#### Human Rights Risks
- Weakening of rights protection
- Unlawful or arbitrary interference in private life
- Limitation of fundamental freedoms

#### Security Risks
- Increased risk of accidents
- Complex threats from malicious actors
- Cybersecurity vulnerabilities

### B. Context of Rapid Technological Acceleration

The Resolution recognizes that the **"rapid acceleration of the design, development, and use of AI systems"** creates urgency for:

- Achieving **global consensus** on safe systems
- Establishing **effective and interoperable safeguards, practices, and norms**
- Avoiding **governance fragmentation**
- Reducing **disparities between developed and developing countries**

---

## VIII. SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS FOR DEVELOPING COUNTRIES

### A. Recognition of Asymmetric Gaps

The Resolution expressly recognizes that:

- There exists **"AI and other digital gaps"**
- **"Various levels of technological development"** among and within countries
- Developing countries **"face unique challenges"** in keeping pace with acceleration
- There are **"obstacles to sustainable development"**
- There are **"existing disparities between developed and developing countries"** in conditions, opportunities, and capabilities

### B. Special Assistance Obligations

To close gaps, States must:

#### Capacity Building
- Strengthening national capacity building
- Qualified technical assistance
- Technology transfer on mutually agreed terms
- Effective, equitable, and meaningful participation of developing countries

#### Financial Assistance
- Contribution of funds related to AI systems
- Adjusted to national needs, policies, and priorities
- Urgent mobilization of implementation means

#### Removing Obstacles
- Reducing disparities in conditions and capabilities
- Access to technological innovations
- Access to funds for research and innovation
- Participation in international processes and forums

### C. Emphasis on Leaving No One Behind

Operational paragraph 6 specifically emphasizes that promoting AI systems must be done **"taking especially into account developing countries and leaving no one behind."**

This formulation suggests an obligation of **differentiated equity** in benefits and regulatory compliance burden.

---

## IX. PRIVATE SECTOR REGULATION

### A. Corporate Responsibility

Operational paragraph 9 **Encourages** the private sector to:

- **Adhere to** international and national laws
- **Act in accordance with** United Nations Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights
- **Promote inclusive and equitable access** to benefits of safe AI systems

### B. Competition and Open Markets

States are **encouraged** to develop policies that:

- Promote **competition** in AI systems
- Support **small businesses and entrepreneurs**
- Facilitate **free competition** in AI markets
- Make **critical investments** especially in developing countries
- Create **fair, open, inclusive, and non-discriminatory markets**

---

## X. CONNECTION WITH ONGOING UN PROCESSES

### A. Summit of the Future

The Resolution anticipates that the upcoming **Summit of the Future** will formulate a **"Global Digital Pact"** that will serve as a complementary framework for AI governance.

This connection suggests that **78/265 is a transitional document** toward a more comprehensive framework.

### B. Information Society Review

A **general review in 2025** of progress since the World Summit on the Information Society is anticipated, which will allow assessment of AI implementation obligations.

## XI. LEGAL CONCLUSIONS

### A. Normative Character of the Resolution

Although General Assembly resolutions are not formally binding in the sense of Conventions or Treaties, Resolution 78/265 presents characteristics of international soft law with **high normative value**:

1. **Explicit multilateral consensus** - Adoption on March 21, 2024
2. **Basis in existing international law** - UN Charter, UDHR, treaties
3. **Operational specificity** - Detailed obligations through operational paragraphs
4. **Universal scope** - Applies to all Member States
5. **Connection with global governance** - Integrated with UN system

### B. Derived Legal Obligations

For Member States, the following obligations emerge:

**Mandatory Obligations (Mandate):**
- Bridge AI digital gaps between countries
- Promote safe and reliable AI systems
- Respect and protect human rights throughout lifecycle

**Cooperation Obligations (Urging):**
- Technical and financial assistance to developing countries
- Inclusive participation in international governance
- Technology transfer on mutually agreed terms
- Capacity building

**National Implementation Obligations (Incentive):**
- Development of national regulatory frameworks
- Adoption of technical and organizational safeguards
- Promotion of transparency and human oversight
- Protection of vulnerable populations

### C. Enforceability Mechanisms

Although the Resolution does not create specific international courts, it establishes enforceability mechanisms:

1. **Political accountability** before the General Assembly
2. **UN agency reports** on progress
3. **Periodic review in 2025** of implementation
4. **Peer pressure** through multilateral scrutiny
5. **Integration with binding treaties** (GDPR, EU AI Act where applicable)

### D. Relationship with National and Regional Regulatory Frameworks

The Resolution **does not replace** or **invalidate**:
- National AI regulations
- Data protection frameworks (GDPR)
- National security laws
- Sectoral regulations (financial, health, etc.)

Rather, it establishes **minimum principles** that such frameworks must respect.

### E. Prospects for Normative Consolidation

The Resolution anticipates that:

1. A **Global Digital Pact** at the Summit of the Future will formalize commitments
2. **Interoperable technical standards** will continue to develop
3. **Cross-border data governance** will deepen
4. **Supervision rigor** on compliance will increase

## XII. PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS

### A. For AI System Developers

AI system providers must:

- Incorporate security principles from initial design
- Document complete lifecycle
- Implement human rights impact assessments
- Establish user feedback mechanisms
- Ensure explainability and human oversight
- Protect personal data in accordance with international frameworks
- Mitigate biases through data and algorithm analysis
- Provide transparency on capabilities and limitations

### B. For Regulatory Authorities

Regulators must:

- Develop national frameworks consistent with Resolution
- Create spaces for responsible innovation
- Establish minimum security standards
- Monitor post-deployment risks
- Participate in international governance forums
- Facilitate technology transfer to developing countries
- Ensure equitable access to AI benefits

### C. For Human Rights Organizations

Rights defenders must:

- Monitor compliance with human rights protections
- Document discriminatory impacts of AI systems
- Participate in impact assessments
- Push for effective remedy mechanisms
- Encourage affected community participation
