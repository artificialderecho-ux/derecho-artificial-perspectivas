# Legal Analysis: The EU's €120 Million Penalty to X for Non-Compliance with the DSA

## Chapter 1: Regulatory Evolution and Technological Sovereignty in the European Union

### 1.1. From Limited Liability Regime to Due Diligence

The ecosystem of information society services has undergone a structural transformation since the enactment of Directive 2000/31/EC, transitioning from a model centered on promoting e-commerce to one that recognizes the centrality of intermediary services in civic life and the economy. The current legal framework, crystallized in Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 (hereinafter, DSA), imposes a paradigm shift: the conditional liability exemption—historically static under the safe harbour doctrine—is displaced toward a proactive due diligence obligation. This new regime requires providers to behave "responsably and diligently" to ensure a predictable and safe online environment. The transition is not merely procedural; it represents a shift "from liability to duty," where non-compliance with moderation and risk management standards carries substantive sanctions, regardless of the inherent illegality of hosted content.

### 1.2. The National Adaptation Framework: Democratic Governance in Spain

In the Spanish context, regulatory adaptation is articulated through the "Draft Law for the Improvement of Democratic Governance in Digital Services and Media." This legislative provision is imperative to ensure the effective implementation of Union Law, purging national law of antinomies derived from Law 34/2002 (LSSI). The national execution architecture rests on the designation of the National Commission on Markets and Competition (CNMC) as the Digital Services Coordinator in Spain. Under this new structure, the national authority assumes investigative and enforcement powers, including sanctioning authority for violations of transparency and due diligence duties, thus ensuring supervision that prior regulation did not allow in a harmonized manner.

### 1.3. Technological Sovereignty Objectives and Risk Level Mitigation

The DSA constitutes a cornerstone in the European Union's technological sovereignty strategy, designed to counteract the "monopolistic control" exercised by non-EU corporations. The European legislator identifies very large online platforms (VLOPs) not only as economic actors, but as sources of systemic risks affecting public health, security, and electoral processes. The regulation establishes different risk levels, imposing asymmetrical obligations: the greater the platform's reach, the greater the burden of responsibility to mitigate negative effects on civic discourse. This approach seeks to prevent system design—often based on opaque algorithms—from degrading citizen autonomy or facilitating information manipulation.

### 1.4. Legal Certainty Principle and Fundamental Rights Safeguards

The integration of Union Law into the domestic legal order must be governed by the principle of legal certainty, eliminating uncertainties that may affect both operators and citizens. The DSA framework is founded on absolute respect for fundamental rights enshrined in the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights, with special emphasis on freedom of expression, non-discrimination, and consumer protection. However, the interrelation between AI and law poses critical tensions. The use of automated systems for content moderation, if not subject to full transparency of source code, risks incurring "collateral censorship" or erosion of civil liberties. Therefore, the regulation prescribes that all limitations of rights must pass the "Strasbourg Test": must be provided by law, pursue a legitimate aim, and be necessary and proportionate in a democratic society.

## Chapter 2: Interface Architecture and Deceptive Patterns (Dark Patterns)

### 2.1. Technical Analysis of Article 25 DSA: Prohibition of Deceptive Design

The governance regime for digital interfaces under Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 establishes an imperative prohibition: online platform providers are prohibited from designing, organizing, or operating their interfaces in a way that deceives or manipulates service recipients. This technical provision pursues the neutralization of so-called dark patterns—practices that distort or undermine, whether intentionally or effectively, users' ability to make autonomous and informed decisions. The norm prescribes that system architecture must not "nudge" users toward actions that benefit the provider's commercial interests at the expense of recipient autonomy, thus safeguarding the fundamental rights to free choice and transparency.

### 2.2. The "Blue Verification Check" Case: Dismantling Systemic Trust

The European Commission has identified a substantive violation of Article 25(1) in the reconfiguration of platform X's "blue verification check." Historically, this distinctive served as an indicator of authenticity and notability, based on a proactive identity verification process against impersonation risk. The current model, implemented following the 2022 ownership change, replaced this standard with a paid subscription system (X Premium) that grants the same iconographic sign without meaningful identity verification. This interface architecture incurs inappropriate appropriation of visual standards, maintaining a distinctive that average users associate with historical veracity for accounts that have only satisfied a financial requirement. The Commission concludes this design is inherently deceptive, as it prevents consumers from making informed judgments about the authenticity of content they interact with, distorting reliability perception in the digital ecosystem.

### 2.3. Artificial Algorithmic Amplification and Civic Discourse Distortion

X's technical architecture integrates interface design with algorithmic amplification of paid subscriber responses. Under the "response prioritization" system, X Premium subscribers receive superior visibility in comment threads without the system transparently informing that this relevance is the product of a commercial agreement rather than organic metrics or genuine engagement. This mechanism uses opaque algorithms to artificially simulate the notability of certain actors, which distorts attention flow in public discourse. By treating content differently in algorithmic ranking without clear warning of its advertising or paid nature, the provider manipulates users' ability to discern real information relevance, increasing risk levels for the integrity of democratic communication processes.

### 2.4. Adversarial Abuse Risk and Impersonation Fraud Facilitation

The weakening of verification standards has catalyzed critical adversarial abuse risks by malicious actors. The lack of rigorous ex-ante filtering allows fraudsters to acquire the "authenticity veneer" of the blue check to execute financial scams, including cryptocurrency frauds and institutional identity impersonation. Technical evidence demonstrates that strategic disinformation networks exploit user confusion about the current meaning of verification to disseminate harmful content with an appearance of legitimacy. In this sense, the platform interface ceases to be a neutral space to become a technical facilitator of large-scale deception, failing in its duty to mitigate systemic risks for public security and consumer rights.

## Chapter 3: Advertising Transparency and Algorithmic Black Boxes

### 3.1. Ad Repository Obligations (Art. 39): Reliability Requirements

Article 39(1) of Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 imposes on very large online platform (VLOP) providers the positive obligation to compile and make public an advertising repository accessible through a searchable and reliable tool. The norm prescribes that such repository must allow multi-criteria queries and be accessible through application programming interfaces (API), guaranteeing that information is accurate and complete throughout the display period and up to one year after its last presentation. This mandate seeks to mitigate systemic risk levels associated with advertising systems, which possess intrinsic capacity to amplify disinformation or manipulative techniques with negative impacts on public health and electoral processes.

### 3.2. Substantive Deficiencies in X's Repository: Omission of Critical Data

Technical investigation has determined that platform X's advertising repository systematically violates transparency standards by omitting essential information for public scrutiny. Specifically, generated reports lack data on the legal entity funding the advertisement and its actual content, limiting the latter case to providing a uniform resource locator (URL) that can be altered or deleted by the advertiser, nullifying the evidence permanence required by law. Additionally, the system has been detected to not allow reliable identification of whether the advertisement was paid by a natural or legal person other than the advertiser, constituting operational opacity that prevents detecting hybrid threat campaigns or coordinated information operations.

### 3.3. Technical Barriers and Operational Opacity: Use of Delay Algorithms

X's repository architecture incorporates access barriers that the European Commission qualifies as deliberate design decisions to frustrate independent research. Expert testing has demonstrated that the tool imposes an artificial delay of three minutes and twenty seconds to process each report—a latency that does not respond to infrastructure technical limitations, but to a configuration that forces the browser to perform repeated status queries before releasing the file. Additionally, API access proves dysfunctional in practice; researchers encounter systematic errors (Code 453) even after satisfying costly commercial subscriptions, which de facto nullifies the institutional algorithmic transparency channel prescribed by the DSA.

### 3.4. Generative AI and Grok: Disinformation Risks and Synthetic Content

Within systemic risk supervision, the Commission has initiated additional proceedings to assess the impact of generative AI Grok and its recommendation systems. Regulatory concern centers on the dissemination of AI-manipulated explicit sexual content (deepfakes) and the potential of these opaque algorithms to degrade the fundamental rights of women and minors. Lack of robust controls on synthetic content and insufficient human supervision over Grok-generated response moderation elevate the platform's risk profile, especially regarding protection of mental well-being and civic discourse integrity against large-scale algorithmic manipulation.

## Chapter 4: Data Access and the Social Function of Research

### 4.1. Article 40 DSA as a Subjective Right: Real-Time Public Data Access

The transparency architecture of Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 transcends mere passive accountability to institute a subjective right to data access in favor of the research community. Article 40(12) imposes on very large online platform (VLOP) providers the imperative obligation to facilitate access, without undue delay and free of charge, to data that is publicly accessible on their interface, including real-time data where technically feasible. This provision is autonomous and directly applicable, not subject to the adoption of delegated acts or Digital Services Coordinators (DSC) mediation. The ultimate purpose of this regime is to correct information asymmetries and enable independent scrutiny regarding the evolution and severity of systemic risks in the Union.

### 4.2. Contractual Obstacles and the Scraping Conflict: Analysis of X's Prohibition

Compliance with Article 40(12) requires providers not only to provide technical tools like APIs, but also to refrain from imposing contractual barriers that prohibit independent access. Technical investigation of platform X reveals that its terms of service generally prohibit the use of automated techniques like data scraping or crawling. This contractual restriction constitutes a violation of the "do not impede" mandate for using public data for risk research. Independent access techniques are qualified by the regulator as indispensable instruments for auditing opaque recommendation algorithms and verifying the quality of information supplied through the provider's official channels.

### 4.3. Infrastructure and the Role of Coordinators (DSC): Coimisiún na Meán's Position

The management of requests for access to non-public data under Article 40(4) falls on a decentralized structure where DSCs act as necessary intermediaries between researchers and platforms. Since most VLOPs have their primary establishment in Ireland, Coimisiún na Meán assumes a strategic and disproportionate responsibility in processing and validating credentials of qualified researchers. This institutional infrastructure seeks to harmonize security and confidentiality standards, ensuring requests are proportionate and duly protect the provider's trade secrets. However, the lack of legal deadlines for concluding infringement procedures and dependence on platform cooperation creates an uncertainty environment that may compromise system effectiveness.

### 4.4. Impact on Systemic Risk Detection: The Deterrent Effect of Data Blocking

The imposition of financial and procedural barriers by X has generated a chilling effect that has forced the cancellation or suspension of hundreds of research projects on disinformation and hate speech. Specifically, the displacement of researchers toward prohibitive payment levels (e.g., USD 5,000 monthly) and the reduction of data quotas by 1000%—compared to the prior academic program—suffocates the capacity to detect coordinated manipulation campaigns. By rejecting requests based on restrictive interpretations of geographic location or institutional affiliation, the provider degrades the social surveillance necessary to mitigate risk levels affecting electoral process integrity and public security in the Union.

## Chapter 5: Supervision, Enforcement, and Sanctioning Regime

### 5.1. Competent Authorities: The Supervision Architecture in Spain

The execution of Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 (DSA) is articulated through a decentralized governance structure that imposes on Member States the obligation to designate independent supervisory authorities. In the Spanish legal order, the Digital Services Coordinator (DSC) is the National Commission on Markets and Competition (CNMC), to which responsibility for controlling due diligence compliance is attributed. This competence is exercised concurrently with the Spanish Data Protection Agency (AEPD), which specifically assumes control of prohibitions related to profiling-based advertising for minors and processing special categories of personal data under Articles 26.3 and 28.2 of the Regulation. The CNMC, through its Digital Services Directorate, acts as the central node in the European supervision network, collaborating closely with the European Commission and other DSCs, particularly the Irish Coimisiún na Meán, given that most VLOPs have their establishment in that jurisdiction.

### 5.2. The Enforcement Procedure: The X Case and the Single Economic Unit

The infringement proceeding against platform X constitutes the first jurisprudential milestone in DSA coercive enforcement. On December 5, 2025, the European Commission imposed a monetary sanction of €120 million following the finding of serious violations in transparency and interface design. The decision is doctrinally significant for the application of the Single Economic Unit principle, extending joint and several liability not only to X Corp (formerly Twitter), but also to X Holdings Corp., X.AI Holdings Corp., and personally to Elon Musk. The gravity of the infringement is based on the impact on more than 109 million monthly users in the Union, which elevates the systemic risk level for civic discourse and democratic process integrity. The breakdown of the penalty—€45 million for deceptive blue check design, €35 million for the advertising repository, and €40 million for data blocking for researchers—reflects a deterrent sanctioning will that can reach up to 6% of annual global turnover.

### 5.3. Inspection Powers and Algorithm Access

To guarantee supervision effectiveness, the DSC and the Commission possess invasive inspection powers that allow penetration of technical systems opacity. Authorized officials have the legal mandate to enter provider premises and demand full access to their organization, computer systems, and algorithms. These faculties include sealing commercial premises and the right to request detailed technical explanations on the functioning of content recommendation and moderation systems. In cases where inspection implies restriction of the fundamental right to inviolability of the home, Spanish regulation prescribes the need for prior judicial authorization, which must be resolved within a maximum of 48 hours to not compromise investigative speed.

### 5.4. Procedural Guarantees: Defense Rights and the "Data Rooms" Regime

The imposition of massive sanctions requires scrupulous respect for fundamental defense rights and effective judicial protection. During the proceeding against X, a relevant procedural conflict arose regarding access to the file. The Commission implemented a "Data Room" system, allowing only qualified external advisors to review confidential documents to protect trade secrets, while the company's direct access was limited to documents cited in preliminary conclusions. Although X alleged violation of legal privilege and undue restriction of its defense, the Commission defended that this mechanism balances the need for swift law enforcement with protection of third-party confidentiality. Any final decision of non-compliance is appealable to the General Court of the European Union, guaranteeing that Commission discretion is subject to full jurisdictional control.

## Chapter 6: Fundamental Rights Conflicts and Future Perspective

### 6.1. Freedom of Expression vs. Content Moderation: The Risk of Collateral Censorship

The implementation of Regulation (EU) 2022/2065 (DSA) institutes a dialectical tension between digital environment security and freedom of expression preservation, a right enshrined in Article 11 of the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights. Doctrine warns that regulatory pressure for expedited information removal, under the shelter of "actual knowledge" clauses (Art. 16), may induce providers to over-block legitimately protected content. This phenomenon, characterized as collateral censorship, is aggravated by the use of opaque algorithms and "decision black boxes" lacking the contextual sensitivity necessary to distinguish between abject discourse and legitimate political criticism. In this sense, doctrinal critics have qualified these provisions as "extraterritorial speech codes" that empower the Commission to exercise global narrative control, eroding the pluralism inherent in a democratic society.

### 6.2. Independence and Media Pluralism: Convergence with Regulation (EU) 2024/1083

Platform governance must necessarily converge with the protection framework for independent media services, articulated in Regulation (EU) 2024/1083 (European Media Freedom Act). This normative corpus seeks to shield editorial independence against arbitrary moderation by very large online platforms (VLOPs), requiring specific safeguards when restricted content comes from media providers subject to ownership transparency standards and conflict of interest control. The regulation prescribes the creation of state media registries under CNMC supervision in Spain, ensuring that the fight against disinformation does not translate into undue restriction of media pluralism or manipulation of informational relevance by algorithmic systems with commercial interests.

### 6.3. Judicial Predictability in the AI Environment: Toward Evidence-Based Regulation

The transition toward effective digital governance requires shifting administrative discretion toward an evidence-based regulation model. The integration of data analysis tools to monitor systemic risk levels allows both Digital Services Coordinators and accredited researchers to identify non-compliance patterns with scientific rigor. This empowering approach seeks to correct the information asymmetry between technology corporations and public authorities, facilitating judicial predictability that protects user autonomy. However, the success of this model depends on complete algorithm transparency and the legal system's capacity to subject automated decisions to real—not merely formal—human control.

### 6.4. Conclusions and Legal Gaps: Procedural Indeterminacy and Conceptual Vagueness

Despite its ambition, the current regulatory framework presents critical uncertainties that compromise legal certainty. First, concerning procedural deadline indeterminacy, as the DSA does not prescribe a legal limit for concluding formal infringement proceedings, which may result in persistent administrative harassment situations without immediate judicial protection. Second, the concept of systemic risk suffers from conceptual vagueness that grants the European Commission excessive discretion to define what content affects public security or civic discourse. Finally, the coexistence of safe harbour doctrines and the "good samaritan clause" creates a legal gap where platforms can act as private censors to obtain sanctioning immunity, displacing the jurisdictional function of fundamental rights balancing toward private entities not subject to the same accountability standards as the State.
