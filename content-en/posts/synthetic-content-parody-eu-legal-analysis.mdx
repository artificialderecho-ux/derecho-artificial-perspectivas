# Legal Analysis of Synthetic Content Creation: Identity, Transparency, and Parody Exception in the European Union

©Ricardo Scarpa (derechoartificial.com)

## 1. The New Transparency Paradigm: The AI Act (EU 2024/1689)

The entry into force of the Artificial Intelligence Act (AIA) on August 1, 2024, marks a milestone in global algorithmic governance, establishing a due diligence framework that transcends mere technical adequacy. Under this regime, synthetic content occupies a critical surveillance zone where transparency is a legal imperative to preserve trust in the digital single market. It is essential to note that while most provisions will apply from August 2, 2026, the prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI practices have been mandatory since February 2, 2025, setting an immediate compliance scenario for operators and developers.

**Article 50** of the AIA imposes enhanced transparency obligations on providers of AI systems that generate deepfakes. Labeling is not merely a safeguard against misinformation but an accountability attribution mechanism that impacts user perception and content traceability. Providers must guarantee that the artificial nature of content is recognizable through robust and persistent technical solutions.

**Technical requirements for machine-readable watermarks:**

- **Origin identification**: The system must incorporate metadata or watermarks that unambiguously identify AI intervention.
- **Marking resilience**: Labels must be resistant to common modifications or deletions during file processing.
- **Traceability and detection**: The output must allow other systems to identify content as artificially generated or manipulated.
- **Direct citation**: *"Providers of AI systems that generate or manipulate image, audio or video content constituting a deepfake shall ensure that the output of the system is marked in a machine-readable format"* (Art. 50, Regulation (EU) 2024/1689).

This transparency regarding content origin is the first line of defense to protect the legal essence of the individual: their biometric identity.

## 2. The Identity Dimension: Biometric Protection under the GDPR (2016/679)

Within European digital law, voice and image have ceased to be mere aesthetic representations to become critical legal assets protected by the data protection regime. Voice cloning and physical feature recreation through generative AI imply processing special categories of data when they enable unique identification of the subject, triggering enhanced protection under **Article 9** of the GDPR.

It is essential to integrate the recent CJEU jurisprudence (Case C-203/22, judgment of February 27, 2025), which clarifies the scope of the right of access to "meaningful information about the logic applied" (Art. 15.1.h). In contexts of synthetic content creation based on profiles, the controller must provide an intelligible explanation about how data was processed to arrive at the visual or audio output, enabling the data subject to understand the influence of their data on the automated decision or generated replica.

## 3. Intellectual Property and the Escape Valve: Parody and Pastiche Exception

**Directive (EU) 2019/790** operates as the necessary counterweight between author exclusivity and freedom of expression in digital culture. In Spain, transposition through **Royal Decree-Law 24/2021** has been characterized by doctrine as "restrictive," for example, omitting the impossibility of excluding scientific research exceptions by contract, creating a legal uncertainty zone for AI model development.

However, **Article 17.7** of the Directive shields "quotation, criticism, review, caricature, parody, or pastiche" as mandatory safeguards. The concept of **"pastiche"** acquires superior strategic relevance: it is defined as *"transformation of a disclosed work consisting of taking certain characteristic elements of an artist's work and combining them so as to give the impression of being an independent creation."* Unlike parody, pastiche does not require a burlesque intent, enabling the transformative and combinatory use characteristic of contemporary synthetic content.

*"Member States shall ensure that users may rely on the following exceptions or limitations... for the purposes of caricature, parody or pastiche."* — Directive (EU) 2019/790 on copyright and related rights in the Digital Single Market.

This creative freedom must be harmonized with platform responsibility, which cannot totally delegate content legality to automated filtering systems without contextual sensitivity.

## 4. The Implementation Conflict: "Overblocking" vs. Freedom of Expression

The operational tension on digital platforms manifests in the deployment of detection algorithms (*flagging*). The risk of "overblocking" threatens freedom of expression when automated systems remove legitimate content protected by legal exceptions, simply due to technical coincidence with protected material.

The **CJEU (Case C-401/19)** has been categorical: protection measures cannot result in systematic blocking of lawful content. The court demands proportional balance where filtering technology must be capable of distinguishing between clear infringement and creative use.

## 5. Strategic Synthesis and Legal Conclusions

The convergence of the **AI Act**, the **GDPR**, and **Directive 2019/790** configures a high-complexity regulatory ecosystem. The interaction of these regulations dictates that any generative AI deployment must be analyzed under a threefold prism: technical transparency, training data legitimacy, and the transformative nature of the output.

To mitigate sanction risk that can reach **4% of the global annual turnover of the corporate group** (calculated on the entire economic unit, per **CJEU C-383/23**), the following imperatives are dictated:

1. **Proactive and Chronological Transparency**: Machine-readable labeling of synthetic content is mandatory. Strict adherence to the AIA application schedule must be observed: prohibitions in effect since February 2025 and general transparency since August 2026.

2. **Consent and Explainability Respect**: Biometric data processing requires explicit consent. Furthermore, per **CJEU C-203/22**, the user has the right to an understandable explanation about the profiling logic applied to generate their digital replica.

3. **Transformative Character Defense (Pastiche)**: Against platform filtering systems, the key to lawfulness lies in pastiche. The synthetic work must contribute sufficient originality to present it as an independent creation, distancing itself from mere servile copying.

For detailed consultation of the complete regulatory framework, access the EU AI Act (EU 2024/1689).
