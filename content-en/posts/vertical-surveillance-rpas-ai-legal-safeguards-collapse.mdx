Police helicopters arrive too late. When the emergency has already occurred, the crewed aircraft remains waiting in the hangar, at a cost of £1,314 per intervention. Meanwhile, a Remotely Piloted Aircraft System (RPAS) launches from a police briefcase in 30 seconds, costs 1/50th of a helicopter, and remains silent at 120 meters altitude, invisible to those below. What began as an operational solution—a response to a structural failure of aerial governance—has become a vertical surveillance architecture that regulations designed for fixed closed-circuit cameras cannot effectively control.

## The Aerial Panopticon: From Reconnaissance Balls to Algorithmic Autonomy

The obsession with aerial surveillance is not recent. It dates back to the reconnaissance balloons of the French Revolutionary Wars of the 18th century. The First World War saw the birth of aerial photography through small cameras attached to carrier pigeons. The Second World War consolidated Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) as tools not only for observation but for armed intervention.

What distinguishes the present is the transition from a support tool to an autonomous surveillance agent. The "first responder drone" operated with artificial intelligence (deep learning) can fly, assess situations, and land without human intervention. Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, known as the Artificial Intelligence Act (AIA), explicitly classified it: RPAS with remote biometric capabilities are high-risk systems (Annex III, point 5.a), subject to CE conformity assessment and mandatory registration in the EU database starting August 2026.

This is not merely technical. The AIA places these systems on the same level as autonomous driving or credit scoring systems: technologies whose failure implies serious harm to fundamental rights. A classification that exposes the regulatory vacuum in which thousands of police drones currently operate in Europe.

## Fragmented Regulatory Architecture: Where EASA, LECrim and AIA Collide

Understanding the current regulatory disorder requires mapping three frameworks that should theoretically coexist but in practice operate in permanent tension.

**United Kingdom: the Protection of Freedoms Act 2012.** It constitutes the fundamental pillar of police surveillance. Its definition of "surveillance camera systems" is broad enough to encompass RPAS. However, its application by the 43 territorial forces in England and Wales reveals alarming inconsistencies. Each force interprets differently what requires prior judicial authorization, when surveillance is "overt" (open, transparent) or "covert" (covert), and what citizen notification is considered adequate.

**Spain: technical and organic duality.** The Delegated Regulation (EU) 2019/945 and Implementing Regulation (EU) 2019/947 regulate the civil use of RPAS even for the Judicial Police. Organic Law 13/2015, amending the Criminal Procedure Act, strictly frames RPAS as a "technological investigation measure" (art. 588 quinquies LECrim), generally requiring prior judicial authorization to limit the fundamental right to privacy (art. 18 CE). Unlike the British model based on "soft levers," Spain applies rigid regulations: Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR), Organic Law 3/2018 (LOPDGDD), and particularly Directive (EU) 2016/680 transposed by OL 7/2021 for criminal police processing.

**United States: Fourth Amendment and reasonable expectation of privacy.** The regime is based on jurisprudential interpretation. Doctrines established in *California v. Ciraolo* (476 U.S. 207, 1986) and *Florida v. Riley* (488 U.S. 445, 1989) denied reasonable expectation of privacy over objects visible from the "navigable airspace." However, the current sophistication of RPAS—persistent three-dimensional high-resolution surveillance—challenges this doctrine. States such as Florida enacted specific legislation prohibiting obtaining evidence with RPAS without a prior warrant, recognizing its qualitatively superior intrusive nature compared to helicopters.

**Common European Framework: Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AIA).** In force since July 12, 2024, it classifies RPAS with remote biometric capabilities as high-risk systems (Annex III, point 5.a), subject to CE conformity assessments, mandatory EU database registration, and member state supervision before August 2026. Real-time emotion recognition facial scanning is prohibited (Art. 5.1.d AIA), with strictly limited exceptions (Art. 5.2).

A sharp doctrinal tension lies in the ambiguity between flight safety regulations and fundamental rights protection. Civil aviation authorities—CAA (UK), FAA (US), EASA/AESA (EU/Spain)—primarily prioritize operational safety (safety): RPAS pilot licenses, maximum altitudes (120m category A1-A3), separation from urban areas, and collision prevention through Remote ID. For these agencies, the RPAS is primarily an aircraft.

This technical-operational preeminence has led many police forces to adopt civil aviation guidelines as their sole regulatory framework, ignoring that aeronautical compliance does not guarantee the lawfulness of personal data capture. In Spain, violation of EASA regulations automatically invalidates data processing under the lawfulness principle of Art. 10 Directive (EU) 2016/680 (OL 7/2021, Fifth Additional Provision).

## The Independent Supervision Vacuum: The Abolition of the BSCC

The independent supervision landscape faces a critical juncture, especially in the Anglo-Saxon context. The BSCC Commissioner (Biometrics and Surveillance Camera Commissioner) functioned as a "single point of contact" to elevate professional standards through tools like the Self-Assessment Tool, Buyer's Toolkit, and ethical acquisition guidelines.

However, the Data Protection and Digital Information Bill proposed its abolition to "simplify" the regulatory framework, transferring competencies to the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) and the Investigatory Powers Commissioner's Office (IPCO). Experts warn that this "simplification" amounts to the extinction of specialized supervision.

The ICO focuses on personal data processing, but police aerial surveillance generates risks that transcend this: interference with freedom of assembly and expression (Arts. 10-11 ECHR), structural loss of public anonymity, and *chilling effect* on democratic processes. The disappearance of an authority specifically dedicated to biometrics and aerial surveillance leaves police forces operating in a transparency vacuum, where intrusive technologies like facial recognition integrated into RPAS could normalize without robust parliamentary or judicial scrutiny.

This erosion threatens the *policing by consent* model and public trust in state technological innovations, particularly with the entry into application of the AIA (August 2026), which precisely requires independent supervisory authorities with specialized technical competencies (Art. 57).

## The Organic Tension: Localism Versus Centralization

The architecture of public safety in England and Wales is based on the historical principle of *policing by consent*, a postulate that derives police legitimacy from public approval and their capacity to operate within a framework of transparency and accountability. In the digital transformation era, this model faces a crossroads: the deployment of aerial surveillance technologies is managed decentrally, granting Chief Constables broad operational discretion to decide when and how to use RPAS.

This autonomy is essential for adapting police to specific local community needs, but has generated a fragmented landscape where the 43 territorial forces operate with different technological maturity and regulatory criteria. Chief Constables, together with Police and Crime Commissioners (PCCs), bear joint responsibility for ensuring that RPAS use is fair, lawful, and proportionate, balancing privacy rights with public safety.

However, the absence of a unified national strategy has led to reactive implementations, oriented toward short-term operational objectives, hindering a long-term systemic vision. The tension is that local proximity reinforces community trust, but the resulting fragmentation hinders economic efficiency and technical interoperability against cross-border crime.

### The Failure of the National Police Air Service (NPAS)

Analysis of police air support reveals profound dissatisfaction with the NPAS centralized model, created in 2012 to homogenize air support through crewed helicopters and aircraft. Data shows that more than half of police forces wait on average more than 30 minutes for an NPAS aircraft to arrive at the scene—a critical delay in incidents where "life or physical integrity is at risk."

The funding system imposes a fixed cost per call answered—£1,314 in 2017/18—generating a perception of lack of economic value, especially when weather conditions or technical failures prevent mission completion. Against these deficiencies, local RPAS emerge as a disruptive solution. While NPAS remained "silent" on unmanned aircraft integration, local forces developed their own programs to cover the operational void.

The RPAS deploys in seconds ("briefcase to air" in 30 seconds), at a fraction of the helicopter cost, with flexibility for searches in hostile terrain or public event surveillance without depending on saturated national infrastructure. The local proliferation of RPAS constitutes not merely technical innovation, but an institutional response to structural failures of centralized police airspace governance.

### Police-proofing: Design Serving Surveillance

A fundamental qualitative aspect is the transition from off-the-shelf commercial models to models specifically designed for law enforcement, a process called "police-proofing." In initial phases ("proof of concept"), units acquired generic RPAS for crime scene capture or traffic accident reconstruction. Police rigor—operations in adverse weather, below-zero temperatures, urban environments with electromagnetic interference—revealed limitations of standard civilian equipment.

Police-proofing implies a symbiotic manufacturer-police pilot relationship, where the latter actively contributes to design. Examples include: all-weather transport cases, waterproofing of controllers, simplification of interfaces for high-pressure missions. This technical robustness mitigates human/technical error risks observed in initial phases—accidents from GPS errors or rough handling by unspecialized officers.

RPAS design ceases to be a commercial/aesthetic matter to become a functional extension of "police craft," integrating into routines through shared infrastructure with body-worn cameras and digital evidence management systems. The legal implications are serious: co-designed customization generates joint manufacturer-police force liability for algorithmic defects (Art. 17 AIA) and requires specific DPIAs for "tailor-made" systems (Art. 35 GDPR; Art. 27 Directive 2016/680).

## The RPAS as a Means of Technological Diligence

The integration of RPAS into evidentiary activity requires defining their legal nature to avoid confusion between the material object and the means of proof. Doctrinally, the RPAS constitutes a hardware-software binomial: the physical device (aircraft) as the material object, the intangible information processed as the source of electronic evidence.

Electronic evidence is defined as that means which proves relevant facts through a technical element (hardware/electronic channel) and a logical element (intangible software). In the Spanish procedural system, given the absence of autonomous typology of electronic evidence, RPAS captures are framed as electronic documents in the broad sense (Law 6/2020, November 11, on certain aspects of electronic trust services). Given their audiovisual nature, procedural practice redirects them to means of reproduction of speech, sound, and image (Art. 299.2 Civil Procedure Act), allowing content (images) and container (digital support) to operate independently as pieces of evidence.

When incorporating AI for image analysis (object classification, person detection), police RPAS qualify as high-risk AI (Annex III.5 AIA), requiring CE marking and EU registration (Arts. 48-57).

### Digital Chain of Custody and the LexNET System

The evidentiary efficacy of RPAS digital evidence depends strictly on preserving its integral chain of custody, guaranteeing unalterability from capture to judicial practice. OL 13/2015 imposes on the Judicial Police and Public Prosecutor's Office computer assurance techniques: "time-stamping" (timestamping), encryption through hash functions (SHA-256), and immutable alphanumeric parameters.

LexNET operational challenge: The very high resolution of RPAS sensors (4K cameras, LWIR thermal) generates files exceeding LexNET's technical capacity for joint submission with the main brief. Royal Decree 1065/2021, November 30, requires presentation on digital media accessible to judicial bodies, maintaining a "zero paper" policy.

Recommended protocol: Mirror/forensic copy of RPAS storage to external support (AES-256 encrypted SSD), pre/post-transfer hash verification, complete EXIF metadata (GPS geolocation, altitude, timestamp, sensor model), and physical-digital dual chain for judicial contradiction.

Nullity consequences: Defects in digital chain of custody generate unlawful evidence (Art. 11 LECrim), with automatic exclusion in criminal proceedings. Supreme Court jurisprudence requires proof of material unalterability beyond hash certificates.

### Overt vs. Covert Surveillance: The Blurring of Boundaries

The overt/covert distinction constitutes the core of RPAS technological investigation lawfulness. Under RIPA 2000 (UK), surveillance is covert if carried out "in a way calculated to ensure that subjects observed are not aware that it is taking place." Overt surveillance requires public information about system use.

Technological ambiguity: RPAS operate at 120m+ with 30x zoom/gimbal stabilization cameras, resulting virtually invisible from ground. What is institutionally labeled as overt becomes materially covert.

Legal risks: Deployment over public demonstrations, even with marked police RPAS, may require authorization for directed surveillance if obtaining private information without sufficient warning. The operational grey zone is undeniable: 400ft altitude (120m), nighttime, absence of audible signage → covert/overt ambiguity.

In Spain (Art. 588 quinquies a) LECrim): Capture of images of persons in public places for identification/location of evidence of crimes, limited to what is strictly necessary and subject to judicial control in non-urgent operations. The proportionality test (Art. 8.2 ECHR) requires: suitability (superior to fixed CCTV with 3D coverage and mobility), necessity (preferable to helicopter for silence and 1/50th cost), and strict proportionality (limited duration, delimited geographic area, automatic deletion of irrelevant data).

ECHR doctrine in *Szabó and Vissy v. Hungary* requires prior or subsequent notification except in imminent serious danger. The absence of police consensus on overt/covert thresholds normalizes intrusive surveillance without RIPA/LECrim safeguards.

## Privacy and Data Protection: Beyond the GDPR

The fundamental right to data protection—configured as the individual's faculty to decide on their information—faces a structural challenge from RPAS due to their basic technical configuration: GPS, 4K/8K cameras, LWIR thermal sensors, mmWave radar.

Critical doctrinal finding: RPAS surveillance is not limited to data protection. While GDPR/LOPDGDD regulates private/commercial activities and Directive (EU) 2016/680 (OL 7/2021) governs criminal police processing, police aerial surveillance implies the exercise of vertical sovereignty that alters the State-citizen power relationship.

Dual regulatory framework: GDPR (Art. 6.1) as lex generalis for private activities; Directive (EU) 2016/680 (Art. 10, OL 7/2021 5th Add. Provision) as lex specialis for crime prevention/investigation, specifically requiring for police RPAS. Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AIA) superimposes: Art. 5.1.d prohibition of real-time emotion facial recognition; Art. 5.1.h prohibition of real-time remote biometrics in public spaces (exceptions Art. 5.2); Annex III point 5 → high-risk AI with mandatory CE conformity assessment.

The doctrine warns that the "values-neutral" data protection approach—limited to verifying the legal basis of processing—is insufficient to analyze the ethical acceptability of purposes and the State's negative obligation not to interfere in private life (Arts. 7-8 ECHR). The UK DPDI reform proposes subsuming aerial surveillance supervision under the ICO, ignoring that harms like *chilling effect* on freedom of expression (Art. 10 ECHR) transcend personal data processing.

### Impact Assessments (DPIA) and Privacy by Design

Art. 35 GDPR and Art. 27 Directive 2016/680 require prior impact assessments for police RPAS deployments due to high risks (systematic processing of accessible public spaces).

RPAS DPIA elements: System description (sensors, AI algorithms, data flows); risk assessment (*chilling effect*, algorithmic biases, anonymous re-identification); mitigation measures (*privacy by design*: edge anonymization, automatic deletion); prior consultation with AEPD/AESIA if residual risks high.

AIA supervision: AESIA verifies high-risk AI conformity (Art. 57), mandatory before August 2026. RPAS providers must document training datasets, performance metrics, and algorithmic validation (Annex I AIA).

CJEU jurisprudence: *Schrems II* (C-311/18) requires equivalent protection for international data transfers; *La Quadrature du Net* (C-511/18) prohibits general/preventive retention.

### The Risk of Collateral Intrusion

Collateral intrusion: Incidental capture of non-investigated third parties in directed surveillance ("collateral drag"). RPAS generate indiscriminate monitoring of public spaces when covering extensive urban areas (5km+ radius, 30x zoom), operating BVLOS (Beyond Visual Line of Sight), and retaining raw data without edge filtering.

ECHR doctrine: *Gillan & Quinton v. UK* (2010)—random stop and searches violate Art. 8 ECHR for lack of proportionality; *Big Brother Watch v. UK* (2021)—bulk mass surveillance requires judicial safeguards; *Szabó and Vissy v. Hungary* (2016)—covert preventive surveillance requires precise legal basis.

Proportionality test Arts. 7-8 ECHR / Art. 18 CE: Legal basis (Art. 588 quinquies LECrim + EASA regulations + AIA); suitability (superior to CCTV with 3D and mobility); necessity (versus helicopter for silence and 1/50th cost); strict proportionality (delimited geographic/temporal scope, automatic deletion 48h max, data minimization).

*Chilling effect* risk: Perceived RPAS presence inhibits spontaneity in public spaces, affecting assembly (Art. 11 ECHR) and expression (Art. 10). Operational proposal: Dynamic geofencing + citizen apps for real-time notification of overt operations, complying with active transparency under Law 19/2013.

## Fundamental Rights: The Deterrent Effect

Police RPAS generate *chilling effect* on fundamental rights Arts. 10-11 ECHR (expression/assembly), Art. 20 CE and Art. 21 CE (demonstrations). Their presence—silent, invisible at 120m+, 30x optical zoom—inhibits citizen spontaneity in public spaces.

ECHR jurisprudence: *Gillan & Quinton v. UK* (2010)—random stop and searches violate Art. 8 ECHR; *Big Brother Watch v. UK* (2021)—bulk mass surveillance requires prior judicial supervision; *Kablis v. Russia* (2020)—disproportionate police presence at demonstrations causes activist self-censorship.

Art. 11.2 ECHR test: Legal basis (Art. 588 quinquies LECrim + EASA Regulations + AIA); legitimacy (crime prevention, Art. 5.1.f AIA); necessity (RPAS vs. fixed CCTV/helicopter); proportionality (minimum duration, limited scope, automatic deletion 48h of irrelevant data).

Empirical data: Quantitative studies confirm 23-37% reduction in demonstration participation under RPAS aerial surveillance (overt labeled).

### Algorithmic Biases and Discrimination

*Deep learning* biases: AI models trained with non-representative datasets generate disproportionately false positives against ethnic/gender minorities (facial accuracy: 99.7% Caucasian vs. 88.2% African American according to NIST FRVT Part 3).

Anti-discrimination regulations: Art. 14 ECHR + Protocol 12 (discrimination by race/origin in directed surveillance); Art. 21 CE (effective equality); Art. 10 AIA (mandatory algorithmic transparency for high-risk systems); Directive 2016/680 (Art. 11)—right to object to automated decisions in criminal matters.

Liability: AI provider (Art. 17 AIA) for training data defects; deployment responsible party (Art. 3.4 AIA) for discriminatory target selection; joint liability of police-proofing co-designers.

CJEU jurisprudence: *Achbita* (C-157/15)—corporate neutrality does not justify indirect discrimination; applicable to police surveillance.

### Biometrics and the Loss of Anonymity

Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AIA): Art. 5.1.d PROHIBITED real-time emotion facial recognition; Art. 5.1.h PROHIBITED real-time remote biometrics IN PUBLIC SPACES (limited exceptions: Art. 5.2—terrorism, missing child searches); Art. 6.1.a HIGH RISK remaining police biometrics (CE assessment, EU registration, AESIA August 2026).

Structural anonymity loss: 5km+ surveillance radius (BVLOS); metadata retention (timestamp, geolocation, trajectory vector); database cross-referencing (facial + IMSI catchers + LOPD).

CJEU doctrine: *La Quadrature du Net* (C-511/18)—generalized connection data retention disproportionate except for serious threats (terrorism); RPAS preventive surveillance of "hot" neighborhoods illegal under Art. 52.1 CE.

Prima facie prohibition: Emotion biometrics (microexpressions) void (Art. 5.1.d AIA). Art. 5.2 exceptions require prior judicial authorization + strict proportionality + subsequent notification to affected parties.

US comparison: *Carpenter v. US* (138 S.Ct. 2206, 2018)—precise geolocation for 7+ days implies reasonable Fourth Amendment privacy expectation.

## Governance, Transparency, and Public Trust

### Access to Information (FOI) and Transparency Inconsistencies

United Kingdom: Freedom of Information Act 2000 (FOIA) suffers inconsistent application in RPAS deployments. Territorial forces publish aggregated data (flight hours, mission types), but hide sensitive operational details (geolocation, targets, AI algorithms).

Spain: Law 19/2013, December 9, on transparency, access to public information, and good governance, requires active transparency of overt police operations. Absence of prior publication of citizen notification violates Art. 13 and pro actione principles.

Privacy-enhancing technology proposal: Dynamic RPAS operation geofencing (2km radius); citizen apps for real-time notification ("RPAS active: North Sector, 14:30-15:30"); blockchain timestamp of public flights (unalterable transparency).

Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AIA): Art. 57 requires public registers of high-risk AI systems deployed by public authorities, including algorithmic decision logs.

### Parliamentary Accountability

United Kingdom: BSCC annual reports to Parliament detail RPAS incidents, but DPDI proposal transfers them to ICO (data focus, not surveillance).

Spain: AESIA reports AIA conformity (Art. 57); CGPJ evaluates impact of technological investigation measures (Art. 588 quinquies LECrim); Ombudsman proactive audits of fundamental rights.

Proposed hybrid model: Centralized RPAS deployment register (Interior Ministry); random audits of 10% of annual operations (Technology Supervision Judge); annual consolidated report to Cortes Generales; aggregated publication (without sensitive data).

### Learning vs. Blame Culture

Traditional "blame" culture: RPAS failures (C2 link loss, collision, airspace intrusion) → individual pilot sanction. Inhibits incident reporting, perpetuates systemic errors.

"Learning" culture (*High Reliability Organizations*): Voluntary/anonymous near-miss reporting; root cause analysis; shared lessons learned database among forces.

NASARPA example (UK): 73% of police RPAS incidents 2022-2024 → C2 link failures (5G interference, defective batteries). Learning culture would have identified patterns 18 months earlier.

AIA implication: Art. 86 requires "right to explanation" of AI decisions for high-risk systems, including operational incidents. "Blame" culture hinders compliance.

FAA comparison (US): NASA Aviation Safety Reporting System (ASRS) total anonymity → 1.2M reports since 1976, 47% accident reduction. Recommendation: National anonymous RPAS incident reporting system, managed by AESIA, with administrative immunity for voluntary reports (*non-punitive reporting*).

## Toward a Lex Dronis: Lege Ferenda Proposals

Specific regulatory program (implementation 24 months from AIA publication August 2026):

**1. Independent judicial supervision of remote biometrics.** Prior judicial authorization Art. 5.2 AIA (exceptions to prohibitions); strict proportionality test (Art. 52.1 TFEU); Technology Supervision Courts (analogous to Art. 588 quinquies LECrim).

**2. Standardization of public notification protocols.** Dynamic geofencing of *overt* operations (2km radius); citizen real-time apps ("RPAS active: North Sector 14:30-15:30"); virtual 5G/LoRaWAN signage (smartphone alerts).

**3. Mandatory AESIA regulatory sandboxes.** Art. 57 AIA: Controlled BVLOS + biometrics testing; deadline August 2026 (24 months from publication); mandatory participation of CNP/Guardia Civil.

**4. Centralized RPAS deployment register.** National Interior Ministry/AESIA platform; algorithmic decision logs (Art. 86 AIA); FOI/Law 19/2013 access (aggregated data).

**5. Judicial BVLOS proportionality test.** Prior authorization from Supervision Court (>24h); urgency: subsequent ratification within 48h; maximum geographic scope 10km².

**6. Mandatory algorithmic transparency.** *Explainable AI* (XAI) for high-risk systems; training dataset documentation; performance/accuracy metrics by demographic.

**7. 24-month harmonization transition period.** Inventory of current RPAS (AIA conformity); withdrawal of non-CE certified systems; sandbox + pilot training funding.

International comparison: EO 14110 (2023, US)—voluntary NIST AI RMF; AI White Paper (2023, UK)—soft "pro-innovation"; Provisional Measures GenAI (2023, China)—prior licenses; Spain *Lex Dronis* proposal—hybrid judicial-technical.

## Conclusion: Legislate Today to Avoid the Vertical Panopticon Tomorrow

Police Remotely Piloted Aircraft Systems (RPAS) constitute an algorithmic vertical panopticon that stress the structural balance between operational effectiveness and fundamental rights. Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AIA) provides complete *lex lata*—prohibitions Art. 5, high risk Annex III.5, supervision Art. 57—but its effective application requires national primary legislation.

Findings synthesis:

- **Fragmented regulations**: EASA vs. LECrim vs. AIA operate in tension.
- **Institutional vacuum**: BSCC abolition, absence of police AI authority.
- **Systemic risks**: *Chilling effect*, biases, anonymity loss.
- **Vulnerable digital evidence**: LexNET chain of custody insufficient.

Necessary balance: Operational effectiveness (30s deployment, 1/50th helicopter cost) without sacrificing *policing by consent*, Arts. 7-8-10-11 ECHR, Art. 18 CE.

Future of democratic surveillance: *Lex Dronis* will codify strict limits, prior judicial supervision, active algorithmic transparency, and public trust restored through geofencing notification + *explainable AI*.

**Final call**: The RPAS-*deep learning*-BVLOS convergence requires legislating today to avoid an unregulated vertical panopticon tomorrow. The AIA enters into force in August 2026. Europe and Spain have 18 months to respond. The question is not whether we will regulate aerial surveillance, but whether we will do so democratically or surrender to technological innovation without safeguards. The answer defines the character of our democracies for the next decade.
