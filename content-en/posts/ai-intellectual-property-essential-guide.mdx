# AI and Intellectual Property: Essential Guide to Understanding the Great Conflict

Imagine for a moment an infinite library where books write themselves, but the librarian does not know whom to pay copyright royalties to. That is the heart of the paradox we face today. As copyright law scholars, we are confronted with the most fascinating challenge of the 21st century: **how to apply laws created for humans to machines that "learn" from us to outperform us in speed, but not necessarily in originality?**

## 1. The Engine of the Conflict: How Does AI Learn?

Generative AI is not a mystical creator; it is a hungry **machine learning** pattern-recognition engine. The legal conflict is born in the technological "kitchen," where the system consumes human creations to distill its own response capability.

This technical process is articulated in a critical chain that every jurist must master:

**Extraction → Processing → Computation → Output**

1. **Extraction:** The system massively collects billions of data points from the internet, including copyrighted works.
2. **Processing:** Information is downloaded and processed for analysis by deep learning algorithms.
3. **Computation:** Here we enter the **"Black Box."** The machine performs billions of mathematical operations to identify patterns. What is crucial for the law is that this phase involves total **opacity**: it is impossible to predict the final output or know exactly how much a specific training datum influenced the result.
4. **Output:** AI generates new content (text, image, code) based on the statistical probabilities of its computations.

⚠️ Key legal point: Because the volume of data is so vast, the machine is incapable of autonomously discriminating the ethical or legal nature of the information it consumes. This algorithmic opacity presents unique challenges for traceability and legal liability.

## 2. The Fuel: Raw Data vs. Protected Works

For the algorithm, a historical fact and an award-winning novel are simply bit sequences. However, for the law, the distinction is radical. The problem arises because AI mixes these ingredients in a digital blender where traceability is lost.

## 3. Europe's Response: Text and Data Mining Exception

**Directive (EU) 2019/790** (DSM Directive) introduced in **Articles 3 and 4** a copyright exception for text and data mining (TDM), with crucial differences:

### Article 3: Non-Commercial Use (Scientific Research)

- Permits TDM for **scientific research**
- Research organizations and cultural heritage institutions
- Requirement: Legal access to works

### Article 4: Commercial Use

- Permits TDM for **any purpose**, including commercial
- **BUT** with right of *opt-out* by the rightholder
- Rightholders may **expressly reserve** this right in machine-readable form
- Example: Labels in `robots.txt` or specific metadata

📌 Practical implication: Commercial generative AI companies operating in the EU must respect express reservations by rightholders. Non-compliance may lead to copyright infringement lawsuits and force retraining models excluding reserved works.

## 4. The Smudged Mirror: Risks in the Output

Even if training were legal, AI output can generate additional problems:

1. **Unintentional Plagiarism:** AI can reproduce substantial fragments of protected works without user knowledge. Recent cases have documented near-literal reproductions of specific code, texts, and images. **Documented case:** GitHub Copilot was sued for reproducing code under restrictive licenses without proper attribution, violating open-source license terms like GPL.
2. **Involuntary License Activation:** If the model was trained on open-source code subject to *copyleft* licenses (like GPL), the user might be legally obligated to open their entire commercial project code, representing a catastrophic risk for trade secrets.
3. **Deep Synthesis and Deepfakes:** The capability to imitate voices and faces generates risks of:Image rights violationIdentity fraud and impersonationDefamationCountries like **China** have implemented specific "deep synthesis" regulations (*Deep Synthesis Provisions*, 2023) to mitigate these damages.

*Who should respond when this digital mirror returns to us an image that belongs to someone else?*

## 5. The Authorship Puzzle: Who Owns It?

The world map of ownership is today a mosaic of divergent criteria. The fundamental question is: **can AI-generated work receive copyright protection?**

### International approaches:

- **Countries protecting computer-generated works:** **United Kingdom** (since Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988), **India**, **Ireland**, **New Zealand**, and **South Africa** permit protection of works without human authors, assigning rights to whoever makes the "arrangements necessary" for its creation.
- **Ukraine's exception:** Has innovated by creating a specific right for "non-original" software-generated objects, a unique category in the international landscape that recognizes legal value without requiring human originality.
- **Systematic rejection of non-human author:** The **"DABUS"** case is the global example; patent offices in the USA, UK, and EPO have denied registrations where AI is named as inventor, always requiring a human behind. **DABUS precedent:** Stephen Thaler attempted to register patents with "DABUS" (AI) as inventor in multiple jurisdictions. All rejected the application, establishing that only natural persons can be inventors/authors.
- **The "Aesthetic Criterion" in China:** The **Beijing Internet Court** (2023) recognized authorship because the user made constant adjustments to *prompts* and parameters, demonstrating **human creative choice** rather than mere technological chance. **Ratio decidendi:** Sufficient human intervention in configuration, selection, and refinement of output can constitute protectable creative contribution.
- **United States - Copyright Office:** The US Copyright Office has established it will only recognize copyright in AI-generated content when there is **sufficient human creative contribution**. Zarya of the Dawn case (2023): recognized copyright over the complete comic but NOT over individual images generated by Midjourney.

### Glossary of Key Terms

- **Copyright:** Legal framework protecting original creations of the human spirit, granting exclusive exploitation rights to the author.
- **Deep Synthesis:** AI technologies to generate or manipulate audiovisual content (like deepfakes), under strict regulation in jurisdictions like China (*Deep Synthesis Provisions*, 2023).
- **Open Source:** Software whose source code is public, but which often carries **attribution** obligations or *copyleft* restrictions that AI may inadvertently activate.
- **Generative AI:** Artificial intelligence systems capable of creating new content (text, images, code, audio) in response to human instructions (*prompts*), based on massive data learning.
- **Text and Data Mining (TDM):** Automated technique for analyzing texts and data in digital format to extract patterns, trends, and knowledge. Regulated by Arts. 3 and 4 of the DSM Directive.
- **Opt-out:** Right of copyright holders to expressly reserve use of their works for data mining, through machine-readable mechanisms.
- **Human-in-the-loop:** Concept of significant human supervision and contribution in automated systems, essential to claim authorship over AI-generated works.

## Conclusion: Balancing Innovation and Protection

Human oversight is not a brake on technology; it is the engine that guarantees innovation is **responsible, ethical, and, above all, legally defensible**. In an ecosystem where machines learn from us, our responsibility as legal professionals is to ensure that learning respects the pillars of human creativity that have driven progress for centuries.

The challenge is not to stop generative AI, but to **legalize it**: create frameworks that permit its development while protecting the rights of those who, with their human talent, generated the knowledge these machines feed on.

💡 Final recommendation: Given current legal uncertainty, the most prudent strategy is exhaustive documentation of human intervention and preventive legal consultation before commercializing AI-generated products.
