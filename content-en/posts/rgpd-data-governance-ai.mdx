# GDPR and Data Governance: Complete Legal Guide for the Artificial Intelligence Era

*By Ricardo Scarpa | Updated: February 9, 2026 | Reading time: 55 minutes*

---

## Executive Summary

**Data governance in the artificial intelligence era** represents the most significant regulatory challenge for Spanish and European organizations in 2026. The **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)** (EU) 2016/679, in convergence with the **AI Act** (EU Regulation 2024/1689), establishes a dual regulatory framework of unprecedented complexity where compliance is no longer optional but rather the competitive differentiator ensuring business sustainability.

**Critical Context:** The **Spanish Data Protection Agency (AEPD)** has imposed record fines totaling **35.6 million euros in 2024**, focusing on "structural failures" in algorithmic governance. The energy sector (€11.68M), financial sector (€5.35M), and internet services (€4.50M) lead in violations, demonstrating that negligence in Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIA) and lack of proactive accountability are the primary factors in sanctionable risk.

**Competing Paradigms:**
- **GDPR:** Protection of individual privacy, informational self-determination (rights-based approach)
- **AI Act:** Systemic risk management, AI product safety (product/risk-based approach)
- **Result:** **Complementary and cumulative** application, NOT substitutive

**Immediate Critical Obligations:**
1. **Proactive Accountability** (Art. 5.2 GDPR): Demonstrate compliance at all times
2. **Preventive DPIA** (Art. 35 GDPR): Prior to deployment of high-risk systems
3. **Controlled Supply Chain:** Due diligence in selecting AI providers
4. **72-Hour Breach Notification** (Arts. 33-34 GDPR): Absolute transparency in incident response
5. **Valid Legal Basis** (Art. 6 GDPR): Consent is fictional in large-scale AI

**Sanction Regime:**
- **Tier 1:** Up to **€20M or 4% global turnover** (Art. 83.5 GDPR)
- **Tier 2:** Up to **€10M or 2% turnover** (Art. 83.4 GDPR)
- **Spain LOPDGDD:** Additional restrictions (Art. 9 - ideological consent limitations)

**Landmark Cases 2023-2025:**
- **AENA** (€10M): Non-existent DPIA for airport biometrics
- **LaLiga** (€1M): Disproportionate facial recognition
- **Endesa** (€6.1M): Security breach concealment
- **Enérgya-VM** (€5M): Provider supervision negligence

This guide provides comprehensive legal analysis using the **IRAC** (Issue-Rule-Application-Conclusion) methodology from Harvard Law School, applied to 12 real practical cases from the Spanish AI ecosystem.

## 1. Strategic Introduction: The Paradigm Shift in Algorithmic Regulation

We are facing an **unprecedented legal metamorphosis** in European digital law history. The transition we observe is not merely technical but **ontological**: we are moving from a regulatory framework centered on protecting individual privacy (the GDPR paradigm) to one that addresses **systemic risk of emerging technologies** (the AI Act paradigm). This legislative convergence constitutes the cornerstone of the European Union's digital sovereignty and redefines the playing field for any organization seeking to operate in the Spanish market.

### The Dialectical Tension: Innovation vs. Regulation

The dialectical tension between innovation and regulation has reached its maturity point. The **AI Act should not be understood as a *lex specialis*** that overrides privacy law, but as an **ex-ante oversight layer** that叠加 to data protection obligations.

**Whereas:**
- **GDPR** is based on the **informational self-determination** of the data subject (Art. 1.2: "protection of natural persons with regard to the processing of personal data")
- **AI Act** introduces a **product safety** and risk management logic requiring companies to have a holistic view of their algorithmic architecture

**Paradigmatic Example:**  
An AI system for credit scoring faces:
1. **GDPR Obligations:** Legal basis Art. 6.1, principles Art. 5, DPIA Art. 35, rights Arts. 12-23
2. **AI Act Obligations:** High-risk classification (Annex III.5.b), requirements Arts. 9-15, conformity assessment
3. **Result:** **Cumulative** compliance, NOT alternative

### "So What?": Compliance as a Market Asset

For senior management of Spanish companies, regulatory compliance has ceased to be a **negative externality** or a mere audit "*check*" to become a **trust asset**. In an ecosystem where the AEPD has demonstrated record sanctioning capability, data governance is the **competitive differentiator** guaranteeing investment sustainability.

**Quantifiable Impact Data:**
- **Average GDPR fine in Spain (2024):** €2.1 million per proceeding
- **Estimated reputational loss:** 15-30% stock price decline post-public sanction
- **AI projects cancelled by AEPD:** 47 in 2024 (precautionary suspension order)

**Central Thesis:** An AI that is not explainable or that processes data without a legal basis is not merely a legal risk; it is a **project destined for reputational and operational failure**.

### Comparative Table: Rights-Based vs. Risk-Based Approach

| Characteristic | Rights-Based Approach (GDPR) | Product/Risk-Based Approach (AI Act) |
|----------------|----------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Primary Object** | Protection of the natural person and their dignity | Safety, reliability, and robustness of the system |
| **Control Basis** | Informational self-determination | Systemic risk mitigation and conformity |
| **Key Mechanism** | Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA - Art. 35 GDPR) | AI risk management and conformity assessment (Arts. 9, 43 AI Act) |
| **Supervision** | Data Protection Authorities (AEPD) | AI Office / Supervisory Agency (AESIA - pending) |
| **Starting Point** | Existence of personal data processing | Classification of the system according to use (High Risk, Prohibited, etc.) |
| **Guiding Principle** | Minimization, purpose limitation, accuracy | Precision, robustness, cybersecurity, human oversight |
| **Temporal Scope** | Applied from May 25, 2018 | Phased application 2025-2027 |
| **Maximum Sanction** | €20M or 4% global turnover | €35M or 7% turnover (prohibited practices) |

**Practical Implication:**  
An AI service provider in Spain must demonstrate:
1. **GDPR:** Ongoing compliance with data processing obligations
2. **AI Act:** Technical conformity of the system as a product
3. **LOPDGDD:** Compliance with Spanish specificities (Art. 9, 10, 22, etc.)

Deep comprehension of the AI Act necessarily requires mastering the **technical-legal definitions** that serve as the foundations of this legal architecture.

## 2. Technical-Legal Taxonomy: Critical Definitions for Compliance

In the field of Artificial Intelligence, **terminological imprecision** constitutes a *probatio diabolica* for the DPO (Data Protection Officer). The distinction between roles in the value chain is more complex than ever due to the nature of **General-Purpose AI (GPAI) models**.

### Data Controller

**Legal Definition (Art. 4.7 GDPR):**  
> "The natural or legal person, public authority, agency or other body which, alone or jointly with others, **determines the purposes and means of the processing** of personal data."

**Critical Problem in AI Ecosystems:**  
Who is the controller when a Spanish company implements a language model developed by a third party in the USA but **customizes it with its own** customer data?

**Jurisprudential Analysis:**  
The CJEU in *Wirtschaftsakademie* (C-210/16) established that those who **jointly** determine the purpose are **joint controllers** (Art. 26 GDPR), requiring an agreement dividing obligations.

**Typical Shared Liability Scenarios in AI:**

| Scenario | Controller(s) | Legal Basis for Division |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| Company uses ChatGPT API for customer analysis | **Company:** Sole controller (determines analysis purpose)<br>**OpenAI:** Processor (processes on company's behalf) | Art. 28 GDPR - Processor Contract |
| Two companies co-develop AI model with shared data | **Both:** Joint controllers (jointly determine) | Art. 26 GDPR - Joint Controller Agreement |
| Hospital uses diagnostic AI from external provider that trains with patient data | **Hospital:** Controller of patient data<br>**Provider:** Controller of model training (own purpose) | Independent - Separate Assessments |

**Practical Implication:**  
Liability becomes **shared or diluted**, requiring extremely precise **processor contracts (Art. 28 GDPR)** defining:
- Who determines which aspects of processing
- Technical limits of processing
- Intellectual property of model vs. input data
- Technical audit rights

### Data Processor

**Legal Definition (Art. 4.8 GDPR):**  
> "A natural or legal person, public authority, agency or other body which **processes personal data on behalf of the controller**."

**Critical Risk in AI SaaS:**  
The opacity of AI Software-as-a-Service solutions poses the risk that the processor becomes the **de facto controller** if it makes unilateral decisions about:
- The algorithm's logic
- Data retention for own training
- Use of sub-processors without prior authorization

**AEPD Case PS/00224/2020 (Xfera Móviles):**  
€8.15M fine for allowing a processor (telemarketing provider) to determine the means of processing → The processor became **de facto controller**, but Xfera was **not exempt** from liability due to supervision negligence.

**Lesson:** The controller **cannot disengage** by delegating to a third party. Obligations under Arts. 28.1 and 32.1 GDPR to guarantee security and legality of processing by processors.

### Privacy by Design

**Legal Basis:** Art. 25.1 GDPR

> "...the controller shall implement appropriate technical and organizational measures... both at the **time of determining the means for processing** and at the **time of the processing itself**."

**In AI, this means:**
1. **Architectural Design:** Privacy cannot be a post-hoc patch
2. **Engineering Requirement:** From the first code block
3. **Full Lifecycle:** Training, validation, deployment, maintenance

**Privacy by Design Implementation Techniques in AI:**

| Technique | Description | AI Example |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Minimization** | Collect only strictly necessary data | Train prediction model with aggregated, not individual, data |
| **Pseudonymization** | Replace direct identifiers with codes | Tokenize names before NLP processing |
| **Homomorphic Encryption** | Process data without decrypting | Model infers on encrypted client data |
| **Federated Learning** | Model travels to data, not data to model | Distributed training without centralizing sensitive data |
| **Differential Privacy** | Add statistical noise to anonymize | Algorithm introduces noise in training data |

**Paradigmatic Case - Apple vs. Google Analytics:**  
Apple implements **on-device ML** (local processing) for Siri, avoiding transferring voice data to servers. Traditional Google Analytics centralizes all data → Apple complies with PbDD, Google requires robust DPIA.

### Biometric Data

**Legal Definition (Art. 4.14 GDPR):**  
> "Personal data obtained from a **specific technical processing**, relating to the **physical, physiological or behavioral** characteristics of a natural person which allow or confirm **unique identification**..."

**Recognized Types:**
- **Physiological:** Fingerprint, iris, DNA, facial geometry, vein pattern
- **Behavioral:** Handwritten signature, keystroke pattern, voice, gait

**Strict AEPD Position:**  
Mass use of biometrics in public spaces is, **almost without exception, disproportionate** (AEPD Facial Recognition Guide, 2021). User "convenience" is NOT a valid legal basis.

**Recent Biometric Sanctions:**
- **AENA** (€10M, 2025): Airport biometric implementation without valid DPIA
- **LaLiga** (€250K + expansion €1M, 2019-2021): Facial recognition app to detect pirate bars - Manifest disproportionality

**Consequence:** Any biometrics project in Spain requires **prior AEPD assessment** via consultation Art. 36.3 GDPR before deployment.

### "So What?": The Cost of Misclassification

Incorrectly classifying an AI system (e.g., considering "limited risk" what the authority classifies as "high risk") has **direct financial implications**:

1. **Fine for non-compliance:** Up to €20M or 4% (Art. 83.5 GDPR)
2. **Market withdrawal order:** Total loss of CAPEX invested in development
3. **Reputational damage:** Impact on stock price (median -18% first 30 days post-public sanction)
4. **Civil liability:** Compensation to affected parties for defective automated decisions

**Quantified Example:**  
Development of medical diagnostic AI system:
- **Initial investment:** €2M (R&D, datasets, validation)
- **Error:** Not conducting DPIA considering system "low risk"
- **Result:** AEPD orders suspension + €5M fine
- **Total loss:** €7M + 2 years of project lost

**Conclusion:** Investment in compliance (€50-150K) is **marginal** vs. sanction risk.

## 3. Regulatory Architecture: Hierarchy and Relationship Between GDPR, LOPDGDD, and AI Act

The legal framework is not a **sum of isolated laws** but a **complex mosaic** where the AI Act acts as *lex specialis* on the firm ground of GDPR. It is essential to understand that the **AI Act does NOT exempt from GDPR compliance**; on the contrary, it assumes it.

### Fundamental Principle: Regulatory Accumulation

**Golden Rule:**  
Any AI system processing personal data in Spain must comply **simultaneously** with:
1. Regulation (EU) 2016/679 (GDPR)
2. Organic Law 3/2018 (LOPDGDD)
3. Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act) - Phased application 2025-2027
4. Specific sectoral regulations (Law 41/2002 healthcare, Law 34/2002 LSSI, etc.)

**No option:** Compliance with one does NOT replace the other.

### The "Dual Supervision" Mosaic

Spain faces a **unique administrative challenge**: the coexistence of the **AEPD** with the new **Spanish Agency for AI Supervision (AESIA)** (creation pending Royal Decree, Feb. 2026).

**Projected Competence Distribution:**

| Aspect | Competent Authority | Legal Basis |
|--------|---------------------|------------|
| **Fundamental rights** (dignity, privacy, non-discrimination) | **AEPD** (exclusive) | Arts. 51-59 GDPR |
| **AI product technical safety** | **AESIA** | Arts. 70-75 AI Act |
| **Algorithm technical conformity** (accuracy, robustness) | **AESIA** | Arts. 9, 15 AI Act |
| **Personal data processing** (legal bases, principles) | **AEPD** (exclusive) | Arts. 5-6 GDPR |
| **Impact assessment** (DPIA + FRIAS fundamental rights) | **Both** (coordination) | Art. 35 GDPR + Art. 27 AI Act |
| **Sanctions** for data infractions | **AEPD** | Art. 83 GDPR |
| **Sanctions** for AI technical infractions | **AESIA** | Art. 99 AI Act |

**Bureaucratic Friction Risk:**  
The same system may be inspected by **two authorities** with potentially divergent criteria. **Friction** is inevitable if a robust institutional communication channel is not established.

**Proposed Solution (DPO Guide):**  
Create a unified dossier where:
- AESIA documentation (technical conformity assessment)
- AEPD documentation (DPIA, legal bases, rights)
→ Are **consistent and NOT contradictory**

**Regulatory pyramid applicable to AI in Spain:**

**EU Primacy Principle:**  
In case of conflict between national law (LOPDGDD) and EU law (GDPR), **EU law prevails**. Spanish law CANNOT reduce the GDPR's level of protection.

**Example:**  
Art. 9 LOPDGDD establishes that **consent alone is NOT sufficient** to process ideological/religious data → This rule is **more restrictive** than GDPR (Art. 9.2.a allows explicit consent) → **Valid** because it increases protection, NOT reduces it.

### RGPD-AI Act Interaction: Use Cases

**Case 1: AI HR Screening System**

| Regulation | Specific Obligation |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **GDPR** | • Legal basis Art. 6.1.b) (contract performance) or 6.1.f) (legitimate interest balancing)<br>• Minimization principle (only relevant data)<br>• Mandatory DPIA (Art. 35.3.a - large-scale automated assessment)<br>• Rights of access, rectification, opposition, explanation (Arts. 15, 16, 21, 22.3) |
| **AI Act** | • Classification: High risk (Annex III.4 - employment)<br>• Provider obligations: Risk management, unbiased data quality, technical documentation, human oversight (Arts. 9-15)<br>• Conformity assessment + CE marking<br>• Mandatory FRIAS if deployer >250 employees (Art. 27) |
| **LOPDGDD** | • Art. 88 Workers' Statute: Prohibition on processing worker personal data without prior information and worker representative consultation<br>• Art. 22 LOPDGDD: Right to digital disconnection |

**Cumulative Compliance:** The system must satisfy **all** obligations from **all** regulations simultaneously.

**Case 2: Customer Service Chatbot**

| Regulation | Classification | Obligations |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| **GDPR** | Personal data processing (customer conversations) | Legal basis Art. 6.1.b) or f), clear information Art. 13, security Art. 32 |
| **AI Act** | Limited risk (transparency) | Art. 50: Inform user they are interacting with AI (unless obvious from context) |
| **LOPDGDD** | Information society services | Law 34/2002 LSSI: Pre-contractual information, cookies |

### "So What?": Friction Mitigation Strategy

**Practical Recommendation for DPOs:**

1. **Unified Dossiers:** Create a single compliance dossier including:
   - GDPR assessment (DPIA)
   - AI Act assessment (FRIAS + technical conformity)
   - Cross-mapping of requirements

2. **Internal Coordination:** The DPO must **closely** collaborate with:
   - Engineering leads (technical architecture)
   - Compliance officers (internal audits)
   - Legal (provider/processor contracts)

3. **Preventive Consultation:** Before deploying critical systems:
   - AEPD consultation Art. 36.3 GDPR (if doubt about DPIA)
   - AESIA consultation (when operational) for borderline high-risk systems

4. **Cross-Documentation:** Ensure that:
   - AI Act conformity declaration does NOT contradict GDPR privacy policy
   - DPIA and FRIAS are **complementary**, NOT duplicated

**Practical Tool:** Unified dossier template available in GDPR-AI Act compliance resources.

## 4. Legal Bases for Artificial Intelligence: Beyond Consent

Training large-scale AI models (LLMs - *Large Language Models*) often **conflicts with the rigidity of traditional consent**. In many cases, "free and informed consent" is a **legal fiction** when the user has no real alternative to access an essential service.

### The Consent Problem in Large-Scale AI

**Art. 4.11 GDPR - Consent Definition:**  
> "Any freely given, **specific, informed and unambiguous** indication of the data subject's wishes... either by a statement or by a clear affirmative action..."

**Cumulative Requirements:**
1. **Free:** Genuine option to refuse without detriment
2. **Specific:** For each concrete purpose
3. **Informed:** With understandable information about processing
4. **Unambiguous:** Clear affirmative action (NO silence or inaction)

**Why Consent Fails in AI:**

| Problem | Description | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Power Asymmetry** | User in subordinate or dependent position | Worker "consents" to AI monitoring by employer → Consent NOT free (Recital 43 GDPR) |
| **Impossible Granularity** | AI uses data for multiple purposes difficult to segregate | Language model trained on millions of web texts → Impossible to get specific consent from each author |
| **Incomprehensible Information** | Algorithmic logic too complex to explain | 175 billion parameter neural network → How to provide "understandable" information? |
| **Impracticable Revocation** | Machine unlearning technically very difficult | User revokes consent → How to "unlearn" data from already-trained model? |

**AEPD Doctrine Conclusion:**  
Consent is **inadequate as the primary legal basis** for AI systems involving:
- Training with massive datasets
- Continuous processing of aggregated data
- Automated decisions with significant effects

### Art. 6 GDPR and Legitimate Interest

**Art. 6.1.f) GDPR - Legitimate Interest:**  
> "Processing is necessary for the purposes of the legitimate interests pursued by the controller or by a third party, **unless the interests or fundamental rights and freedoms** of the data subject prevail..."

**Three-Part Balancing Test:**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  STEP 1: Does a legitimate interest exist?     │
│  → Must be lawful, articulated, current          │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  STEP 2: Is processing necessary?             │
│  → No less invasive alternative exists          │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  STEP 3: Balancing Test                        │
│  ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────────────┐  │
│  │ COMPANY INTEREST │ vs │ INDIVIDUAL RIGHTS │  │
│  │ (Weight on scale)│     │(Weight on scale) │  │
│  └─────────────────┴─────────────────────────┘  │
│  → Does the first prevail? → Legitimate         │
│  → Does the second prevail? → Not legitimate     │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Applied Example - AI Bank Fraud Detection:**

**STEP 1 - Legitimate Interest:**
- ✅ Protecting customers from fraud (unquestionable legitimate interest)
- ✅ Complying with anti-money laundering legal obligations (Law 10/2010)

**STEP 2 - Necessity:**
- ✅ Manual detection impossible (millions of transactions/day)
- ✅ AI is the only effective real-time method

**STEP 3 - Balancing:**

| Factor | Company Weight | Client Weight | Result |
|--------|--------------|--------------|-----------|
| Data Type | Financial transactions (sensitive but expected) | +2 | +3 | ⚖️ Balanced |
| Reasonable Expectation | Client expects fraud protection | +3 | +1 | ✅ Company |
| Individual Impact | Temporary blocking if false positive | +1 | +2 | ⚖️ Balanced |
| Safeguards | Human supervision + appeal | +2 | -1 | ✅ Company |
| **TOTAL** | **+8** | **+5** | ✅ **Valid Legitimate Interest** |

**Conclusion:** Bank can use fraud detection AI under Art. 6.1.f) **IF it implements safeguards** (human supervision, right to object, decision review).

### Art. 9 LOPDGDD: The Consent Limit in Spain

**Art. 9.1 LOPDGDD - Special Categories Restriction:**  
> "The data subject's consent **alone shall not be sufficient** to lift the prohibition on processing data whose main purpose is to **identify their ideology, trade union affiliation, religion, sexual orientation, beliefs** or racial or ethnic origin."

**AEPD Interpretation:**  
This rule is **more restrictive** than Art. 9 GDPR. While GDPR allows explicit consent (Art. 9.2.a), **Spain requires additional legal authorization** for ideological/religious data.

**Implication for AI:**  
AI systems that analyze social media or public profiles to **infer** political/religious trends:
- ❌ **Cannot rely solely on consent** (not even explicit)
- ✅ **Require legal authorization** of higher rank (organic law, essential public interest Art. 9.2.g)

**Landmark Case - Constitutional Court Judgment 76/2019 (Nullity Art. 58 bis LOPD):**

**Facts:** Art. 58 bis LOPD (prior to LOPDGDD) allowed political parties to collect political opinions expressed publicly on the internet without specific consent.

**Issue:** Does it violate the fundamental right to data protection (Art. 18.4 Spanish Constitution)?

**Holding:** YES. The Constitutional Court declared the rule **unconstitutional**.

**Rationale:**
1. Political opinion data is a **special category** (Art. 9 GDPR)
2. "Public" nature of the statement does NOT eliminate protection
3. Absence of **specific and adequate guarantees** (there were no temporal limits, precise purposes, or effective right to object)
4. Risk of **massive manipulation** and **political profiling** without control

**Practical Conclusion:**  
**Ideological data scraping for algorithmic purposes** in Spain is an **un-crossable red line** without robust legal authorization + enhanced guarantees.

### Alternative Legal Bases to Consent for AI

**Comparison of Art. 6 GDPR Legal Bases:**

| Legal Basis | Art. | When Applicable to AI | Advantages | Disadvantages | Example |
|------------|------|---------------------|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Consent** | 6.1.a | Optional services without power asymmetry | Clear legitimacy | Revocation complicates operations | User accepts personalized eCommerce recommendations |
| **Contract Performance** | 6.1.b | AI necessary to provide contracted service | Solid if AI is essential part | Limited to contractual relationship | Insurance uses AI to calculate premium (part of contract) |
| **Legal Obligation** | 6.1.c | Regulatory compliance | Shielded against challenges | Requires clear enabling norm | Anti-money laundering AI (Law 10/2010) |
| **Vital Interest** | 6.1.d | Medical emergencies | Ethically justifiable | Very restrictive (only life/health) | Diagnostic AI in emergency without time for consent |
| **Public Interest** | 6.1.e | Essential public services | Democratic legitimacy | Requires legal basis + DPIA | AI for urban traffic management |
| **Legitimate Interest** | 6.1.f | Fraud detection, security, balanced marketing | Flexible if robust balancing | Requires complex test + documentation | AI Netflix recommendations (preferences vs. privacy) |

<a name="5-responsabilidad-proactiva"></a>
## 5. The Imperative of Proactive Accountability in AI

**Proactive accountability** is the driving principle of all modern data governance. It is not enough to comply with the law; it is **mandatory to be able to demonstrate** such compliance at any time.

### Regulatory Foundation: Art. 5.2 GDPR

**Art. 5.2 GDPR - Proactive Accountability Principle:**  
> "The controller shall be **responsible for compliance** with the provisions set out in paragraph 1 [processing principles] and **shall be able to demonstrate** it (*accountability*)."

**CJEU Interpretation:**  
In *Fashion ID* (C-40/17), the Court established that responsibility is **continuous and documentable**. It is not enough to claim compliance; it must be **proven** with objective evidence.

### Accountability Tools in AI Systems

**1. Record of Processing Activities (RoPA)**

**Legal Basis:** Art. 30 GDPR

**Mandatory Minimum Content for AI:**

| Field | Description | AI Credit Scoring Example |
|-------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| **Name and Contact** | Controller + DPO | FinTech XYZ S.L. + dpo@fintechxyz.es |
| **Purposes of Processing** | Specific purposes | Creditworthiness assessment of loan applicants |
| **Categories of Data Subjects** | Who are the affected individuals | Natural person credit applicants |
| **Categories of Data** | Types of data processed | Identification, financial, credit history |
| **Categories of Recipients** | To whom data is communicated | Credit bureaus, financial institutions |
| **International Transfers** | If any, with what guarantees | AWS Ireland server (EU, no transfer) |
| **Retention Periods** | When data is deleted | 5 years post-denial / 10 years post-approval |
| **Security Measures** | Technical and organizational | AES-256 encryption, RBAC access control, audit logs |

**AI-Specific Details That MUST Be Included:**
- Algorithm logic description (abstract level, NOT source code)
- Training datasets used (origin, characteristics)
- Model accuracy and bias metrics
- Human supervision procedures
- Model update and retraining

**2. Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)**

**Legal Basis:** Art. 35 GDPR

**Mandatory Requirement:**  
DPIA is **mandatory** when processing may pose a "high risk to the rights and freedoms" of natural persons (Art. 35.1).

**Mandatory Cases for AI (Art. 35.3):**

| Scenario | Description | AI Example |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| **a) Systematic and Exhaustive Evaluation** | Automated profiling with legal or significant effects | Scoring system that automatically decides credit denial |
| **b) Large-Scale Processing of Special Categories** | Sensitive data Art. 9 or criminal data Art. 10 | Medical diagnostic AI processing health data of millions of patients |
| **c) Systematic Large-Scale Observation** | Monitoring of public accessible space | Facial recognition at train stations |

**CRITICAL:** The AEPD published in 2020 a **list of processing requiring mandatory DPIA**, including:
- AI systems with profiling for automated decisions
- Biometric processing
- Mass geolocation
- Intelligent video surveillance systems

**Minimum DPIA Content for AI (Art. 35.7):**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  AI SYSTEM DPIA STRUCTURE                           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  1. SYSTEMATIC DESCRIPTION                          │
│     - AI purposes and operation                     │
│     - Lifecycle: training → inference              │
│     - Data flow diagram                             │
│     - Roles: Controller, processors, users           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  2. NECESSITY AND PROPORTIONALITY                  │
│     - Why is AI necessary?                          │
│     - Are there less invasive alternatives?         │
│     - Strict proportionality test                   │
```
