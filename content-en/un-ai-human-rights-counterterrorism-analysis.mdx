# Legal Analysis: Artificial Intelligence, Human Rights and Counter-Terrorism

## Executive Summary

The position paper of the UN Special Rapporteur on the Promotion and Protection of Human Rights while Countering Terrorism (December 2025) raises fundamental legal questions about the compatibility of AI systems in counter-terrorism operations with international human rights law obligations. This analysis examines, using the IRAC methodology, four critical legal issues emerging from this instrument.

---

## I. PRIVACY AND DATA PROTECTION IN MASS AI SURVEILLANCE

### Legal Issue

**Are AI systems that enable differentiated mass surveillance of populations in counter-terrorism contexts compatible with the right to privacy enshrined in international human rights law treaties?**

### Applicable Rule

#### Referenced International Legislation

The right to privacy constitutes a fundamental right protected by multiple instruments:

- **Art. 17 International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR):** Considers protection against arbitrary interference in private life
- **Art. 8 European Convention on Human Rights (ECHR):** Guarantees the right to respect for private and family life
- **UN General Assembly Resolution 45/95:** Recognizes the right to privacy as an inalienable human right

#### Established Jurisprudence

The European Court of Human Rights has established in **Big Brother Watch and Others v. United Kingdom** (2021) that:

1. Mass surveillance requires a precise legal basis that is necessary
2. Search "selectors" must be individually justified as necessary and proportionate
3. Independent "end-to-end" supervision must exist at each phase of the process

The UN Human Rights Committee has affirmed (General Comment No. 16, para. 8) that any restriction on the right to privacy must meet three cumulative requirements:
- **Legality:** Clear, accessible, and foreseeable legal basis
- **Necessity:** Pursuit of legitimate security objective
- **Proportionality:** Balance between security and privacy

#### Relevant Soft Law

The UN position paper establishes that AI systems that analyze massive data through machine learning can:
- Enable inferences about behavioral patterns imperceptible to human analysis (para. 21)
- Generate "social engineering" effects through continuous monitoring
- Amplify historical privacy violations through surveillance intensification

### Application of Law

#### Subsumption of Facts into Normative Hypotheses

The UN document identifies the following factual hypothesis:

> "AI can process millions of data points simultaneously, identifying correlations and behavioural patterns that may elude human analysts... creating unprecedented opportunities for monitoring citizens' political opinions, associations, and ideological leanings." (para. 1.6)

**Compatibility analysis:**

1. **Legality requirement:** Although many states have legislated on electronic surveillance, the document evidences that:
   - No "clear and specific legal authorization" exists for AI systems in mass surveillance (para. Recommendation 16)
   - Authorities frequently act under broad "national security" powers
   - Lack of precision in "selectors" used by machines

2. **Necessity and proportionality:** The proportionality test requires:
   - **Suitability:** Does it effectively prevent terrorism? Evidence is limited (para. 2)
   - **Necessity:** Are less invasive means available? The document suggests yes (para. 11)
   - **Proportionality stricto sensu:** Does the security benefit justify the intrusion?
     - Data: The document notes "the number of actual terrorists in most populations is very small, serious dataset limitations can prevent meaningful extrapolation" (para. 2)
     - Risk of massive false positives

3. **Harm intensification:** Applying AI to prior surveillance data:
   - Amplifies past violations (historically disproportionate against minorities)
   - Creates "shadow effect": preventive self-censorship (para. 6)
   - Violates data minimization principle

#### Exception Analysis

No valid exceptions apply under international law:
- States of emergency (Art. 4 ICCPR) require threat to nation's life, not ordinary terrorism
- Proportionality is per se analysis, not relative to perceived threat

### Conclusion

**Conclusion:** AI systems for mass surveillance in counter-terrorism contexts **are presumably incompatible** with international human rights law obligations, unless they jointly comply with:

1. Specific and accessible legal basis identifying:
   - Categories of authorized "selectors"
   - Individual justification of each selector as necessary and proportionate

2. Independent supervision in "cascade":
   - Prospective judicial authorization
   - Continuous supervision during operation
   - Ex post facto effectiveness review

3. Prohibition of undifferentiated mass surveillance

**Operational recommendation:** States must repeal broad "national security" frameworks and implement specific laws linking each AI operation to verifiable threats.

## II. NON-DISCRIMINATION IN FACIAL RECOGNITION AND BIOMETRIC SYSTEMS

### Legal Issue

**Can facial recognition and biometric systems trained with biased datasets comply with constitutional and international guarantees of equality and non-discrimination when deployed in identification operations in security contexts?**

### Applicable Rule

#### International Non-Discrimination Obligations

- **Art. 2 ICCPR + Art. 26:** Prohibits discrimination based on "race, color, sex, language, religion, political opinion or other social condition"
- **Art. 14 ECHR:** Guarantees enjoyment of rights "without distinction on any ground such as sex, race, color, language, religion..."
- **International Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Racial Discrimination (CERD):** Art. 2.1(a) obligation to eliminate discriminatory practices

#### International Tribunal Jurisprudence

The Committee on the Elimination of Racial Discrimination (CERD) in its General Recommendation No. 36 (2020) establishes:

> "Before procuring or deploying such systems States should adopt appropriate legislative, administrative and other measures to determine the purpose of their use and to regulate as accurately as possible the parameters and guarantees that prevent breaches of human rights. When the results of an assessment of a technology indicate a high risk of discrimination or other human rights violations, States should take measures to avoid the use of such a technology."

The CJEU in jurisprudence on discriminatory algorithms affirms that:
- Algorithms "neutral in appearance" can produce indirect discrimination
- Developers are responsible for auditing datasets for bias

#### Specific Regulatory Framework

EU AI Regulation (2024/1689) Art. 10 establishes that:
- Training data must be "representative, unbiased, and of high quality"
- Known biases must be identified and documented
- Discriminatory effects require mitigation before deployment

### Application of Law

#### Bias Problem Characterization

The UN document notes:

> "Empirical studies have demonstrated disproportionate error rates across demographic groups, particularly affecting individuals with darker skin tones, women and marginalised communities... such disparities stem from the continued reliance by developers on unrepresentative training datasets and the continued reinforcement of the underlying algorithmic architectures that perpetuate systemic inequities." (para. 3)

**Legal analysis of bias:**

1. **Discriminatory nature:**
   - Errors are not random but **systematic and predictable**
   - Disproportionately affect protected groups
   - Meet definition of indirect discrimination

2. **Attribution of responsibility:**
   - Developers: Responsibility for deficient design (Art. 10 AI Act)
   - Deploying states: Responsibility for negligent acquisition and use
   - Both: Joint responsibility for negligence in audit

3. **Violation of proportionality test:**
   - **Suitability:** A system with unequal error rates is NOT suitable
   - **Necessity:** Less biased technologies exist (multimodal recognition)
   - **Proportionality:** Cannot justify use on minority populations

#### Specific Application Cases

The document describes:

a) **Myanmar:** Creation of integrated national biometric system "to combat crime and protect national security" (para. 2) → Risk of:
   - Ethnic profiling of Rohingya minorities
   - Use for discriminatory political repression

b) **U.S. border:** Documented inequalities in facial recognition (US Commission on Civil Rights, 2024)
   - Error rates of 34.7% in Black women vs. 0.8% in Caucasian men
   - Systematic use in migrant detentions

#### Corporate Responsibility

Under UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights:
- Companies have human rights due diligence obligation
- Must evaluate differential impact on vulnerable groups
- Cannot invoke "technical uncertainty" as excuse for inaction

### Conclusion

**Conclusion:** Facial recognition and biometric systems with unequal error rates **violate international non-discrimination obligations** and **generate international state responsibility** for deploying them, especially when:

1. Dataset has not been audited for bias
2. Mitigation measures are not implemented
3. Deployed in contexts where they affect fundamental rights
4. Applied to historically discriminated populations

**Operational recommendation:**
- Prohibition of deployment until independent audit demonstrates error parity
- Implementation of Art. 29.3 AI Act: "fundamental rights impact assessments" obligation
- Civil liability for damages caused by algorithmic discrimination

## III. FREEDOM OF EXPRESSION AND ONLINE CONTENT MODERATION

### Legal Issue

**Can AI systems to moderate content considered "terrorist" online comply with constitutional and international guarantees of freedom of expression when operating without human supervision of cultural context and author intent?**

### Applicable Rule

#### International Protection of Freedom of Expression

- **Art. 19 ICCPR:** "Everyone has the right to freedom of expression" with restrictions ONLY if they are "prescribed by law" and "necessary for... the protection of the rights of others"
- **Art. 10 ECHR:** Similar, recognizing very limited exceptions

#### Valid Restriction Test (ECHR Jurisprudence)

Following **Lingens v. Austria** (1986) and subsequent cases, a restriction is valid IF:

1. **Legality:** Clear, accessible, foreseeable legal basis
2. **Legitimate Aim:** Public safety, rights protection (Art. 10.2 ECHR)
3. **Necessity in a Democratic Society:** Proportionate, not excessive, responds to pressing need

#### Jurisprudence on Incitement to Terrorism

The UN Human Rights Committee and Special Rapporteur on Freedom of Expression have established:

> "Incitement requires intent and high probability of harm. Mere criticism, even if harsh, of counter-terrorism policies does not constitute incitement." (Special Rapporteur on Freedom of Expression, A/80/341)

The Rabat Declaration (2012) on hate speech establishes six cumulative elements for valid restriction:
1. Context and circumstances
2. Public position of speaker
3. Intended audience
4. Nature and scope of expression
5. **Intent of speaker**
6. Actual probability of incitement to violence

#### Limitations of Automated Systems

The UN document recognizes:

> "Automated content moderation systems... are ill-suited to detecting and moderating implicit terrorist or violent extremist content online, namely content that 'conveys harmful messages through coded language, irony, humour, or cultural references'... algorithms have even amplified harmful content to maximise user engagement." (para. 11)

### Application of Law

#### Automated Moderation Problems

1. **Inability to evaluate required elements:**
   - **Intent:** Algorithms cannot access author intent
   - **Context:** They do not understand irony, satire, cultural references
   - **Probability of incitement:** Probabilistic test requiring human analysis

2. **Documented examples of over-moderation:**

The document illustrates:

> "Staff and resources devoted to moderation have been scaled down by major online platforms since 2022... investment in AI moderation has expanded, along with reliance on unreliable 'community' or crowd-sourced approaches." (para. 11)

Legal implication:
- Platforms prioritize efficiency over accuracy
- Generates removal of legitimate content
- Violates Art. 19 ICCPR

3. **Differentiated analysis by context:**

In situations of:
- **Active conflict:** Risk of censorship of legitimate criticism of states
- **Authoritarian regimes:** "Terrorism" used as repression category
- **Marginalized minorities:** Expression of ethno-religious minorities interpreted as "extremism"

#### Applicable Standards Difference

| Context | Required Standard | AI Capability | Result |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|--------|
| Common online moderation | Legality, Necessity | Partial (context) | INSUFFICIENT |
| "Terrorism" moderation | Rabat Order (6 elements) + Actual probability | Minimal (intent, context) | **INADEQUATE** |

#### State and Platform Obligations

Per General Comments of the Human Rights Committee:

States must:
1. Guarantee precise legal definition of "incitement to terrorism"
2. Require human supervision of removed content
3. Prohibit discriminatory censorship against minorities
4. Guarantee effective remedy against removals

Platforms must:
1. Publish moderation criteria
2. Provide meaningful human moderation
3. Publish transparency reports
4. Allow effective appeal

### Conclusion

**Conclusion:** AI systems for moderating "terrorist" content **violate freedom of expression obligations** when:

1. Operate without human supervision of **intent and context**
2. Do not apply Rabat Order test (6 cumulative elements)
3. Generate high rates of documented "false positives"
4. Lack meaningful appeal mechanisms

**Operational recommendation:**
- Prohibition of fully automated moderation of "terrorist" content
- Requirement of human supervisor for all removals
- Publication of data on: removals, appeals upheld, demographics of affected
- Platform liability for damages from negligent moderation

**Recommended test (Para. 17 UN Recommendations):**
> "Expression may only be restricted... in accordance with the requirements of legality, necessity, proportionality and legitimate aim, consistent with articles 19 and 20(1) of the ICCPR"

## IV. HUMAN CONTROL IN MILITARY AUTONOMOUS SYSTEMS

### Legal Issue

**Is it compatible with International Humanitarian Law (IHL) principles to delegate to AI systems control over military targeting decisions that may result in death when the final analysis is unverifiable computational ("black box")?**

### Applicable Rule

#### Applicable IHL Principles

According to Additional Protocol I to the Geneva Conventions (1977) and Customary Law:

**Article 36 AP I:** Every State must examine "new weapons... to determine if their employment would be prohibited in particular circumstances"

**Fundamental IHL principles:**

1. **Principle of Distinction (Art. 48 AP I):** Attacks must be directed at military objectives, NOT civilian population
2. **Principle of Proportionality (Art. 51.5.b AP I):** Incidental damage CANNOT be excessive in relation to military advantage
3. **Precaution Principle (Art. 57 AP I):** Take all feasible precautions to minimize civilian harm

#### ICRC Jurisprudence

The International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC) in its Submission to UN Secretary-General on AI in Military Domain (2025) affirms:

> "Meaningful human control requires that humans understand and can explain the basis for AI system recommendations... [AI systems must not] replace human decision-makers... must be limited to supporting them" (para. 131)

The ICRC distinguishes:

- **Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS):** System that identifies, selects, and attacks targets WITHOUT human intervention
- **AI-Enabled Decision Support Systems (AI DSS):** System that provides analysis/recommendations human CAN reject

#### Responsibility Framework

Under IHL:
- **Military commander:** Responsible for IHL compliance in operations
- **Responsibility by omission:** Failure to supervise tools
- **Command chain:** Each level must understand system functioning

### Application of Law

#### Subsumption of Factual Hypothesis

The document describes:

> "AI has reportedly been used in recent conflicts in military decision making and targeting processes, as in Gaza, Ukraine, Iraq, Syria and Yemen, although the specifics of particular technologies and the circumstances and parameters of their use often remain opaque." (para. 10)

Documented cases:
- "Lavender" system (Israel, 2024): AI targeting system with documented error rates
- "Deep sensing" systems generating "real-time threat assessments"

#### IHL Compliance Analysis

1. **Principle of Distinction:**

| Requirement | Can AI DSS comply? | Problem |
|-----------|----------------------|----------|
| Civilian/military classification | Yes (data analysis) | But how does commander verify? |
| Positive identification | Partial | Probabilistic analysis, not verification |
| Exclusion of civilians | Not always | Incomplete data, false positives |

**Critical analysis:** AI system that classifies persons as "terrorists" with X% probability is NOT equivalent to "positive identification" required by IHL.

2. **Principle of Proportionality:**

The principle requires commander compare:
- **Anticipated military advantage** (destroy combatant/infrastructure)
- **Expected civilian harm** (civilians in area)

**Problem with AI:**
- AI systems generate probabilistic "threat scores," not deterministic
- Document evidence: "AI systems may lack adequate human control and analysis, resulting in over-reliance on automated decision-making lacking in nuance, context and responsiveness to human rights considerations" (Introduction)
- Commander may suffer "automation bias": tendency to defer to machine

3. **Precaution Principle:**

AP 57 requires:
- Feasible precautions to verify target
- Feasible precautions to minimize civilian harm
- Abstention if civilians are present

**Analysis:** Automation CAN REDUCE precautions:
- Less time for human verification
- Higher speed = less contextual analysis
- Risk of error chain (garbage in, garbage out)

#### Accountability Question

**Critical accountability gap:**

When AI DSS causes civilian death from classification error:

- **Responsible = algorithm programmer?**
  - Problem: Does not know real operational context

- **Responsible = commander who authorized use?**
  - Problem: If they don't understand functioning, can they be responsible?
  - Criminal precedent: "obedience to machine orders" is no excuse

- **Responsible = state that acquired system?**
  - Problem: Private providers frequently deny transparency for "trade secrets"

The document affirms:

> "The deployment of AI-enabled systems has raised particular challenges for the attribution of responsibility and accountability in relation to violations of IHL and human rights law" (para. 10)

#### "Meaningful Human Control" Test

The ICRC proposes three-element test:

1. **Understandability:** Human understands how system arrives at recommendation
   - **AI context:** Deep learning systems = "black box"
   - **Result:** INSUFFICIENT

2. **Capacity for Intervention:** Human has time/capability to reject
   - **AI context:** "Real-time targeting" systems do NOT allow deliberation
   - **Result:** INSUFFICIENT

3. **Responsibility:** Clear command chain on final decision
   - **AI context:** Opacity on roles
   - **Result:** INSUFFICIENT

### Conclusion

**Conclusion:** AI systems for military targeting **do not meet minimum IHL requirements** when:

1. Not "understandable" (black box architecture)
2. Do not allow "meaningful human control" (operation speed)
3. Generate "dispersed" responsibility without clear attribution

**Required standards (per ICRC):**

- **PROHIBITION:** Systems that make final attack decision without verifiable human intervention
- **PERMISSIBLE WITH CONDITIONS:** AI DSS if:
  - System designed for maximum transparency ("explainable AI")
  - Protocol requiring human verification of each critical recommendation
  - Commander trained in functioning and limitations
  - Explicit responsibility assigned to identified person

**Operational recommendation (Para. 30-31 UN Recommendations):**

> "States should urgently negotiate by 2026 a treaty regulating autonomous weapons systems... to ensure effective human supervision and timely intervention and deactivation"

   - Private providers deny transparency

   - Independent oversight bodies are weak
   - "Oversight bodies must have guaranteed independence, sufficient human, financial and technical resources" (Para. 25 Recommendations)

4. **Non-Existence of Corporate Responsibility**
   - Developer companies evade responsibility for impact
   - Governments acquire systems without human rights due diligence

## VI. GENERAL CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### Legal Synthesis

The IRAC analysis of four central issues reveals that **AI systems deployed in counter-terrorism operations generate massive and systematic violations of internationally protected human rights**, particularly:

- **Right to privacy:** Mass surveillance without clear limits
- **Right to non-discrimination:** Biased systems disproportionately affect minorities
- **Freedom of expression:** Automated moderation censors legitimate criticism
- **Right to due process:** Lack of transparency prevents effective defense
- **IHL compliance:** Automatic targeting violates distinction and proportionality principles

### Root Cause

**Regulation "Lag":**

The document emphasizes there is "a real and growing gap between the deployment of technologies and regulatory capacity" (Introduction). The international human rights legal architecture **presupposes human agency**, not autonomous systems operating without comprehensible intermediation.

### Minimum Required Standards

Per international jurisprudence and soft law, any AI system in security/counter-terrorism contexts must comply with:

1. **Specific Legal Basis** (Art. 36 AP I, ECHR Legality)
   - General "national security" legislation is insufficient
   - Must specify what AI, in what contexts, with what controls

2. **Prior Human Rights Assessment** (Art. 14 EU AI Act, UN Soft Law)
   - Differential impact analysis on vulnerable groups
   - Risk identification before deployment

3. **Effective Human Supervision** (Art. 14 EU AI Act, ICRC Recommendations)
   - "Meaningful human control" - not simulated
   - Time and capacity to reject recommendations

4. **Algorithmic Transparency** (Art. 22 GDPR, Art. 3.4 ECHR)
   - Explainability of decisions
   - Access to training data for audit
   - Limited exceptions for "trade secrets"

5. **Remedy Mechanisms** (Art. 13 ICCPR)
   - Right to challenge automated decisions
   - Access to legal representation
   - Compensation for damages

6. **Categorical Prohibitions** (Art. 5 EU AI Act, UN Recommendation 3)
   - Single scoring system based on profiling = PROHIBITED
   - Real-time facial recognition without supervision = PROHIBITED
   - Fully automatic targeting = PROHIBITED

### Public Policy Recommendations

**For States:**

1. Repeal broad "national security" frameworks that exempt AI from regulation
2. Adopt specific legislation linking each AI operation to verifiable threats
3. Constitute independent oversight bodies with veto power
4. Require independent audit before deployment
5. Implement personal (not corporate) responsibility on officials authorizing AI

**For Private Sector:**

1. Conduct bias audits on datasets before sale
2. Document known system limitations
3. Refuse to sell systems with high violation risk
4. Disclose algorithms to independent auditors (confidentiality licenses)
5. Implement product liability (recall of defective systems)

**For International Community:**

1. Binding treaty on autonomous weapons (2026, per ICRC proposal)
2. Specific human rights protocol for AI in security
3. Independent audit standards
4. Compensation fund for AI-mediated violation victims

## Final Conclusion

The UN position paper of December 2025 represents a critical milestone in the convergence of **security law** and **human rights law**. Its systematic analysis demonstrates that **current regulation is insufficient** to protect fundamental rights against AI systems in counter-terrorism contexts.

The urgency is maximum: States continue deploying these systems without specific legal basis, human rights audit, or independent supervision. International jurisprudence establishes clear standards. What is lacking is **political will** for implementation.

**Legal prospective:** It can be expected that:
1. First condemning sentences against states for AI-mediated violations (CJEU, ECTHR) 2026-2027
2. Massive reparation obligations for surveillance without legal basis
3. Accelerated regulatory pressure as victims sue
4. Defense regulation reform to include human rights audit in system acquisition

**The law exists. What is lacking is its application.**
