## Can a criminal judgment be annulled because a judge used AI without verifying its responses?

In Colombia, the answer is already yes. On December 2, 2025, the Superior Court of Bogotá (Criminal Chamber) declared the nullity of a conviction to 198 months' imprisonment because the first-instance judge built their legal reasoning on at least sixteen sources—jurisprudence, doctrine, and regulations—that either do not exist or whose content does not correspond to what was attributed to them.

The Court concluded, based on the highest probability, that these errors are products of the termed 'hallucinations' of generative artificial intelligence tools used without the human supervision required by Colombian law. This is the first time a Colombian criminal chamber declares the nullity of a conviction for this reason.

## What happened: the case in three paragraphs

Defendant Luis Alberto Contreras Fandiño was convicted on October 21, 2025, by the Twenty-First Criminal Circuit Court of Bogotá for the crime of aggravated sexual assault of a person incapable of resisting (Arts. 210-211 of the Criminal Code), with a sentence of 198 months' imprisonment. The process traces back to acts occurring in June 2013 and charges filed in January 2016.

When resolving the appeal, the Superior Court of Bogotá (Presiding Justice: Jarol Estibens Echeverry Giraldo) reviewed the sources cited in the first-instance judgment and found that provisions of the Supreme Court of Justice, Constitutional Court judgments, and doctrinal works invoked to support the conviction either do not exist in official repositories or contain content radically different from what was attributed to them.

The plurality, systematicity, and nature of the errors—impossible to overlook in a careful reading of the ruling—led the Court to conclude that the judgment was prepared with generative AI without verification. On December 2, 2025, it declared total nullity and ordered referral of copies to the Sectional Judicial Discipline Commission.

## The 16 errors that brought down the conviction

The Court identified, among others, the following false or distorted sources in the first-instance judgment:

Supreme Court of Justice provisions that do not appear in their official repositories (CSJ SP-1123-2018, CSJ SP-4959-2021, CSJ SP-1769-2023). Constitutional Court judgments whose real content bears no relation to what was attributed to them—such as STC T-301/2016, which actually deals with voluntary interruption of pregnancy, not on the gender approach in evidentiary assessment. A doctrinal work by Velásquez Velásquez titled 'Colombian Criminal Procedural Law' that the author never wrote. Articles of Law 906 of 2004 applied to scenarios for which they were not created. Textual citations from real provisions whose text does not match the authentic ruling.

## Why the Court declared nullity and did not issue a substitute ruling

The Supreme Court jurisprudence (CSJ SP2956/2018) classifies motivation defects into four categories. Only 'sophistical or apparent motivation'—that which grotesquely contradicts proven truth—can be resolved with a substitute decision by the appellate court. The other three defects (absolute absence, incomplete motivation, and dilogical) generate nullity.

The Court identified the vice produced by AI as sophistical motivation, which in principle would enable a substitute decision. However, it concluded that falsity so integrally permeates both the legal basis and evidentiary assessment foundations that issuing a new ruling from the Court would mean depriving the defendant of the constitutional guarantee of double instance. Nullity was, thus, the only constitutionally compatible solution.

## What this means for judicial operators and attorneys

**For judges:** Verification of each jurisprudential, normative, and doctrinal citation before signing a ruling is no longer a best practice recommendation. It is an obligation whose non-compliance can generate nullity of the proceeding and disciplinary responsibility. Agreement PCSJA24-12243 of the Superior Council of the Judiciary (December 16, 2024) expressly prohibits using AI to assess evidence, solve the central legal problem, or motivate decisions without real human supervision.

**For defense attorneys:** This ruling opens a new avenue for appeal. If a conviction contains legal citations that do not exist or whose content does not correspond to what was attributed, nullity for violation of due process (Art. 457 CPC) is proper, regardless of the merits of the case.

**For prosecutors:** Loss of material evidence and imprecisions in victim statements—two central points of this case—must be supported by verified jurisprudence in the new ruling to be issued. The risk of criminal prescription, in a process dating from 2013, is real and urgent.

## Conclusions and next steps

The TSB Criminal Chamber case, Rad. 1714, establishes that in Colombia, a judicial judgment produced with AI without supervision is not a defective judgment: it is a simulation of a judgment, legally null. The Court was explicit that it does not condemn the use of AI in the judiciary—which it encourages—but the delegation of judicial reasoning to the tool without effective human control.

The precedent also comes at a time when the international legal community converges in the same direction: the Criminal Chamber of Esquel (Chubut, Argentina) annulled in October 2025 a provision in whose text appeared literally the instruction of a generative AI. Judicial rationality, concludes the Superior Court of Bogotá, is non-delegable.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**What is the legal basis for the nullity declared by the TSB?**

Article 457 of the Criminal Procedure Code (Law 906 of 2004), which establishes nullity for violation of the right of defense or due process. Judicial motivation based on non-existent sources prevents the accused from exercising effective contradiction, violating the essential core of the right of defense enshrined in Article 29 of the Constitution.

**Can a judge use artificial intelligence in Colombia?**

Yes, but with restrictions. Agreement PCSJA24-12243 and judgment T-323/2024 of the Constitutional Court allow the use of AI for administrative and management tasks. However, they prohibit its use for assessing evidence, solving the central legal problem, or motivating the decision, when done exclusively based on tool responses without human verification and supervision.

**What disciplinary consequences can the first-instance judge face?**

The Court ordered referral of the case file to the Sectional Judicial Discipline Commission to investigate whether the judge's conduct constitutes a disciplinary infraction. The outcome of that investigation is independent of the nullity declared in the criminal proceeding.
