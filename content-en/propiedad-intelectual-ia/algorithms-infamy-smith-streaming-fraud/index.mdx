# Introduction: The End of Innocence in Digital Consumption

The music industry stands at the threshold of what various analysts and academics have termed its Napster moment,[^1] a technological disruption that not only challenges established business models but also questions the very ontology of artistic creation and consumption in the digital environment. However, unlike the late twentieth-century piracy crisis, the current challenge does not arise from the unauthorized distribution of human-created works, but from the convergence of two disruptive forces: generative artificial intelligence (AI) and massive consumption automation through bot networks.[^2] The case of Michael Smith, recently prosecuted in the Southern District of New York, constitutes the first major federal precedent in the United States on this phenomenon, revealing a sophisticated scheme of electronic fraud that enabled the diversion of more than ten million dollars in royalties.

The subject of this article is the detailed legal analysis of the scheme orchestrated by Smith between 2017 and 2024.[^3] The operation was not aimed at traditional artistic success, but at the industrialization of what its own creators termed *instant music*.[^4] Through the production of hundreds of thousands of algorithmically generated songs and their subsequent playback by an army of more than one thousand automated accounts, Smith generated approximately 661,440 daily streams, earning income exceeding one million dollars annually at the expense of the common royalty fund that legitimately belonged to actual artists and songwriters.

This thesis argues that the Smith case is not an isolated anomaly, but a symptom of a systemic vulnerability in streaming platform design. The predominant income distribution model, technically known as *pro rata* or GLOBALPROP,[^5] allocates payments based exclusively on market share of total global streams. Under this mechanism, a massive flow of automated streams, however individually minute, has the capacity to capture rents disproportionately, diluting the value of human labor and turning the algorithm into an instrument of illicit asset capture.

Throughout this article, the necessity of transitioning toward inherently fraud-resistant distribution models, such as the *user-centric* model or scaled variants that limit the weight of consumers with non-human behavioral patterns, will be defended. Ultimately, the Michael Smith case marks the end of an era where the click was synonymous with human preference, inaugurating a phase of algorithmic suspicion that demands a robust, technical, and coordinated legal response among the various actors in the musical value chain.

## Part I: The Digital Taxable Event

### Genesis and Evolution of Fraud (2017-2024)

The typical conduct did not arise from total automation, but from an opportunistic progression. In the initial stages, Smith used a limited catalog of music he owned, but soon realized that the volume of his work was insufficient to generate significant income without triggering fraud detection system alerts. After unsuccessfully attempting to sell his traffic generation capacity as a service to other musicians, Smith opted for vertical integration of the deception.

The turning point occurred in 2018, when Smith began collaborating with the executive director of an AI-based music company and a music promoter. This alliance enabled overcoming the content scarcity barrier.[^6] The evolution was meteoric: by June 2019, Smith informed his accomplices that they had reached 88 million total streams, generating monthly income of approximately $110,000. By 2024, Smith himself boasted in private communications having generated more than 4 billion streams since the inception of his operations.

### The Technical Triad: Massive Production, Bot Farms, and Algorithmic Evasion

The fraud's effectiveness rested on an operational loop composed of three interdependent technological pillars. First, Smith uploaded hundreds of thousands of algorithmically created tracks. These pieces were characterized by their own providers not as music, but as *instant music*.[^7] To give this content an appearance of legitimacy against platform algorithms, Smith assigned randomly generated song and artist names: Calliope Bloom, Calm Baseball, Zygophyte.

Second, the listener army consisted of thousands of automated accounts. At certain periods, Smith managed up to 10,000 active streaming accounts simultaneously.[^8] To optimize operational costs, the defendant purchased thousands of false email accounts in bulk and hired overseas personnel to perform the manual account registration work. Additionally, Smith used cloud computing services to execute multiple virtual machines that played music continuously through macros.

Third, aware that unusual activity spikes on a single work would raise suspicions, Smith distributed the billions of streams across a vast catalog of thousands of songs. Under this strategy, each song received only a few thousand listens, making the fraud statistically invisible[^9] to basic audits. Complementarily, he used virtual private networks (VPNs) to hide that all traffic originated from his residence in North Carolina, simulating a global and dispersed audience.

### The Scale of Harm: Economic Magnitude and Systemic Damage

The economic magnitude of the Smith scheme challenges prior precedents in the digital industry. By configuring 52 cloud service accounts, each with 20 bots, Smith generated approximately 661,440 daily streams. At an estimated royalty rate of half a cent per stream, daily profit amounted to $3,307.20, projecting annual income exceeding $1.2 million.

It is essential to underline that these funds were not generated by the platform, but diverted from a common royalty pool. Every dollar captured by Smith's scheme was directly withdrawn from the fund that legally belonged to artists, songwriters, and legitimate rights holders. As part of his guilty plea, Smith has agreed to the forfeiture of $8,091,843.64, a figure representing the illicit proceeds traceable by federal authorities.[^10]

## Part II: Typical Characterization and Criminal Typification

### Federal Charges and Legal Basis

The punitive framework applied to Smith rests on three fundamental axes codified in Title 18 of the United States Code. The first charge, conspiracy to commit wire fraud under 18 U.S.C. § 1349,[^11] imputes to Smith the voluntary agreement with other individuals to execute a scheme intended to defraud and obtain money through false representations and promises. The essence of the charge lies in the use of wire communications in interstate and foreign commerce, specifically emails and data transfers over the internet.

The second charge, wire fraud under 18 U.S.C. § 1343,[^12] derives from the material execution of the scheme. Each electronic transmission intended to artificially inflate streaming data to divert royalty payments constitutes a violation of this statute. The court emphasized that, although the technology was advanced, the criminal core was classic fraud: obtaining property through deception.

The third charge, conspiracy to commit money laundering under 18 U.S.C. § 1956(h),[^13] imputes to Smith the use of fraud proceeds to finance and promote the continuation of the criminal activity itself. Specifically, Smith transferred approximately $1.3 million in fraudulently obtained royalties to accounts at a U.S. financial institution under the name SMH Entertainment, funds that were subsequently used to acquire corporate debit cards for payment of new bot accounts.

### Falsity as a Constitutive Element of Fraud

The typification in this case is nourished by the systematic falsity proffered by Smith before three key actors: streaming platforms, distribution companies, and performance rights organizations. The deception was not merely omissive, but involved an affirmative declaration of compliance with regulations that Smith was actively subverting.[^14]

Streaming platforms explicitly prohibit the manipulation of streams through bots or automated processes in their user guidelines. Smith, by accepting these terms to distribute his music, committed fraud by simulating that the users of his bot accounts were actual human consumers with autonomous choice.[^15] Likewise, before distribution companies, Smith warranted in writing that he would not participate in illegal streaming boosting or *juicing* activities.

The gravity of the conduct intensifies in his communications with the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC). When the MLC suspended payments in 2023 due to suspected fraud, Smith and his representatives categorically denied any manipulation, falsely asserting that Smith was a human author capable of composing music rapidly and that his works were not computer-generated.[^16] This fictional humanization of the creative and consumption process is the core element of the typical deception.

### The Guilty Plea as a Federal Precedent

On March 19, 2026, Michael Smith pleaded guilty to one count of conspiracy to commit wire fraud before Judge John G. Koeltl of the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York. This procedural act marks the end of the presumption of innocence in what authorities have characterized as the first criminal case of its kind involving AI-assisted stream manipulation.[^17]

The guilty plea carries severe financial and criminal consequences. Smith has agreed to the forfeiture of more than $8 million, representing the gross proceeds derived from the scheme. The charge to which he pleaded guilty carries a maximum sentence of five years' imprisonment, with the final sentencing to be determined later. This precedent sends an unequivocal signal to the industry: the use of cutting-edge technologies to economically exploit intellectual property systems does not exempt one from criminal liability under wire fraud statutes.

## Part III: The Crisis of the Pro Rata Model

### Systemic Vulnerabilities of the GLOBALPROP Model

The Michael Smith case is not merely the chronicle of individual cleverness, but the empirical demonstration of the collapse of an economic distribution model that, under an appearance of mathematical neutrality, incentivizes and rewards algorithmic manipulation. The predominant model, technically termed GLOBALPROP or the *pro rata* rule,[^18] constitutes the de facto standard in major current streaming platforms. The architecture of this system is, ultimately, the original sin that enabled the diversion of millions of dollars.

Under the GLOBALPROP model, the platform consolidates all net income derived from subscriptions into a common royalty pool or *pool*. Distribution to rights holders is performed strictly proportionally to the market share each artist holds over the absolute total of streams on the platform. Mathematically, payment to an artist is defined by the quotient between their individual streams and the aggregate sum of all streams of all users, multiplied by the total income pool. This structure generates a critical legal and economic consequence: users who consume music massively—regardless of whether they are humans or automated processes—exercise disproportionate influence on the final destination of the royalty pool.

### Computational Intractability as an Evidentiary Barrier

One of the most perturbing revelations from the GLOBALPROP analysis is that detecting suspicious benefits under this rule is not merely a matter of vigilance, but a problem of computational intractability.[^19] Academic research has demonstrated that identifying the set of artists that maximize potentially suspicious benefits within a pro rata ecosystem is an NP-Hard class problem,[^20] meaning its computational resolution grows exponentially with the data set size.

This technical complexity means that, as long as platforms use the pro rata model, fraudsters will always have a structural advantage. By distributing fraudulent traffic across thousands of different songs and artists, the scheme becomes statistically invisible to traditional audit systems, which typically search for activity spikes on individual works, not rent-capture patterns in massive and dispersed catalogs.

## Part IV: Intellectual Property, AI, and the Denaturation of the Work

### Instant Music: A Concept Alien to Copyright Law

Classical copyright doctrine requires that, for a creation to be eligible for protection, there must exist an imprint of the author's personality or, at least, a minimum of human creativity. In the Smith scheme, this premise was replaced by algorithmic industrialization. In private communications, Smith's collaborators were explicit in defining the produced material: "Keep in mind what we are doing musically here... this is not 'music,' it is 'instant music.'"

This characterization is not merely semantic; it describes a denaturation of the creative process where AI autonomy displaces human intervention toward the periphery of the system. While the legal framework seeks to reward effort and originality, the Smith scheme saturated platforms with hundreds of thousands of tracks whose sole function was to serve as a vehicle for royalty capture. A fundamental jurisprudential tension is raised here: can an audio file generated without aesthetic purpose, intended exclusively to deceive a payment algorithm, be considered a work?

### False Metadata and Artistic Identity Impersonation

To facilitate the fraud, Smith orchestrated a sophisticated architecture of false metadata intended to simulate nonexistent artistic diversity. The assignment of randomly generated artist and song names was intended to make the content appear created by real and independent artists in the eyes of platform auditors. Paradigmatic examples include Calliope Bloom, Calm Baseball, or Zygophyte—names corresponding to no identifiable human artist.

This fraudulent use of metadata constitutes a violation of the intellectual property system's integrity. Smith even went so far as to sign service agreements warranting that he owned the copyrights to these works, while using overseas personnel to manually register bot accounts. The gravity of the deception reached its zenith before the Mechanical Licensing Collective, where Smith's representatives falsely asserted that the works were not computer-generated and that the defendant was a human author capable of composing with exceptional rapidity. This fictional humanization of the algorithm is a core element of the ideational falsity that enabled the illicit capture of rents.

### Erosion of the Royalty Pool and Harm to Legitimate Artists

The economic impact of this phenomenon is a zero-sum game. Streaming platforms operate with a finite common royalty pool. Therefore, each stream of instant music generated by Smith's scheme was not a neutral event, but an act of asset dilution that directly withdrew money from artists, songwriters, and legitimate rights holders whose works were actually listened to by real human beings.

The industry estimates that AI-assisted streaming fraud diverts hundreds of millions of dollars annually from the music ecosystem. In the Smith case, the diversion magnitude exceeded ten million dollars, representing a transfer of wealth from genuine human talent to criminal automation infrastructure. This erosion of the royalty pool not only harms the livelihoods of independent artists but also distorts consumption metrics that record labels and promoters use to identify and develop new talent, creating an unfair competitive environment where Smith's algorithm drowned out legitimate human creative work.

## Part V: The Challenge of Digital Evidence and Data Forensics

The prosecution of fraud in the streaming ecosystem presents a highly complex technical evidentiary crossroads. While the Michael Smith scheme culminated in a guilty plea, the architecture of the deception reveals current limitations of data forensics to uniquely identify, at an industrial scale, both the artificial origin of content and the inauthentic nature of its consumption.

Digital evidence in this case bifurcates into two fronts: detection of AI-generated audio signal and behavioral analysis of bot networks. Detectors based on convolutional neural networks (CNN) have achieved accuracies exceeding 99.8 percent in controlled environments through spectral representation analysis of audio. However, their effectiveness in a real forensic environment is limited, particularly against AI models not seen during their training or against basic audio manipulations such as pitch shifting, time stretching, or re-encoding in different formats.

Given the difficulty of proving that the music is false, the industry has shifted its focus toward proving that the listener is false. Platforms employ proprietary models analyzing metadata such as IP location, listening patterns, and user interface interaction to distinguish humans from automated scripts. In the Smith's case, the use of VPNs to hide that traffic originated from a single location in North Carolina and the programming of macros to simulate human listening behaviors were the central elements the FBI had to dismantle.

However, profit forensics under the pro rata distribution model faces an insurmountable mathematical barrier: computational intractability. This implies that, in a catalog of millions of songs, identifying distributed fraud is computationally unmanageable through standard audits. Digital forensics finds itself in an arms race where AI not only facilitates crime but also obscures the evidentiary trail by simulating insurmountable statistical complexity.

## Part VI: Industry Response and Compliance Policies

### The Music Fights Fraud Alliance: Transnational Collaboration

One of the most significant milestones in defense of the digital ecosystem was the creation, in June 2023, of the Music Fights Fraud Alliance (MFFA). This nonprofit organization represents a transnational collaborative effort between distributors and digital service providers (DSPs) such as Spotify and Amazon Music.

The MFFA's mission transcends mere reporting; it focuses on creating a common front to detect, prevent, and eliminate streaming fraud through data exchange. A critical component of this strategy is its collaboration with the National Cyber-Forensics and Training Alliance (NCFTA), enabling members to access a shared database of identified fraud markers. This cooperative approach seeks to neutralize the advantage of fraudsters who, like Smith, operate across multiple platforms to dilute their criminal trail.

### Payment Retention Protocols and Financial Guarantees

Platforms have tightened their terms and conditions to include specific clauses on *Artificial Streaming*. The Smith case demonstrated that early detection is useless if not accompanied by mechanisms for immediate economic sanctions. Distribution companies like RouteNote now explicitly establish in their contracts that, upon reasonable suspicion of manipulation, the company has discretionary authority to retain, suspend, or permanently confiscate royalties attributable to such activities.

This payment withholding protocol was effectively applied in the case at hand. In 2023, the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) halted payments to Smith after identifying massive irregularities in his metrics. Although Smith attempted to appeal this decision through false declarations about the human authorship of his works, the MLC maintained the suspension based on its legal authority to ensure that only legitimate streams are eligible for royalty collection. This capacity of collective management organizations to act as last-resort guarantors is fundamental to preventing the emptying of the common pool before a criminal judgment is rendered.

### Verified Metadata and Unique Identifiers

Finally, compliance in the AI era demands greater precision in work traceability. The requirement for unique and verified identifiers, such as IPI (*Interested Parties Information*) for authors and ISNI (*International Standard Name Identifier*) for artists, has become an indispensable compliance requirement.

The use of false or randomly generated metadata by Smith was one of the red flags that enabled investigators to track the inauthenticity of his catalog. The industry is moving toward a standardization where metadata accuracy at the time of work upload is not merely an administrative matter, but a technical barrier against the injection of synthetic content intended for fraud.

## Part VII: Proposals de Lege Ferenda

The resolution of the Michael Smith case through criminal prosecution does not exhaust the need for a structural legal response. The exploited vulnerability does not reside in an accidental technical failure, but in the very conception of the economic distribution model of streaming platform income. Therefore, it is imperative to propose reforms that transition from reactive surveillance toward inherently fraud-resistant distribution mechanisms.

### From the Pro Rata Model to the User-Centric Model

As analyzed in previous sections, the current model (GLOBALPROP) fails to satisfy the axioms of fraud resistance and bribery resistance. By allowing individual users to exercise unlimited influence over the common royalty pool, the system rewards artificial hyperactivity.

The first reform proposal consists of the generalized adoption of the USERPROP (*user-centric*) model. Under this scheme, a user's subscription is distributed exclusively among the artists that user has listened to during the billing period. Legally, this transforms the consumption event: Smith's bot could no longer withdraw funds from artists he did not listen to. Each of his 10,000 bot accounts would have a maximum contribution ceiling equivalent to the net value of its subscription. Under this model, fraud becomes economically unviable, since the cost of maintaining the bot account would always equal or exceed the maximum royalty that bot can generate.

### SCALEDUSERPROP: A Robust Compromise Mechanism

Technical doctrine warns that pure USERPROP may not be sufficiently just in not rewarding the intensity of real fans' engagement. To reconcile fraud resistance with distributive fairness, the SCALEDUSERPROP mechanism proposal emerges.[^21]

This innovative model functions through a scaling constant that adjusts distribution according to the interaction level. Essentially, the system acts as GLOBALPROP (the current model) for users with normal listening patterns, but automatically limits the influence of users whose activity significantly exceeds the average, like Smith's bots that played music 24 hours a day. The system takes a larger commission from very low-consumption users to protect the common pool, eliminating the marginal benefit that motivates the injection of false accounts. Experiments with real data demonstrate that SCALEDUSERPROP is the most robust mechanism for reducing per-stream payment disparity and disincentivizing massive manipulation.

### Regulatory Framework for AI Training and Transparency

The dimension of synthetic content in Smith's fraud also demands, moreover, a reform of the licensing regime. Two complementary regulatory action paths are proposed.

First, the creation of a statutory licensing framework for *Machine Learning*. Given the technical and administrative impossibility of negotiating individual licenses for the billions of data points needed to train an AI, it is urgent to establish a statutory license that reduces transaction costs and ensures that artists are compensated for the use of their work as training data, closing the legal vacuum that fraudsters like Smith exploited.

Second, it is necessary to legally mandate transparency in training data sets and the obligation to inform consumers when content is AI-generated.[^22] The use of verified metadata (IPI, ISNI) must cease to be an industry recommendation to become a legal requirement for royalty collection eligibility, making the creation of fictional artistic identities more difficult.

## Conclusion: Toward a Jurisprudence of Authenticity

The resolution of the criminal prosecution against Michael Smith in the Southern District of New York should not be interpreted as the closing of an isolated episode of digital criminality, but as the opening of an unavoidable debate about the integrity of the cultural market in the age of silicon. Through this analysis, we have demonstrated that Smith's scheme was the product not of a fortuitous technical failure, but the logical consequence of an economic distribution model that rewards scale over essence.

The Smith case has served to reveal the fragility of metrics as a source of legal and economic value. Between 2017 and 2024, the defendant demonstrated that, under the global proportionality rule, digital consumption can be totally simulated without traditional audit systems achieving automatic detection, given that identifying suspicious benefits is, mathematically, a problem of computational intractability.

The criminal typification applied—specifically, conspiracy to commit wire fraud—has fulfilled a necessary reactive function, establishing that the use of cutting-edge technologies does not constitute a letter of marque for contractual deception. However, the forfeiture of more than $8 million is a pecuniary reparation that does not heal the systemic wound: the dilution of the common royalty pool that legitimately belonged to thousands of human artists whose works were drowned out by algorithmic noise.

Ultimately, Copyright was born to protect the imprint of human personality. The Michael Smith scheme put this fundamental premise to the test by flooding the ecosystem with instant music consumed by listeners that do not exist. If we allow the algorithm to substitute human preference as the engine of artistic remuneration, we risk converting culture into a mere financial arbitrage exercise.

Future jurisprudence must be, above all, a jurisprudence of authenticity. The industry's success in coming years will depend not only on the power of its AI systems, but on its capacity to reaffirm that music remains an art, not an algorithm for illicitly printing money. The Michael Smith case is the definitive warning: in the kingdom of data, truth is not optional; it is the only viable assumption for the survival of genuine human talent.
