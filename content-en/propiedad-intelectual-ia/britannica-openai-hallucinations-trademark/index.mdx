# Britannica v. OpenAI on Hallucinations: The Battle for Verified Truth

## When the System Invents, the Brand Pays

On March 13, 2026, Encyclopædia Britannica and Merriam-Webster sued OpenAI for something no litigant had ever pleaded before: it was not enough that ChatGPT replicated their articles. What truly mattered to the courts was that it *invented information under the Britannica brand*.

Two months ago, a user asked ChatGPT about a historical fact. The AI responded confidently. It cited Britannica. Except Britannica never published that. The model had hallucinated. And the hallucination bore the trademark of a 250-year-old institution dedicated, precisely, to verifiable accuracy.

This is not a technical detail. It is the fracture that changes the entire litigation.

## The Triple Trap: Copyright, RAG, and Trademark

For years, OpenAI defended itself by claiming that training models on public texts was "fair use" under U.S. copyright doctrine. Courts in the Southern District of New York began looking more closely. In October 2025, Judge Sidney H. Stein denied OpenAI's motion to dismiss, arguing that ChatGPT's outputs—the detailed summaries, curated lists, editorial selection patterns—captured protectable creative elements, not just raw facts.

But Britannica went further. It documented that OpenAI not only ingested its 100,000 online articles without consent. It also documented that ChatGPT's Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system actively accesses Britannica's servers in real-time whenever a user submits a query. This is not an infringement that occurred in the past during training. It is ongoing infringement, query by query, user by user, without compensation or attribution.

And then there is what no OpenAI lawyer anticipated: Britannica invoked the Lanham Act trademark law. Not merely to claim copyright on its articles. To claim that its brand—a symbol of accuracy—was being degraded when ChatGPT presented false information under its name.

## The Market Exists and OpenAI Knows It

This is where OpenAI's "transformative fair use" argument completely collapses.

The plaintiffs presented documentation showing that OpenAI signed paid licensing agreements with News Corp, Associated Press, and Axel Springer. In other words: OpenAI acknowledges that a content market exists, that this market is commercially viable, and that it is willing to pay for verified quality content when legal claims demand it.

So why did it not pay Britannica? According to the lawsuit, because ChatGPT's profit margins would be lower if it had to compensate its original sources. OpenAI chose not to compensate. Not because it was impossible. Because it was more profitable not to do so.

The fourth factor in the fair use analysis—the effect on the potential market of the copyrighted work—is almost always decisive in these cases. And here, the evidence is overwhelming: OpenAI actively cannibalized traffic that traditionally would have gone to Britannica, preventing its users from having to "dive through links" to reach the original source. It transformed Google's neutral search engine into a direct competitor offering final answers without intermediaries.

## The Question That Cannot Be Avoided

What strikes me most about this litigation is not the potential magnitude of damages—it could reach $10 billion in the most severe scenario if the court classifies each article as a separate work eligible for punitive damages.

It is the question it forces: **Who is responsible for truth in the age of artificial intelligence?**

Britannica invests human resources—hundreds of researchers, editors, subject-matter experts—to verify information. This investment in rigor is what makes Britannica a name synonymous with reliability. When ChatGPT presents "information from Britannica" that Britannica never wrote, it not only steals its words. It destroys the economic value of its fundamental promise: verified accuracy.

In other words: if Britannica cannot capture the economic value of its investment in verification, it loses incentives to verify. And if Britannica disappears, ChatGPT loses the high-quality data source on which it depends to be useful.

Britannica argues this is precisely what is happening. A downward spiral where OpenAI "starves" its sources to maximize margins, knowing that in the long term it will be sabotaging its own product.

## The RAG System: Real-Time Infringement

One of the most powerful findings in the lawsuit is technical but legally devastating: OpenAI eliminated the "Books1" and "Books2" datasets—pirated compilations from Library Genesis containing hundreds of thousands of stolen books—in the summer of 2022, just before legal claims intensified.

A magistrate of the federal court determined that this elimination was not "technical maintenance" but deliberate destruction of evidence. And she compelled OpenAI to produce all internal communications regarding that decision.

This is the door through which "willfulness" enters the legal analysis. Because *if* OpenAI acted with "willfulness" or "reckless disregard" for intellectual property rights, statutory damages multiply exponentially. The court can move from $750-$30,000 per work to $30,000-$150,000 per work.

With 100,000 Britannica articles, that changes everything.

## Actionable Conclusions

- **Hallucinations are trademark infringement.** Not just copyright infringement. Britannica is demonstrating that attributing false content to a registered trademark is actionable under the Lanham Act, opening a line of attack other plaintiffs are already copying.

- **RAG is ongoing infringement, not historical.** While initial training could theoretically be defended as transformative, active real-time access to servers to extract fresh content is clear infringement. And it is what makes ChatGPT useful.

- **The existing licensing market destroys the fair use defense.** OpenAI negotiated paid agreements with other publishers. It cannot simultaneously argue that no market exists for verified content.

- **Willfulness multiplies damages exponentially.** Dataset elimination and procedural contradictions open the door to punitive damages that could reach $10 billion.

- **This does not end with copyright.** The true significance of this litigation is the reconfiguration of responsibilities for truth in algorithmic systems. If Britannica wins, every hallucination becomes potentially costly. If OpenAI wins, the era of verified information may be coming to an end.
