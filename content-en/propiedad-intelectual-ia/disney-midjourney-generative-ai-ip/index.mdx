On June 11, 2025, three of the largest Hollywood studios—Disney, Universal, and Warner Bros.—filed a joint lawsuit against Midjourney. It was not merely another case questioning whether training models on copyrighted works is lawful. The focus was far more uncomfortable for the tech companies: the tool generated, upon request, images of Darth Vader, Spider-Man, the Minions, and Shrek with a precision the plaintiffs characterized as a "virtual vending machine" of infringement. Six months later, Disney announced a $1 billion investment in OpenAI, the creator of Sora and ChatGPT. What happened in between? The answer encapsulates the evolution of the legal and commercial conflict between the entertainment industry and generative artificial intelligence throughout 2025, a year that has redefined the rules of the game.

## The Shift in Focus: From Input to Output

During 2023 and 2024, litigation centered on the training phase: is scanning millions of books and images to create statistical models fair use? The June 2025 rulings in *Bartz v. Anthropic* and *Kadrey v. Meta* brought some relief for tech companies. Judge Alsup, in *Bartz*, characterized training as "spectacularly transformative," equating it to human learning. However, he added a crucial caveat: if data comes from "shadow libraries" (pirated sources), storing those copies is an independent infringement not protected by fair use.

Nevertheless, the studios' lawsuit against Midjourney shifted the target. It was no longer about how the model was trained, but about what it is capable of generating. The plaintiffs submitted expert evidence showing that the AI can regurgitate nearly identical frames and iconic characters with simple prompts. The legal debate thus moved to "design infringement": when a system is designed to produce results that directly compete with the original works, does the same transformative defense still apply?

## The Landmark Precedents: Bartz and Kadrey

Both rulings from the Northern District of California, both from June 2025, consolidated a dual doctrine. On one hand, training itself may be fair use if data is obtained lawfully. On the other hand, the illegal origin of data—as in the Books3 case—bars that defense. In *Kadrey*, Judge Chhabria dismissed the plaintiffs' claims for lack of market harm evidence, but left the door open for rights holders to establish specific injury.

This jurisprudence incentivized the studios to meticulously document how generative AI erodes the value of their franchises. Alleging mass infringement is insufficient; they must demonstrate that the public prefers synthetic content over the original, or that the saturation of "AI slop" degrades the brand. And that is precisely what Disney, Universal, and Warner attempted to prove in their lawsuit against Midjourney.

## The Market Dilution Theory and the Ghost of "AI Slop"

In May 2025, the U.S. Copyright Office published the third part of its report on AI and intellectual property. It introduced a concept that has sparked intense debate: "market dilution." According to the Office, harm is not limited to literal copying; AI's capacity to flood the market with works of the same style or recognizable characters may undermine the incentive to create new original works. This is an expansive view of the fourth fair use factor (the effect on the potential market) that some consider necessary and others view as a threat to creative freedom.

Critics argue that making "dilution" an autonomous criterion could lead to a form of stylistic monopoly, protecting not only specific works but also ideas and styles—something copyright law has traditionally rejected. However, for the studios, this theory is the key that opens the door to claims beyond substantial similarity.

## The Strategic Pivot: When Litigation Is Not Enough, Invest

In late 2025, the litigation path took an unexpected turn. On December 11, Disney announced a $1 billion investment in OpenAI. The deal, structured through stock warrants and three-year licenses, allows Sora to generate videos featuring more than 200 specific Disney characters under strict conditions: exclusion of real talent voices and faces, and an explicit prohibition on using that material to train OpenAI's general model.

This move exemplifies "private ordering": given the slowness of jurisprudence and legislative uncertainty, major rights holders choose to sit on the boards of the tech companies themselves. Intellectual property ceases to be merely an asset to protect and becomes investment capital. And the question no one can avoid is: can a studio sue for infringement while becoming a shareholder of the company allegedly infringing? Is offensive litigation compatible with participation in the technology's profits?

> What is clear is that the future of the relationship between Hollywood and generative AI will not be decided solely in the courts. Private contracts, conditional licenses, and equity stakes are creating a parallel market architecture that, for better or worse, excludes independent creators.

## Key Conclusions

- **The conflict has shifted from input (training) to output (generation)**: the lawsuit against Midjourney focuses on AI's capacity to produce iconic characters, not on how it was trained.
- **2025 jurisprudence consolidates fair use for lawful training, but rejects "shadow libraries"**: *Bartz* and *Kadrey* establish the foundations of a dual doctrine.
- **The market dilution theory gains traction**: the May 2025 Copyright Office report introduces a new harm standard that could reshape the fourth fair use factor.
- **Private ordering emerges as a parallel path**: the Disney-OpenAI $1 billion deal with warrants and restricted licenses points toward contractual governance, accessible only to major players.
- **The fundamental debate persists**: how to balance technological innovation with protection of the human creative incentive without falling into stylistic monopolies?
