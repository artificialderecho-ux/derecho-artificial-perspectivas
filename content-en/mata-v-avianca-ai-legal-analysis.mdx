## CHAPTER I: INTRODUCTION AND PURPOSE OF THE REPORT

### 1.1. Context and Justification: Accelerated Evolution of LLMs in Legal Practice

The global legal environment is experiencing an unprecedented technological transformation. Large Language Models (LLMs), particularly those oriented toward legal content generation tasks, represent tools that promise research automation, assisted drafting, and document analysis at scale. However, this promise is accompanied by technical and ethical risks of considerable magnitude.

Formal Opinion 512 of the American Bar Association (ABA), issued on July 29, 2024, establishes a regulatory milestone as the **"first formal ethical guidance"** from a U.S. institution on the use of generative AI tools in legal practice. This opinion places technological competence and professional supervision as fundamental rights for attorney due diligence.¹

Parallelly, technical frameworks such as the **ISO/IEC 42001** standard (addressed in the SGS Technical White Paper on AI Governance in the legal industry) and the **NIST Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework** (AI RMF 1.0, July 2024 version) have established methodologies for risk classification, technical mitigation, and lifecycle governance of AI systems.

### 1.2. The Critical Incident: Executive Summary of the Controversy at the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York

On **June 22, 2023**, Judge P. Kevin Castel of the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York (SDNY) issued an Opinion and Sanctions Order in the case **Mata v. Avianca, Inc.** (678 F.Sup p.3d 443).² This case represents the **first judicial precedent of international magnitude** where a U.S. federal court expressly sanctions attorneys for technical malpractice derived from irresponsible use of ChatGPT as a legal research tool.

The plaintiff attorneys used ChatGPT to identify precedents on statutes of limitations under the Montreal Convention. The generative model produced citations to non-existent cases, including **"Varghese v. China Southern Airlines,"** which was incorporated into the opposition brief as binding authority. The court not only rejected the false citations but initiated sanctions proceedings under Rule 11 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure (FRCP Rule 11), resulting in fines and public rebukes.³

### 1.3. Report Objectives: Guide on Technical, Legal, and Ethical Impact

This report pursues three integrated objectives:

**(i) Technical Analysis:** Explain the underlying mechanisms of hallucination in LLMs, distinguishing between the **"creative oracle"** (probabilistic generation) and the **"expert archivist"** (verified data retrieval).

**(ii) Legal Analysis:** Evaluate professional responsibility under ethical frameworks (ABA Formal Opinion 512, Spanish CGAE standards), procedural norms (FRCP Rule 11), and emerging regulatory frameworks (EU AI Act, Spanish transparency norms).

**(iii) Governance Analysis:** Propose technical mitigation mechanisms (RAG architecture, hierarchical AI supervision) and institutional mechanisms (tool certification, permanent auditing) to ensure AI in law acts as an **"amplifier of human judgment"** rather than a substitute for critical thinking.

### 1.4. Methodology: Analysis of Specialized Doctrine, Risk Management Frameworks, and Comparative Jurisprudence

The methodology combines:

- **Comparative doctrinal analysis:** Formal opinions from the ABA, guidance from data protection authorities (EDPB, AEPD), and technical analysis from specialists (Dantart Report, CIO LittleJohn, on AI hallucinations in legal contexts).

- **Governance framework evaluation:** International standards (ISO/IEC 42001, NIST AI RMF 1.0, Generative AI Profile) and emerging regulatory norms (EU AI Act effective August 2025; Spanish norms from the CGAE-ICAV White Paper).

- **Jurisprudential analysis:** Study of the Mata v. Avianca judgment (SDNY, June 22, 2023) as precedent and identification of analogous risks in Spanish and European contexts.

## CHAPTER II: TECHNICAL ARCHITECTURE AND THE HALLUCINATION PHENOMENON

### 2.1. Generative AI vs. Consultive AI: The "Creative Oracle" vs. "Expert Archivist" Paradigm

The fundamental distinction in AI systems applied to law lies in their functional architecture:

#### **CREATIVE ORACLE (Pure Generative AI)**

System that generates text probabilistically based on statistical patterns from training. Its objective is to maximize fluency and linguistic coherence. It has no integrated fact-verification mechanism. Example: ChatGPT used without restrictions for legal research.

**Characteristics:**
- (i) Autonomous generation without anchoring to verified sources
- (ii) Tendency to "hallucinate" when lacking training data
- (iii) Apparent confidence in incorrect assertions
- (iv) Extreme risk in high-stakes applications

#### **EXPERT ARCHIVIST (Verified Consultive AI)**

System that retrieves information from certified legal databases and presents it through structured reasoning. Operates under the RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) paradigm, where each response is anchored to verifiable sources. Example: Legal AI with controlled access to normalized legal databases.

**Characteristics:**
- (i) Mandatory source anchoring
- (ii) Pinpoint citation capability
- (iii) Transparency about knowledge limits
- (iv) Significantly reduced risk through technical architecture

The Mata v. Avianca case exemplifies precisely the collapse of transitioning from "expert archivist" (searching in real legal databases) to "creative oracle" (trusting in model generation).

### 2.2. LLM Mechanisms: Probabilistic Functioning and Next-Word Prediction vs. Logical Comprehension

LLMs such as GPT-3, GPT-4, and their variants operate through a **transformer** architecture that performs **sequential token prediction** (words or word fragments). In each iteration, the model calculates a probability distribution over the subsequent vocabulary based on preceding tokens and weights learned during training.

#### **UNDERLYING MECHANISM:**

1. **Tokenization:** Input text is divided into tokens
2. **Embedding:** Each token is represented as a high-dimensional dense vector
3. **Multi-Attention:** The model calculates probabilistic relationships between tokens
4. **Feed-Forward:** Transformation layers are applied mapping to latent space
5. **Next Token Prediction:** Probability distribution over possible tokens is generated
6. **Sampling:** Next token is selected according to strategy (greedy, top-k, etc.)
7. **Iteration:** Repeats until complete sequence is generated

#### **CRITICAL IMPLICATION:**

This mechanism is **statistical, not symbolic**. The model does not "comprehend" formal logic or maintain explicit mapping to real-world referents. It does not distinguish between:
- (i) text that appears frequently in training data
- (ii) generated text that is coherent but false
- (iii) text that is factually correct

According to **Alex Dantart's Technical Report** (CIO LittleJohn, published on arXiv):

> "The challenge of truthfulness in legal AI is not a problem of 'insufficient training' but a fundamental architectural characteristic of generative models that prioritize fluency over factuality."

This tension is **irreducible** in pure generative architectures.

### 2.3. Taxonomy of Legal Hallucinations

A **"hallucination"** in the context of LLM is content generation that is internally coherent but factually incorrect, with apparent confidence in its truthfulness. In legal context, hallucinations can be classified into categories:

#### 2.3.1. AUTHORITY FABRICATION (Non-Existent Cases and Citations)

**Definition:** Generation of references to cases, laws, or rulings that do not exist in verifiable public records.

**Paradigmatic example:** "Varghese v. China Southern Airlines" (Mata v. Avianca case). The model generated party names, case number, and applicable Montreal Convention jurisprudence, with syntax identical to real cases. The attorney, trusting the surface coherence, cited the case not as hypothetical but as binding authority.

**Risk in legal context:** 🔴 **CRITICAL**. A false citation incorporated into a pleading can:
- (i) vitiate the proceeding
- (ii) constitute a serious breach of the duty of candor to the court
- (iii) expose the attorney to sanctions under FRCP Rule 11 (fine, suspension, disbarment)

#### 2.3.2. MISGROUNDING OR ERRONEOUS FOUNDATION OF REAL SOURCES

**Definition:** Attribution of incorrect doctrine, reasoning, or legal conclusion to a source that exists and is verifiable, but that says something different.

**Example:** Model citing "Montreal Convention, Article 35" as the source of the statute of limitations rule, when Article 35 actually deals with air carrier liability (related topic but different regulation).

**Risk:** 🟠 **HIGH**. The reader verifying the primary source will find the cited article, but the extracted doctrine will be distorted. This is harder to detect than pure fabrication because it has "poison in the form of medicine."

#### 2.3.3. JURISDICTIONAL AND TEMPORAL APPLICATION ERRORS

**Definition:** Attribution of incorrect validity, jurisdiction, or applicability to norms or precedents.

**Example:** Application of U.S. state court jurisprudence to federal context; or citation of a judgment modified by subsequent jurisprudence without reference to reversal.

**Risk:** 🟠 **MEDIUM-HIGH**. Especially serious in research on statutes of limitations (critical temporal element) or applicable jurisdiction (critical territorial element).

### 2.4. Technical Causes: Training Data Limitations and Irreducible Tension

The technical causes of hallucination in legal LLMs are multiple and, to a large extent, **structural**:

#### **CAUSE 1: Training Data Cutoff Date**

LLMs are trained with data corpora with fixed cutoff dates (example: GPT-4 trained through April 2024). Cases after that date are unknown. When asked about recent precedent, the model "extrapolates" based on syntactic patterns, generating plausible but fictional cases.

**Implication:** In rapidly evolving jurisprudence (especially AI, data protection), the temporal cutoff creates a particularly dense hallucination zone.

#### **CAUSE 2: Unbalanced Weights in Training Data**

Legal documents included in training are not representative: there is greater density of reported decisions than unreported ones; greater density of high court jurisprudence than lower courts; geographic and linguistic distortion (fewer cases in non-English languages, even for systems trained in Spanish).

**Implication:** Model develops distorted statistics about what a "real" legal case looks like.

#### **CAUSE 3: Incompleteness of Public Legal Databases**

Even if the model had access to all court decisions, many (especially lower court decisions, administrative rulings, arbitral awards) are not publicly indexed. The model cannot hallucinate about data completely absent from its training.

**Implication:** For queries about specific jurisprudence or unreported decisions, hallucination rate is close to 100%.

#### **CAUSE 4: Architectural Incompatibility Between Generation and Factual Accuracy**

The sequential prediction mechanism that makes LLMs efficient for language generation is exactly what prevents guaranteed factual accuracy. A system that maximizes the probability of the next token does not maximize the probability that the complete sentence is true.

**Implication:** There is no "better training" that resolves this. The transformer architecture has fundamental limits for integrated fact-checking.

#### **CAUSE 5: Lack of Abstention Mechanism**

Standard LLMs generate a response for every input. They have no architectural abstention mechanism ("I don't know"). When lacking knowledge, the model confabulates with confidence instead of expressing uncertainty.

**Implication:** Systematic bias toward over-confidence. User experiences not uncertainty but false certainty.

## CHAPTER III: CHRONICLE AND ANALYSIS OF THE MATA V. AVIANCA CASE

### 3.1. Factual Background: Roberto Mata's Claim Against Avianca Airlines

Roberto Mata filed a claim against Avianca Airlines in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of New York (SDNY) after suffering injuries during a flight. The case involved damages claims under the **Convention for the Unification of Certain Rules Relating to International Air Carriage**, commonly known as the **Montreal Convention** (1999).

The Montreal Convention establishes a special regime of liability and statutes of limitations for air carriers. The **statute of limitations is a critical temporal element**: the claim must be filed within specific deadlines or it expires.

### 3.2. The Procedural Error: Use of ChatGPT to Research Precedents on Statutes of Limitations Under the Montreal Convention

The plaintiff's attorneys used **ChatGPT** (standard access, without restrictions) to research how U.S. courts had interpreted statutes of limitations under the Montreal Convention. This strategy reflected growing trust in generative AI tools for preliminary legal research.

According to Judge P. Kevin Castel's Opinion, the attorneys submitted queries to the model such as:

> "Is there jurisprudence supporting that the statute of limitations under the Montreal Convention begins to run from the injury, not from knowledge of the damage?"

ChatGPT responded with citations that appeared to be reported and authoritative. The citations had correct syntax, plausible docket numbers, included years and specific courts. Every surface element suggested:
- Case reported in U.S. judicial systems
- Jurisprudence on specific topic (Montreal Convention + statute of limitations)
- Binding authority for SDNY

### 3.3. The Error Cascade: Incorporation of "Varghese v. China Southern Airlines" and Other Fictitious Citations

The model generated multiple citations, among which:

#### **PRIMARY FICTITIOUS CITATION:**

```
Varghese v. China Southern Airlines Co., Ltd., 925 F.3d 1431 (9th Cir. 2019)
```

**Elements of fabrication:**
- **Plausible parties:** Varghese (common surname in personal injury claims); China Southern Airlines (real airline)
- **Coherent docket number:** 925 (volume) F.3d (Federal Reporter, 3rd Series) 1431 (page)
- **Correct circuit:** 9th Circuit (court that typically resolves Pacific air carrier cases)
- **Plausible year:** 2019 (within period where Montreal Convention was consolidated jurisprudence)

The attorneys incorporated this citation into an opposition brief filed with the court. They treated "Varghese" as authoritative and binding precedent, **not as a hypothetical example or general idea**.

#### **IMMEDIATE PROCEDURAL CONSEQUENCE:**

Judge Castel reviewed the citation as part of standard judicial brief review. When citing "Varghese," he requested the court verify the decision (standard judicial due diligence procedure). The verification revealed: **the case does not exist in any U.S. case database** (Westlaw, Lexis, Google Scholar, etc.).

### 3.4. Judicial Response: Judge P. Kevin Castel's Scrutiny and the Rule 11 Sanctions Hearing

Upon confirmation that "Varghese" was fictitious, Judge Castel initiated sanctions proceedings under **Rule 11 of the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure** (FRCP Rule 11).

#### **FRCP Rule 11 establishes:**

> "The allegations, denials, defenses, and other papers submitted to the court must be certified by an attorney. By presenting a paper, the attorney certifies that: (i) the paper is not presented for purposes of delay or prolongation; (ii) the legal arguments are supported by law (existing or reasonably proposed change in law); (iii) the facts have evidentiary support or will have such support after discovery."

#### **APPLICATION TO THE MATA CASE:**

The citations to "Varghese" and other fictitious cases violated requirement (ii): the legal arguments **were not supported by existing law** (because they cited cases that do not exist). The attorneys had no reasonable factual basis to believe the citations were accurate.

#### **COURT'S NEGLIGENCE FINDINGS:**

Judge Castel concluded that the attorneys committed serious procedural negligence by:

1. Not verifying citations through access to standard databases (Westlaw, Lexis)
2. Not maintaining supervision of the tool (ChatGPT) whose limits they knew or should have known
3. Not implementing due diligence protocol for generative AI output
4. Depositing trust in the AI model without independent validation

#### **SANCTIONS IMPOSED:**

According to Mata v. Avianca Opinion, SDNY June 22, 2023, p. 443-445:

- **Fine per attorney:** USD $5,000
- **Additional firm fine:** USD $10,000
- **Mandatory training order:** in legal research
- **Supervision order:** of future briefs
- **Public rebuke:** (reported judgment and commented in specialized media)

#### **PRECEDENT ESTABLISHED:**

This case establishes international precedent that:
- (i) Use of generative AI for legal research is permitted, but
- (ii) Professional responsibility of the attorney **DOES NOT DIMINISH** with delegation to AI
- (iii) Duty to verify exceeds duty of "reasonable trust" in the tool
- (iv) Negligence in AI supervision constitutes FRCP Rule 11 violation
- (v) Reputational damage is complementary to financial fines

## CHAPTER IV: IMPACTS AND RISKS: ETHICS AND REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

### 4.1. Responsibility and Professional Diligence

#### 4.1.1. Technological Competence under ABA Model Rule 1.1

**ABA Formal Opinion 512** (July 29, 2024) establishes that professional competence ("competence") now necessarily includes **technological competence** regarding AI tools that the attorney uses or considers using in their practice.

According to ABA Opinion 512, Section 1.1 of the Model Rules of Professional Conduct:

> "A lawyer shall be competent when the lawyer possesses the level of knowledge, skill, preparation, and diligence reasonably necessary to represent the client. Professional competence requires the attorney: (1) Technical competence in the substantive and procedural law applicable; and (2) Competence regarding tools and methods used in current practice, including technology."

#### **IMPLICATION FOR GENERATIVE AI:**

If an attorney uses ChatGPT, Claude, Bard, or similar for legal research, **they must possess:**
- Understanding of the tool's technical capabilities
- Understanding of architectural limitations (especially tendency toward hallucinations)
- Knowledge of hallucination rates documented in technical studies
- Verification protocols implemented before use

#### **VERIFIABLE STANDARD:**

Technological competence is not abstract. It can be evaluated through concrete questions:

- ✓ "What is a hallucination in LLM?" → Expected answer: coherent but factually false generation
- ✓ "What is the estimated hallucination rate of ChatGPT in legal queries?" → Answer: recent studies show 15-25% depending on legal domain
- ✓ "What verification protocols does your firm implement?" → Answer: must include cross-check against authorized databases

#### 4.1.2. Duty to Supervise "Non-Human" Assistants under ABA Rule 5.3

**ABA Model Rule 5.3** establishes:

> "A lawyer is responsible for the conduct of non-lawyer persons [associates, assistants, support staff] who are under the direct supervision of the lawyer and who perform functions of the legal practice, regarding conduct that if carried out by the lawyer would constitute a violation of these rules, when: (a) the lawyer orders the conduct or, knowing of such conduct, tacitly approves it; or (b) the lawyer is negligent in supervising the person."

#### **ANALOGOUS EXTENSION TO AI:**

ABA Opinion 512 extends the supervisory duty under Rule 5.3 to AI tools. Although ChatGPT is not a "non-lawyer person" in the technical sense, **it functions analogously**: it is an assistant that performs tasks under the attorney's control.

Under this reading:
- The attorney **must supervise** generative AI output
- Supervision **CANNOT be delegated** to the tool itself
- Supervisor **must implement independent** verification protocols
- Negligence in supervision constitutes Rule 5.3 violation

#### **MINIMUM SUPERVISION PROTOCOL:**

A minimum supervision protocol for generative AI in legal context must include:

**1. VERIFICATION AGAINST PRIMARY DATABASES**
- ✓ Every citation to a case, decision, or ruling must be verified against Westlaw, Lexis, or official database
- ✓ Every citation to law must be verified against official statutory text
- ✓ Verification must be performed by a person different from who generated the AI output

**2. CUTOFF DATE CONTROL**
- Clear documentation of the model's training cutoff date
- Awareness that any query about law developed post-cutoff has high risk
- Special vigilance for rapidly evolving legal areas (AI, data protection, cybersecurity)

**3. ABSTENTION PROTOCOL**
- Implementation of procedure where AI abstains from responding on high-risk queries
- Training of personnel on cases where AI should be proactively rejected

**4. SUPERVISION DOCUMENTATION**
- Record of who verified what output, when, using which primary databases
- Transferable documentation (in case of discovery or audit)

### 4.2. Ethical and Procedural Implications: The Duty of Candor to the Court

The **duty of candor to the court** is a fundamental ethical principle in Anglo-American law and, by analogy, in civil law systems.

#### **DEFINITION:**

The attorney has an affirmative obligation to:
- (i) not present evidence they know to be false
- (ii) not omit information they know to be decisive
- (iii) disclose to the court unfavorable legal authority

#### **APPLICATION TO FALSE CITATIONS:**

If an attorney presents a citation to a fictional case, they commit:
- Violation of duty of candor (presents as "authority" something that does not exist)
- Violation of duty of honesty (induces error in the court)
- Abuse of process (justice system depends on legal authorities being verifiable)

#### **PROTECTION OF ATTORNEY-CLIENT PRIVILEGE:**

The duty of candor creates tension with attorney-client privilege protection. Doctrine establishes:

- Privilege **does not protect** false facts if essential to case determination
- Privilege **does not protect** communication where client requests citing non-existent authority
- If client insists on fraudulent strategy, attorney has the right (and in some jurisdictions, obligation) to withdraw from representation

### 4.3. Global Regulatory Framework

#### 4.3.1. EU AI Act: Risk Classification and Data Governance

**Regulation (EU) 2024/1689** of the European Parliament and Council of June 13, 2024, laying down harmonized rules on artificial intelligence (hereinafter, "AI Act" or "RIA"), entered into force progressively, with **full application expected for August 2025**.

The AI Act establishes classification of AI systems by **"risk levels"**:

##### **PROHIBITED SYSTEMS (Article 5 AI Act):**
- Real-time biometric identification in public spaces (with public safety exceptions)
- Social scoring systems
- Cognitive manipulation of vulnerable groups

**Conclusion:** Prohibited systems cannot be used in law, even if verbally framed as "assisted research."

##### **HIGH-RISK SYSTEMS (Annex III AI Act):**

Include systems that impact fundamental rights or legal decisions. The AI Act does not yet specify whether "AI for legal research" falls into this category. However, conservative doctrinal analysis (reflected in SGS White Paper on AI Governance in the legal industry) suggests:

- **AI that generates legal analysis on specific cases** = HIGH RISK (directly impacts parties' rights)
- **AI that performs lexical search with verifiable result** = MEDIUM RISK
- **AI that generates non-decisive administrative content** = LOW RISK

For **HIGH-RISK** systems, AI Act Articles 9-15 require:
- Fundamental rights impact assessment
- Lifecycle documentation
- Meaningful human supervision (not nominal)
- Transparency measures
- Data governance ensuring accuracy

#### 4.3.2. Policies in Spain: CTEAJE Policy and the "Non-Substitution" Principle

The **General Council of the Spanish Bar** (CGAE) and the **Valencian Bar Association** (ICAV) jointly published a **White Paper on Artificial Intelligence and the Legal Profession** that includes diagnoses and specific recommendations for the sector in Spain.

The CGAE-ICAV White Paper establishes several operating principles:

#### **PRINCIPLE OF "NON-SUBSTITUTION":**

> "AI systems in legal context must be designed and used in a manner that amplifies the attorney's professional competence, not that replaces it. Particularly, in areas where AI cannot guarantee output verifiability (creative generation of analysis, extrapolation to scenarios not directly covered by jurisprudence), the attorney's responsibility and supervision DO NOT DIMINISH but intensify."

This translates into several practical requirements:

**(i) CERTIFIED COMPETENCE:**
- Attorney using generative AI must demonstrate competence in: specific technology, known limits, verification protocols

**(ii) DOCUMENTED PROTOCOL:**
- Every firm using generative AI must maintain documented policy specifying: which tools are authorized, for which tasks, under what supervision, what level of verification is required

**(iii) RESPONSIBILITY SEGREGATION:**
- AI output generation should not be performed by the same person who performs supervision
- Need for **"four-eyes"** (four-eyes principle)

**(iv) PERMANENT AUDIT:**
- Medium or large firms must conduct annual audit on AI use, including sampling of cases for verification of citation and reference accuracy

### 4.4. Sanctioning Consequences: Analysis of Fines, Reputational Damage, and Precedent for the Judiciary

#### **ECONOMIC CONSEQUENCES:**

In the Mata v. Avianca case, sanctions were:
- **Administrative fines:** USD $15,000 (USD $5,000 × 2 attorneys + USD $5,000 additional for law firm)
- **Court costs:** Though not reported in the opinion, typically sanctions hearing costs can add USD $3,000-$8,000

#### **ETHICAL CONSEQUENCES:**
- Permanent reprimand in professional file

#### **REPUTATIONAL DAMAGE:**

- Attorney name searches permanently linked to incident

#### **PRECEDENT ESTABLISHED:**

The Mata v. Avianca opinion has generated:

**(i) SUBSEQUENT JURISPRUDENCE**
- Following the opinion (June 2023), multiple U.S. courts have cited Mata in contexts where AI output accuracy is questioned

**(ii) CHANGES IN PROFESSIONAL STANDARDS**
- ABA issued Opinion 512 (July 2024) partially in response to the Mata precedent

**(iii) INTERNATIONAL REGULATOR REACTION**
- Authorities in EU, UK, and other jurisdictions have cited Mata when developing AI governance frameworks in law

## CHAPTER V: GOVERNANCE STRATEGIES AND TECHNICAL MITIGATION

### 5.1. AI Risk Management: Implementation of ISO/IEC 42001 Standard and NIST Framework

Hallucination risk mitigation is not simply an ad hoc "verification protocol." It requires implementation of formal risk management methodologies that align technical management, organizational governance, and regulatory compliance.

#### 5.1.1. ISO/IEC 42001 STANDARD

The **ISO/IEC 42001** standard (Information technology — Artificial intelligence management system) provides a certifiable framework for AI governance. It was published by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO) on December 5, 2023, and is increasingly adopted in the legal sector.

ISO/IEC 42001 requires:

**ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT (Clause 4):**
- Operating environment analysis: What AI systems are in use? Which are critical?
- Stakeholder identification: Clients, regulators, employees, public
- Determination of needs and expectations: Compliance, security, quality

**AI GOVERNANCE (Clause 5):**
- Responsibility assignment: Who is responsible for each AI system
- Documented policies: What rules govern AI use
- Risk assessment: Systematic methodology to identify risks
- Escalation procedures: What to do when AI system generates risky output

**LIFECYCLE (Clauses 6-8):**
- **Planning:** Before implementing AI, needs and risk assessment
- **Training:** If applicable, training data governance
- **Validation:** Testing before deployment
- **Deployment:** Controlled introduction procedures
- **Monitoring:** Continuous surveillance of performance in real operation
- **Withdrawal:** Procedures to discontinue system if risk becomes intolerable

**TECHNICAL AND OPERATIONAL MEASURES (Clause 8.2):**
Include: decision traceability, auditing, uncertainty documentation, human supervision.

#### 5.1.2. NIST AI RMF 1.0 (JULY 2024) AND ITS GENERATIVE AI PROFILE

The **National Institute of Standards and Technology** (NIST) of the United States published on **July 10, 2024** the **Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework version 1.0** (AI Risk Management Framework 1.0), along with a specific Profile for Generative AI.

The NIST Framework establishes **four continuous functions**:

##### **FUNCTION 1 - MAP (MAP):**

Identify AI systems, their intended use, operating context, and potential risks. In legal case, would include: "Which attorneys have access to ChatGPT? For what specific tasks? What are the consequences of error?"

##### **FUNCTION 2 - MEASURE (MEASURE):**

Evaluate AI system performance, error rate, deviations from expected behavior. Includes: documented hallucination tests, citation accuracy metrics in generation, output auditing.

##### **FUNCTION 3 - MANAGE (MANAGE):**

Implement controls and mitigations. Includes: RAG architecture (see Section 5.2), human supervision, escalation protocols, user training.

##### **FUNCTION 4 - GOVERN (GOVERN):**

Establish policies, responsibility assignment, accountability procedures. Includes: documentation of decisions on which systems to use, periodic audit, complaint procedures.

#### **GENERATIVE AI PROFILE (GENERATIVE AI PROFILE):**

The specific Profile recognizes that LLMs such as GPT-4, Claude, Bard have special characteristics:

**Characterized risks:**
- 🔴 Hallucination (generation of false facts)
- 🟠 Training bias (reproduction of demographic biases in training data)
- 🟠 Prompt injection (malicious user manipulates input to generate undesired output)
- 🟡 Privacy (regurgitation of sensitive training data)

**Specifically recommended measures:**
- Adversarial testing (attempting to make model hallucinate, measuring frequency)
- Bias auditing (reviewing output for discriminatory patterns)
- Technical limitations (restriction of certain query types)
- Documentation of training temporal cutoff
- Periodic evaluation of model performance changes (new versions may have different properties)

### 5.2. The RAG Paradigm (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)

#### 5.2.1. How to Anchor Responses in Verified Legal Databases

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** is an AI architecture that **directly solves** the hallucination problem documented in Mata v. Avianca. Instead of generating response based purely on model weights, RAG:

1. **RETRIEVES** relevant documents from verified database (e.g., official case database, statutory repository)
2. **PROVIDES** those documents as additional context to the model
3. **GENERATES** response that is explicitly anchored to retrieved documents
4. **INCLUDES CITATIONS** with pinpoint references (page, paragraph, exact phrase from source document)

#### **OPERATIONAL EXAMPLE:**

**User queries:** "Is there jurisprudence on statute of limitations under the Montreal Convention?"

**PROCESS WITHOUT RAG (Standard ChatGPT):**
```
→ Model generates: "Varghese v. China Southern Airlines, 925 F.3d 1431 (9th Cir. 2019) 
   establishes that statute of limitations under Convention begins from..."
→ Result: Plausible response, but completely false.
```

**PROCESS WITH RAG:**

**(1) Retrieval:** System searches in official case database. Identifies:
- Stratis v. Soriano, 717 F.3d 1014 (9th Cir. 2013)
- Katz v. Household International, Inc., 490 F.3d 1031 (9th Cir. 2007)
(real cases on Montreal Convention)

**(2) Augmentation:** Model receives:
- User input: "Is there jurisprudence on statute of limitations under Montreal Convention?"
- Retrieved context: [Complete texts of Stratis and Katz]
- Explicit instruction: "Respond based ONLY on provided documents. If you find no answer, indicate 'No information in retrieved documents.'"

**(3) Generation:** Model produces:

```
"According to Stratis v. Soriano, 717 F.3d 1014, ¶ 1032 (9th Cir. 2013), 
the statute of limitations under the Montreal Convention is two years from death 
or injury. The court in Katz v. Household International, Inc., 490 F.3d 
1031, ¶ 1045 (9th Cir. 2007), confirmed this interpretation."
```

**(4) Verifiability:** User (and supervising attorney) can:
- Click on Stratis to see complete decision
- Confirm that ¶ 1032 actually says what it says
- Verify citation is exact and complete

#### **RAG ADVANTAGES:**

✅ Eliminates hallucination on source content (because it generates response from real documents)
✅ Provides complete traceability (each assertion has documented origin)
✅ Reduces over-confidence (model "knows" it can only speak of retrieved documents)
✅ Facilitates human supervision (verification is mechanical: just confirm that retrieved documents actually say what the summary indicates)
✅ Compatible with ethical standards (attorney can be confident citations exist)

#### **RAG LIMITATIONS:**

❌ Requires access to high-quality databases (not all courts publish decisions online)
❌ Limited to knowledge domain in database (if query is about unindexed recent jurisprudence, RAG cannot answer)
❌ Implies operational cost (maintaining database, updating regularly)
❌ Does not solve misinterpretation problem (model may misinterpret what it reads)

#### 5.2.2. Optimization of Retrieval Phase and Structural Chunking

RAG effectiveness critically depends on how retrieved documents are structured. This is the technology called **"chunking"**:

#### **CHUNKING PROBLEM:**

A legal document (e.g., 30-page decision) contains hundreds of information fragments:
- Facts (pp. 2-5)
- Prior procedure (pp. 5-7)
- Each party's arguments (pp. 8-15)
- Court's law analysis (pp. 16-25)
- Conclusion and judgment (pp. 26-30)

If system retrieves the complete document, model may:
- Become confused by volume of information
- Assign incorrect weight to sections (e.g., giving equal weight to dicta —non-binding comments— as to holding —binding conclusion—)
- Mix case facts with general legal analysis

#### **SOLUTION: STRUCTURAL CHUNKING**

Documents are divided into optimized-sized and content **"chunks"**:

**LEVEL 1 - MACRO CHUNKS** (one section per chunk):
- "Case Facts"
- "Plaintiff Argument A"
- "Defendant Argument A"
- "Court's Judicial Analysis of Issue 1 (Statute of Limitations)"
- "Court's Judicial Analysis of Issue 2 (Damages)"
- "Conclusion"

**LEVEL 2 - MICRO CHUNKS** (subsections within analysis):
- "Introductory paragraph: citation of legal standard"
- "Analysis paragraph: application of standard to facts"
- "Conclusion paragraph: conclusion on this sub-point"

#### **METADATA:**

Each chunk includes structured metadata:

```yaml
Document: Stratis v. Soriano, 717 F.3d 1014
Court: United States Court of Appeals, Ninth Circuit
Date: 2013-09-20
Content Type: Legal Analysis
Topic: Montreal Convention - Statute of Limitations
Holding or Dicta: Holding (binding)
Specific Paragraph: 1032
Relevance to Law: Interpretation of Article 29(3) Montreal Convention
```

#### **ENHANCED RETRIEVAL:**

When user asks "What is the statute of limitations period under the Montreal Convention?", system:

1. **Searches metadata:** documents where "topic = Montreal Convention - Statute of Limitations" AND "content type = legal analysis"
2. **Prioritizes:** Cases where "holding or dicta = Holding" (binding decisions on exact point)
3. **Retrieves:** Specific micro chunks (e.g., "Paragraph 1032 of Stratis"), not complete document
4. **Presents:** Micro chunk + complete reference + instruction to model that focus should be on this paragraph

### 5.3. Hierarchically Aware Agents: Integration of Kelsen's Pyramid into AI Logic

Although RAG solves hallucination on facts, the interpretation problem persists: How to guarantee AI does not misinterpret legal authority?

The legal theory of **Hans Kelsen** provides a conceptual framework that can be integrated into AI logic. **Kelsen's Pyramid** establishes the hierarchy of norms:

#### **KELSEN'S PYRAMID (Hierarchy of Norms):**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│ LEVEL 1 (Superior):            │
│ Constitution (supreme source)  │
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│ LEVEL 2: Laws / Codes          │
│ (ordinary legislation)          │
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│ LEVEL 3: Regulations / Decrees │
│ (executive norms)               │
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│ LEVEL 4 (Inferior):            │
│ Judgments / Administrative     │
│ Decisions (application)        │
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

A lower-level norm is valid only if compatible with higher-level norms. A law cannot contradict constitution; a regulation cannot contradict law; a judgment cannot be arbitrary regarding law.

#### **INTEGRATION IN LEGAL AI:**

A **"hierarchically aware agent"** is an AI system that:

1. **Explicitly identifies** hierarchy of authorities in response
2. **Assigns differentiated weight** according to hierarchical level
3. **Detects and flags** hierarchical conflicts
4. **Requires human escalation** if conflict cannot be resolved automatically

#### **OPERATIONAL EXAMPLE:**

**Query:** "Is it permitted to use AI for legal research without supervision?"

**WITHOUT HIERARCHICAL CONSCIOUSNESS** (Standard ChatGPT):

```
Response: "Several judges have commented that AI can be valuable 
for research. Stratis v. Soriano suggests that..."
[Cites case as if it had direct normative weight]
```

**WITH HIERARCHICAL CONSCIOUSNESS:**

```
Structured response:

┌─ CONSTITUTIONAL LEVEL 
│  (Right to effective justice, due process)
│  └ No explicit constitutional provision on AI
│  └ Applicable: General principle of accessible justice
│
├─ LEGISLATIVE LEVEL (Primary norm)
│  ├ USA: Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, Rule 11 
│  │      (requires "certification" of briefs)
│  ├ Spain: AI Act 
│  │      (Art. 5, prohibited systems; Art. 9-15, high-risk systems)
│  ├ ABA Model Rules 
│  │      (Rule 1.1 competence; Rule 5.3 supervision)
│  └ CONCLUSION: Legislation requires supervision, 
│      competence, documentation
│
├─ REGULATORY LEVEL (Operational guidance)
│  ├ ABA Opinion 512 (2024): Technological competence obligation
│  ├ ISO/IEC 42001: AI governance standard
│  ├ NIST AI RMF: Risk management framework
│  └ CONCLUSION: Standards indicate mandatory meaningful supervision
│
└─ JURISPRUDENTIAL LEVEL 
   (Precedents, instructive although not binding in hierarchy)
   ├ Mata v. Avianca (SDNY 2023): Negligence in supervision → sanctions
   └ CONCLUSION: Jurisprudence confirms risk if supervision not implemented
```

#### **ADVANTAGES OF HIERARCHICAL CONSCIOUSNESS:**

✅ Prevents judgment from being cited as if it were law
✅ Requires analysis to begin with correct legal framework
✅ Automatically detects if advice is "contrary to law"
✅ Facilitates human review (attorney sees hierarchical structure and can identify if AI omitted or misinterpreted level)

### 5.4. The "Human Filter": Expert Supervision as an Irreducible and Non-Negotiable Component

**All previous technical strategies** (RAG, chunking, hierarchical consciousness) are **necessary but insufficient**. The **"human filter"** is the element that **cannot be delegated** to the machine.

According to **Alex Dantart's Technical Report** (CIO LittleJohn):

> "Hallucination in legal AI will not be technically resolved because it is an architectural characteristic of LLMs. What CAN be minimized is its IMPACT, through introduction of human verification steps where error cost is unacceptable."

#### **THREE-TIER SUPERVISION ARCHITECTURE:**

##### **TIER 1: INITIAL GENERATION AND PRESENTATION**

AI system generates complete response. Includes:
- Textual response
- Citations with pinpoint references
- Confidence indication (e.g., "High confidence," "Medium confidence," "Low confidence")
- Gap indication (if there are aspects of the query it could not respond to)

**Example output:**

```
Question: Is there jurisprudence on statute of limitations under Montreal Convention?

Response: [Stratis v. Soriano citation with detail]

Confidence: High (document is reported decision, citation is verifiable)

Gaps: I found no specific jurisprudence on interpretation of 
Montreal Convention in Spanish courts; additional research 
may be required in Spanish case database
```

##### **TIER 2: SPECIALIZED SUPERVISION (First-Level Review)**

Junior attorney or legal assistant (with AI use training) performs verification of:
- (i) Source existence: Do the cited cases exist?
- (ii) Citation accuracy: Does the decision say what the summary indicates?
- (iii) Relevance: Are the decisions actually relevant to the case?
- (iv) Completeness: Are there more relevant authorities the system did not retrieve?

**Processes:**
- For each citation, search in Westlaw/Lexis
- Read decision (not complete, but at least cited paragraph)
- Annotation in document: "✓ Verified - Accurate" or "✗ Error - [Description]"
- Documentation in file: Who verified, date, result

**Typical documentation:**

```markdown
# CITATION VERIFICATION
**Case:** Mata v. Avianca, Inc.
**Performed by:** [Legal Assistant Name]
**Date:** 2024-02-15

## Citation 1: Stratis v. Soriano, 717 F.3d 1014 (9th Cir. 2013)
- **Verified in:** Westlaw
- **Status:** ✓ Accurate - ¶ 1032 confirmed
- **Note:** Case is binding authority in applicable jurisdiction

## Citation 2: Katz v. Household International, Inc., 490 F.3d 1031 (9th Cir. 2007)
- **Verified in:** Westlaw
- **Status:** ✓ Accurate - But note it was partially overruled by [Decision X, 2015]
- **Note:** Must include in response that doctrine was limited by subsequent cases

## Citation 3: Varghese v. China Southern Airlines, 925 F.3d 1431 (9th Cir. 2019)
- **Verified in:** Westlaw, Lexis, Google Scholar, RECAP
- **Status:** ✗ DOES NOT EXIST - Case is FICTITIOUS
- **Note:** CRITICAL - Completely remove from brief. Investigate why AI generated it.
```

##### **TIER 3: EXPERT REVIEW (Senior Review)**

Senior attorney (at least 5+ years of experience in specific legal area) performs review of:
- (i) Soundness of legal analysis (beyond fact verification)
- (ii) Completeness of authority treatment (Is there contradictory jurisprudence not retrieved?)
- (iii) Applicability to specific case (Does AI analysis actually address client's problem?)
- (iv) Procedural strategy (Does this analysis advance client's position?)

**Documentation:**

```markdown
# EXPERT REVIEW
**Case:** Mata v. Avianca, Inc.
**Performed by:** [Senior Attorney Name]
**Specialization:** Transportation Law
**Date:** 2024-02-20

## FINDINGS:

1. **Analysis of statute of limitations under Montreal Convention:** 
   Correct in factual and jurisprudential terms.

2. **Completeness:** AI analysis retrieved Stratis and Katz, which are main authorities. 
   Additionally, I identify [Recent Decision X, 2024] that modifies 
   Stratis' interpretation. 
   **RECOMMENDATION:** Include this authority.

3. **Applicability:** Our case presents variable [X] that is not directly 
   covered by Stratis/Katz. 
   **RECOMMENDATION:** Perform additional analysis on how [X] interacts 
   with holding of these cases.

4. **CONCLUSION:** AI output is solid but incomplete. Requires complementary 
   analysis before presenting to court. **Estimated:** 2-3 hours of additional 
   senior attorney research.
```

#### **BENEFIT OF THREE-TIER ARCHITECTURE:**

✅ **Tier 1** (AI): Efficiency (rapid research of base facts)
✅ **Tier 2** (Junior + verification): Quality control (ensures no false facts)
✅ **Tier 3** (Senior + analysis): Expert judgment (ensures legal reasoning soundness)
✅ **Complete documentation:** If there is a later problem, can trace exactly where supervision failed
✅ **Scalability:** Architecture allows senior attorneys to delegate fact verification to juniors, maintaining conceptual analysis supervision

#### **MATA CASE UNDER THIS ARCHITECTURE:**

Attorneys used: **Tier 1 only** (AI generated citations) without Tier 2 (citation verification) or Tier 3 (expert review).

If they had implemented this architecture:
- Tier 2 would have immediately identified that "Varghese" does not exist
- Tier 3 would have reviewed brief before filing with the court

The time cost of a junior attorney (USD $50-$100/hour) would have been **minimal** compared to subsequent sanctions (USD $15,000+).
