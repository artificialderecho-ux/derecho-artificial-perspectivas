## Abstract

The ruling of the Superior Court of Bogotá dated December 2, 2025, issued in the case against Luis Alberto Contreras Fandiño for the crime of aggravated sexual assault of a person incapable of resisting, marks a milestone in Colombian jurisprudence on the use of artificial intelligence in the administration of justice. For the first time, a criminal appellate chamber declares the nullity of a conviction due to the irresponsible use of generative AI tools, whose termed 'hallucinations' contaminated the entirety of the typicity analysis, congruence, and evidentiary assessment with non-existent legal sources. This commentary analyzes the legal basis of the decision, its coherence with the current regulatory framework, and its implications for the exercise of judicial function in Colombia.

## I. Problem Statement

The incorporation of large language models (LLMs) in judicial practice has generated, globally, a structural tension between technological efficiency and due process guarantees. The efficiency these tools promise faces a specific and well-documented risk: the generation of plausible but false information, technically termed 'hallucination.' When this risk materializes in the reasoning of a judge who has delegated their decisory function to AI without rigorous control, the result is not a mere hermeneutic error but a motivation pathology that empties the constitutional guarantee of effective judicial protection of its content.

The case at hand illustrates this scenario with dramatic clarity. The first-instance judge rendered a conviction to 198 months' imprisonment in a process of extremely serious nature—sexual assault of a person with a disability—supporting their reasoning on at least sixteen legal sources that, when verified by the appellate court, prove to be non-existent, false, or distorted in their content. The plurality and systematicity of errors led the Court to conclude, based on the highest probability, that the judgment was produced using generative AI tools without adequate human supervision.

## II. The Constitutional Standard of Motivation and its Technological Projection

The obligation to motivate judicial decisions constitutes not a formal requirement but a substantive guarantee of the Rule of Law. The Constitutional Court, in judgment C-037 of 1996, established that the legitimacy of the judicial apparatus rests on the trust citizens place in their institutions, trust that only consolidates when decisions reflect real and deep knowledge of situations submitted for determination. This constitutional mandate translates, in criminal procedure, into the requirement of Article 162 of Law 906 of 2004, which demands factual, evidentiary, and legal basis with indication of reasons for assessment and dismissal of evidence.

The Criminal Cassation Chamber of the Supreme Court has constructed a taxonomy of motivation defects that the Court applies in this case: absolute absence, incomplete motivation, dilogical or ambivalent, and sophistical or apparent. Pursuant to ruling CSJ SP2956 of 2018, only the first three vices lead to nullity, while the fourth—sophistical motivation—enables a substitute decision. The Superior Court of Bogotá identifies the vice produced by AI as constituting sophistical or apparent motivation, but concludes that, given that falsity permeates both the legal basis and evidentiary assessment foundations, it is impossible to issue a substitute decision without recognizing in the Court a de facto first instance, with the correlative suppression of the double instance constitutionally guaranteed.

## III. The Colombian Regulatory Framework on Judicial AI and its Application

The commented decision operates in a rapidly forming regulatory context. Judgment T-323 of 2024 of the Constitutional Court established twelve guiding criteria for the use of AI in judicial chambers, among which the criterion of non-substitution of human rationality occupies a central place: AI tools cannot be used to substitute the logical reasoning that corresponds to the judge in the interpretation of facts, evidence assessment, motivation, and decision adoption. Said ruling instructed the Superior Council of the Judiciary to establish specific guidelines, which materialized in Agreement PCSJA24-12243 of December 16, 2024.

The Agreement categorically prohibits the use of AI for the assessment of evidentiary means, scrutiny of facts, making value judgments, solving legal problems, and motivation of decisions, when done exclusively based on responses offered by the tool. The commented ruling is the first to make these prohibitions effective in the realm of procedural nullity.

## IV. Nullity as a Necessary Legal Consequence

The Court's decision to declare nullity based on Article 457 of the Criminal Procedure Code is legally coherent for three structural reasons.

First, because the violation of the duty of motivation constitutes a form of injury to the right of defense, insofar as it prevents the accused and their defense counsel from exercising effective contradiction against the real grounds of conviction. Second, because the vice was neither caused nor aided by the party alleging nullity—the defense—which satisfies the requirement of trascendentality that jurisprudence has established as a prerequisite for its declaration. Third, because nullity is the only remedy that preserves the guarantee of double instance, compelling the natural judge to produce a genuinely motivated decision subject to real appeal.

It must be underscored, moreover, that the Court acts ex officio in declaring nullity, exercising the priority principle that obliges the appellate court to resolve substantial irregularities affecting due process before addressing the merits of the appeal. This proceeding has support in the jurisprudence of the Criminal Cassation Chamber since decision CSJ SP of December 2, 2007, case file 28432.

## V. Comparative Law: The Argentine Precedent

The Court incorporates into its analysis the decision of the Criminal Chamber of Esquel (Chubut, Argentina) of October 15, 2025, which declared the nullity of a judicial provision after detecting in its text the instruction of a generative AI: 'Here is point IV redrafted, without citations, ready to copy and paste.' The mention of this precedent, although declared non-binding, fulfills an argumentative consolidation function: the international legal community converges in that the delegation of judicial reasoning to AI systems, without supervision, produces constitutionally defective provisions.

## VI. Systemic Implications and Prospective View

The studied ruling will foreseeably have effects beyond the specific case. First, it establishes a reinforced duty of diligence for judicial operators using AI: verification of each jurisprudential, normative, and doctrinal citation incorporated into their provisions is not optional but exigible under penalty of nullity. Second, it reinforces the relevance of Agreement PCSJA24-12243 and anticipates possible disciplinary consequences for those who violate it, as evidenced by the referral of copies to the Sectional Judicial Discipline Commission ordered in the second resolutive paragraph. Third, it brings up the question of criminal prescription: a process initiated in 2014, with facts from 2013, in which there is still no final judgment, dangerously approaches the prescription limit, with serious consequences for the victim's rights.

## VII. Conclusion

The commented ruling constitutes a paradigmatic pronouncement that articulates, for the first time in the Colombian criminal system, the doctrine of procedural nullity with the irresponsible use of artificial intelligence tools. Its most significant contribution does not lie in the condemnation of technological use per se—which the Court expressly encourages—but in the affirmation that judicial rationality is non-delegable: the judge may rely on AI for management and support tasks, but can never transfer to it the function of reasoning, assessing, and deciding. When this occurs, the product is not a judgment but a simulation of a judgment, legally ineffective and incapable of satisfying the constitutional requirements of due process.
